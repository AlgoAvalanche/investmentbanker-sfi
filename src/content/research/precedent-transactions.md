# Research Notes: Precedent Transactions Analysis

## Sources Consulted

### 1. Wall Street Prep - Precedent Transaction Analysis (Transaction Comps Tutorial)
- URL: https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/precedent-transaction-analysis/
- Key content: WSP's guide is the most comprehensive practitioner-oriented resource. It details the full five-step process (compile transactions, market research, input financial data, calculate multiples, apply multiples) and emphasizes that enterprise value multiples are preferred in M&A work because they are capital structure neutral. WSP explicitly states that control premiums "typically range from 25% to 50%+" above unaffected market trading prices, and that hostile takeovers command higher premiums than friendly acquisitions. The guide distinguishes strategic buyers (higher premiums due to synergies) from financial buyers (lower, standalone-value-focused premiums) and notes that all-cash deals generally command higher valuations than stock-based consideration. WSP also stresses that "quality over quantity" applies - a handful of truly comparable transactions is more informative than a long list of tangential deals.

### 2. Macabacus - Precedent Transactions Analysis
- URL: https://macabacus.com/valuation/precedent-transactions
- Key content: Macabacus, widely used by practitioners in deal work, frames precedent transactions as revealing "control premiums" that are absent from trading comps, and notes that every transaction has unique circumstances making direct comparability rare. The guide emphasizes cross-verification: do not trust SDC database accuracy - always verify transaction values against news runs, research reports, and SEC filings. Macabacus notes that financial databases "often have inaccurate or imprecise metrics" and that analyst reports must be sourced from before the announcement date to get clean forward multiples. For the statistical output, they recommend presenting minimum, 25th percentile, median, mean, 75th percentile, and maximum multiples - never a single point estimate.

### 3. Street of Walls - Precedent Transaction Analysis
- URL: https://www.streetofwalls.com/finance-training-courses/investment-banking-technical-training/precedent-transaction-analysis/
- Key content: Street of Walls provides a practitioner-level walkthrough using an actual J.Crew transaction as a worked example: offer price of $43.50/share, equity value of $3,120M, enterprise value of $2,828M, LTM revenue of $1.711B, and LTM EBITDA of $320.78M. The guide presents the premium calculation formula explicitly: Premium Paid (%) = (Acquisition Price / Last Trading Price - 1) x 100. It stresses that when computing equity value from a stock-for-stock deal, analysts must calculate the exchange ratio (Offer Price Per Share / Acquirer Share Price). It also flags that precedent transaction analysis focuses on the value of a business "as of the time an acquisition can be completed" rather than today, because deals take months or even years to close - a nuance often missed by candidates.

### 4. Career Principles - Precedent Transaction Analysis
- URL: https://www.careerprinciples.com/resources/precedent-transaction-analysis
- Key content: Career Principles identifies one of the most common analyst errors: mistakenly prioritizing the acquirer's characteristics rather than the target's comparability when screening transactions. The guide recommends using median multiples rather than averages to minimize outlier distortion, and presenting results as valuation ranges using the 25th-to-75th percentile band. It describes the "football field" visualization as the standard output format comparing precedent transactions against DCF, comparable company analysis, equity research price targets, and 52-week trading range. The guide states that when comparable transactions are scarce or non-comparable, analysts should assign lower weighting to this method relative to DCF or trading comps in the final valuation conclusion.

### 5. StableBread - Precedent Transaction Analysis
- URL: https://stablebread.com/precedent-transaction-analysis/
- Key content: StableBread provides one of the most technically detailed guides on data requirements and equity value calculation mechanics. For mixed consideration deals, it gives a concrete worked example: a $15/share offer (60% stock, 40% cash) on 2M shares produces a $12M cash component and an $18M stock component for a $30M total equity value, with an exchange ratio of 0.36. The guide establishes a hierarchy for data sources: SEC filings (8-K, proxy statements, Schedule 14A, 10-K/10-Q) are primary; company press releases are secondary; financial databases (SDC, Capital IQ, FactSet, PrivCo) are tertiary and require verification. It recommends a minimum of 5-10 high-quality comparable deals, with a preferred timeframe of trailing twelve months (TTM) and an acceptable outer limit of 3-5 years. It uses a real Mersana Therapeutics oncology acquisition case study to illustrate how analysts identify 10 precedent transactions across SEC filings and industry databases.

### 6. Wall Street Playbook - Trading Comps vs. Precedent Transactions
- URL: https://www.wallstreetplaybook.org/blog/trading-comps-vs-precedent-transactions
- Key content: This source provides the clearest articulation of the valuation hierarchy: precedent transactions typically yield the highest values (control premium included), followed by DCF (assumption-dependent), with trading comps lowest (minority, no-control value). The control premium of 20-40% is broken down by buyer type - strategic buyers pay 30-40% because they can realize synergies, while financial buyers pay 20-30% because they are focused on standalone value. The guide flags a key interview red flag: selecting comps that are "too broad" - stating two companies are comparable simply because they are both "tech companies" lacks rigor and signals weak analytical thinking. The preferred interview answer when forced to choose one valuation method is to emphasize DCF as the foundation, then cross-check against comps for reasonableness.

### 7. Breaking Into Wall Street - Precedent Transaction Analysis
- URL: https://breakingintowallstreet.com/kb/valuation/precedent-transaction-analysis/
- Key content: BIWS frames the standard transaction selection window as 8-15 comparable deals from the past 3-5 years in similar industries. It confirms that deal premiums typically run 20-40% above trading multiples due to control and synergy capture. The guide highlights that Bloomberg, FactSet, and Capital IQ are the primary institutional databases for deal data, with Capital IQ being the most widely used in IB. The median is explicitly preferred over the mean because it is resistant to outlier distortion. When presenting the valuation output, analysts should establish a low, mid, and high-end range rather than a single implied value.

## Key Themes

- Precedent transactions analysis (also called "transaction comps" or "M&A comps") values a target by examining what acquirers actually paid for comparable businesses in past deals, inherently capturing the control premium
- The control premium - typically 20-40% above the unaffected trading price, sometimes 25-50%+ in competitive processes - is the central distinction from trading comps and the reason precedent transactions almost always yield the highest valuations in the football field
- Enterprise value multiples (EV/Revenue, EV/EBITDA) are preferred over equity value multiples in M&A work because they are capital structure neutral and enable more direct comparison across differently leveraged companies
- Transaction selection quality matters more than quantity: 5-10 highly comparable deals are more useful than 20+ loosely related ones; recency is critical because multiples from different macro environments are not directly applicable
- Data sourcing requires primary source verification - financial databases (SDC, Capital IQ, FactSet) contain errors and must always be cross-checked against SEC filings (8-K, proxy statements, Schedule 14A) and company press releases
- The three primary valuation methods - precedent transactions, comparable company analysis (trading comps), and DCF - are used together in a football field chart; no single method is used in isolation
- Buyer type materially affects multiples: strategic buyers pay more (synergy realization) than financial/PE buyers (standalone value focus); auction processes produce higher prices than negotiated deals; all-cash consideration commands higher valuations than stock
- LTM (Last Twelve Months / TTM) financials are standard; forward projections are avoided because they are difficult to obtain and may embed synergy assumptions that distort comparability
- Calendarizing financial data is required when target companies have different fiscal year-ends to ensure apples-to-apples comparison

## Interview-Ready Facts

- Control premium typical range: 20-40% above unaffected share price; can exceed 50% in highly competitive auction processes
- Strategic buyers: 30-40% typical premium range; financial buyers: 20-30% typical premium range
- Precedent transactions generally produce the highest valuation output in the football field - higher than trading comps and often higher than DCF
- Control premium formula: (Offer Price Per Share / "Unaffected" Stock Price) - 1
- Premium paid formula: (Acquisition Price / Last Trading Price - 1) x 100
- Enterprise Value = Equity Value + Total Debt - Cash +/- Minority Interest / Preferred Equity
- Equity Value for public targets = Offer Price Per Share x Diluted Shares Outstanding
- Standard output statistics: minimum, 25th percentile, median, mean, 75th percentile, maximum - always present a range, never a single figure
- Recommended peer group size: 8-15 transactions (BIWS), minimum 5-10 high-quality deals (StableBread)
- Recommended time window: past 3-5 years; TTM is most relevant; anything older than 5 years requires explicit justification
- Use median multiples (not mean) to minimize outlier distortion
- J.Crew real example: $43.50/share offer, $3.12B equity value, $2.83B enterprise value, $1.71B LTM revenue, $320.78M LTM EBITDA
- Data source hierarchy: (1) SEC filings - 8-K, proxy statements, Schedule 14A, 10-K/10-Q; (2) company press releases; (3) financial databases - SDC, Capital IQ, FactSet (require verification)
- All-cash deals command higher valuations than stock-based or mixed consideration deals
- Hostile takeovers produce higher premiums than friendly acquisitions
- Auction processes produce higher purchase prices than negotiated/bilateral transactions
- The football field standard output compares: precedent transactions, trading comps, DCF, equity research price targets, and 52-week trading range
- When comparable transactions are scarce, assign lower weighting to this method in the final valuation conclusion
- Analyst reports used for forward multiples must be sourced from before the announcement date - post-announcement research reflects deal pricing, not standalone value

## Common Mistakes

- Screening based on the acquirer's characteristics rather than the target's - the target company profile drives comparability, not the buyer
- Including transactions from materially different macroeconomic environments without adjustment or justification
- Selecting transactions that are too broad in industry definition (e.g., "both are tech companies") without granular sector and operational alignment
- Using the mean instead of the median, which allows outlier transactions (overpaid deals, distressed sales) to skew the output
- Trusting database figures without primary source verification - SDC, Capital IQ, and FactSet frequently contain inaccurate or imprecise metrics
- Using post-announcement research reports to calculate forward multiples - these already reflect the deal price and are not independent estimates
- Mixing LTM data with prior fiscal year data across different transactions in the same analysis
- Including one-time / non-recurring items (restructuring charges, asset sale gains, litigation settlements) in EBITDA or revenue figures, distorting multiples
- Overlooking minority interest line items that introduce unrelated income/expense into comparisons
- Failing to verify that the pre-announcement stock price is "unaffected" - prices may already be inflated by leaks, rumors, or insider trading activity
- Excluding convertible securities from diluted share counts, understating equity value
- Failing to calendarize financial data when target companies have non-calendar fiscal year-ends
- Using forward projections instead of actual TTM/LTM figures - forward estimates are hard to obtain and may embed synergy assumptions
- Ignoring transaction structure: Section 338(h)(10) tax elections and other deal terms affect effective economics but are not captured in headline multiples
- Including deals where the buyer clearly overpaid (destroying shareholder value) without flagging this as a distorting factor
- Presenting a single-point valuation estimate rather than a range derived from percentile statistics
