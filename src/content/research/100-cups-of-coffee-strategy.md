## Sources Consulted

### 1. Mergers & Inquisitions - The Investment Banking Informational Interview: The Full Guide
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banking-informational-interview/

The definitive practitioner-written guide to IB informational interviews. States that doing this correctly requires 300-500 total emails and a 5-10 hour/week commitment over 6 months - totaling 100+ hours. Recommends targeting junior-to-mid-level bankers (Analysts, Associates, early VPs) first, not MDs. Initial email must stay under 5 sentences. After no response: follow up 2-3 times over 3-4 weeks, then deprioritize and check back in 1-2 months. A key tactical point: do not propose specific meeting times in the initial cold email - wait for their response first.

### 2. Mergers & Inquisitions - Investment Banking Networking: Strategies, Tips & Templates
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banking/recruitment/networking/

Covers three networking channels: (1) informational interviews for relationship-building over time, (2) cold emailing for direct position asks, (3) cold calling (least effective). The guide stresses that bankers live in their email inboxes - email beats LinkedIn and phone. The full cycle runs 3-6 months before recruiting: find bankers on LinkedIn and alumni networks, verify emails, request meetings with 2-3 follow-up attempts, conduct 15-20 minute conversations with heavy listening, make a "mini-ask" (resume pass or referral), then follow up before making the direct ask during recruiting season. Six critical mistakes identified: trying too hard to impress, using LinkedIn DMs instead of email, writing overly long messages, failing to follow up persistently, asking inappropriate questions, and not making a direct request for help.

### 3. IB Interview Questions - Coffee Chat Etiquette: DOs and DON'Ts for IB Networking
**URL:** https://ibinterviewquestions.com/blog/coffee-chat-etiquette-ib-networking

Comprehensive etiquette guide structured around the full coffee chat arc. Key strategic principle: the 80/20 rule - the banker should talk 80% of the time, the candidate 20%. Quality-over-quantity framing: aim for 30-50 coffee chats total with 3-5 contacts per target firm to build genuine advocates, not just checkbox contacts. Bankers are actively evaluating judgment, professionalism, and client-facing potential throughout - it is an informal screen. Never directly ask for an interview referral or endorsement during the call. Thank-you note must be sent within 24 hours and reference specific things they said. Stay in touch every 2-3 months with relevant updates, keeping follow-up messages to 4-6 sentences.

### 4. University of Virginia Economics Department - Coffee Chat Advice for Networking (Investment Banking Focused)
**URL:** https://economics.virginia.edu/coffee-chat-advice-networking-investment-banking-focused

Academic-practitioner hybrid guide emphasizing three sequential goals: (1) make a positive impression, (2) learn about opportunities and timelines, (3) get introduced to additional contacts. Goal 1 is a prerequisite for goals 2 and 3 - you cannot skip the relationship step. Preparation standard is 10-15 questions ready, opened in a document before the call. Questions must be specific to that person's career and expertise - never ask anything answerable by a Google search. Question categories to cycle through: their experience, firm specifics, lifestyle and culture (only once rapport is established), then recruiting details. Around the 30-minute mark, check the time and prepare to close gracefully.

### 5. Wall Street Oasis - Coffee Chat Advice (Forum + Templates)
**URL:** https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/investment-banking/coffee-chat-advice-0
**Supporting URL:** https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/resources/templates/word-templates/cold-email-template

WSO practitioner community consensus: the primary goal of a first coffee chat is simply to be remembered positively by name. Do not ask for a job or opportunity during the first interaction. Being "reasonably fun to talk to" and likable is more important than demonstrating technical knowledge - do not fire off banking questions for 30 minutes straight. After 5-10 informational interviews, candidates typically develop mutual connections, earn referrals, or get flagged by HR. Cold email timing tip from the community: send Tuesday-Thursday, 8-10 a.m., to maximize open rates. Follow-up after one week with a single brief message bringing the email back to the top of their inbox.

## Key Themes

**1. Volume is non-negotiable**
The 100-cups metaphor is grounded in real numbers. Practitioners consistently cite 300-500 outreach emails and 100+ total hours of work to see results. 30-50 actual coffee chats is the functional target, not 5-10. The phrase "100 cups of coffee" captures this scale without being intimidating.

**2. Relationship-first, ask-second**
Every source reinforces the same sequencing: relationship before ask. The first call is purely to be remembered positively. The direct ask (resume pass, referral, interview) comes 2-3 months later, once rapport exists. Violating this sequence is the single most common and costly error candidates make.

**3. Targeting junior bankers is strategic, not second-best**
Analysts and Associates are more accessible and more likely to respond. They remember recruiting recently. They often have more influence over resume reviews than candidates assume. MDs come later, after you have warm intros.

**4. The call is an informal interview screen**
Bankers are evaluating communication skills, professionalism, and client-facing potential throughout. The casual framing is partially a fiction. Candidates who treat it as a relaxed chat and not a screen often hurt themselves without realizing it.

**5. Follow-up consistency is what separates successful networkers**
Sending the initial email is easy. Maintaining contact every 2-3 months with relevant, brief updates - and timing the direct ask correctly relative to recruiting season - is where most candidates drop off.

**6. Personalization beats volume at the individual level**
While total volume must be high, each individual email must be personalized. Generic emails get ignored. Reference their specific deals, career path, or school. This is not optional.

**7. The 80/20 conversation rule**
The banker should talk 80% of the time. Candidates who dominate the conversation or interview the banker with rapid-fire questions consistently underperform those who ask one good question and listen deeply.

## Interview-Ready Facts

- 300-500 cold emails is the realistic volume needed to build a meaningful network from scratch, per Mergers & Inquisitions
- 5-10 hours per week over 6 months = 100+ total hours minimum investment for serious IB recruiting
- Start outreach 6-12 months before recruiting begins; 3-6 months before is the minimum viable window
- 10-15 minutes is the standard coffee chat length; 15-20 minutes per M&I guidance
- 30-50 total coffee chats is the quality target; 3-5 contacts per target firm builds advocates
- After 5-10 informational interviews candidates typically receive referrals or get flagged by HR
- Send cold emails Tuesday-Thursday, 8-10 a.m. for best open rates
- Thank-you note within 24 hours is the non-negotiable standard
- Follow-up cadence post-chat: every 2-3 months until recruiting season
- Initial cold email: 5 sentences maximum, no proposed times, clear ask at the end
- Initial outreach list: start with 20-30 names before sending any emails

## Common Mistakes

1. **Asking for the job or referral on the first call** - destroys rapport before it is built; the direct ask must come later in the relationship cycle

2. **Targeting MDs first** - they are least accessible and least likely to respond; start with Analysts and Associates who remember recruiting recently

3. **Sending overly long cold emails** - anything over 5-7 sentences drops response rate sharply; brevity signals you respect their time

4. **Using LinkedIn messages instead of direct email** - LinkedIn DMs are treated as lower priority; find the direct email address

5. **Proposing specific meeting times in the first email** - presumptuous; wait for a positive response, then propose times

6. **Asking googleable questions** - asking about firm size, office locations, or deal counts signals you did no preparation; ask only what requires their personal experience to answer

7. **Dominating the conversation** - candidates who talk more than 20% of the time fail the informal screen; questions should be designed to get the banker talking

8. **Treating coffee chats as checkboxes** - going through the motions without genuine curiosity is immediately apparent; bankers have done hundreds of these calls

9. **No follow-up after the call** - failing to send a thank-you note, or letting 6+ months pass without contact, means the relationship goes cold before recruiting begins

10. **Inconsistent follow-up cadence** - sending one great cold email and then disappearing is one of the most common failure modes; the candidates who win internships are those who maintain contact at 2-3 month intervals with brief, relevant updates

11. **Asking about compensation or hours too early** - signals wrong priorities; lifestyle questions belong later in the conversation, once rapport is established

12. **One-firm-only networking** - candidates who network exclusively at their top-choice firm have no fallback options; spread across 3-5 target firms minimum
