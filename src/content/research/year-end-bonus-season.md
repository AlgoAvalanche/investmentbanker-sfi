## Key Themes

### 1. The Bonus Season Calendar
- Year-end bonuses cover the prior calendar year (January-December performance)
- Most banks announce and pay bonuses in January-March of the following year
- "Bonus season" colloquially refers to January-February, when most BB and EB bankers receive their payouts
- Some regional and mid-market banks (RBC, Raymond James, Scotia) pay earlier - December or early January - creating lateral market timing advantages
- The period from October through December is a "dry season" for lateral recruiting as professionals sit tight waiting for payouts

### 2. The Stub Bonus - What New Analysts Actually Receive First
- Analysts hired in June/July start mid-calendar year and cannot receive a full year-end bonus in their first January
- A "stub bonus" (also called a "partial year bonus") is issued in January of their first full calendar year, covering the ~5-6 months they worked before December 31
- Typical stub bonus range: $25,000-$40,000 at bulge brackets
- Stub bonuses are prorated approximations, not a precise fraction of the full-year bonus
- After the stub, the analyst's bonus cycle aligns with the standard calendar year
- MBA associates joining in June/July receive a similar stub structure; typical MBA stub: $35,000-$50,000

### 4. The Bucket System
- Banks use 3-5 performance buckets to determine bonus as a percentage of base
- Typical analyst buckets: top (90-105% of base), upper-middle (80-90%), middle (70-80%), lower-middle (60-70%), bottom (50-65%)
- For a first-year analyst at $110K base: top bucket = ~$105K bonus; bottom bucket = ~$60K bonus - a $45K gap
- Bucket placement determined by: individual performance and attitude, group revenue, overall bank profitability
- Critical nuance: a top-bucket analyst in a weak-revenue group can earn less in absolute dollars than a mid-bucket analyst in a record-revenue group

### 5. Firm Type Differentials
- Elite boutiques (Centerview, Evercore, PJT Partners, Perella Weinberg): 20-40% premium over bulge brackets at analyst/associate levels; 100% cash at some firms
- Bulge brackets (Goldman, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan, etc.): the market benchmark for published ranges
- Middle-market banks: 15-25% below bulge bracket for equivalent titles
- Bank of America 2025 outlier: paid Year 3 Associates $110K-$150K vs. $200K-$300K+ at comparable firms

### 6. Senior Skew in 2025 Bonus Season
- 2025 payouts showed a pronounced seniority skew: +5% for analysts/associates, +10-15% for VPs/directors, +25%+ for MDs
- VP3 bonuses at bulge brackets jumped ~56% (from ~$309K to ~$483K average) driven by retention pressure
- Senior MD and group head compensation can reach $5M+; median MD range $1M-$2M
- At some banks, MD bonuses exceeded 120% of base salary

### 7. Deferred Compensation
- Analysts receive 100% cash - no deferred component
- Deferred compensation begins at associate level (10-20%) and increases with seniority
- Deferred amounts vest over 2-5 years and are forfeited upon voluntary departure before vesting
- This creates "golden handcuffs" - the strategic retention mechanism intensifies at VP and MD levels
- Deferred comp is typically bank stock or cash equivalents on a vesting schedule

## Common Mistakes / Misconceptions

1. **Assuming bonus = percentage of deal fees closed.** Junior bankers receive bonuses from a pool allocated top-down, not a direct commission on deals they worked. A first-year analyst who staffed a $500M acquisition does not receive a percentage of that fee.

2. **Confusing stub bonus with first-year bonus.** The stub bonus ($25K-$40K) is not the same as a full year-end bonus. New analysts sometimes expect a full bonus in their first January; the full cycle begins only in the second January.

3. **Treating published ranges as guaranteed.** The bucket system creates a $40K+ spread on the same base salary. Published comp surveys report averages and ranges, not guaranteed outcomes.

4. **Ignoring group-level revenue as a lever.** Individual performance within a weak-revenue group is insufficient for top-dollar bonuses. Group placement matters enormously - a strong year in M&A advisory vs. a quiet year in restructuring produces materially different bonus pools.

5. **Assuming elite boutique = better comp at all levels.** EBs pay 20-40% premiums at analyst/associate levels, but MD comp structures are more variable and not universally superior to bulge brackets.

6. **Forgetting deferred comp in total compensation math.** An associate receiving a $200K bonus where 20% is deferred for 3 years is not equivalent to one receiving $200K in cash - particularly if they plan to leave for private equity within 2 years.

7. **Conflating bonus season across markets.** U.S. banks pay January-March; European banks follow different calendars; Asian offices may have different fiscal year structures. A "January bonus" is a U.S.-centric assumption.

8. **Expecting 2025's senior skew to be permanent.** The 2025 MD-heavy bonus dynamic reflects specific cycle conditions (post-drought M&A rebound, retention pressure). Historically, junior and senior bonuses move more in tandem.

## Additional Context: Bonus Season Psychology and Culture

- Bonus day is one of the most anxious days of the banking year - the announcement of bucket placement and dollar amount typically comes in a brief manager meeting
- Inter-firm comparison culture is intense; Wall Street Oasis megathreads aggregate real-time data as bankers post their numbers on anonymous forums within hours of receiving them
- The gap between what analysts expected and what they received is a leading driver of lateral movement - many analyst-to-PE moves are triggered by a disappointing bonus
- Senior bankers use deferred comp strategically to retain high performers who might otherwise leave; unvested deferred balances are a retention tool built into the compensation architecture
- "Dry season" lateral paralysis from October-December means that most good lateral opportunities surface in February-April after bonuses clear
