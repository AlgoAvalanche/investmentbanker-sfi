### 1. Wall Street Oasis - "Investment Banking Analyst: 15 Things I Wish I Knew"
**URL:** https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/investment-banking/investment-banking-analyst-15-things-i-wish-i-knew
**Type:** Community forum / practitioner insight
**Accessibility:** Paywalled (403), content extracted via search snippet

Key content:
- Double and triple checking work is critical - use a highlighter on printed output and check off each change
- Analysts must communicate their workload clearly to seniors or risk being overwhelmed silently
- Always take notes during calls and meetings with senior bankers - they do not like repeating themselves
- Email discipline: read emails aloud before sending, leave the "To" field blank until ready to send, have a peer analyst review it, run spell check, use Ctrl+F to find inconsistent word usage and double spaces
- Print presentations before submitting - errors that are invisible on screen are obvious on paper
- Ask another intern to review your presentation well before the deadline (first for content, second pass for formatting)
- Sleep deprivation causes big mistakes - impaired judgment occurs after 2-3 hours of sleep

### 2. Mergers & Inquisitions - "How to Succeed On the Job in Investment Banking: STARR vs TWAT"
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banking-on-the-job-success/
**Type:** Practitioner career blog (Brian DeChesare, former Goldman Sachs / boutique banker)
**Accessibility:** Successfully fetched

Key content:

**The TWAT archetype (Technical Wizard with Antisocial Tendencies) - what NOT to be:**
- Excel mastery but cannot articulate concepts clearly to senior bankers
- Treats peers as competition; hoards information and macros
- Avoids social events and co-worker networking
- Neglects administrative tasks unrelated to spreadsheets
- Fails to verify logistics (meeting dates, attendee counts, directions)
- Career outcome: lower rankings, smaller bonuses, worse exit opps despite technical superiority

**Five critical on-the-job mistakes:**
1. Spending first 3-6 months volume-proving instead of identifying "Defender" senior bankers who will mentor and allocate meaningful deal work
2. Asking senior bankers questions that peers or junior analysts could answer - wastes their time, damages standing
3. Handing entire tasks to admin/support staff instead of completing 80% yourself and requesting refinement
4. Treating peers as competition (zero-sum thinking) - STARRs share macros, cover for absent colleagues, and organize social activities
5. Contacting senior bankers inefficiently - all questions in one batch email/visit, never piecemeal

**Communication protocol for senior bankers:**
- Batch all questions into a single email or office visit
- Prepare both long and short versions of any presentation
- Anticipate objections and weaknesses before any meeting
- Include a summary sentence before sharing any attachment
- Pre-answer four questions: purpose, conclusion, assumption sensitivity, data gaps
- Use "Sounds good, will do!" not just "OK" - attitude signals are judged before work quality is proven

**Relationship building:**
- First 3-6 months are irreplaceable for reputation building
- Poor early impressions create lasting damage
- Support staff: complete 80% yourself, give appreciation through small gestures

**Bottom line stat:** In ~90% of cases, the STARR's career trajectory significantly exceeds the technically superior TWAT's.

## Key Themes

**1. Attention to Detail is the #1 Differentiator**
Every source converges on this. Errors in models, formatting, fonts, numbers, footnotes - any of these signal incompetence. The fix: print everything, review on paper, use checklists, have peers review before submitting.

**2. Communication Discipline (Upward and Lateral)**
First-year analysts either over-communicate (pestering seniors with questions peers could answer) or under-communicate (going silent when drowning). The correct model: batch questions, communicate proactively about delays, notify seniors of workload before it becomes a problem.

**3. Relationship Strategy - Not Just Technical Output**
The STARR/TWAT framework from M&I quantifies what practitioners know intuitively: technical skills are table stakes. Relationship capital - with seniors, peers, and support staff - determines bonus rankings and exit opportunities.

**4. Time and Deadline Management**
Multiple sources treat missing deadlines or showing up late as near-fireable offenses. Punctuality and delivery reliability are non-negotiable. The recovery play is always early warning, never silence.

**5. Modeling and Excel Hygiene**
Hard-coded numbers, inconsistent formatting, broken cell references, and non-auditable models are among the fastest ways to lose senior banker trust. Models must be readable, flexible, and dynamic.

**6. Over-Promising and Under-Delivering**
New analysts often say yes to everything. The correct play is transparent workload communication so seniors can reprioritize rather than discover a problem at deadline.

**7. Zero-Sum Peer Thinking**
New analysts who hoard information, refuse to help peers, and view colleagues as competition end up socially isolated, lack peer advocates, and miss out on informal knowledge transfer. Generosity with macros, research, and cover-for-me behavior compounds into reputation capital.

**8. Senior Banker Protocol Errors**
Going to a VP with a question that took 30 seconds to formulate - without first checking with peers, without batching it with other questions, without offering a proposed solution - is a category error that damages standing fast.

**9. Administrative and Logistical Failures**
Getting meeting times wrong, not printing enough copies, mixing up attendee lists, not knowing building addresses for client meetings - these are often the "career-limiting" moments nobody warns analysts about.

**10. Attitude and Professionalism**
Tone in emails, body language in meetings, showing up visibly tired, not participating in team social events - all of these are read by senior bankers before work quality becomes measurable. "Sounds good, will do!" versus "OK" matters.

## Common Mistakes - Draft List for Article

1. **Failing to double-check work before submission** - Models with wrong numbers, decks with formatting inconsistencies, emails with typos. The fix is the print-and-review habit, peer review, and structured checklists before anything goes upstairs.

2. **Asking senior bankers questions peers could answer** - The correct escalation ladder: Google it, ask other analysts, ask the associate - only then ask the VP. And batch all questions into one visit.

3. **Going silent when overwhelmed** - Not communicating workload pressure until a deadline is blown. The correct play: flag early, communicate the crunch, ask for help reprioritizing.

4. **Over-promising on timelines** - Saying yes to a 2-hour turnaround on a task that needs 5 hours. Better to negotiate the deadline up front than miss it.

5. **Poor Excel and model hygiene** - Hard-coded values, inconsistent formatting, broken references, no labels on assumptions. Models must be auditable by a stranger.

6. **Treating peers as competition** - Hoarding macros, refusing to cover for colleagues, not participating in team social activities. Kills reputation and peer advocacy.

7. **Not taking notes** - Missing details from calls and meetings with MDs who will not repeat themselves. Note-taking is a form of respect and also protects you when tasks are disputed.

8. **Arriving late or showing up visibly unprepared** - Investment banking is not university. Ten minutes late to a morning standup is career-damaging in the first 90 days.

9. **Neglecting relationship-building with support staff** - Admin staff control print queues, booking systems, and catering at 2am. Analysts who hand over raw tasks and never show appreciation get deprioritized when it matters.

10. **Misreading the culture - perfectionism paralysis vs. speed** - Some analysts freeze on revisions because nothing feels done. Others rush to show speed and submit sloppy work. The correct calibration: quality first, communicate timing if you need more of it.
