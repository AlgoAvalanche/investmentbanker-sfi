## Sources Consulted

### 1. Mergers & Inquisitions - "Investment Banking League Tables: Neutral Arbiter of Bank Rankings or Marketing Manipulation?"
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banking-league-tables/

Investment banking league tables rank banks by involvement in transactions during specific periods, measured through deal count, dollar volume, or advisory fees. The primary function is marketing - allowing banks to claim "#1 status" in pitch books rather than serving as objective performance assessments. IB analysts spend considerable time screening deal lists with selective criteria to manufacture favorable narratives - for example, filtering to "healthcare M&A deals between $400M-$1B" or "industrial equity offerings of $100M-$500M." Data discrepancies of 20-30% exist between providers, and undisclosed deal sizes are especially problematic in restructuring and lower-middle-market transactions. The key takeaway: "League tables are not designed for you, the job seeker" - they primarily serve as bank marketing instruments.

### 2. IB Interview Questions - "Understanding Investment Banking League Tables"
**URL:** https://ibinterviewquestions.com/blog/understanding-investment-banking-league-tables

League tables rank investment banks using quantitative metrics like deal value, transaction count, and market share across different transaction types, regions, and sectors. The three major providers - Refinitiv, Dealogic, and Bloomberg - use meaningfully different methodologies: Refinitiv focuses on announced deals with full credit to all advisors; Dealogic differentiates credit by role and accepts bank submissions with proof of engagement; Bloomberg uses conservative, verified attribution via confirmed filings. A critical practitioner insight: "The same bank can hold vastly different rankings depending on which provider you check and how narrowly you filter the data." Some firms tie analyst and associate bonuses directly to league table performance, which creates internal incentives to pursue co-advisory roles purely for credit rather than economic return.

### 3. Corporate Finance Institute - "League Table - Know the World's Leading Investment Bankers"
**URL:** https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/career-map/sell-side/capital-markets/league-table/

CFI positions league tables as educational benchmarks within IB career mapping, noting that rankings document "popular services provided by investment bankers" across financial services categories. The resource was last updated November 2023 and contextualizes league tables within sell-side capital markets career paths. League tables are used to identify top-ranked institutions across M&A advisory, equity underwriting, debt underwriting, leveraged finance, and restructuring - banks often rank differently across categories, enabling strategic positioning in narrow niches.

### 4. Cornell Johnson Library - "What Are League Tables and How Do I Find Them?"
**URL:** https://johnson.library.cornell.edu/faqs/what-are-league-tables-and-how-do-i-find-them/

This academic library guide identifies the three primary platforms for accessing league table data: Bloomberg Terminal (LEAG function for underwriters, MA function for M&A advisors), LSEG Workspace/Refinitiv (with SDC module for historical data back to the 1970s), and Capital IQ (custom screening via Screening > Transactions). Each platform allows creation of custom searches and monitoring alerts. The guide emphasizes the flexibility of custom search parameters as the key advantage of modern platforms, enabling users to construct highly specific league tables - which is precisely the mechanism banks exploit when building pitch books.

### 5. Dealogic Investment Banking Scorecard
**URL:** https://dealogic.com/investment-banking-scorecard/

Dealogic is one of the three dominant league table providers and is widely trusted because its methodology is considered rigorous. Unlike pure announcement-based systems, Dealogic accepts bank submissions for deals not yet publicly documented - banks can submit engagement letters as proof to include transactions. This makes Dealogic rankings preferred in many pitch books, but also means the database reflects what banks choose to report (with verification) rather than purely objective third-party observation. Dealogic's platform distinguishes between announced and completed deals, and differentiates credit allocation based on advisory role rather than giving full credit to every advisor on a transaction.

### 6. Wall Street Oasis Community Discussions
**URL:** https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/investment-banking/ltm-us-and-global-ma-league-tables

WSO community members note that league tables "don't matter and aren't always indicative of franchise strength, particularly because they may not capture unannounced deals in progress." The LTM (last twelve months) vs. YTD framing matters significantly - a bank that closed a mega-deal 13 months ago will not appear in current LTM tables. Practitioners observe that league table positioning influences recruiting and perception more than it reflects actual advisory capability. Community consensus: use tables to understand rough market position but do not use them to differentiate between similarly-ranked institutions.

## Key Themes

### 1. League Tables as Marketing, Not Truth
The fundamental reality is that league tables are marketing documents that banks selectively cite in pitch books. Every major bank can claim to be "#1" in some sufficiently narrow category by choosing the right provider, timeframe, deal type, geography, and size band. Analysts and associates spend real hours constructing these screens - it is a known and expected part of pitch book preparation.

### 2. The Three-Provider Ecosystem
- **Refinitiv (LSEG):** Broadest database; full credit to all advisors on a deal; historical data to the 1970s via SDC; tends to produce the highest aggregate market volumes
- **Dealogic:** Role-differentiated credit; accepts bank deal submissions with proof; widely used in pitch books; considered the most rigorous
- **Bloomberg:** Conservative inclusion criteria; verified attribution; accessible via Terminal LEAG/MA functions; strongest among fixed-income desks

### 3. Ranking Metric Choices Produce Different Winners
- Deal value: favors bulge-bracket banks handling mega-cap transactions
- Deal count: favors middle-market and advisory boutiques with high volume
- Market share %: allows meaningful YoY comparison regardless of market size
- A single mega-deal can vault a bank from #8 to #2 in deal value rankings while barely moving its deal count position

### 4. Credit Allocation Distortions
When five banks advise on one transaction, every provider gives full (or near-full) credit to each advisor. This means the sum of all banks' "advised deal value" in a given year far exceeds actual total M&A volume. It incentivizes banks to join deals as co-advisors or fairness opinion providers purely for credit accumulation.

### 5. Volume vs. Quality - The Core Limitation
League tables capture quantity (number of deals, dollar volume) but say nothing about:
- Whether the deal was value-creating for the client
- Whether the bank provided substantive strategic advice vs. a rubber-stamp fairness opinion
- Client satisfaction or repeat business rates
- Execution quality (timeline, price achieved, deal structure)

### 6. Senior Banker Mobility Invalidates Long-Term Comparisons
Client relationships follow individual bankers, not institutions. When a top-ranked M&A banker moves from Goldman Sachs to Centerview, they bring clients and deals with them. Multi-year league table comparisons do not account for talent flow, making year-over-year ranking changes hard to interpret without knowledge of team composition changes.

## Interview-Ready Facts

- The three major league table providers are Refinitiv (LSEG), Dealogic, and Bloomberg - each uses a different methodology, so the same bank can rank differently across all three for the same time period
- Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, and JPMorgan consistently occupy top-3 positions in global M&A advisory by deal value; boutiques like Evercore, Lazard, and Centerview lead in fee-based rankings
- League tables segment by: M&A advisory, ECM (equity capital markets), DCM (debt capital markets), leveraged finance, and restructuring - a bank's position varies significantly across these categories
- Analysts spend time in pitch book prep specifically filtering league table data to find the most favorable cut - this is standard practice, not manipulation in a legal sense
- LTM (last twelve months) tables are more commonly cited than YTD because they reduce seasonality effects; full-year annual tables are the most stable benchmark
- Bloomberg Terminal: use LEAG for underwriting tables, MA for M&A advisory rankings
- Refinitiv SDC has historical data to the 1970s - useful for demonstrating long-term franchise strength
- Capital IQ allows custom screening via Screening > Transactions with saved search and alert functions
- Some banks tie junior banker compensation partly to league table performance - an incentive structure that shapes deal pursuit decisions
- A bank ranked #10 globally with deep sector expertise often delivers more value than a #2-ranked generalist for a specific transaction type
- The "20-30% discrepancy" between data providers on the same set of deals is well-documented and expected

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Treating League Tables as Objective Rankings
League tables are self-reported marketing data, not independent audits. Banks submit deals to providers, choose which tables to cite in pitch books, and filter data to produce favorable narratives. Treating them as neutral performance rankings fundamentally misunderstands how they are constructed.

### Mistake 2: Using Global Rankings to Evaluate Fit for a Specific Role
A candidate interviewing for a healthcare M&A role at a bank ranked #15 globally but #3 in healthcare M&A is making a mistake by citing the global ranking as a weakness. Always use the most relevant sector/regional table.

### Mistake 3: Comparing Rankings Across Providers
Citing "we're #2 in Dealogic but #4 in Refinitiv" without understanding why reveals a lack of methodology knowledge. The differences are structural (deal inclusion criteria, credit allocation) not errors - interviewers will probe this.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Timeframe Sensitivity
A bank that closed one $50B deal last quarter looks completely different in YTD vs. LTM vs. 3-year rolling rankings. Always note the timeframe when citing a position.

### Mistake 5: Confusing Deal Value Rankings with Fee Rankings
Global M&A advisory by deal value is dominated by bulge brackets. Fee-per-deal and independent advisory market share is dominated by boutiques (Evercore, Lazard, PJT, Centerview). These tell entirely different stories about business models and career experiences.

### Mistake 6: Assuming Rankings Reflect Current Team Strength
Because senior bankers move frequently and take client relationships with them, a league table ranking reflects the institutional franchise plus the current team - a bank that lost three senior MDs in the past year may still show strong rankings from deals those MDs sourced before departing.

### Mistake 7: Not Checking Footnotes in Pitch Books
Every league table citation in a pitch book has a footnote specifying the provider, date range, and category filters. Candidates who reference pitch book tables without reading those footnotes are citing numbers they do not fully understand.

## Additional Context for Article Writing

**How pitch books use league tables in practice:**
An analyst preparing a pitch book for a biotech M&A mandate will run screens on Refinitiv, Dealogic, and Bloomberg using various filters (sector, size, geography, timeframe) until finding the most favorable combination for their bank. They then cite this in the pitch with a precise footnote: "Source: Dealogic, Global Healthcare M&A, deals >$250M, Jan 2023-Dec 2025." This is not considered deceptive - it is standard practice that all parties understand.

**The boutique vs. bulge-bracket dynamic:**
Boutiques like Evercore, Lazard, Centerview, and PJT Partners often rank below bulge brackets in deal volume tables but deliver higher fee revenue per transaction and higher advisory quality scores in client surveys. The Mergers & Inquisitions article specifically notes that "PwC leads middle-market by deal count but ranks below Houlihan Lokey by quality" - illustrating how count-based tables favor volume players over quality players.

**Career implications - what tables actually signal:**
For candidates, the relevant question is not "what is the bank's global ranking" but rather "what is the bank's ranking in the specific sector/product/geography where I want to work." A bank ranked #20 globally but #2 in restructuring advisory offers a fundamentally different (and potentially superior) restructuring career experience than a #1-ranked bank with a small restructuring practice.
