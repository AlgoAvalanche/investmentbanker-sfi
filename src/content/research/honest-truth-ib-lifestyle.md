## Sources Consulted

### 1. Mergers & Inquisitions - Investment Banking Hours
URL: https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banking-hours/

**Key facts and insights:**

- Analyst-level standard: 70-80 hours per week is the expectation
- In-office hours typically 9 AM to 12 AM on weekdays, plus weekend work
- Senior bankers (Associates to VPs): 50-60 hours weekly, 9 AM to 7 PM with weekend work and travel
- Important nuance: bankers are not "working" the entire time they are in the office - they often wait for comments, edits, and client responses, which inflates the perceived hours
- **The real driver of long hours is culture, not workflow demands** - banks use long hours as a filtering mechanism to attract and retain committed candidates
- Secondary drivers: unpredictable client demands, large fees ($5M+) creating urgency, deals spanning months/years making shift-based work impractical, senior bankers expecting juniors to match their own historical experience
- What will NOT fix hours: individual lawsuits (e.g. Kathryn Shiber v. Centerview), AI/automation (Capital IQ launched 1998 and never shortened analyst weeks - AI increases review burden rather than reducing total hours), unionization (unlikely due to high turnover and supply/demand dynamics favoring banks)
- Author's prognosis: fundamental change requires IB to become less desirable relative to other careers - timeline possibly 2050-2060, not within the next decade

URL: https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/resources/careers/jobs/investment-banking-hours
URL: https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/investment-banking/wso-2024-investment-banking-work-conditions-survey-results-4th-annual

**Key facts and insights:**

- WSO 2024 IB Working Conditions Survey (4th Annual) - primary data source
- JPMorgan analysts: 50% working 90+ hours per week, 13% working 100+ hours per week
- Goldman Sachs analysts: 50% working 90+ hours per week, 25% working 100+ hours per week
- Separate survey finding: 80% of people working less than 70 hours in IB, with nearly 40% working 40-50 hours weekly (likely includes more senior roles and regional banks)
- Firm-level breakdown:
  - Bulge Bracket (Goldman Sachs, J.P. Morgan, Bank of America): 60-80 hours weekly standard
  - Elite Boutique (Evercore, Lazard, Centerview): 80-90 hours weekly, higher compensation
  - Regional Boutique (SunTrust, Piper Jaffray, Raymond James): 50-70 hours - best work-life balance
- M&A and capital markets teams work the longest hours due to time-sensitive deal nature
- JPMorgan 2024 policy: capped junior banker hours at 80/week, "pencils down" Friday 6 PM to Saturday noon, one full weekend off every three months (live deals exempt)
- Goldman Sachs "Saturday Rule": no work Friday 9 PM to Sunday 9 AM - but enforcement varies widely by team and deal stage

### 3. Investment Banking Council of America - The Truth Behind 100-Hour Work Weeks
URL: https://www.investmentbankingcouncil.org/blog/the-truth-behind-100-hour-work-weeks-in-investment-banking

**Key facts and insights:**

- Realistic peak workload range: 70-90 hours per week for analysts during intense deal phases
- Average workweek: 50-70 hours across most roles when not in a live deal
- The 100-hour week is real but not the permanent baseline - it originates from high-pressure M&A deals
- Five primary drivers of extended hours:
  1. Client demands requiring constant availability during critical deal phases
  2. Competitive, high-pressure environment
  3. Complex, time-sensitive projects (financial modeling, due diligence, deal structuring)
  4. Industry culture emphasizing "face time" as proof of dedication
  5. Promotion dependent on meeting demanding work expectations
- Physical effects: reduced sleep, poor nutrition, lack of exercise leading to cardiovascular disease risk
- Mental health: stress, anxiety, and burnout from inability to disconnect
- Career sustainability: short-term financial rewards offset by long-term health consequences and increased turnover rates
- Emerging changes: flexible scheduling, hybrid work, automation of repetitive tasks, mental health support programs

### 5. Neurolaunch - Investment Banking Burnout: Strategies to Recognize, Address, and Prevent Career Fatigue
URL: https://neurolaunch.com/investment-banking-burnout/

**Key facts and insights:**

- Investment bankers work 80-100 hours weekly, with active deal peaks beyond that
- 2021 Goldman Sachs internal survey: 95-hour average weeks, 5 hours of sleep nightly
- Working 55+ hours weekly increases stroke risk by 33% and coronary heart disease risk by 13% vs. standard schedules (WHO/ILO research)
- World Health Organization defines occupational burnout through three dimensions: energy depletion, mental distancing from work, and reduced professional effectiveness - IB systematically triggers all three simultaneously
- **The Compensation Paradox**: "Financial compensation alone cannot buffer burnout when demands structurally exceed available resources." Higher bonuses may accelerate burnout cycles rather than interrupt them, because they validate unsustainable work patterns
- Most analysts depart within 2-3 years - not primarily for better-paying roles, but for structural relief from the demands
- Replacement costs: $150,000-$200,000 per analyst
- Burnout reliably predicts clinical depression at rates exceeding most occupational hazards
- Continuous work connectivity (checking emails on weekends) prevents neurological recovery - brain requires genuine psychological disengagement to restore executive function
- Monitoring markets during vacation provides no actual rest from a neuroscience perspective
- Most firm interventions (wellness apps, meditation sessions) are performative and do not address underlying demand-resource imbalances
- 72% of bankers report considering quitting to avoid burnout
- 51% aware their colleagues are planning an exit
- Only 43% of bankers feel very satisfied with existing support systems
- 96% of bankers want additional support systems implemented

## Key Themes

**1. The Hours Are Real, But Variable**
The 100-hour week is not a myth, but it is not the permanent daily reality either. The honest range is 70-85 hours in a normal week, spiking to 95-100+ during live deal execution. Firm type matters significantly: elite boutiques push harder than bulge brackets; regional boutiques are closest to livable.

**2. Culture Is the Primary Driver, Not Necessity**
The M&I analysis is the most credible on this point: long hours function as a filter and a cultural ritual, not purely as a workflow requirement. The "waiting around" phenomenon is real - significant time is spent available rather than productive. This makes the experience psychologically harder, not easier.

**3. The Physical and Mental Toll Is Documented and Severe**
The Goldman 2021 leaked survey is the industry's most cited data point. 95-hour weeks, 5 hours of sleep, 3 AM bedtimes. The WHO data on cardiovascular risk at 55+ hours per week adds objective medical context. Burnout is not weakness - it is the documented neurological consequence of sustained demand exceeding recovery.

**4. The Compensation Argument Is More Complex Than It Appears**
At 80 hours per week, a $170K all-in analyst earns ~$42/hour. The per-hour rate is not exceptional compared to other professional services. The real value proposition is the exit opportunities and the credential compression - two years of IB experience opens doors that would otherwise take a decade to reach.

**5. Most People Leave in 2-3 Years By Design**
The two-year analyst program was structured partly to manage attrition. The majority of analysts exit to private equity, hedge funds, corporate development, or MBA programs. This is a feature of the model, not a dysfunction. The question is whether you use those two years or are used by them.

**6. Policy Reform Has Limits**
"Protected Saturdays" and hour caps sound meaningful but are widely acknowledged as loosely enforced during live deals. The structural economics of IB - large deal fees, client-first culture, small deal teams - create relentless pressure that no weekend policy fully neutralizes.

**7. AI Will Not Save Analysts in the Near Term**
Historical precedent (Bloomberg Terminal, Capital IQ, financial modeling software) shows that technology tools have consistently increased review complexity and the volume of work rather than reducing total hours. The M&I analysis suggests genuine change requires IB to lose its competitive desirability against alternatives.

## Common Misconceptions to Address in Article

**Misconception 1: "IB analysts work 100 hours every single week."**
Reality: 70-85 hours is the baseline; 100+ hours happens during live deal sprints, not as a permanent state. The experience is more like alternating periods of brutal intensity and relative calm (50-60 hrs).

**Misconception 2: "The hours are worth it because of the pay."**
Reality: Per-hour compensation at analyst level (~$42/hr at 80 hrs) is not exceptional. The real value is the credential and exit option compression - not the hourly rate.

**Misconception 3: "IB is getting much better - banks are fixing the culture."**
Reality: Policy changes (Protected Saturdays, hour caps) exist but are poorly enforced during live deals. Structural economics - large fees, small teams, client-first culture - create relentless pressure. Meaningful change is likely decades away.

**Misconception 4: "Burning out means you were not tough enough."**
Reality: Burnout is a documented neurological and physiological consequence of sustained overwork. The WHO defines it as an occupational syndrome. It is not a character flaw - it is a predictable outcome of the demand structure.

**Misconception 5: "You can't have any life in IB."**
Reality: Lifestyle varies meaningfully by: firm type (BB vs. EB vs. RB), group (M&A vs. DCM vs. ECM vs. sector coverage), deal cycle timing, and team culture. Some groups at some firms are genuinely manageable; others are brutal. Research the specific group, not just the bank.

**Misconception 6: "Most analysts leave because they failed."**
Reality: The two-year analyst program is structured as an intentional exit funnel to PE, hedge funds, MBA programs, and corporate development. Leaving after two years is the normal and expected path - not a failure.

**Misconception 7: "AI will fix the hours soon."**
Reality: Bloomberg Terminal (1981), Excel (1985), Capital IQ (1998) all promised efficiency gains - none shortened analyst weeks. AI tools increase the volume and complexity of work reviewed, not reduce total hours. M&I analysis places structural change on a 2050-2060 timeline.
