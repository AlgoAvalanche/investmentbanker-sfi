### 2. Wall Street Prep - "Cash-Free Debt-Free (CFDF) Transaction Structure"
**URL:** https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/cash-free-debt-free-cfdf/

Authoritative technical reference on CFDF deal structures, the dominant framework for private M&A. In a CFDF deal, Purchase Price = Enterprise Value because the buyer assumes no net debt and the seller retains excess cash. The distinction between "operating cash" (transfers with business) and "excess cash" (retained by seller) is a frequent negotiation flashpoint. Example: JoeCo acquired at 10.0x EBITDA on $100M EBITDA = $1B enterprise value; with $200M existing debt and $25M total cash ($5M operating, $20M excess), seller nets $820M equity proceeds. CFDF structures align valuation methodology (EBITDA multiples) with deal mechanics. Notes CFDF is standard in LBOs but less common in public company acquisitions.

## Key Themes

### 1. The Cash-Free, Debt-Free Framework
Working capital adjustments exist within the CFDF deal structure that dominates private M&A. The buyer pays enterprise value and receives a business with no net debt and an agreed level of operating working capital. The adjustment mechanism ensures the "operating working capital" component delivers exactly what was priced into the deal - no more, no less. Cash is excluded from the NWC calculation because it is handled separately (seller retains excess, buyer receives only agreed operating cash).

### 2. Purpose: Protection Against Manipulation
Without the adjustment mechanism, sellers have a clear incentive to strip working capital pre-close: accelerate A/R collections, delay A/P payments, and draw down inventory. The NWC peg creates a contractual floor that prevents this. The peg also protects sellers: if the business generates above-normal working capital by closing, the seller receives an upward adjustment rather than gifting that value to the buyer.

### 3. The Peg Is Negotiated, Not Calculated
The working capital target (peg) is not simply the last balance sheet figure. It is a negotiated number representing "normalized" operating working capital. Standard methodology: trailing 12-month average, adjusted for seasonality, growth trends, and one-time items. Buyers prefer a higher peg (harder to meet = more likely seller pays at close). Sellers prefer a lower peg (easier to meet = higher net proceeds). Common resolution: 12-month average of historical monthly figures.

### 4. Definitions Matter More Than the Number
A $10M peg under one account definition can have materially different economic implications than a $10M peg under a different definition. Which accounts are in/out (deferred revenue, customer deposits, related-party payables, accrued bonuses) determines the real value of the peg. This is where sophisticated buyers and sellers fight the hardest - and where less-prepared parties lose value silently.

### 5. Post-Close Disputes Are Common and Expensive
Working capital adjustments drive approximately 50% of all post-closing M&A disputes. High-risk accounts: inventory obsolescence reserves, allowances for doubtful accounts, accrued expenses. These require estimation and are sensitive to period-end timing. The four-phase resolution process (closing statement, objection, negotiation, neutral accountant) can take 6-9 months and significant legal/advisory fees. Proactive documentation during deal negotiation is the primary preventive measure.

### 6. Collar Mechanics Reduce Friction
A negotiated collar (tolerance band) around the peg (e.g., +/- $250K on a $10M peg) means no adjustment is triggered for minor deviations. This prevents disputes over immaterial timing differences and reduces deal friction. Collar size should reflect actual historical NWC volatility, not an arbitrary round number.

### 7. True-Up vs. Locked Box
Two competing mechanisms exist in the market: (1) Traditional post-close true-up - actual NWC calculated after closing and compared to peg, common in US private M&A; (2) Locked box - price fixed at a historical balance sheet date with no post-close adjustment, common in European deals and PE seller transactions. The locked box eliminates post-close uncertainty but requires the buyer to accept stale balance sheet data and relies on "leakage" provisions to prevent value extraction between the locked box date and closing.

## Interview-Ready Facts

**Mechanics:**
- Working capital in M&A = current assets (excluding cash) - current liabilities (excluding debt)
- In a CFDF deal, Purchase Price = Enterprise Value; the NWC adjustment ensures the operational working capital component is delivered as priced
- Adjustment is dollar-for-dollar: $1 above peg = +$1 to seller; $1 below peg = -$1 from seller proceeds
- True-up period: 60-90 days post-closing in most agreements; buyer prepares closing statement within 90-120 days

**The Peg:**
- Peg is typically set as the trailing 12-month average of monthly NWC, normalized for seasonality and one-time items
- Collar/tolerance band (e.g., +/- $250K) prevents adjustments for immaterial deviations
- The peg must be defined consistently across the LOI, purchase agreement, and closing statement - inconsistency creates disputes

**Market Data (cite SRS Acquiom 2025):**
- 90%+ of private-target M&A now includes a NWC PPA (up from 50% a decade ago)
- 75% of transactions include a separate escrow for working capital adjustments (often ~1% of transaction value)
- 70% of sellers accept the buyer's initial closing statement calculation
- Median resolution timeframe: 2 months post-close
- Working capital disputes account for ~50% of all post-closing M&A disputes

**Valuation Link:**
- Change in NWC flows through cash flow statement operating activities and directly impacts free cash flow
- In DCF models, increasing NWC (e.g., growing A/R or inventory) is a cash outflow; decreasing NWC is a cash inflow
- For subscription/SaaS businesses with high deferred revenue, NWC can be negative (healthy) - treatment in M&A negotiations must be carefully defined

**Buyer vs. Seller Dynamics:**
- Buyer prefers higher peg (more protection) and broader asset definitions
- Seller prefers lower peg (maximizes proceeds) and narrower asset definitions
- Buyer typically prepares the closing statement - this structural advantage means sellers must be prepared to object
- Only 30% of sellers actually dispute the buyer's closing statement calculation (per SRS Acquiom data)

**Locked Box vs. True-Up:**
- True-up: post-close calculation, common in US, buyer-friendly as buyer controls closing statement preparation
- Locked box: price fixed at historical balance sheet date, common in Europe and PE-to-PE deals, seller-friendly as it eliminates post-close uncertainty
- Leakage provisions in locked box deals prevent sellers from extracting value between lock date and closing

## Common Mistakes

### By Sellers (Most Costly)
1. **Negotiating the peg number before defining the accounts** - The definition is more economically significant than the number. Sellers who agree to a $10M peg without specifying which accounts are included often discover the effective peg is much higher than anticipated.
2. **Ignoring seasonality** - A retailer with a fiscal year-end in January (post-holiday) will have very different NWC than the same retailer in October (pre-holiday inventory build). Accepting a trailing average without seasonality adjustment can systematically disadvantage one party.
3. **Failing to align peg with Quality of Earnings** - If the QofE adjusts EBITDA for a one-time item, that same item must be excluded from the NWC peg. Double-adjusting (once in EBITDA, once in NWC) destroys seller value silently.
4. **Waiting too long to reconcile trial balance** - Monthly NWC figures must reconcile to trial balance. Sellers who cannot provide clean monthly schedules lose negotiating leverage on peg definition.
5. **Not anticipating the buyer's closing statement advantage** - Buyers prepare the initial closing statement, which typically reflects their most favorable interpretation. Sellers who are not prepared to review and dispute within the objection period default to the buyer's number.
6. **Related-party payables misclassification** - Including intercompany payables or shareholder loans in the NWC calculation inflates or deflates the metric in ways that are not representative of normal operations.

### By Buyers
1. **Accepting vague account definitions** - "Accounts receivable" can mean gross A/R, net A/R (after bad debt reserve), or net A/R after specific exclusions. Vague language in the purchase agreement creates expensive disputes post-close.
2. **Not modeling NWC seasonality in financial projections** - A business acquired in a low-NWC month with a peg based on annual average will require an immediate working capital injection post-close.
3. **Underestimating inventory obsolescence** - Inventory on the balance sheet at cost may be worth significantly less at market. Failing to properly reserve for obsolete inventory in the NWC definition leaves buyers overpaying.
4. **Missing deferred revenue complexity** - In SaaS or subscription businesses, deferred revenue (a liability) can represent future cash earnings, not a cost. Treating it identically to a payable in the NWC calculation may produce an artificially low peg that the buyer must fund.
5. **Ignoring the collar size relative to actual volatility** - A $50K collar on a business with $500K monthly NWC swings means the collar provides almost no protection and disputes are nearly inevitable.

### In Deal Negotiation (Both Parties)
1. **LOI/purchase agreement/closing statement definition inconsistency** - Using different account lists or accounting policies across documents is the single largest avoidable source of post-close disputes.
2. **Failing to define "consistent with past accounting practices"** - This standard phrase in purchase agreements sounds protective but is frequently contested when sellers used informal or undocumented practices.
3. **No explicit cutoff rules for transactions near closing** - Invoices sent on the last day before closing, payments received but not yet processed, inventory in transit - all require explicit treatment rules in the purchase agreement.
4. **Underusing the neutral accountant provision** - Many parties settle disputes for suboptimal amounts rather than invoking the neutral accountant process, which typically splits costs and provides binding resolution within defined timeframes.

## Additional Technical Depth

### NWC in Different Business Models

**High NWC businesses (capital-intensive, cyclical):**
- Manufacturers: large inventory + A/R, modest A/P
- Distributors: high inventory turns but large gross NWC balances
- Seasonality risk is highest; peg negotiation most contentious

**Low/Negative NWC businesses (capital-light, cash generative):**
- SaaS/subscription: deferred revenue liability can make NWC negative
- Retailers with fast inventory turns and strong A/P terms (e.g., large grocery chains)
- Restaurants: collect cash immediately, pay suppliers on terms
- Negative NWC in these businesses is normal and not a distress signal - it must be treated carefully in M&A

**Services businesses:**
- Often minimal inventory; NWC driven by A/R (unbilled and billed), accrued revenue, and payroll accruals
- WIP (work in progress) for project-based firms creates significant estimation and timing issues

### The Escrow/Holdback Mechanism
- Approximately 75% of deals include a separate working capital escrow (per SRS Acquiom 2025)
- Typical size: 1% of transaction value
- Funds held in escrow cover any shortfall payment the seller owes post-close
- Excess: seller receives the escrowed amount plus any true-up payment
- Without escrow, buyer must pursue seller for any shortfall - collection risk increases with time post-close

### Accounting Policy Hierarchy in Purchase Agreements
- Tier 1: GAAP, applied consistently with the target's historical practices
- Tier 2: Target's specific historical accounting policies (documented)
- Tier 3: GAAP without reference to historical practice (fallback)
- Disputes most often arise at the boundary between Tier 1 and Tier 2 - particularly when the seller's "historical practice" was informal or undocumented
