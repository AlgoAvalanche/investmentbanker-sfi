## Key Themes

### 1. The Countercyclical Mechanism
RX is the only major product group in investment banking where deal flow increases when the broader economy deteriorates. When interest rates rise rapidly (as in 2022-2023), overleveraged companies that issued debt during low-rate periods face refinancing walls they cannot clear, triggering distress. When M&A volume collapses and IPOs dry up, restructuring mandates surge. This creates a natural hedge: an RX banker is relatively insulated from the cyclical layoffs that periodically devastate M&A and ECM desks.

The mechanism works as follows:
- Cheap credit era (2010-2021): Companies issue high-yield debt, lever up, covenant-lite structures proliferate
- Rate shock (2022+): Refinancing becomes prohibitively expensive; cash flow deteriorates; covenants breach
- Distress wave (2023-2025): Companies unable to refinance seek RX advisory; defaults rise
- Resolution phase (2026+): $1.2 trillion maturity wall (2027-2029) ensures sustained pipeline even as macro stabilizes

### 2. Elite Boutiques vs. Bulge Brackets
Bulge brackets (Goldman, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan) have largely retreated from restructuring advisory because their lending and underwriting relationships create inherent conflicts of interest when advising a distressed borrower. You cannot credibly advise a company to restructure a bank loan when your institution is the lender holding that loan.

Elite boutiques - PJT, Houlihan Lokey, Evercore, Lazard, Moelis, Guggenheim, Rothschild - dominate because they have no lending balance sheets and no conflicts. Houlihan Lokey runs the largest RX practice by volume (88 deals in 2024). PJT is the most prestigious for debtor-side flagship cases. Evercore runs a balanced practice with strong sponsor relationships.

### 3. The Liability Management Revolution
The nature of restructuring has shifted dramatically since 2020. Liability Management Transactions (LMTs) now represent 54% of U.S. high-yield defaults (2024 data), surpassing traditional in-court restructurings as the primary distress resolution mechanism. The two dominant LMT structures:
- Uptier exchanges: Amend credit agreements to create super-priority debt, effectively subordinating non-participating lenders
- Drop-down financings ("J.Crew trapdoor"): Transfer assets to unrestricted subsidiaries, raising new debt outside original lender groups

The Serta Simmons Fifth Circuit ruling (December 2024) reset uptier mechanics, pushing the market toward more consensual structures. Candidates must be fluent in this evolution.

### 4. The RX Ecosystem
Every restructuring involves four key players who must understand each other's incentives:
1. Investment bank (debtor-side and creditor-side advisors are separate, never combined)
2. Bankruptcy counsel (Kirkland & Ellis, Weil Gotshal, Davis Polk, Latham, Akin Gump, Paul Weiss)
3. Financial/operational turnaround consultants (Alvarez & Marsal, AlixPartners, FTI Consulting)
4. Distressed credit funds (often the fulcrum security holders who become new equity owners post-emergence)

### 5. Distress Is Broader and Shallower in the Current Cycle
The 2025 cycle is structurally different from prior downturns (2008-2009, 2020). Mega-bankruptcies (>$1B assets) actually declined from 42 to 31 cases in 2025, even as total Chapter 11 filings hit a decade high of 7,940. Distress is concentrated in small/mid-cap companies, industrials, consumer discretionary, and healthcare - not the systemic crises that defined prior cycles.

## Interview-Ready Facts

**Market Data:**
- 2025 commercial Chapter 11 filings: 7,940 - a decade high (fourth consecutive year of increases)
- Mega-bankruptcies (>$1B): declined from 42 (2024) to 31 (2025) despite higher total filings
- Loan defaults and distressed exchanges averaged 4.3% of issuers in 2025 (vs. 2-3% pre-pandemic norm)
- LMTs represented 54% of U.S. high-yield defaults in 2024
- ~12% of borrowers now have negative cash flow (up from ~7-8% one year prior)
- ~13% of borrowers have interest coverage below 1.0x (up from ~7-8%)
- $1.2 trillion maturity wall from 2027-2029 ensures sustained deal pipeline
- S&P projects U.S. speculative-grade default rate potentially hitting 4.25% by mid-2026

**Compensation Data:**
- Year 1 RX analyst at top elite boutiques: $200-$270K all-in
- Director level (Lazard recent cycle): ~$842,000
- MD level: $1.5M+
- Bonus volatility is real: compensation depends heavily on deal flow cycles

**Deal Economics:**
- Typical Chapter 11 cost: ~6.5% of assets
- Standard DIP financing pricing (2024-2025): 12-18% all-in (SOFR + 700-900 bps)
- Typical stalking horse breakup fee: 1-3% of purchase price
- First Brands DIP: $4.4B ($1.1B new money + $3.3B roll-up)
- LMT participant vs. non-participant recovery: 73-85% vs. near-zero (Envision case study)

**Landmark Cases/Rulings:**
- Serta Simmons (Fifth Circuit, December 2024): Reset non-pro-rata uptier mechanics
- Purdue Pharma (Supreme Court): Rejected nonconsensual third-party releases, reshaping mass-tort restructuring
- J.Crew trapdoor: Canonical example of drop-down financing structure

**Sector Risk Concentrations for 2026:**
- Consumer products and retail: value-consumer pressure, inability to pass cost increases
- Automotive: tariff-driven supplier distress, EV market stagnation
- Healthcare: Medicaid funding cuts, Medicare Advantage erosion, labor cost inflation
- Industrials: tariff-driven inventory disruption, rerouting costs

## Common Mistakes

**In Interviews:**
1. Weak "why restructuring?" answer - candidates cite prestige or countercyclical job security without demonstrating genuine interest in the finance-law-process integration that makes RX technically distinct
2. Conflating debtor-side and creditor-side advisory - each side runs entirely separate workstreams; banks advise only one side; candidates must understand both perspectives exist but are never combined at one firm
3. Confusing recovery percentages across claim classes - recovery varies dramatically by seniority; the fulcrum security is where the enterprise value waterfall "breaks" (cumulative claims exceed enterprise value at that instrument)
4. Ignoring recent case law - the Serta ruling and Purdue Pharma decision are mandatory knowledge; showing fluency in 2024-2025 legal evolution is a strong differentiator
5. Missing the ecosystem - answering RX questions without mentioning bankruptcy lawyers and turnaround consultants as essential counterparties reveals shallow preparation
6. Confusing RX with "fixing broken companies operationally" - RX bankers advise on capital structure and financial claims; operational turnaround is AlixPartners'/A&M's job

**Conceptual Mistakes to Avoid in Written Work:**
- Treating the countercyclical nature as a simple binary (busy in recessions, dead in booms) - in reality, liability management transactions now sustain deal flow even outside traditional downturns
- Overstating mega-bankruptcy activity in 2025 - the cycle is broader and shallower, not a 2008-style systemic event
- Implying bulge brackets compete for top RX mandates - they largely do not, for structural conflict-of-interest reasons
- Treating LMTs as exotic novelties - they now represent the majority of high-yield default resolutions and are standard practice

## Additional Context for Article

**The "Why Now" Angle (2025-2026):**
The current cycle is interesting precisely because it is not a classic recessionary wave. Rate hikes from 2022-2023 created a slow-motion refinancing crisis for companies that issued covenant-lite, floating-rate debt during the ZIRP era. This has produced sustained RX activity without a formal recession - a sign that the market has evolved. The maturity wall ($1.2T in 2027-2029) means the deal pipeline looks constructive for several more years regardless of macro conditions.

**Career Positioning Insight:**
The surge in student interest in RX is partly rational (countercyclical protection, superior exit opportunities into distressed funds) and partly reflects the post-2020 boom creating visibility. The 2020 pandemic mandates were so numerous that banks turned down work - an event that created a generation of analysts who saw RX outperform all other groups. That experience is now embedded in recruiting culture.

**The Private Credit Complication:**
The $1.5 trillion private credit market has created a new wrinkle: more distressed companies are restructuring bilaterally with private lenders rather than through public markets or bankruptcy. The First Brands case (fraud allegations, $4.4B DIP) exposed lender due diligence gaps in private credit that may drive increased RX advisory demand as that market matures and stresses accumulate.
