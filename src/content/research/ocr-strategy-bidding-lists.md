## Sources Consulted

### 1. Wall Street Oasis - On Campus Recruitment and Networking: A Guide
**URL:** https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/investment-banking/on-campus-recruitment-and-networking-a-guide

OCR encompasses banks holding on-campus information sessions, coffee chats, networking events, dinner events, and Handshake application postings. The guide stresses that students should build a target list of 20-30 firms they genuinely resonate with - not just based on prestige, but on fit, relevance, and realistic accessibility. This list becomes a personal hit list for networking and avoids the "spray and pray" approach. The value of being at a target school lies primarily in the extra networking opportunities and the fact that dedicated alumni recruiting teams come to campus at least once a semester to give firm presentations and host coffee chats with analysts.

### 2. Mergers and Inquisitions - Investment Banking Recruitment Guide: The Full Process
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banking/recruitment/

The M&I guide frames IB recruitment as a selection mechanism rather than an attraction strategy - banks receive over 100,000 applications for thousands of positions globally. The U.S. on-campus model compresses into a narrow window: large banks recruit over a year in advance, with undergraduates needing to begin recruiting in Year 2 for third-year summer internships. The 7-step process runs: win steppingstone internships, develop a personal narrative, build a banker-focused resume, network to secure interviews, prepare technically and for fit, complete the process, then reassess if unsuccessful. Summer internships function as extended job interviews; modern banking rarely hires full-time analysts without prior internship experience.

### 3. Mergers and Inquisitions - Investment Banking University Student Guide
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banking-university-student-high-school-student/

The recruiting timeline in the U.S. is "insane" - some banks open applications over 1.5 years in advance. By the time Year 2 applications open, successful candidates should have a GPA of at least 3.5, one solid finance internship plus leadership experience (or two finance internships), 30-40 completed coffee chats with bankers, and approximately 30 hours of interview preparation. Year 1 priorities are securing a first finance internship (search funds, VC, regional boutiques, valuation firms) and front-loading easier classes to build GPA. Critical mistakes include transferring universities (timing derails everything), taking difficult classes Year 1, delaying networking until Year 2, and prioritizing clubs over internship procurement.

### 4. Wall Street Prep - The Investment Banking Interview Process and Superday
**URL:** https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/the-investment-banking-interview-process-and-superday/

On-campus first-round interviews are conducted by alumni bankers who have institutional investment in their school's candidates - these rounds focus heavily on technical competency. Candidates who advance are invited to Superday, an all-expenses-paid event including hotel accommodations, an informal networking dinner or happy hour the evening prior, and a full day of back-to-back interviews at corporate offices. The source is explicit: the pre-Superday dinner is an assessment event, not a social one - "hiring decisions sometimes occur after evening networking events." Banks use two models post-Superday: a match process (candidates and groups rank each other) or a general pool model. Offers typically come within hours to 24 hours; waiting days signals a waitlist position.

### 5. Wall Street Oasis Community Threads - OCR Mechanics and Slot Competition
**URLs:**
- https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/investment-banking/whats-ocr-like-at-your-school
- https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/investment-banking/i-go-to-a-target-can-someone-explain-ocr
- https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/investment-banking/ocr-and-bank-interview-pattern
- https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forums/only-1-ocr-interview

Interview slots through OCR are extremely limited - typically only 12 to 24 slots per school per firm. With 100+ applicants per school carrying 3.9+ GPAs and multiple banking internships, networking is often the only way to secure an interview. Firms participating in formal OCR are required to make a set number of interview slots available to bid candidates (students who apply through the school's platform, e.g. Handshake), and firms cannot deny bid candidates their first-round interview - but the number of those protected slots is small. At target schools, the main advantage is having a dedicated alumni recruiting team look at resumes and host in-person coffee chats. Applicants commonly apply to 20 positions across bulge brackets, middle-market firms, and boutiques; bulge brackets often interview more candidates in absolute terms than middle-market firms at target schools.

### 6. Wall Street Oasis - UG Recruiting Part I: How a Resume Becomes an Interview
**URL:** https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/investment-banking/ug-recruiting-part-i-how-a-resume-becomes-an-interview

A resume drop typically means an alum from your school is recruiting there - the firm will look at all submitted resumes but allocates only a certain number of interview slots for that alum, creating a filtration bottleneck. Information sessions are the single best way to "network" with people in the formal OCR process, because the same bankers who present the info session are involved in your school's recruiting. Banks track who attends information sessions, and attendance plays directly into interview and offer decisions - signing the attendance sheet is mandatory. The session content itself is largely irrelevant; the contacts made are what matter. The only reliable path to an interview is having someone vouch for you, which only happens through sustained networking.

### 7. Wall Street Oasis - MBA/B-School Career Bid System
**URL:** https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/school/can-someone-explain-the-b-school-career-bid-system

The MBA bidding system allocates a fixed number of "bid points" (or "tokens") to each student at the start of recruiting. Students then allocate these points across firms and roles they want to interview for - higher bids increase the probability of winning an interview slot, but spending points on one firm means fewer available for others. Firms are required to guarantee interview slots to students whose bids clear a threshold (these are "bid" candidates); firms cannot deny these slots. Finance is noted as resume-driven, making bidding harder to win on bids alone than in consulting. Optimal strategy at the MBA level involves identifying 3-5 high-priority firms and concentrating bids rather than spreading them evenly - most students who spread bids thinly win mediocre outcomes across the board.

### 8. Wall Street Oasis - OCR Timeline and Information Session Strategy
**URLs:**
- https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/investment-banking/ocr-timeline
- https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/investment-banking/how-useful-are-information-sessions
- https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/investment-banking/getting-crushed-ocr

OCR timelines vary by school but generally follow a pattern: info sessions and networking events in September-October, resume drops in October-November, first-round interviews in November-January, and Superdays in January-February. Recruiting has become so accelerated that by the time formal OCR is available, many processes have already started or finished - some top firms that most candidates want are no longer available through OCR by the time the platform opens. The "getting crushed" phenomenon - receiving only one or zero OCR interviews - is common and can result from late networking, low GPA, weak experience, or insufficient info session attendance. The recommended recovery strategy is pivoting to boutique banks and middle-market firms, many of which recruit on a slower, less formal timeline.

## Key Themes

1. **OCR is the structured surface; networking is the actual mechanism.** Formal OCR slots are a floor, not a ceiling. The bankers who attend info sessions are the same people who pull resumes - relationship-building before the resume drop is what converts an application into an interview.

2. **Slot scarcity is the defining constraint.** With only 12-24 interview slots per firm per school and 100+ qualified applicants, competition is not about qualifications alone. Being remembered by a recruiter at an info session can be the differentiating factor.

3. **Bidding (MBA) and resume drops (undergrad) share the same logic.** In both cases, candidates are allocating limited access to a limited number of slots. The strategy question is always: concentrate on high-conviction targets or spread broadly? Data favors concentration with a backup pipeline.

4. **The timeline is brutally compressed and moving earlier every year.** Applications open 12-18 months in advance. Students who start networking in Year 1 are better positioned than those who start in Year 2. MBA students must prepare before arriving on campus.

5. **Attendance tracking is real and consequential.** Firms track info session attendance. Non-attendance is interpreted as low interest and reduces the probability of receiving an interview invitation.

6. **The pre-Superday dinner is an interview.** Social conduct at the pre-Superday event directly influences hiring decisions. Candidates who treat it as purely social risk eliminating themselves before the formal interviews begin.

7. **Target school status is not binary.** A school can be a target for West Coast firms and a non-target for Wall Street firms. Students should build tiered firm lists that reflect their school's actual placement history, not perceived prestige.

8. **Recovery paths exist.** Students who do not receive OCR interviews from bulge brackets can pivot to boutiques and middle-market firms, which recruit on a slower timeline and are often more accessible through direct outreach.

## Interview-Ready Facts

- Goldman Sachs reportedly receives over 250,000 applications for approximately 2,900 summer internship slots - an acceptance rate of around 1.16%.
- Typical OCR first-round interview slot allocation: 12-24 slots per firm per school.
- With 100+ qualified applicants per slot, networking is the primary selection mechanism, not resume quality alone.
- The standard OCR timeline at U.S. target schools: info sessions (September-October) - resume drops (October-November) - first rounds (November-January) - Superdays (January-February).
- From first round to Superday is typically two to three weeks.
- At Superday, candidates go through 5-10 interviews lasting 30-45 minutes each; offer probability is approximately 35-40%.
- Offers typically arrive within hours to 24 hours of Superday; waiting multiple days usually signals waitlist status.
- Return offer rates from summer associate internships historically run above 70% in normal markets.
- Large banks recruit over 1.5 years in advance - Year 2 students should begin preparation by the end of Year 1.
- The top 15 target schools account for approximately half of top investment banking jobs.
- MBA OCR: applications open September-October Year 1; interviews peak January-February; summer internship runs 10 weeks between Year 1 and Year 2.
- Banks track info session attendance and factor it into first-round interview decisions.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Waiting until Year 2 to start networking.** The first-mover advantage in OCR is significant. Students who begin coffee chats in Year 1 have 30-40 conversations completed before peers have started.

2. **Treating info sessions as passive events.** Showing up but not introducing yourself to the attending bankers means missing the primary purpose of the session. The content of the presentation is secondary to the relationships formed.

3. **Not signing the attendance sheet.** Firms track who attends. Missing from the list = no demonstrated interest = deprioritized in the resume review.

4. **Spreading bids or applications too thin.** At the MBA level, spending a small number of bid points on every firm wins nothing. At the undergrad level, applying to 30 firms without meaningful networking at any of them produces the same result.

5. **Ignoring boutiques and middle-market firms.** Students fixated on bulge brackets and elite boutiques miss the substantial opportunity in the middle market, which recruits later, has less competition per slot, and often provides more deal exposure during internships.

6. **Treating the pre-Superday dinner as social time.** This event is an evaluation. Unprofessional conduct - including excessive drinking - has ended candidacies before the formal interviews began.

7. **Poor geographic targeting.** Applying to firms nationally without understanding that a school's target status varies by geography leads to wasted applications. A West Coast school is a target for LA/SF firms and a non-target for New York banks - the firm list must reflect this.

8. **Underestimating the GPA threshold.** With 100+ applicants carrying 3.9+ GPAs per slot, a 3.4 GPA at a target school does not automatically translate into interviews. Networking becomes even more essential when GPA is below the median of the applicant pool.

9. **Pivoting too late.** Students who realize OCR is not producing results in November or December are often too late to pivot to boutiques, which may have already filled their classes through informal outreach and referrals.

10. **Confusing target school status with guaranteed access.** Being at a target school means the opportunity exists - not that it is automatic. OCR still requires preparation, networking, and strong fundamentals.
