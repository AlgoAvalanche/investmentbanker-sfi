## Sources Consulted

### 1. Mergers & Inquisitions - The Investment Banking Informational Interview: The Full Guide
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banking-informational-interview/

The definitive practitioner guide to IB informational interviews, covering the full 8-step process from finding contacts to making the direct ask. M&I emphasizes that this strategy requires 300-500 outreach emails, 5-10 hours per week for 6 months, and 100+ total hours to see results. The guide stresses starting 6-12 months before recruiting, targeting Analysts and Associates first (not senior bankers), keeping initial emails to five sentences maximum, and making a "mini-ask" at the end of each call (asking for referrals or permission to follow up). The "real ask" - directly requesting guidance into formal interviews - only comes when recruiting season officially opens. Audio calls are preferred over video for first conversations, and calls should stay within 10-15 minutes unless the banker extends them.

### 2. Mergers & Inquisitions - Investment Banking Networking: Strategies, Tips & Templates
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banking/recruitment/networking/

M&I's top-level networking strategy guide, defining IB networking as "the process of finding, contacting, and speaking with investment banking professionals to build relationships, gain knowledge, and eventually win interviews and job offers via those relationships." The guide identifies three main approaches: informational interviews (3-6 months before recruiting), cold emailing (best for early undergrads and incoming MBAs), and cold calling (limited to boutiques). Six critical mistakes are documented: trying too hard to impress, using LinkedIn for direct communication (it is for finding people only), writing long or generic emails, failing to follow up consistently, asking about compensation or hours, and being indirect about what you want. The site reports 115,341+ professionals have downloaded their email toolkit, indicating the scale of candidates using these approaches.

### 3. RecruiterBase - From Networking Call to Interview in Investment Banking
**URL:** https://recruiterbase.co/guide/from-networking-call-to-interview

Focuses specifically on the conversion mechanic - how calls become interviews. The core insight is to never ask directly for a job or internship; instead use indirect framing: "What's the best way to be in the loop regarding recruitment?" or "How can I best prepare myself to be considered?" This invites the contact to champion you rather than forcing a yes/no hiring decision. A three-phase approach is laid out: express genuine interest during the call, ask about recruitment timing at closing (without requesting placement), then reference that timeline in follow-up emails. The guide provides a conversion framework: approximately 25% of networking calls convert to interviews, meaning roughly 120 calls are needed to generate 30 interviews. However, the site notes outcomes vary dramatically - some candidates get offers from 20 calls, others need 80+. One strong internal advocate is sufficient to get into formal interviews at a firm.

### 4. Mergers & Inquisitions - How to Cold Email for an Internship
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/how-to-cold-email-for-an-internship/

The authoritative cold outreach guide for IB roles. A 5-10% response rate is described as normal; rates below 1% indicate message quality or targeting problems. Initial emails should be no more than 5 sentences, use formal titles (Mr./Ms.) for senior bankers, and name-drop a university, firm, or mutual connection in the subject line. The full outreach sequence is 3-4 total emails maximum per person, with the first follow-up sent after one week. The guide is blunt that "most networking consists of following up repeatedly" - it is more about repeated effort than any single insight. For boutique firms specifically, expect 5-10 responses per 50-100 contacts, yielding 1-2 actual calls and potentially one offer. Candidates must be ready on calls to deliver a 1-2 sentence condensed story, explain specific firm interest, and discuss recent deals the firm has worked on.

### 5. Robin Judson Partners - Step-by-Step Guide to Acing Informational Interviews in Investment Banking
**URL:** https://www.robinjudsonpartners.com/a-step-by-step-guide-to-acing-informational-interviews-for-investment-banking/

A recruiter-perspective guide emphasizing that informational interviews are particularly critical for non-target school candidates. The guide stresses prioritizing in-person meetings over calls when possible, and being explicit about what you are looking for without wasting their time: open with "I am particularly interested in your advice because of your success in [function] at [company]..." Questions should be divided into two categories - about the organization (culture, project structure, competitive differentiation, advancement paths) and personal-to-you questions (what qualifications they seek, how people typically enter the profession, resume feedback). Alumni from schools with fewer banking alumni tend to be more helpful because they feel a stronger connection to other graduates from their school. Never treat the conversation as a formal job interview unless the banker redirects it that way.

## Key Themes

**1. Indirection is the mechanism**
The conversion from call to interview is never direct. The ask is never "can I have a job" - it is always "how can I position myself to be considered." The interview offer comes through relationship capital building over multiple touches, not a single transaction.

**2. Volume plus personalization**
Both elements are mandatory. M&I cites 300-500 emails over 6 months. But every email must reference specific deal work, career history, or connection points. Generic outreach has near-zero conversion.

**3. Junior bankers first, always**
Analysts and Associates have more time, more recent recruiting memory, and are more likely to respond. Senior bankers come later, introduced through junior contacts.

**4. Follow-up is the actual work**
Multiple sources describe follow-up as the core activity, not the initial outreach. One week between touches, 2-3 total follow-ups per person, maintained relationship contact every 6-18 months after initial conversations.

**5. One strong advocate is sufficient**
Multiple sources converge on this: you do not need a broad network at one firm. A single full-time banker willing to push your resume into the process is enough. Once you have that person at a firm, redirect networking energy elsewhere.

**6. Pre-call research is non-negotiable**
Research the specific person's deals, career transitions, and background before every call. This is what enables personalized questions. Bankers can tell within 30 seconds whether a candidate has done homework.

**7. Timing window: 6-12 months out**
Starting too late (under 3 months before recruiting) means relationships are too shallow to convert. Starting too early (over 12 months) means contacts rotate out or lose track. The 6-12 month window is the consensus optimal range.

- Normal cold email response rate for IB outreach: 5-10%; below 1% signals a messaging problem
- RecruiterBase conversion framework: ~25% of networking calls convert to interviews; 120 calls yields approximately 30 interviews
- For boutique firms: 50-100 emails produces 5-10 responses, 1-2 calls, and potentially one offer
- Optimal informational interview duration: 10-15 minutes (do not extend unless invited)
- Maximum emails per contact: 3-4 total (initial + 2-3 follow-ups), first follow-up after one week
- Start networking 6-12 months before recruiting season begins
- Target Analysts (2nd year) and Associates before VPs and MDs
- Audio calls are preferred over video for first conversations - lower stakes, easier for the banker
- Alumni from schools with fewer banking alumni are disproportionately helpful

## Common Mistakes

1. **Asking directly for a job** - The fastest way to end a conversation and burn a contact. Ask about process and how to position yourself instead.

2. **Contacting senior bankers first** - MDs and VPs have the least time and fewest reasons to help unknown candidates. Analysts and Associates first.

3. **Long or generic emails** - Any email over 5-6 sentences gets ignored. Generic outreach (no reference to their specific work) gets deleted.

4. **Using LinkedIn as a communication channel** - LinkedIn is a discovery tool. Emails to work or personal addresses convert at higher rates.

5. **No follow-up after the call** - Failing to send a thank-you within 24 hours, or failing to maintain periodic contact afterward, wastes the relationship capital built during the call.

6. **Extending the call past 15 minutes uninvited** - Bankers are busy. Going over time signals poor social awareness and damages the impression.

7. **Asking inappropriate questions** - Asking about compensation, hours, or internal politics during an informational interview is a relationship-ender.

8. **Making it feel like a job interview** - The informational interview format must feel like genuine curiosity and learning. The moment it feels transactional, bankers become guarded.

9. **Stopping after one contact at a firm** - You need a champion, not just a friendly conversation. If the first contact is lukewarm, keep networking at that firm until you find someone willing to advocate.

10. **Starting too late** - Beginning outreach 6-8 weeks before recruiting means relationships are too shallow to produce referrals. Six to twelve months is the minimum runway.
