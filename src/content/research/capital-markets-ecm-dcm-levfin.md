## Sources Consulted

### 1. Equity Capital Markets (ECM): The Definitive Guide - Mergers & Inquisitions
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/equity-capital-markets/

ECM sits at the intersection of investment banking and sales & trading - bankers advise companies on raising equity capital and execute transactions, while staying closely tied to markets and investor sentiment on a daily basis. The group is divided into sub-teams: Equity Origination (pitches and executes IPOs, follow-ons, rights issues), Syndicate (coordinates with other banks and builds the order book), and Convertible Bonds / Equity-Linked (structures and places convertible instruments). ECM bankers typically work more regular hours than M&A bankers but earn lower compensation at senior levels and have narrower exit opportunities. The IPO process managed by ECM teams involves selecting bookrunners, drafting the prospectus and S-1, setting a price range using peer multiples discounted 10-20% for demand cushion, running an 8-14 day roadshow, and then pricing and allocating shares the night before listing. Key products include IPOs, follow-on offerings, accelerated bookbuilds (ABBs), block trades, rights issues, and PIPEs (private investment in public equity).

### 2. Debt Capital Markets (DCM): The Definitive Guide - Mergers & Inquisitions
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/debt-capital-markets/

DCM groups advise clients on raising capital through debt instruments - primarily investment-grade bonds and loans - and are structured into four internal sub-teams: origination, structuring, execution, and syndication. The defining characteristic separating DCM from LevFin is credit quality: DCM clients are investment-grade borrowers (BBB-/Baa3 or above) raising capital for general corporate purposes such as capex, refinancing, or working capital, while LevFin handles sub-investment-grade (speculative) borrowers often financing acquisitions or LBOs. Day-to-day work involves advising on optimal debt structure (fixed vs. floating rate, tenor, currency, bond vs. loan format), drafting term sheets, preparing investor presentations, running the book of orders, and pricing the deal based on benchmark spreads. Bond issuance fees are substantially lower than IPO fees - typically 0.3-2% of proceeds compared to the standard 7% gross spread on US IPOs - reflecting the higher volume and more commoditized nature of debt deals. DCM bankers interact heavily with credit rating agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch) to position a new issuance and achieve the best possible rating outcome for the client.

### 3. Leveraged Finance (LevFin): Ultimate Guide - Wall Street Prep
**URL:** https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/ultimate-guide-to-debt-leveraged-finance/

LevFin groups arrange debt financing for leveraged buyouts (LBOs), acquisitions, recapitalizations, and refinancings - primarily for private equity sponsors and their portfolio companies. The two main products are leveraged loans (floating-rate, senior secured, typically Term Loan A and Term Loan B tranches) and high-yield bonds (fixed-rate, typically senior unsecured or senior secured). In a typical LBO capital structure, debt comprises 60-70% of total financing, layered by seniority: first-lien bank debt at the top, second-lien secured debt, senior unsecured high-yield bonds, subordinated mezzanine, and equity at the bottom. The loan syndication process involves the lead arranger (investment bank) committing to underwrite the full loan amount, preparing a Confidential Information Memorandum (CIM), hosting a bank meeting, and then selling down portions to institutional investors such as CLOs, hedge funds, and loan mutual funds. High-yield bond issuances follow a similar roadshow process to IPOs, with management presentations over 1-2 weeks, a preliminary offering memorandum, and final pricing set at a spread over benchmark Treasuries. LevFin is widely considered one of the best investment banking groups for exits into private equity and credit funds, given daily workflow interaction with PE sponsors and deep understanding of LBO capital structures.

### 4. Leveraged Finance: Careers, Salaries & Exit Opportunities - Mergers & Inquisitions
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/leveraged-finance/

LevFin bankers perform substantial credit analysis work alongside deal execution, evaluating a company's ability to service debt at various leverage levels through cash flow modeling, LBO modeling, and sensitivity analysis. The group sits closer to M&A in terms of analytical intensity and deal complexity than to vanilla ECM or DCM, making it a preferred training ground for those targeting private equity. Primary exit opportunities include: private equity (especially for those covering PE-sponsor-driven deals), direct lending and private credit funds, distressed debt hedge funds, credit-focused hedge funds, and corporate development roles at PE portfolio companies. Mega-funds such as KKR, Apollo, and Blackstone now operate their own internal credit and leveraged finance groups, creating competition for deal flow with traditional investment banks and also serving as a new hiring destination for senior LevFin bankers. Compensation in LevFin generally tracks M&A levels more closely than ECM/DCM levels at both the analyst and associate tiers, reflecting the comparable technical demands. The intense interaction with PE sponsors means LevFin analysts build a strong sponsor network early in their careers, which is a differentiating advantage when recruiting for PE roles.

### 5. Capital Markets vs. Investment Banking: Detailed Comparison - Mergers & Inquisitions
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/capital-markets-vs-investment-banking/

This source draws the clearest structural distinction between capital markets groups (ECM, DCM, LevFin) and M&A advisory: M&A bankers work on a single deal for months or years, building deep company- and industry-specific expertise through financial modeling, valuation, and negotiation, while capital markets bankers execute higher volumes of transactions with shorter timelines, spending more time tracking market conditions and investor appetite. ECM and DCM are described as removing "both the worst and best parts" of traditional IB - fewer all-nighters and less weekend work, but also less technical skill development, lower senior compensation, and narrower exit paths compared to M&A. M&A advisory fees are contingent success fees (typically 1-5% of enterprise value), while capital markets fees are underwriting spreads earned regardless of trading aftermarket performance. LevFin is an exception - it is categorized as capital markets but behaves more like M&A in terms of hours, analytical depth, and exit outcomes. For recruiting purposes, a capital markets role at a bulge-bracket bank is generally considered superior to a non-IB finance role, but junior bankers seeking buy-side exits are better served by M&A or LevFin than by ECM or DCM.

### 6. SPAC Market Report 2023 and 2024 Review - Kroll / SPACInsider / Woodruff Sawyer
**URL:** https://www.kroll.com/en/reports/m-and-a/spac-market-report-2023-highlights-and-year-end-review
**URL (supplementary):** https://www.spacinsider.com/news/headline-post/full-year-2024-spac-review

The SPAC boom of 2020-2021 represented a significant but short-lived revenue windfall for ECM groups: total SPAC IPO proceeds peaked at $149 billion in 2021 before collapsing to $5.3 billion in 2022 and $3.4 billion in 2023 - a decline of over 97% from peak. Key causes of the collapse included SEC regulatory scrutiny of SPAC accounting practices and disclosure rules, disappointing post-merger (de-SPAC) stock performance (de-SPAC companies lost an average of 67% of their value from 2021 to 2023), rising interest rates that inflated the cost of holding SPAC trust accounts, and a broader risk-off shift in equity markets. By 2023 the SPAC IPO count fell to just 24 completed deals, down dramatically from the 613 deals completed in 2021. A partial recovery emerged in 2024, with 57 SPAC IPOs raising $11.2 billion - roughly matching 2019 pre-boom activity levels rather than a return to 2021 excess. The SPAC era collapse is a cautionary tale for ECM groups about fee revenue concentration in a single product structure, and it refocused ECM bandwidth toward traditional IPOs, follow-on offerings, and convertible bonds in 2024-2025.

### 7. Leveraged Finance Annual Report 2025 - Baker McKenzie / White & Case Debt Explorer
**URL:** https://www.bakermckenzie.com/-/media/files/insight/publications/2025/02/2025-in-the-know-annual-report.pdf
**URL (supplementary):** https://debtexplorer.whitecase.com/leveraged-finance-commentary/high-yield-market-bounces-back-but-maturity-wall-looms

High yield bond issuance was severely depressed in 2022 as the Federal Reserve and other central banks began aggressive rate hike cycles, with US issuance falling to $96.5 billion. Recovery began in 2023 with US high-yield issuance rising 58.4% to $152.9 billion, and accelerated sharply in 2024 with US issuance reaching $233 billion (up 52.4% year-on-year). European high-yield issuance followed a similar trajectory, rising 85.9% year-on-year in 2024 to $149 billion. Average US high-yield yields peaked at approximately 9.6% in October 2023 before declining to 8.53% by year-end 2023 and 7.67% by Q4 2024 as the rate environment stabilized. The primary driver of 2024 LevFin volume was refinancing activity: companies that had deferred issuance during the 2022-2023 high-rate period rushed to lock in financing as yields moderated. A "maturity wall" - a large cluster of leveraged loans and high-yield bonds maturing in 2025-2027 - remains a key structural challenge requiring sustained refinancing volume, which is constructive for LevFin revenue pipelines but creates credit risk if refinancing conditions tighten.

### 8. Global Investment Banking Fees and Revenue 2024-2025 - BCG / Evalueserve / MadeMarket
**URL:** https://media-publications.bcg.com/Capital-Markets-Investment-Banking-Update-2024-2025.pdf
**URL (supplementary):** https://www.evalueserve.com/blog/investment-banking-fy24-update/
**URL (supplementary):** https://www.mademarket.com/blog/q4-investment-banking-fees-climb-39-on-cautious-optimism-for-2025

The global investment banking fee pool surpassed $130 billion in 2025, driven by a resurgent M&A cycle, recovering IPO markets, and record debt issuance volumes. In 2024, total IB fees grew approximately 11% year-on-year after the sharp decline in 2022-2023. ECM revenues were among the strongest performers, rising 54% year-on-year in 2024 (from approximately $13 billion to $20 billion globally), driven by convertible bond issuance and follow-on offerings rather than IPOs, which remained below historical norms. DCM fee growth was also robust at approximately 39% year-on-year, supported by investment-grade refinancing and high-yield issuance recovery. Q4 2024 investment banking fees climbed 39% quarter-on-quarter, signaling improving sentiment heading into 2025. US banks dominate global fee pools, with the top five American firms (JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Bank of America, Citigroup) capturing approximately 55-60% of global IB revenue. Global IPO volumes reached $47.7 billion in Q4 2024, the highest quarterly figure since Q1 2022, suggesting a genuine IPO market rebound was underway entering 2025. For 2025, forecasters projected 13% further fee growth driven by M&A activity and continued capital markets issuance.

## Key Data Points for Article

- Standard US IPO gross spread: 7% of proceeds (paid by issuer to underwriting syndicate)
- Investment-grade bond underwriting fee: 0.3-2% of proceeds
- High-yield bond underwriting fee: approximately 1.5-3.5% of proceeds
- SPAC IPO proceeds peak: $149bn (2021) vs. $3.4bn trough (2023) vs. $11.2bn (2024)

## Key Structural Distinctions for Article

- ECM vs. M&A: ECM is execution-heavy and market-driven; M&A is advisory-heavy and company-specific. ECM fees are spread-based; M&A fees are contingent success fees.
- DCM vs. LevFin: DCM = investment-grade borrowers, general corporate purposes, lower risk, lower fees. LevFin = sub-investment-grade, acquisition/LBO financing, higher risk, higher fees.
- LevFin vs. M&A: LevFin provides the debt financing that funds M&A deals, particularly PE-sponsored LBOs. The two groups collaborate closely on every leveraged buyout.
- Hours: ECM and DCM = better work-life balance than M&A. LevFin = comparable to M&A in intensity.
- Exits: M&A and LevFin = strong PE exits. ECM and DCM = weaker PE placement, more often lead to capital markets careers at asset managers, corporate treasury, or staying in banking.

## Trends to Emphasize

1. SPAC era collapse (2022-2023) and its effect on ECM volumes and ECM banker morale/utilization
2. Rate hike cycle (2022-2023) crushed LevFin and high-yield volumes; recovery in 2024 was dramatic
3. Private credit (direct lending) gaining share from broadly syndicated LevFin - PE sponsors increasingly bypassing investment banks for financing
4. IPO window remains selective in 2024-2025 despite recovery - quality bar is higher post-SPAC era
5. Convertible bonds had a resurgence in 2024 as companies sought to lower coupon costs vs. straight debt
6. Maturity wall in leveraged loans (2025-2027) creates both pipeline opportunity and credit risk for LevFin groups
