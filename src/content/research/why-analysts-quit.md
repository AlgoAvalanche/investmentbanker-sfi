## Sources Consulted

### 1. Mergers & Inquisitions - How to Quit Your Investment Banking Job
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/quit-investment-banking-job/
**Type:** Practitioner blog (Brian DeChesare, former banker)

Key extracts:
- Lifestyle dissatisfaction is the dominant driver - analysts describe being "incredibly out-of-shape, had no life outside work, no friends"
- The exit decision follows a predictable annual cycle: "midway through every year, a whisper starts growing" about leaving
- The 1-year mark and post-bonus window (January-March) are the most common exit points
- Banks escort departing employees out immediately due to confidential data concerns - resignations are same-day terminations
- Practical advice: secure a plan before announcing, go directly to MD in-person, tell no one in advance to prevent rumors

### 2. Mergers & Inquisitions - Investment Banking Analyst Job Description
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banking-analyst-job/
**Type:** Practitioner reference guide

Key extracts:
- Analysts spend 70-85 hours per week in the office but are effectively on call 24/7
- The role is structurally temporary: designed as a 2-3 year stint, not a career
- Most analysts never reach Associate - they leave for PE, HF, VC, or corporate development
- Total compensation 2026: $165K-$225K (base $100K-$125K + 0.5x-1.0x bonus)
- The "up-or-out" culture means advancement is not guaranteed even for strong performers

### 3. UpSlide Investment Banking Burnout Report
**URL:** https://upslide.com/investment-banking-burnout-report/
**Type:** Industry survey report (200+ finance professionals)

Key extracts:
- 72% of bankers are considering quitting to avoid burnout
- 51% are aware that colleagues are actively planning to leave
- Burnout is the single top cause of banker attrition
- Bankers spend up to 40 hours per week on manual, administrative tasks - half their working week
- Manual work costs firms approximately $2,000 per FTE per week
- Only 43% feel satisfied with existing workplace support systems
- 96% want additional support systems implemented
- Under 30% of banks are currently implementing productivity or workflow improvements
- Top desired redirections of time: client-facing work (25%), strategic tasks (23%), creative/ideation (23%)
- 90% believe document automation in Microsoft 365 would benefit them

### 4. AlphaSense - The Toll of Analyst Burnout
**URL:** https://www.alpha-sense.com/blog/trends/toll-of-analyst-burnout/
**Type:** Industry analysis / fintech perspective

Key extracts:
- A Bank of America associate's death in May 2024 catalyzed major industry-wide action on working hours
- 2021 Goldman Sachs internal survey: 77% of respondents acknowledged "workplace abuse"
- JPMorgan implemented an 80-hour weekly cap with "pencils down" windows (Friday evening to Saturday noon, except during live deals)
- Goldman Sachs introduced "Protected Saturday" (Friday evening to Sunday morning)
- Bank of America enforced stricter timekeeping with consequences for violations
- Industry response also included higher base salaries and wellness perks
- AI platforms (e.g., AlphaSense) can reduce early-stage due diligence from hours to approximately 15 minutes - reported 50-75% time savings
- The AI solution is being positioned as a way to maintain productivity while reducing hours

### 5. The Econ Review - Price of Admission: Goldman Sachs 2021 Analyst Survey
**URL:** https://theeconreview.com/2021/04/19/the-price-of-admission-employee-burnout-when-a-demanding-job-becomes-an-abusive-one/
**Type:** Academic/editorial analysis of the Goldman Sachs viral internal survey

Key extracts:
- 13 first-year Goldman analysts documented conditions in a widely-circulated internal presentation
- Average hours: 98 per week; peak week (ending Feb 13, 2021): 105 hours
- Average nightly sleep: 5 hours
- Mental health score before GS: 9.0/10 - after joining: 2.8/10 (drop of 6.2 points)
- Physical health score before GS: 8.8/10 - after joining: 2.3/10 (drop of 6.5 points)
- 100% of respondents reported facing unrealistic deadlines
- 77% reported experiencing workplace abuse (being shouted at, sworn at, publicly criticized, shunned)
- One analyst: "I can't sleep anymore because my anxiety levels are through the roof"
- Another: "My body physically hurts all the time and mentally I'm in a really dark place"
- A third characterized conditions as "inhumane"
- Majority indicated they were unlikely to stay if conditions did not improve
- Goldman CEO responded by introducing Protected Saturday and raising junior banker base salaries
- U.S. context: average American work week is 34.6 hours - GS analysts worked nearly 3x that

## Key Themes

### 1. Structural Hours Problem
The role is not just long - it is unpredictably long. Analysts cannot plan their time. Being on call 24/7 for 2-3 years is qualitatively different from working a fixed 80-hour schedule. The inability to predict or control one's time destroys all external relationships and personal health.

### 2. Health Deterioration Is Measurable and Severe
The Goldman 2021 data is the starkest evidence: mental health dropped from 9.0 to 2.8, physical health from 8.8 to 2.3 on a 10-point scale. These are not anecdotes - they are scored declines of 6+ points in under a year. Sleep averaging 5 hours per night over a sustained period causes cognitive and physical damage.

### 3. The Role Is Designed to Be Temporary
Unlike most professions where leaving early signals failure, IB is structured around a 2-3 year analyst cycle. Banks expect most analysts to leave. The question is not whether analysts quit but when and to what. The "best analysts usually leave" observation from M&I is telling - retention of top talent is not a solved problem.

### 4. Exit Opportunity Pull Is as Strong as Burnout Push
Analysts leave not only because they are miserable but because better options actively recruit them. PE firms recruit analysts aggressively before they complete their two-year programs. The combination of a difficult present and an attractive future makes staying longer economically irrational for many analysts.

### 5. Administrative Work Dilutes Meaning
UpSlide's finding that 40 hours per week are spent on manual tasks is critical. Analysts expected to do high-level financial work; instead much of their time is spent formatting pitch books, tracking buyer/seller lists, and making repetitive revisions. The gap between expectation and reality is a primary driver of disillusionment.

### 6. Cultural Toxicity Compounds Workload
77% abuse claim from GS is not an outlier - WSO forums document shouting, public humiliation, and favoritism as endemic. The hierarchical structure means junior staff absorb all negative consequences with no recourse.

### 7. The Bank's Response Has Been Incremental
Protected Saturdays and 80-hour caps exist on paper. But "except during live deals" carve-outs mean the protections collapse precisely when work is heaviest. Under 30% of banks are implementing real workflow improvements. The gap between stated policy and actual conditions remains wide.

## Common Mistakes (Analysts Make When Deciding to Leave)

1. **Announcing before having a plan** - Banks terminate access immediately upon resignation notice; announcing without a signed offer leaves analysts stranded
2. **Telling colleagues first** - Office gossip reaches management fast; control of the timeline is lost
3. **Quitting after a bad week, not a bad year** - Burnout cycles mean some periods are worse than others; impulsive resignations during crunch phases are regretted
4. **Underestimating the buyside recruiting window** - PE and HF firms recruit analysts on a narrow, process-driven timeline; missing the window by quitting too early or too late closes doors permanently
5. **Conflating burnout with wrong career** - Some analysts need to leave banking entirely; others need a different group, a different bank, or a different deal type; not diagnosing correctly leads to lateral moves that repeat the same problems
6. **Negotiating at resignation** - Attempting to extract counter-offers or new terms when handing in notice is almost universally ineffective and damages the professional relationship
7. **Leaving without completing a full deal cycle** - Analysts who leave before closing a deal have a harder time telling a coherent story in PE/HF interviews; timing exit after at least one closed transaction strengthens positioning
8. **Burning the bridge** - The IB network is small; MDs and VPs who were awful managers are often LPs, clients, or references five years later; the exit conversation should be professional regardless of circumstances
