### 1. Mergers & Inquisitions - Investment Banking Spring Weeks: The Full Guide
**URL:** https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banking-spring-weeks/

Full-length guide covering the entire spring week landscape in the UK. Key extracts:

- Spring weeks are 1-2 week mini-internships offered primarily in the UK to first and second-year university students. They do NOT exist in the US, Canada, or Latin America - this is a UK/European concept.
- All major bulge bracket banks run programs, plus elite boutiques (Lazard, Evercore, PJT), middle-market firms (Jefferies, Houlihan Lokey), and firms like RBC and Wells Fargo.
- Applications open August-October; offers sent December-January; scheduling confirmed February. First-come, first-serve - apply immediately when applications open.
- Application process: online app with CV + cover letter + competency questions (150-300 words each) - then numerical/verbal/logic assessments - then HireVue video or phone interviews (fit questions, occasional technical).
- Acceptance rates: below 5% per application.
- Target universities: Oxford, Cambridge, LSE, UCL. Strong grades (2:1 or above) required. Diversity candidates receive preference at many banks.
- Conversion rates to summer internship: 20-40% typical, with Citi reportedly reaching 80-90%. Roughly 50% advance from spring week to assessment center; ~50% of those receive summer offers.
- What happens during the week: shadow bankers, attend training workshops, practice skills with non-client companies, extensive networking. Live deal work is restricted due to confidentiality.
- Biggest downside: completing a spring week does NOT guarantee a summer internship. Students still need to network, gain experience, and prepare in parallel.
- Alternative paths if no spring week: summer internships at boutique PE/VC, off-cycle internships, lateral hiring post-graduation.

### 2. IB Interview Questions - Best Freshman and Sophomore Internships for IB
**URL:** https://ibinterviewquestions.com/blog/best-freshman-sophomore-internships-ib

Comprehensive tiered breakdown of what freshman and sophomore students can realistically target in the US market:

**Tier 1 - Direct Banking Opportunities:**
- Bulge bracket sophomore programs (Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan, Bank of America, Citi) - 2-3 weeks, primarily diversity candidates and target school students.
  - Goldman Sachs: Sophomore Programs + Virtual Insight Series (apps open February-March)
  - Morgan Stanley: Early Insights Program (January deadline)
  - JPMorgan: Launching Leaders Fellowship (January 13, 2026 deadline)
  - Acceptance rates: under 5%
  - Top performers receive fast-tracked interviews for junior year summer analyst positions.
- Middle-market IB internships (Houlihan Lokey, Lincoln International, Harris Williams): real analytical work - financial modeling, pitch books, valuation. Hired primarily through networking January-March. "Essentially guarantees interviews" for future roles.

**Tier 2 - Strong Stepping Stones:**
- Big Four Transaction Advisory Services (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG): M&A due diligence, valuation, deal support. $20-30/hr. Apps open September-November. "Most realistic high-value target for most students."
- Corporate Finance at Fortune 500 companies: FP&A, treasury, corporate development, IR. Microsoft, Google, Amazon, GE, P&G, Nike. $20-35/hr. "Very accessible for freshmen and sophomores with decent academics."
- Equity Research at regional firms (Raymond James, Baird, Stephens): financial modeling, industry research, valuation. $15-25/hr. Apply January-March via networking.
- Boutique investment banks (1-10 person firms, lower middle-market): real deal work, often unpaid. "Very accessible if willing to work for free and network aggressively."

**Freshman Year Strategy:**
- Start networking spring of freshman year.
- Join finance clubs, pursue leadership positions.
- Target any finance-related role, even unpaid boutique positions.
- "Experience matters more than compensation freshman summer."

**Sophomore Year Strategy:**
- Apply early to diversity programs September-October.
- Target middle-market IB, Big 4 advisory, equity research, corporate development.
- Leverage freshman internship to demonstrate commitment.
- With two finance summers, "extremely competitive for junior year banking recruiting."

**Application volume:** Target 80-100+ opportunities combining formal apps and networking. Mix: 80% realistic Tier 2 targets, 20% reach applications.

**Key principle:** "Students with Big Four or middle-market IB sophomore internships regularly win offers at Goldman, Morgan Stanley, and elite boutiques. The stepping stone approach works, but only if you start early and execute strategically."

## Key Themes

### Theme 1: UK Spring Weeks vs. US Sophomore Programs - Two Separate Tracks
Spring weeks are a UK/European concept only. US students have entirely different programs - "sophomore summer analyst programs" and "early insight" or "diversity" programs at major banks. An article must distinguish clearly between the two geographies or risk confusing readers.

### Theme 2: The Pipeline Logic
The universally understood pipeline: Spring Week (UK Year 1) - Summer Analyst Internship (UK Year 2 / US Junior Year) - Full-Time Offer. Sophomore programs in the US serve the same gateway function as spring weeks in the UK. Both are designed to identify talent 1-2 years before the main graduate recruiting cycle.

### Theme 3: Early Action is Everything
Both tracks share the same core lesson: apply as early as possible. UK spring week apps open August-October for March/April programs. US sophomore apps open September-October for the following summer. Delay is the single biggest structural mistake.

### Theme 4: Conversion Rates Are Not Guaranteed
Spring weeks convert to summer internships at 20-40% typical rates (higher at some firms like Citi at ~80-90%). Sophomore programs at BBs convert at varying rates. Neither pathway is a guarantee - parallel networking and preparation must continue regardless.

### Theme 5: Target School and Diversity Dynamics
Both tracks heavily favor students at target universities and diversity candidates. Non-target students face compounding difficulty and must compensate with aggressive networking, boutique stepping stones, and earlier starts.

### Theme 6: The Stepping Stone Strategy
For students who cannot access BB/EB programs directly, the consensus across all sources is clear: take any finance-adjacent role (Big 4 TAS, corporate finance, boutique IB, equity research at regional firms) as a freshman or sophomore, then leverage that experience into BB summer analyst recruiting as a junior. This works reliably when executed early.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Waiting until sophomore year to start** - both tracks require freshman-year groundwork: networking, GPA protection, first finance experience. Starting sophomore year is already late for the most competitive programs.

2. **Only applying to elite banks** - applying only to Goldman/Morgan Stanley/JPMorgan as a sophomore without parallel boutique/Big 4 applications is a losing strategy. The stepping stone model is validated across all sources.

3. **Treating spring week completion as a finish line** - converting a spring week into a summer internship is not automatic (20-40% overall). Students who stop networking after a spring week offer are making a structural error.

4. **Refusing unpaid boutique roles** - unpaid boutique internships generate real deal experience and are the most accessible entry point. Turning them down for resume prestige reasons leaves students with nothing.

5. **Applying late to spring week programs** - UK banks allocate spots on a first-come, first-serve basis. Applying in November for programs that opened in August guarantees rejection regardless of qualifications.

6. **Neglecting GPA early** - difficult course loads freshman year that damage GPA are a compounding mistake. A GPA below 3.5 filters candidates out at the application stage for most formal programs.

7. **Not following up after spring week / insight programs** - bankers cannot judge work product during a spring week (no live deal access). Networking quality and follow-up are the primary evaluation signals. Failing to collect contacts and follow up is the most common performance mistake during the week itself.

8. **Ignoring the diversity program track** - students from underrepresented groups who do not apply to diversity-specific programs (JPMorgan Launching Leaders, Goldman Possibilities Series, similar) are leaving accessible fast-track pathways on the table.

9. **Not targeting the right year for UK programs** - UK spring weeks target Year 1 students (or Year 2 at four-year courses). Applying in the wrong year wastes an application cycle and signals a lack of preparation.

10. **Treating spring internships (academic year) as inferior** - a spring semester internship at a boutique, even unpaid, is a valid and effective bridge to BB summer analyst programs. It is not a fallback - it is a legitimate strategy.
