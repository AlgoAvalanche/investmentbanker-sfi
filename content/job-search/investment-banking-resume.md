# Investment Banking Resume: The Hard Rules from Recruiters Who Read 5000 a Year

Your investment banking resume will be read for approximately six seconds on the first pass. That is not a figure of speech. Eye-tracking studies on recruiter behavior - and anyone who has sat in a resume review session at a bulge bracket during analyst recruiting season - will tell you the same thing: the first cut is brutal, fast, and almost entirely based on pattern recognition.

This is not a place for creativity. It is a place for precision.

What follows is a synthesis of hard-won recruiter feedback, guidance from sources like Mergers & Inquisitions and Wall Street Oasis, and the kind of institutional knowledge that gets passed around in analyst classes but rarely written down clearly.

---

## What Recruiters Are Actually Looking For

Before you touch a single line of formatting, understand what the person reading your investment banking resume is trying to do. They are not trying to hire you. They are trying to eliminate you quickly so they can focus on a smaller stack.

The filter criteria, roughly in order of how fast they apply:

1. **School** - Target or semi-target. Yes, this is the first thing. No, it is not fair.
2. **GPA** - Is it visible? Is it above threshold?
3. **Internship brand names** - Goldman, Lazard, top PE fund, top consulting firm. Known names get read; unknown names get skimmed.
4. **Bullet quality** - Does this person understand finance, or are they a communications major who attended two finance club meetings?
5. **Formatting** - Does it look like the standard template or does it look like a college brochure?

Recruiters at large banks have told outlets like Mergers & Inquisitions that they can eliminate 70-80% of resumes in the first pass purely on criteria 1 and 2. The remaining 20-30% get a real read. Your job is to make it into that stack and then outperform once you're there.

---

## The One-Page Rule Is Not Negotiable

For analysts and associates below the VP level: one page, no exceptions. This is not an opinion. Every senior recruiter interviewed by WSO and M&I over the past decade says the same thing. Two-page resumes from candidates with two years of experience are a red flag, not a sign of impressive depth.

If your resume does not fit on one page, you are either including irrelevant information or using too much space on things that do not matter. Cut ruthlessly.

## Investment Banking Resume Format: The Only Template That Works

This section is going to sound repetitive if you have done any research. That is because there is genuinely only one format that works consistently. It is the one that looks like every other banking resume. The reason it works is precisely because it looks like every other banking resume - recruiters can find information without thinking.

**Structure:**

- **Header**: Name, phone, email, LinkedIn URL. No photo. No address. No "Objective" statement.
- **Education**: University, degree, GPA, graduation date. If you have a CFA Level 1 pass or relevant coursework, it goes here.
- **Experience**: Reverse chronological. Job title, firm name, location, dates. Three to five bullets per role.
- **Skills**: Technical skills (Excel, Bloomberg, Capital IQ, Python if genuinely proficient). Languages if business-level.
- **Activities/Leadership**: Finance club, case competitions, anything demonstrating genuine engagement.

That is it. No "Profile Summary." No "Core Competencies" box with icons. No color gradients. Black ink, white page, consistent margins, one font (Garamond or Times New Roman at 10-11pt, or Arial/Calibri if you prefer sans-serif).

Mergers & Inquisitions offers a free template that is used by thousands of analysts each year. It is worth using as a baseline not because it is creative but because it is correct.

## GPA on Resume: When to Include It, When to Hide It, and When to Spin It

The GPA question causes more anxiety than almost anything else in banking recruiting. Here is the reality:

**Bulge bracket minimum**: 3.5 at most BBs, 3.7 at the most competitive (Goldman, Evercore, Centerview). These are soft minimums that get you past the initial screen, not guarantees of anything.

**What to do with a low GPA:**

If your GPA is below 3.3, you have a few options, none of them perfect:

- **Include it and compensate elsewhere**: Extraordinary internship experience, military service, working 30 hours a week while in school. These need to be stated explicitly - recruiters will not infer.
- **Include your major GPA if it is higher**: "Major GPA: 3.6" is legitimate and used routinely when overall GPA drags from a breadth requirement course that went badly.
- **Leave it off**: Only do this if you have enough other material to justify it and you are not applying to firms with automatic GPA screens. Be aware that many firms will ask for a transcript anyway, and omitting GPA reads as a signal.

One nuance from Wall Street Oasis discussions: if you are applying to boutiques or smaller advisory shops, the GPA threshold is often lower and the human read is more genuine. A 3.2 with three relevant internships and a clear technical skill set will get further at a Houlihan Lokey or Lincoln International than at Goldman.

## How to Write Bullets That Actually Work

This is where most resumes fail even after they pass the initial filter. The bullet structure is formulaic by design.

**The formula**: Action verb + specific task/deal/model + quantified result or context.

Bad: "Assisted in financial modeling and analysis for various transactions."

Good: "Built three-statement LBO model for $240M carve-out acquisition; identified 180bps of margin improvement opportunity that shaped final bid price."

The bad bullet is useless because it tells the recruiter nothing they could not assume from the job title. The good bullet tells them you know what an LBO is, you worked on a real transaction, you understood the output well enough to derive insight, and you can quantify your work.

**Sourcing the numbers**: If you are early career and your work was supporting, you can still cite deal size, number of models built, pages of CIM reviewed, number of companies in a screening list. Not every number has to be a direct personal impact metric.

**Verb list that signals finance fluency**: Built, modeled, analyzed, structured, evaluated, synthesized, benchmarked, screened, diligenced, presented, proposed. Avoid: "helped with," "assisted in," "was responsible for," "participated in."

## The Sections Candidates Get Wrong Most Often

**Skills section**: Do not list Microsoft Word or PowerPoint. Listing "Microsoft Office Suite" is the single most reliable signal that someone does not know what they are doing. List Excel (and be specific - "financial modeling, DCF, LBO"), Bloomberg, Capital IQ, FactSet, Python (only if you can write working code), SQL. If you have completed BIWS or Breaking Into Wall Street courses and can demonstrate the modeling output, that is worth noting.

**Activities section**: Finance club president is good. Finance club member is filler. Case competition finalist at a recognized event is good. "Attended investment banking seminars" is not worth the line. Be selective. Three strong entries beat seven weak ones.

**Dates**: Use consistent formatting. "May 2024 - Aug 2024" or "05/2024 - 08/2024" - pick one and use it throughout. Inconsistency is a minor signal but recruiter patience is not unlimited.

## The GPA Threshold Problem: A Real Talk Moment

From a thread on Wall Street Oasis that got substantial engagement: a user with a 3.45 GPA from a semi-target was getting rejected from every BB but getting through at several EBs and MM banks. The advice from people in the thread who were analysts at those firms: "Your GPA is a 3.45. Stop applying to Goldman and start applying to Rothschild, William Blair, and Raymond James. Get a year of deal experience and then re-evaluate."

That is blunt. It is also correct. The banking resume is a filtering document, and if you are fighting the filter rather than working with it, you are wasting cycles. Understand where your profile fits before you spray applications.

## What Recruiters Specifically Hate

A few items from recruiter feedback compiled by various sources including recruiting firms like Oxbridge Research and SG Partners:

- **Objective statements**: Nobody needs to read "Seeking a challenging role in investment banking where I can leverage my analytical skills." Delete it.
- **Functional resumes**: Any format that is not reverse-chronological signals that you are trying to hide something. It also makes recruiters work harder to find information. They will not do that.
- **Decorative formatting**: Tables, columns, sidebars, icons, color. These also break applicant tracking systems, which means your resume may never reach a human reader.
- **High school information**: If you are past your first year of university, your high school achievements are irrelevant. The exceptions are extraordinary: represented your country in a science olympiad, achieved something that has an obvious adult-world analog.
- **References available upon request**: This line has not been used since 2003. Removing it saves you a line.

## The ATS Problem

Most large banks and many boutiques now run applications through an Applicant Tracking System before a human sees them. ATS systems parse text, so formatting that looks great as a PDF can come out as gibberish in the system.

The practical implications: use a clean, simple template with no tables or text boxes for the core content. Save and submit as PDF. If the portal specifically requests Word, submit Word. Test your resume by copy-pasting it into a plain text editor - if the structure survives that, the ATS will handle it correctly.

BIWS has written extensively on ATS optimization for banking resumes and it is worth reading their guidance on file format and keyword placement.

## Tailoring the Resume for Different Groups

The same resume should not go to every group without modification. The differences are subtle but they matter:

- **M&A and coverage groups**: Emphasize deal experience, financial modeling, pitch deck work.
- **Restructuring**: Flag any distressed analysis, credit work, bankruptcy coursework.
- **Leveraged Finance / DCF**: Quantitative modeling experience, credit analysis, any fixed income coursework.
- **ECM/DCM**: Client interaction experience, presentation work, any capital markets coursework.

These are not dramatic rewrites. They are reorderings and reemphases. Move the most relevant bullet to the top. Adjust your skills section to highlight what that group cares about.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Download the M&I or BIWS resume template** and reformat your current resume to match it exactly. Do not start from a blank page - start from a known-correct structure.

2. **Audit every bullet against the action-number-context formula.** Any bullet that does not have a specific task and at least an attempt at quantification should be rewritten or cut.

3. **Run your GPA through the filter honestly.** If you are below 3.5, identify which firms have lower thresholds and target those first before attacking BBs. Get deal experience first if you need to.

4. **Submit your resume for critique on Wall Street Oasis or to a recruiter before you send it anywhere.** Fresh eyes find things you have stopped seeing. This is not optional - a resume you have worked on for weeks has blind spots.

5. **Check the [LinkedIn optimization guide](/job-search/linkedin-optimization-ib/) to make sure your LinkedIn profile is consistent with your resume.** Recruiters cross-reference, and inconsistencies raise questions. Then review the [cover letter guide](/job-search/cover-letters-not-chatgpt/) - a strong resume with a weak cover letter is still a weak application at firms that read both.

When you have done all of that, read through the [common interview questions](/interview-prep/investment-banking-interview-questions/) - because if your resume is doing its job, you will need to be ready for what comes next.
