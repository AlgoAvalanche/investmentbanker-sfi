# Investment Banking Resume: The Hard Rules from Recruiters Who Read 5000 a Year

Your investment banking resume will be reviewed for somewhere between ten and thirty seconds on the first pass. That range is not rhetorical. [Street of Walls](https://www.streetofwalls.com/finance-training-courses/investment-banking-overview-and-behavioral-training/investment-banking-resume/) - whose IB training material is used by thousands of candidates annually - puts the figure at 10 to 15 seconds when banking analysts are doing the initial review. [Mergers and Inquisitions](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banking/recruitment/resumes/) and [Wall Street Prep](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/investment-banking-resume-template/) both land closer to 30 seconds for a first-pass read. The difference depends on who is holding the stack. Analysts at target schools during peak recruiting season move faster than an HR generalist with a full afternoon scheduled for review.

Either way, the implication is the same: the first cut is mechanical, fast, and based almost entirely on pattern recognition. There is no room for creativity in this document. There is only room for precision.

What follows is a synthesis of hard-won recruiter feedback, guidance from the most credible sources in the industry - including [M&I](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/free-investment-banking-resume-template/), [WSO](https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/resources/templates/word-templates/investment-banking-resume-template), and [Wall Street Prep](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/investment-banking-resume-template/) - and the kind of institutional knowledge that gets passed around in analyst classes but rarely written down clearly.

## What Recruiters Are Actually Looking For

Before you touch a single line of formatting, understand what the person reading your investment banking resume is trying to do. They are not trying to hire you. They are trying to eliminate you quickly so they can focus on a smaller stack.

[Street of Walls identifies four pillars](https://www.streetofwalls.com/finance-training-courses/investment-banking-overview-and-behavioral-training/investment-banking-resume/) that banking reviewers are evaluating, in roughly this order:

1. **Quantitative and analytical ability** - Problem-solving, data synthesis, coursework rigor. Can you model? Can you think?
2. **Initiative and motivation** - Did you seek out relevant experience, or did you stumble into finance at senior year?
3. **Communication skills** - Can you write a clean bullet point? Your resume is evidence of your writing ability before you walk in the door.
4. **Finance interest** - Investment clubs, case competitions, relevant coursework, any demonstrated engagement with markets.

But before any of those four pillars get evaluated, the resume passes through a much blunter pre-filter. The filter criteria, roughly in order of how fast they apply:

- **School** - Target or semi-target. Yes, this is the first thing. No, it is not fair.
- **GPA** - Is it visible? Is it above threshold?
- **Internship brand names** - Goldman, Lazard, top PE fund, top consulting firm. Known names get read; unknown names get skimmed.
- **Bullet quality** - Does this person understand finance, or are they a communications major who attended two finance club meetings?
- **Formatting** - Does it look like the standard template, or does it look like a college brochure?

Candidates who pass the pre-filter then get evaluated on the four pillars. Candidates who fail the pre-filter never get there. Your job is to clear the pre-filter reliably, then make the most of the deeper read.

## The One-Page Rule Is Not Negotiable

For analysts and associates below the VP level: one page, no exceptions. [M&I's comprehensive resume guide](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banking/recruitment/resumes/) is unambiguous on this - one page maximum for undergraduates and most professionals, with a second "transaction page" only becoming acceptable for VP-level candidates with 10 to 20 or more completed deals.

Two-page resumes from candidates with two years of experience are a red flag, not a sign of impressive depth. The signal they send is that you do not understand the norm, or that you cannot edit your own work ruthlessly - both of which are problems in banking.

If your resume does not fit on one page, you are including irrelevant material or wasting space on things that do not matter. The fix is always to cut, never to expand the page count.

One regional note from M&I: Australia commonly requires two to three pages even at entry level. If you are applying to Australian banks, adjust accordingly. For US and European banks, one page is the rule.

## Investment Banking Resume Format: The Only Template That Works

[M&I's free template page](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/free-investment-banking-resume-template/) states this plainly: there is only one format that works for Wall Street. That is not hyperbole. The reason the standard template works is precisely because it is standard - recruiters can locate information without thinking, which means your content gets evaluated faster and more fairly.

The technical specifications from M&I:

- **Margins**: Minimum 0.5 inches, preferably 0.75 inches or greater. Never reduce margins below 0.5 to force content onto one page - reduce font size first.
- **Font size**: Avoid size 8. If the page looks like an eye chart that requires zooming, it will be passed over.
- **Dates and locations**: Right-aligned using tab stops. Not the spacebar. Inconsistent alignment is a minor signal but reviewer patience is not unlimited.
- **File format**: Submit as PDF unless the portal specifically requests Word. [Wall Street Prep](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/investment-banking-resume-template/) makes this point explicitly - submitting a Word document risks formatting shifts between your machine and the reviewer's screen.
- **Name**: Centered at the top in a larger font. Not left-aligned. Not in a header text box.

**Structure:**

- **Header**: Name, phone, email, LinkedIn URL. No photo. No address. No objective statement.
- **Education**: University, degree, GPA, graduation date. Current students lead with this section. Professionals with experience place it below work history.
- **Experience**: Reverse chronological. Job title, firm name, location, dates. Two to four major entries.
- **Skills**: Languages first, then technical tools (Excel, Bloomberg, Capital IQ, Python if genuinely proficient). No Microsoft Office Suite - this phrase is the single most reliable signal that someone does not understand what they are doing.
- **Activities/Leadership**: Finance club, case competitions, anything demonstrating genuine engagement with finance. Three strong entries beat seven weak ones.

No "Profile Summary." No "Core Competencies" box with icons. No color, no gradients, no sidebars. Black ink, white page, one font. Garamond or Times New Roman at 10-11pt, or Arial/Calibri for sans-serif preference.

## GPA on Resume: When to Include It, When to Hide It

The GPA question causes more anxiety than almost anything else in banking recruiting. Here is the reality from people who have looked at these resumes for years.

**The M&I rule**: Include your GPA even if it is below 3.5. [Their template guidance](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/free-investment-banking-resume-template/) is explicit - omitting GPA entirely signals to reviewers that it is below 2.0. A 3.2 with everything else on the page is better than a blank that triggers the worst possible inference.

**Bulge bracket thresholds**: Most BBs use 3.5 as a soft minimum for initial screening. The most competitive shops - Goldman, Evercore, Centerview - are closer to 3.7. These are screening thresholds, not guarantees of anything. A 3.8 from a non-target will not outweigh a 3.5 from a target in the initial sort.

**What to do with a low GPA:**

- **Include major GPA if it is meaningfully higher**: "Major GPA: 3.6" is legitimate and used routinely when an early breadth requirement course dragged down the overall. Be accurate - do not invent a major GPA calculation that does not correspond to how your school calculates it.
- **Compensate explicitly**: Extraordinary internship experience, military service, working significant hours during school. These need to be stated, not inferred. A recruiter will not assume you had a reason for the 3.2.
- **Target appropriately**: [WSO's template and forum guidance](https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/resources/templates/word-templates/investment-banking-resume-template) reflects what working analysts say consistently - boutiques and middle market firms run more genuine reads than BBs. A 3.2 with three relevant internships and a clear technical skill set will get further at Houlihan Lokey or Lincoln International than at Goldman. Understand where your profile fits before you spend months applying to firms whose filters will screen you out automatically.

**SAT scores**: Include only if above 1400 on the old scale or 2100-plus on the new scale, per M&I. Below those thresholds, including them costs you credibility.

## How to Write Bullets That Actually Work

This is where most resumes fail even after they pass the initial filter. [M&I's bullet framework](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/free-investment-banking-resume-template/) is a two-part structure:

**(1) Specifically what you did + (2) Results achieved**

Connect the two parts with a semicolon or the word "by."

**Weak**: "Assisted in financial modeling and analysis for various transactions."

**Strong**: "Built three-statement LBO model for $240M carve-out acquisition; identified 180bps of margin improvement that shaped the final bid price."

The weak bullet tells the recruiter nothing they could not assume from the job title. The strong bullet tells them you know what an LBO is, you worked on a real transaction, you understood the output well enough to derive insight, and you can quantify your work.

[WSO's template guidance](https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/resources/templates/word-templates/investment-banking-resume-template) adds a practical constraint: keep bullets to a maximum of two lines, with 1.5 lines as the ideal. Bullets that run longer than two lines force the reviewer to slow down, and you do not want to create friction anywhere in a 30-second read.

One exception on length: sub-bullets for transaction experience. If you completed a notable deal during an internship and need a second line to describe what you specifically contributed, a well-constructed sub-bullet is acceptable - and can actually demonstrate more finance fluency than a single compressed line would.

**Sourcing the numbers**: If you are early career and your work was supporting, you can still cite deal size, number of models built, pages of CIM reviewed, number of companies in a screening list. Not every number has to be a direct personal impact metric. Context quantification ("supported $1.2B acquisition process") is better than nothing.

**Verb list that signals finance fluency**: Built, modeled, analyzed, structured, evaluated, synthesized, benchmarked, screened, diligenced, presented, proposed. Avoid: "helped with," "assisted in," "was responsible for," "participated in." The passive construction tells the reader you were present. The active verb tells them you did something.

## The Sections Candidates Get Wrong Most Often

**Skills section**: Do not list Microsoft Word. Do not list PowerPoint. "Microsoft Office Suite" is a filler phrase that signals no genuine technical skill. What to list instead: Excel (be specific - financial modeling, DCF, LBO), Bloomberg Terminal, Capital IQ, FactSet, Python (only if you can write working code and defend it in an interview), SQL if relevant. Languages go first per M&I's template guidance. If you have completed BIWS or Wall Street Prep courses and can demonstrate the modeling output, that is worth noting - it signals deliberate preparation.

**Activities section**: Finance club president is good. Finance club member is filler. Case competition finalist at a recognized event is good. "Attended investment banking seminars" is not worth the line. [Street of Walls](https://www.streetofwalls.com/finance-training-courses/investment-banking-overview-and-behavioral-training/investment-banking-resume/) flags finance interest as one of the four pillars banks evaluate - but the activities section demonstrates that interest. Padding it with low-signal entries does the opposite of what you intend.

**Objective statement**: Delete it. "Seeking a challenging role in investment banking where I can leverage my analytical skills" tells the recruiter nothing they did not know from the fact that you sent a resume to an investment bank. The line costs you space and patience.

**High school**: Past your first year of university, high school is irrelevant. Remove it. The exceptions are extraordinary - national olympiad placements, achievements with a direct adult-world analog. For everyone else, the high school line reads as filler that a confident candidate would not need.

**Dates**: Consistent formatting throughout. "May 2024 - Aug 2024" or "05/2024 - 08/2024" - pick one, use it everywhere. Right-align with tab stops, not spaces.

## The ATS Problem Most Candidates Ignore

Most large banks and many boutiques now route applications through an Applicant Tracking System before a human reads them. ATS systems parse text, so formatting that renders beautifully as a PDF can arrive as scrambled characters in the system.

The practical implications: use a clean template with no tables, text boxes, or columns for core content sections. [Wall Street Prep](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/investment-banking-resume-template/) emphasizes PDF as the standard submission format specifically because formatting survives the conversion. If the portal requests Word, submit Word - but keep the underlying structure clean.

Test your resume by copying the full text into a plain text editor like Notepad. If the structure - education, experience, skills - survives that conversion with correct ordering and no garbled characters, the ATS will handle it correctly. If sections collapse or lines appear in the wrong order, the template has structural problems that formatting alone will not fix.

The rule is also simpler than many candidates make it: avoid anything decorative. Tables, sidebars, colored boxes, icons, columns - all of these create ATS parsing failures at the same time they signal creative formatting that banks do not want to see. The conservative template is not a stylistic preference. It is the technically correct approach.

## Tailoring the Resume for Different Groups

The same resume should not go to every group without modification. The differences are subtle but they matter at the margin - and at competitive banks, the margin is where decisions get made.

- **M&A and coverage groups**: Emphasize deal experience, financial modeling depth, pitch deck and CIM work.
- **Restructuring**: Flag any distressed analysis, credit work, or bankruptcy coursework. If you took a corporate restructuring course, name it. If you analyzed a distressed company for a class, build a bullet around it.
- **Leveraged Finance / LBO**: Quantitative modeling experience, credit analysis, fixed income coursework. LBO modeling experience is highly valued here - name the model and the transaction size.
- **ECM/DCM**: Client interaction experience, presentation work, any capital markets coursework or relevant thesis work.

These are not dramatic rewrites. They are reorderings and reemphases. Move the most relevant bullet to the top of each role. Adjust the skills section to highlight what that specific group cares about. Keep a master version and derive targeted versions from it.

## The Review Process: Who Actually Reads Your Resume

[Street of Walls documents](https://www.streetofwalls.com/finance-training-courses/investment-banking-overview-and-behavioral-training/investment-banking-resume/) how the review hierarchy actually works at most banks:

- **Internship and analyst applications**: Banking analysts review the resumes. People who were in your position 12 to 24 months ago are making the initial cuts. They know what good looks like because they went through the same process recently.
- **Core school applicants**: The alumni team - typically analysts and associates who attended target schools - handles screening. They are pattern-matching against their own application.
- **Associate positions**: Associates and above review. The bar for technical depth in bullets rises.

The implication for your formatting choices: the person reviewing your resume is not an HR professional with a checklist. They are a 23 or 24-year-old analyst in the middle of deal work who has been given 200 resumes and a deadline. The conservative template does not just signal that you know the norm - it actively reduces the cognitive load on the person making the first call. That is why it works.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Download the M&I or BIWS template** and reformat your current resume to match it exactly. Do not start from a blank page - start from [a known-correct structure](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/free-investment-banking-resume-template/). Do not add creative elements to differentiate yourself.

2. **Audit every bullet against the two-part formula**: specifically what you did, then the result. Any bullet without a specific task and at least an attempt at quantification should be rewritten or cut.

3. **Run your GPA through the filter honestly.** If you are below 3.5, identify which firms have lower thresholds and target those first before attacking BBs. Get deal experience first if you need to. The resume is a filtering document - work with the filter, not against it.

4. **Test the ATS resilience** of your template by copying the full text into a plain text editor. If it breaks, fix the underlying structure.

5. **Submit your resume for critique before you send it anywhere.** Post it to [Wall Street Oasis](https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/resources/templates/word-templates/investment-banking-resume-template), send it to a recruiter, or ask an analyst at the firms you are targeting. A resume you have worked on for three weeks has blind spots that a fresh reader will find in three minutes.

6. **Check the [LinkedIn optimization guide](/job-search/linkedin-optimization-ib/)** to make sure your LinkedIn profile is consistent with your resume. Recruiters cross-reference, and inconsistencies raise questions. Then review the [cover letter guide](/job-search/cover-letters-not-chatgpt/) - a strong resume with a weak cover letter is still a weak application at firms that read both.

When you have done all of that, read through the [common interview questions](/interview-prep/investment-banking-interview-questions/) - because if your resume is doing its job, you will need to be ready for what comes next.
