# The 100 Cup of Coffee Strategy: What Actually Works

The "hundred cups of coffee" metaphor has been in finance recruiting circles long enough that it has acquired the status of received wisdom - which means it gets repeated without being examined. The idea is simple: talk to enough bankers, and eventually someone will help you get a job. The reality is more nuanced and more instructive.

The number is not the point. The nature of the conversations is the point. Fifty genuinely productive conversations will beat two hundred transactional ones. A candidate who has had three memorable exchanges with people at a target firm is in a better position than one who has collected a hundred polite "good luck" responses from bankers who will not remember them in two weeks.

This guide is about the quality side of the equation - what actually makes a networking conversation convert into a career outcome.

---

## Why the Coffee Chat Strategy Works (When It Works)

Investment banking hiring, particularly at boutiques and in lateral contexts, is substantially relationship-mediated. Before any application goes through a formal process, someone has to believe you are worth the read. At large banks during on-cycle recruiting, that someone is a recruiter with a keyword filter. But at mid-size firms, small boutiques, and in off-cycle situations, that someone is a person who has an opinion about you.

The coffee chat strategy exploits this reality. Its purpose is not to transmit information about yourself - your resume does that. Its purpose is to become a specific person in the mind of someone who can advocate for you.

The mechanism is simple: humans hire people they know. When a group has a headcount to fill and the choice is between a pile of anonymous resumes and one resume attached to a person who three current analysts have spoken to, the known quantity wins at the margin. The coffee chat strategy is how you become the known quantity.

## Before the Call: The Preparation That Actually Matters

Most candidates prepare for networking calls the way they prepare for a casual conversation - which is to say, they do not prepare at all. They think of a few questions and show up. This is the single biggest waste of the opportunity.

Preparation for a networking call should take thirty to forty-five minutes per person. It should include:

**Research on the person:**
- LinkedIn profile: school, career trajectory, specific roles
- Any public content they have produced: conference panels, published commentary, firm announcements they are quoted in
- Any deals their group has announced recently that they might have worked on

**Research on the firm:**
- Two or three recent notable transactions
- The group's positioning within the bank or advisory landscape
- Any publicly available commentary from senior leadership about firm direction

**Research on the role:**
- What a first-year analyst in that group actually does day-to-day
- What the typical deal flow looks like
- What skills or backgrounds the group tends to value

This preparation serves two functions. First, it allows you to ask questions that demonstrate genuine engagement rather than generic curiosity. Second, it gives you material to reference in the conversation that signals you have done the work.

There is a third function that is not obvious until you are in the conversation: when you are well-prepared, you are less anxious. And when you are less anxious, you come across as more confident and more natural. The preparation is, among other things, a confidence investment.

## The Call Structure That Converts

A productive networking call with a banker has a loose structure that you should follow without making it feel like an interview.

**Opening (2-3 minutes)**: Thank them for their time, briefly establish the connection (mutual contact, their school, the specific thing that made you reach out to them specifically). Make it clear this is not a random cold call - you sought them out for a reason.

**Their background (5-7 minutes)**: Ask about their path. How did they get into banking? What made them choose this firm over others? What was the transition from school to their first role actually like? People enjoy talking about their own story, and this segment builds rapport while you gather genuine intelligence.

**The work and the firm (5-10 minutes)**: Ask about what the group actually works on, what a typical week looks like, what they find most and least interesting about the work. This is where your firm research pays off - reference a specific deal or initiative rather than asking "what does the group do."

**Your situation (3-5 minutes)**: Briefly explain your background and what you are trying to accomplish. Keep this short and specific. Not a life story. Something like: "I have spent two summers doing credit analysis at [firm]. I am trying to move into advisory work, and specifically into restructuring because of the complexity of the stakeholder dynamics. I am curious whether my background would be relevant to what your group does."

**Ask for their honest take (2-3 minutes)**: "Given what I just described, do you think my background is a reasonable fit for what you look for, or are there gaps I should be working on?" This is the most valuable question most candidates never ask. Honest recruiters and bankers will tell you something useful here. Even if they give you a diplomatic non-answer, how they frame it is informative.

**The close (1-2 minutes)**: Thank them. Ask if there is anyone else on their team or in their network who they think it would be worth speaking with. This is not aggressive - it is the natural end of a useful conversation.

## The Questions That Get You Remembered

Generic questions produce forgettable conversations. Specific questions create memorable ones.

**Bad questions:**
- "What advice do you have for someone trying to break into banking?"
- "What is the culture like at your firm?"
- "What is the most important skill for success in your group?"

These questions signal that you have not done enough research to ask anything better. Every banker who has done a networking call has heard them hundreds of times.

**Better questions:**
- "You moved from [previous firm] to [current firm] - what was the specific thing that made that move make sense for you at the time?"
- "I read about your involvement in the [specific transaction] - what was the most complicated part of that process that did not make it into the press release?"
- "Your group seems to be more active on the [specific type] side than your competitors. Is that a deliberate positioning, or more a reflection of deal flow?"

These questions accomplish something important: they demonstrate that you already know things and are trying to know more. That is the posture of someone who might be worth hiring, not someone who is looking for a tutorial.

## The Follow-Up Architecture

Most candidates send a thank-you email. A smaller number send a substantive follow-up. A very small number turn the follow-up into a genuine ongoing exchange that eventually produces a referral.

**The initial thank-you (within 24 hours)**:
Reference one specific thing from the conversation that was useful to you. Not "thank you for your time and insights" - something concrete, like "your point about how restructuring mandates often hinge on the relationship with the company's existing lenders rather than the debtor itself was something I had not thought about. I spent some time reading through recent Chapter 11 cases and can see exactly what you mean."

This kind of thank-you does several things: it proves you were paying attention, it demonstrates intellectual engagement, and it gives the person a specific reason to remember the exchange.

**The second contact (3-4 weeks later)**:
Send something useful. An article about a development in their sector, a question that genuinely came up as a result of the first conversation, an update on a project you mentioned. The point is to re-establish the connection without asking for anything.

**The third contact (when you are ready to ask for something)**:
This is where you can ask for a resume referral, a flag to the recruiting team, or an introduction to someone specific at the firm. By the time you make this ask, you have had three exchanges and formed a real relationship. The ask does not come out of nowhere.

## The Volume Problem: When to Send More Emails

For [non-target candidates](/job-search/how-to-network-non-target/), volume matters. The conversion rate from cold outreach to conversation to referral is genuinely low at every step. If you are targeting twenty firms and want two or three advocates at each, you need to have initial conversations with a much larger pool.

A reasonable expectation of conversion rates through the funnel:
- Cold email to reply: 15-30% (higher for alumni, lower for cold non-alumni)
- Reply to completed call: 70-80% (most people who reply will talk to you)
- Completed call to meaningful relationship (second or third contact): 25-35%
- Meaningful relationship to referral or internal advocate: 30-50%

Working backwards: if you want six genuine advocates at your top three firms, you need to have approximately forty completed conversations, which requires approximately sixty people to agree to talk to you, which requires reaching out to approximately two hundred people.

This is not discouraging - it is useful information. It means you need to start early and treat the outreach as a continuous pipeline rather than a burst activity.

## Virtual Coffee Chats: What Changes

The pandemic shifted most networking conversations to phone or video, and the format has largely stayed there. A few things are different in a virtual format:

**Video on by default**: In a video call, having your camera off while the other person has theirs on is an odd social dynamic. Have your camera on. Find a neutral background.

**The social warm-up is shorter**: In-person coffee has a natural five-minute "get coffee, find a table, settle in" warm-up. Video calls start abruptly. Have your opening sentence ready.

**Technical issues happen**: Have a phone number as a backup. If the video call drops, immediately switch to audio and don't make a big deal of it.

**Follow up differently**: In-person conversations often end with a business card or a physical object that serves as a memory anchor. Video calls end with nothing. Your follow-up email is especially important to send quickly because there is no physical artifact of the conversation.

## When the Call Goes Badly

Some calls go badly. The banker is distracted, monosyllabic, or clearly doing you a favor they regret. Some are hostile in a low-key way that communicates "I do not know why I agreed to this."

When this happens: do not panic, do not over-compensate with nervous energy, and do not write the person off. A call that felt bad to you may have been a perfectly normal interaction for them. Send the same thoughtful follow-up you would send after a great call. You have no way of knowing what their impression was.

That said, some calls genuinely go nowhere and the person will not be an advocate for you. This is fine. Not everyone you speak with will become a champion for your candidacy. Move on and focus your energy on the relationships that are developing.

## The Coffee Chat as Interview Preparation

One underrated function of networking calls: they are the best possible preparation for the "why banking" and "why this firm" questions you will face in formal interviews. If you have had thirty conversations with bankers and heard thirty different answers to the question "what do you actually do here," you will have a much richer and more authentic answer when an interviewer asks why you want to do it.

The [networking calls that convert guide](/interview-prep/networking-calls-that-convert/) covers the interview-preparation dimension of this in more detail. The short version: the conversations that make you more knowledgeable also make you more credible, and credibility is what closes the gap between "could probably do this job" and "hire this person."

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Pick five people for your first wave of outreach this week.** Not fifty - five. Write each email specifically rather than from a template. Measure how many replies you get. Adjust the email structure based on what worked.

2. **Build a simple tracking spreadsheet.** Columns: Name, Firm, Group, Date Contacted, Date of Call, Follow-Up Sent, Status. This turns networking from an amorphous activity into a managed pipeline.

3. **Prepare your thirty-second personal pitch before any call.** This is the answer to "tell me about yourself" in a networking context. It should be clear, specific, and conclude with why you are reaching out to this person specifically.

4. **Review your [LinkedIn profile](/job-search/linkedin-optimization-ib/) before you start outreach.** The first thing every person you contact will do is look at your LinkedIn. Make sure it says what you want it to say.

5. **Set a target of one new conversation per week minimum.** Consistency matters more than bursts. One conversation a week for six months is twenty-five conversations - enough to build a meaningful network if each one is followed up properly.
