# The 100 Cup of Coffee Strategy: What Actually Works

The "hundred cups of coffee" metaphor has been circulating in finance recruiting long enough to have acquired the status of received wisdom - which means it gets repeated without being examined. The idea sounds simple: talk to enough bankers and eventually someone will help you get a job. The reality is more instructive, and more demanding, than that summary suggests.

The number is not the point. The nature of the conversations is the point. Fifty genuinely productive conversations will beat two hundred transactional ones every time. A candidate who has had three memorable exchanges with people at a target firm is in a materially better position than one who has collected a hundred polite "good luck" responses from bankers who will not remember the conversation in two weeks.

But here is what the quality-only framing gets wrong: quality without volume does not work either. According to Mergers and Inquisitions, building a meaningful network from scratch requires 300 to 500 cold outreach emails and a commitment of five to ten hours per week over six months - a total investment of more than 100 hours before recruiting even begins.[^1] The candidates who break in are not the ones who sent better emails. They are the ones who sent better emails, in sufficient volume, over a sufficient period of time, and then followed up correctly.

This guide covers the full cycle: what the strategy actually requires, how to prepare for each conversation, how to structure it, how to follow up, and how to avoid the mistakes that cause most candidates to stall out.

---

## Why the Coffee Chat Strategy Works (When It Works)

Investment banking hiring - particularly at boutiques and in lateral contexts - is substantially relationship-mediated. Before any application moves through a formal process, someone has to believe you are worth the read. At large banks during on-cycle recruiting, that someone is a recruiter with a keyword filter. At mid-size firms, small boutiques, and in off-cycle situations, that someone is a person who has an opinion about you specifically.

The coffee chat strategy exploits this reality. Its purpose is not to transmit information about yourself - your resume does that. Its purpose is to become a specific person in the mind of someone who can advocate for you internally.

The mechanism is simple: humans hire people they know. When a group has a headcount to fill and the choice is between a pile of anonymous resumes and one resume attached to a person whom three current analysts have vouched for, the known quantity wins at the margin. The coffee chat strategy is how you become the known quantity before the pile exists.

Wall Street Oasis practitioners put it plainly: the primary goal of a first coffee chat is simply to be remembered positively by name.[^5] Not to demonstrate technical fluency, not to make a formal pitch for yourself, and not to extract a job lead. The goal is to be a specific person the banker would recognize if your resume crossed their desk.

## The Real Numbers: What Volume Actually Looks Like

Most candidates dramatically underestimate the volume required to run this strategy correctly. They send fifteen emails, get four responses, have three calls, and conclude the strategy is not working. The strategy is working - the inputs are just too small to produce outputs.

The functional targets, drawn from practitioner guidance, are:

- **300 to 500 cold outreach emails** to build a network from scratch, per Mergers and Inquisitions[^1]
- **30 to 50 total coffee chats** as the realistic conversion target, not 5 to 10[^3]
- **3 to 5 contacts per target firm** to build genuine advocates rather than a single fragile connection[^3]
- **6 to 12 months before recruiting** as the ideal start point; 3 to 6 months is the minimum viable window[^2]
- After 5 to 10 informational interviews, candidates typically begin receiving referrals or getting flagged to recruiting teams[^5]

Working through the conversion math makes the volume requirement concrete. If you want six genuine advocates spread across three target firms, you need roughly forty completed conversations. Getting forty conversations requires roughly sixty people to agree to speak with you. Getting sixty agreements requires reaching out to at least 200 people - and that assumes a reasonably good email, a reasonable target list, and some alumni overlap.

A reasonable expectation for conversion rates through the funnel: cold email to reply runs 15 to 30 percent (higher for alumni, lower for cold non-alumni contacts); reply to completed call runs 70 to 80 percent; completed call to a meaningful ongoing relationship runs 25 to 35 percent; meaningful relationship to referral or internal advocacy runs 30 to 50 percent.

These numbers are not discouraging - they are useful inputs for planning. They mean you need to start early, build the outreach as a continuous pipeline rather than a burst activity, and resist the temptation to throttle effort after the first round of responses.

## Targeting: Start Junior, Not Senior

One of the most consistent errors candidates make is targeting Managing Directors first. The logic seems reasonable - why not go straight to the decision-makers? The practice is counterproductive for several reasons.

MDs are the least accessible tier. Their time is the most constrained, their inboxes are the most crowded, and their gatekeeping is the most aggressive. They also have the least recent memory of what early recruiting actually looks like, which makes their advice less actionable.

Analysts and Associates are the correct starting targets.[^1] They remember being in your position recently. They have more flexibility to respond to cold outreach. They often have more influence over initial resume reviews than candidates assume - when a VP asks "do we know anyone who applied this year," it is usually an Analyst or Associate who says the name.

Mergers and Inquisitions recommends targeting junior-to-mid-level bankers first and working upward through warm introductions as relationships develop.[^1] An MD introduction obtained through an Associate who likes you is worth more than a cold MD email that goes unanswered.

## Before the Call: The Preparation That Actually Matters

Most candidates prepare for networking calls the way they prepare for a casual conversation - which is to say, they do not prepare at all. They think of a few questions and show up. This is the single biggest waste of the opportunity.

Preparation for a networking call should take thirty to forty-five minutes per person. The University of Virginia Economics Department's networking guide for finance candidates recommends having 10 to 15 questions ready and opened in a document before the call begins.[^4] The standard is specific: questions must be tailored to that person's career and expertise, and you should never ask anything answerable by a Google search.

Preparation should include:

**Research on the person:** LinkedIn profile covering school, career trajectory, and specific roles held. Any public content they have produced - conference panels, published commentary, firm announcements they are quoted in. Any deals their group has announced recently that they plausibly worked on.

**Research on the firm:** Two or three recent notable transactions. The group's positioning within the bank or the advisory landscape. Any publicly available commentary from senior leadership about firm direction or strategic focus.

**Research on the role:** What a first-year analyst in that specific group actually does day-to-day. What the deal flow typically looks like. What backgrounds the group tends to value.

This preparation serves multiple functions simultaneously. It allows you to ask questions that demonstrate genuine engagement rather than generic curiosity. It gives you material to reference in the conversation that signals you did the work. And - less obviously - when you are well-prepared, you are less anxious, and when you are less anxious, you come across as more confident and more natural. The preparation is, among other things, a confidence investment that shows up in how you sound on the call.

## The Cold Email: Five Sentences, No Proposed Times

Before any of this preparation matters, you need the conversation to happen. The cold email is where most candidates either win or lose the opportunity before it starts.

Mergers and Inquisitions is explicit on the structural requirements: the initial email must stay under five sentences, must not propose specific meeting times, and must conclude with a clear but soft ask.[^1] The brevity signals that you respect their time. Proposing specific times in the first email is presumptuous - wait for a positive response, then offer times.

Wall Street Oasis community guidance adds a timing dimension: send cold emails Tuesday through Thursday, 8 to 10 a.m., to maximize open rates.[^5] Follow up once after one week with a brief message that brings the email back to the top of their inbox. Mergers and Inquisitions recommends following up two to three times over three to four weeks before deprioritizing a contact.[^1]

One critical tactical note: use direct email, not LinkedIn messages. Bankers live in their email inboxes.[^2] LinkedIn DMs are treated as lower priority and lower stakes. Find the direct email address. The effort of finding it is itself a signal.

## The Conversation Structure That Converts

A productive networking call with a banker has a loose structure you should follow without making it feel scripted.

**Opening (2 to 3 minutes):** Thank them for their time and briefly establish the connection - mutual contact, shared school, or the specific thing that made you reach out to them in particular. Make it clear this is not a random cold call.

**Their background (5 to 7 minutes):** Ask about their path. How did they get into banking? What made them choose this firm over others at that point in their career? What was the transition from school to their first role actually like? People enjoy talking about their own trajectory, and this segment builds rapport while you gather genuine intelligence.

**The work and the firm (5 to 10 minutes):** Ask about what the group actually works on, what a typical week looks like, what they find most and least interesting about the work. This is where firm-specific research pays off - reference a specific transaction or initiative rather than asking a general question about what the group does.

**Your situation (3 to 5 minutes):** Briefly explain your background and what you are trying to accomplish. Keep this short and specific. Not a life story - something like: "I have spent two summers doing credit analysis at [firm]. I am trying to move into advisory work, and specifically into restructuring because of the complexity of the stakeholder dynamics. I am curious whether my background would be relevant to what your group looks for."

**Ask for their honest assessment (2 to 3 minutes):** "Given what I just described, do you think my background is a reasonable fit for what you look for, or are there gaps I should be working on?" This is the most valuable question most candidates never ask. Honest bankers will tell you something genuinely useful. Even a diplomatic non-answer is informative in how it is framed.

**The close (1 to 2 minutes):** Thank them. Ask if there is anyone else on their team or in their network who they think it would be worth speaking with. This is not aggressive - it is the natural end of a useful conversation.

## The 80/20 Rule: Let Them Talk

The IB Interview Questions coffee chat etiquette guide articulates a principle that cuts against most candidates' instincts: the banker should talk 80 percent of the time, the candidate 20 percent.[^3] Candidates who dominate the conversation or fire off banking questions for thirty minutes straight consistently underperform those who ask one good question and listen with genuine attention.

This matters for a reason that is easy to miss: bankers are evaluating you throughout the call, even though it is framed as informal. The IB Interview Questions guide is explicit - it is an informal screen for communication skills, professionalism, and client-facing potential.[^3] The casual framing is partially a fiction. Candidates who treat it as a relaxed chat and not a screen often damage themselves without realizing it.

The practical implication: design your questions to get the banker talking, not to demonstrate your own knowledge. Questions that produce long answers from them are better questions than questions that produce short answers followed by the ball back in your court.

## The Questions That Get You Remembered

Generic questions produce forgettable conversations. Specific questions create memorable ones.

**Questions to avoid:**
- "What advice do you have for someone trying to break into banking?"
- "What is the culture like at your firm?"
- "What is the most important skill for success in your group?"

These signal that you have not done enough preparation to ask anything better. Every banker who has taken networking calls has heard all of them hundreds of times.

**Better questions:**
- "You moved from [previous firm] to [current firm] - what was the specific thing that made that transition make sense for you at that point in your career?"
- "I read about your group's involvement in the [specific transaction] - what was the most complicated part of that process that did not make it into the press coverage?"
- "Your group seems to be more active on the [specific deal type] side than your direct competitors. Is that a deliberate positioning, or more a reflection of the deal flow you have been seeing?"

These questions accomplish something structurally important: they demonstrate that you already know things and are trying to know more. That is the posture of someone who might be worth hiring. It is the opposite of the posture of someone who showed up looking for a tutorial.

The University of Virginia guide recommends cycling through question categories in a deliberate sequence: their experience first, then firm specifics, then lifestyle and culture only after rapport is established, and recruiting details last.[^4] Questions about hours, compensation, or work-life balance belong at the end of the relationship arc, not the beginning.

## The Follow-Up Architecture

Most candidates send a thank-you email. A smaller number send a substantive follow-up. A very small number turn the follow-up into a genuine ongoing exchange that eventually produces a referral or an internal flag to recruiting.

**The initial thank-you (within 24 hours):** The 24-hour standard is non-negotiable according to every practitioner source.[^3] But the format matters as much as the timing. Reference one specific thing from the conversation that was genuinely useful. Not "thank you for your time and insights" - something concrete: "Your point about how restructuring mandates often hinge on the relationship with the company's existing lenders rather than the debtor itself was something I had not thought about before. I spent time reading through recent Chapter 11 cases after our call and can see exactly what you mean." This proves you were paying attention and demonstrates intellectual engagement beyond the call itself.

**The second contact (3 to 4 weeks later):** Send something useful. An article about a development in their sector, a question that genuinely arose from the first conversation, an update on something you mentioned. The point is to re-establish contact without asking for anything. The IB Interview Questions guide recommends a follow-up cadence of every 2 to 3 months, with messages kept to 4 to 6 sentences.[^3]

**The third contact (the ask):** This is where you can ask for a resume referral, a flag to the recruiting team, or an introduction to someone specific at the firm. By the time you make this ask, you have had three substantive exchanges and formed a real relationship. The ask does not come out of nowhere, which is what makes it likely to succeed.

The Mergers and Inquisitions networking guide describes this full cycle as a 3 to 6 month process before recruiting - find bankers, verify contact information, request meetings with 2 to 3 follow-up attempts, conduct conversations with heavy listening, make a "mini-ask" for a resume pass or referral, then follow up before making the direct ask during recruiting season.[^2]

## The Most Common Failure Modes

The mistakes that cause candidates to stall out are well-documented across practitioner sources and tend to cluster around the same errors:

**Asking for the job or referral on the first call.** This destroys rapport before it is built. The direct ask must come later in the relationship cycle, after multiple contacts have established that you are worth advocating for.[^3][^5]

**Targeting Managing Directors first.** They are the least accessible and least likely to respond. Start with Analysts and Associates who remember recruiting recently and work upward through warm introductions.[^1]

**Sending long cold emails.** Anything over five to seven sentences drops response rates sharply. Brevity signals that you respect their time.[^1]

**Using LinkedIn messages instead of direct email.** LinkedIn DMs are treated as lower priority. Find the direct email address.[^2]

**Asking googleable questions.** Asking about firm size, office locations, or published deal counts signals zero preparation. Ask only what requires their personal experience to answer.[^4]

**Dominating the conversation.** Candidates who talk more than 20 percent of the time fail the informal screen in a way they usually cannot detect because the banker will still be polite.[^3]

**Inconsistent follow-up.** Sending one good cold email and then disappearing is one of the most common failure modes. The candidates who win internships maintain contact at 2 to 3 month intervals with brief, relevant updates until recruiting begins.[^3]

**One-firm networking.** Candidates who network exclusively at their top-choice firm have no fallback and no leverage. Spread effort across a minimum of 3 to 5 target firms.[^3]

## Virtual Coffee Chats: What Changes

The pandemic shifted most networking conversations to phone or video, and the format has largely remained there. A few things are different in a virtual context.

Have your camera on by default. In a video call, having your camera off while the other person has theirs on creates an odd social asymmetry. Find a neutral background.

The social warm-up is shorter than in-person. In-person coffee has a natural five-minute warm-up built into the logistics of getting coffee, finding a table, and settling in. Video calls start abruptly. Have your opening sentence ready before the call connects.

Technical issues happen. Have a phone number as a backup. If the video call drops, switch to audio immediately and do not make a production of it.

The follow-up email is more important after virtual calls than after in-person ones. In-person conversations often end with a business card or a physical artifact that serves as a memory anchor. Video calls end with nothing. Send the thank-you quickly, because there is no physical reminder of the exchange sitting on their desk.

## The Coffee Chat as Interview Preparation

One underrated function of this strategy: networking calls are the best possible preparation for the "why banking" and "why this firm" questions you will face in formal interviews. If you have had thirty conversations with bankers and heard thirty different authentic answers to the question of what the work actually involves day-to-day, you will have a much richer and more credible answer when an interviewer asks why you want to do it.

Credibility is the gap between "could probably do this job" and "hire this person." The conversations that make you more knowledgeable make you more credible, and credibility is what closes that gap at the margin.

## What to Do Next

**Pick five people for your first wave of outreach this week.** Not fifty - five. Write each email specifically rather than from a template. Measure how many replies you get. Adjust the structure based on what worked and what did not.

**Build a simple tracking document.** Columns: Name, Firm, Group, Date Contacted, Date of Call, Follow-Up Sent, Status. This converts networking from an amorphous activity into a managed pipeline with visible gaps.

**Prepare your thirty-second personal pitch before any call.** This is the answer to "tell me about yourself" in a networking context. It should be clear, specific, and conclude with why you are reaching out to this particular person at this particular time.

**Review your [LinkedIn profile](/job-search/linkedin-optimization-ib/) before you start outreach.** The first thing every person you contact will do is look at your LinkedIn. Make sure it reflects what you want it to say.

**Set a target of one new conversation per week minimum.** Consistency matters more than bursts. One conversation per week over six months is twenty-five conversations - enough to build a meaningful network if each one is followed up correctly and the relationships are maintained through recruiting season.

The hundred cups of coffee is not a metaphor for volume. It is a metaphor for sustained, consistent effort over a long enough period that the relationships you build have time to develop into something that actually moves the needle when it counts.
