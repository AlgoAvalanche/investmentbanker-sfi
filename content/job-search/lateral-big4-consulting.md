# Lateral From Big 4 / Corporate Banking / Consulting: Strategy by Path

The lateral move into investment banking from adjacent finance or professional services roles is one of the more nuanced recruiting paths, and the guidance tends to be either too generic ("network a lot and sell your transferable skills") or too specific to one particular background to be widely useful.

The reality is that Big 4 advisory, consulting, and corporate banking are meaningfully different starting points with different obstacles, different credibility profiles, and different strategic approaches. What works for a PwC transaction services analyst trying to move to an M&A advisory role is not the same as what works for a relationship manager at Wells Fargo trying to break into leveraged finance. Conflating them produces generic advice that helps no one.

This article addresses each path separately.

---

## The Core Challenge in Any Lateral Move

Before getting into path-specific strategy, one principle applies to all lateral moves: investment banking is skeptical of laterals in proportion to how far the move is from direct deal experience.

A junior banker moving from one advisory firm to another is not a lateral in any meaningful sense - it is a straightforward hire with clear evidence of relevant capability. A transaction services analyst at a Big 4 firm who has spent two years on buy-side due diligence is a semi-lateral with good credentials. A management consultant moving from strategy work into M&A execution is a real lateral with a genuine credibility gap to close.

The question every hiring banker asks when reviewing a lateral application is: "Has this person done work that is similar enough to what we do that training costs will be low and ramp time will be short?" Your positioning has to answer that question convincingly before it is asked.

---

## Path 1: Big 4 Transaction Services / Advisory

Big 4 transaction advisory services (TAS) is the clearest adjacent path into investment banking. The work involves financial due diligence, quality of earnings analysis, and deal-specific financial modeling on live M&A transactions. The credibility gap with IB is real but narrower than most other paths.

**What transfers:**
- Financial analysis and modeling on real transactions
- Understanding of deal processes and timelines
- Exposure to buyer and seller dynamics from the diligence side
- Technical accounting knowledge that many pure IB backgrounds lack

**What does not transfer:**
- Deal origination and client relationship experience
- Valuation as a primary product (TAS produces financial analysis; IB produces valuation opinions and advisory services)
- The pressure and pace of an advisory transaction timeline versus a due diligence engagement

**The positioning narrative**: The strongest Big 4 to IB pitch is: "I have spent [X] years doing deep financial analysis on real transactions. I understand what happens in a deal room. I want to be on the advisory side rather than the diligence side." This is coherent and credible.

**Where this move works best**: Middle-market M&A advisory boutiques, restructuring groups that value deep financial analysis, and leveraged finance teams that need credit modeling depth. Less natural at pure origination-focused groups where the pitch requires industry relationship-building.

**The GPA and school question for laterals**: For Big 4-to-IB moves at analyst or associate levels, your undergraduate GPA matters less than your current work product. Bring detailed examples of models you have built, deals you have worked on, and insights you derived from the analysis. Make the conversation about current capability rather than pre-professional credentials.

## Path 2: Big 4 Audit

Audit is the most common Big 4 background and the one with the largest credibility gap for IB. The work is important but it is not deal work. Audit procedures, controls testing, and financial statement review are not the same cognitive muscles as transaction modeling and advisory.

This does not mean it is impossible. It means you need to be honest about the gap and have a clear plan to close it.

**The honest gap assessment**: An audit background demonstrates financial statement fluency and process discipline. It does not demonstrate valuation, deal process, or client advisory experience. Banks looking at an audit background want to see evidence that you have been building toward IB, not that you fell into it by default.

**What helps**: Any exposure to M&A-adjacent work within audit (IPO audits, purchase price allocation work, impairment analysis), completion of BIWS or other technical finance training with demonstrable output, and a clear story about why banking specifically rather than a generic "I want to do deals."

**The realistic target set**: Big 4 audit to bulge bracket IB is a stretch. Big 4 audit to middle-market or lower-middle-market boutique, particularly in sectors where your audit experience gives you genuine industry knowledge (healthcare audit background applying to healthcare banking, for instance), is much more realistic.

## Path 3: Management Consulting

Consulting to IB is the most discussed lateral path, partly because both attract a similar talent profile from business schools and target universities, and partly because the exit opportunities from consulting frequently include banking and vice versa.

The credibility gap is real but the soft skills transfer well. Consultants are strong communicators, comfortable with ambiguous problems, and experienced with client presentations. They are typically weaker on pure financial modeling, transaction execution mechanics, and the specific analytical rigor of valuation work.

**What transfers well:**
- Structured problem decomposition
- Client-facing communication
- Presentation and deck quality
- Industry knowledge if you were in a specialized vertical practice

**What does not transfer:**
- Financial modeling depth (three-statement, LBO, DCF)
- Deal execution experience
- Understanding of capital structure and financing mechanics

**The positioning narrative**: The best consulting-to-IB narrative is sector-specific: "I have spent three years advising [industry] companies on strategy. I want to now work on the transaction side of the same decisions I have been analyzing." This is more credible than a generic "I want to do deals" pitch, because it positions you as bringing something (sector expertise) that offsets what you lack (modeling depth).

**What you need to do before applying**: Get your technical finance skills to a demonstrable level before you start the lateral move. Wall Street Oasis has many accounts from successful consulting-to-IB movers, and the common thread is that they spent three to six months doing intensive financial modeling training (BIWS, Macabacus self-study, financial modeling courses) before applying. Showing up to a lateral interview without LBO modeling competency is a clean fail.

## Path 4: Corporate Banking

Corporate banking - relationship-driven credit and lending at commercial banks and large financial institutions - is a path that has genuine finance and deal exposure but a different product focus than IB advisory.

Corporate bankers understand credit structures, lending covenants, and relationship dynamics with corporate clients. They typically have less exposure to M&A processes, valuation, and the advisory side of capital markets.

**What transfers:**
- Credit and financial analysis on real company situations
- Client relationship experience
- Understanding of debt capital structures
- Deal familiarity if you have been involved in syndications or leveraged lending

**The natural landing zone**: Corporate banking to leveraged finance or debt capital markets at an IB is the most natural move. The credit knowledge transfers directly. Corporate banking to M&A advisory is a longer stretch that requires you to build the valuation and deal execution credential that pure credit work does not provide.

**Where this move is underrated**: Restructuring practices at IB firms value credit analysts because restructuring involves understanding debt structures, intercreditor dynamics, and lender negotiations. A corporate banker with exposure to distressed situations or problem credits has a genuinely useful background here.

---

## Path 5: Corporate Development

Corporate development - the internal M&A function at operating companies - is sometimes underrated as a lateral-into-IB path because it reads as a post-IB destination. But candidates coming from corp dev with genuine deal execution experience (not just process management or integration work) have a credible advisory background.

The challenge: corp dev work is often less time-pressured and less technically intensive than IB deal work. Bankers sometimes view corp dev as the slower-paced relative. Your positioning needs to counter this by emphasizing the deal complexity and execution work you did, not the company-specific context.

## The Resume and Application Strategy for All Laterals

For any lateral move, the resume requires a different framing than an entry-level application. See the [investment banking resume guide](/job-search/investment-banking-resume/) for formatting, but the content logic is different:

**Lead with relevance, not chronology.** The most relevant experience should be visible first. If your current role includes deal work, lead with that even if it is not your most senior position.

**Quantify the deal involvement.** Not just job titles and responsibilities - specific deal sizes, transaction types, number of deals worked on, and what your specific role was in each. "Supported two live M&A processes" is weaker than "Built diligence model for $340M healthcare services acquisition and contributed to the quality of earnings working paper."

**Address the gap directly.** In lateral applications, unexplained gaps raise more questions than explained ones. If your current role is not perfect preparation, your resume positioning (and especially your cover letter) should acknowledge what you are adding and what you will need to develop.

## Networking Strategy for Laterals

Lateral hiring is more network-mediated than analyst recruiting. Banks are less likely to find laterals through formal OCR processes and more likely to hire through referrals, headhunters, or direct applications backed by an internal champion.

This means the [networking and coffee chat strategy](/job-search/100-cups-of-coffee-strategy/) is not optional for laterals - it is the primary mechanism. Your target list should include:

- Alumni from your university who are now at banks you are targeting
- Former colleagues who have made similar moves
- Headhunters who specialize in lateral IB placement (search for recruiting firms that focus on junior-to-mid-level banking placements)

Headhunters deserve a specific note: for lateral moves above the analyst level, working with a banking-specialized headhunter is often the most efficient path. They have active mandates from firms looking for specific backgrounds, and they can represent your profile to firms you might not have access to through direct application. Registering with two or three headhunters who specialize in this space costs nothing except time.

## The MBA Question

For laterals who are more than two or three years out from undergraduate and feel the gap is too wide to bridge through direct application, an MBA is sometimes the reset mechanism. Top MBA programs send substantial analyst-level and associate-level classes into banking each year, and the MBA provides the credential, the recruiting infrastructure, and the networking access that the current background does not.

This is not the right answer for everyone. An MBA to bridge into banking only makes sense if: (1) the opportunity cost is worth it given your current compensation, (2) you are targeting firms where the MBA recruiting pipeline is actually strong, and (3) you have a clear plan for how the MBA years are spent to maximize your positioning. More on this in the [MBA recruiting guide](/job-search/mba-recruiting-ib/).

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Identify which of the five paths above describes your current background most accurately**, then focus exclusively on the strategy relevant to your path. Generic lateral advice will waste your time.

2. **Do an honest technical skills audit.** Can you build a three-statement model from scratch? Can you walk through an LBO? If not, fix this before you submit a single application. Free modeling resources from BIWS and Macabacus exist; use them.

3. **Update your resume to lead with relevant deal and analytical work.** Review the [resume guide](/job-search/investment-banking-resume/) and reframe your experience around the work that is most relevant to the advisory role you are targeting. Do not lead with titles and management scope.

4. **Identify three to five target firms where your background is a credible fit.** Do not apply to Goldman M&A as your first lateral application if you have an audit background. Understand the realistic landing zone for your specific background and target those firms first to build momentum and an offer anchor.

5. **Read the [lateral move interview guide](/interview-prep/lateral-move-interviews/)** before you start networking. The questions in lateral interviews are different from analyst recruiting questions - they are more focused on why you are leaving, why now, and what specifically you will add - and you need to have clear, confident answers before any conversation starts.
