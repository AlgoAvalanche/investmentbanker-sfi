# Cover Letters That Don't Sound Like ChatGPT

There is a version of this article that tells you cover letters do not matter in investment banking. That version is wrong in a specific and important way.

Cover letters at bulge brackets during on-cycle recruiting often do not get read - the volume is simply too high. That much is true. But at boutiques, at elite advisory firms, during lateral hiring, during off-cycle applications, and at any firm where a human being is making the first-pass decision rather than a keyword screen - cover letters matter significantly. And at the firms where they do matter, a bad cover letter will end your application faster than almost any other single document.

The other thing that has happened in the last two years: AI-generated cover letters have become so ubiquitous and so uniformly terrible that any letter that sounds like a human wrote it now stands out. This is genuinely good news if you are willing to write.

---

## Why Most Investment Banking Cover Letters Are Unreadable

Open ten randomly selected cover letters from a banking application pool and you will find the same letter ten times with the names changed. The structure is: "I am writing to express my interest in the [role] at [firm]. I have always been passionate about finance. I believe my skills in [generic list] make me an excellent fit. I look forward to contributing to [firm's] continued success."

This letter communicates nothing. It does not tell the recruiter why this firm, why this group, or why this candidate. It is the verbal equivalent of a shrug.

The ChatGPT version is even worse because it adds elaborate vocabulary to the emptiness. "I am deeply enthused by the opportunity to leverage my multifaceted skill set within the dynamic landscape of investment banking" is a sentence that means absolutely nothing and now appears in approximately 40% of all banking applications.

Senior bankers who review applications - and many do, particularly at smaller firms - have said openly in forums like Mergers & Inquisitions Q&A sessions that they can identify an AI-generated letter in three sentences. The tell is not any single phrase. It is the absence of anything specific. Real humans have specific reasons. AI generates plausible-sounding generalities.

## The Structure That Works

A cover letter for banking applications should be three to four paragraphs, one page, and it should answer three questions:

1. Why this firm specifically?
2. Why are you qualified?
3. Why banking, and why now?

That is the entire framework. Everything else is execution.

**Paragraph 1 - The specific hook**: One or two sentences explaining why this firm, not banking in general. This requires actual research. "I am applying to Lazard's Restructuring group because of your advisory role on the [specific transaction] and your reputation for working on operationally complex situations rather than purely financial restructurings" is a real reason. "I am drawn to Lazard's storied history and commitment to excellence" is not.

**Paragraph 2 - The qualification summary**: Two to three sentences mapping your background to what the role requires. This is not a repeat of your resume. It is the narrative thread connecting your experiences. "My two years at [firm] gave me a foundation in leveraged credit analysis, and my work on three live Chapter 11 processes has given me a perspective on distressed operations that most analyst candidates do not have at this stage."

**Paragraph 3 - The why banking / why now**: This is where you connect your trajectory to the role. It should feel inevitable, not aspirational. "I have been building toward this transition since [specific moment]" is more credible than "I have always been passionate about finance."

**Closing paragraph**: Brief, direct, no hollow enthusiasm. "I would welcome the opportunity to discuss how my background aligns with what your team is building. Thank you for your time." That is sufficient.

## The Specific Firm Research Problem

The most common failure mode in banking cover letters is claiming firm-specific interest without demonstrating any firm-specific knowledge. Recruiters see through this instantly because they know their own firm.

Genuine firm research for a cover letter means knowing:

- Two or three recent notable transactions the firm advised on (not just "your work in M&A")
- The group's positioning or specialty within the broader market
- Something about the culture or approach that distinguishes it from competitors - and this has to come from a real source, not the firm's own marketing copy

Sources for genuine firm research: news coverage of recent deals, LinkedIn posts from current analysts and associates, conversations with people at the firm (see [networking guide for non-targets](/job-search/how-to-network-non-target/)), the firm's published tombstones and case studies.

One practical approach: set up a Google Alert for every firm you are targeting three months before applications open. By the time you are writing, you will have accumulated enough real material to write something genuine.

## Tone and Voice: What "Not Sounding Like ChatGPT" Actually Means

The banking industry is not known for valuing literary flair, and that is not what this section is recommending. The goal is not creativity. It is specificity and authenticity.

A few concrete elements that separate human-written from AI-generated:

**Sentence length variation**: AI tends toward uniform medium-length sentences. Humans write a short sentence for emphasis. Then a longer one that qualifies it. Then something brief again. This rhythm is almost impossible to fake.

**Specific proper nouns**: Real transactions, real deal names, real firm names within your experience. "The Sycamore Partners acquisition of Staples's B2B operations" is specific. "A major private equity transaction" is not.

**Admitting a gap and addressing it**: AI almost never acknowledges a weakness because it is optimizing for positive framing. A human can write: "My GPA is not at the level that Goldman typically screens for, but the three summers of deal experience I have built since then reflect something that a GPA cannot." This kind of self-awareness reads as genuine and often lands well with senior bankers.

**No filler transitions**: "Furthermore," "Additionally," "In conclusion," "It is worth noting that" - these are AI tells. Cut every one of them.

## The "Why Investment Banking" Question in Cover Letters

This is covered in more depth in the [Why Investment Banking interview guide](/interview-prep/why-investment-banking/), but the cover letter version of this answer needs to be concise and specific.

The honest answer to "why banking" for most candidates involves some combination of: intellectual challenge of complex transactions, exposure to multiple industries in compressed time, building a financial toolkit early in a career, and yes, compensation. None of these are disqualifying. What is disqualifying is the version that sounds like a mission statement: "I want to create value for clients and society while developing as a professional."

A WSO forum thread from a few years ago had an honest post from an analyst who said his cover letter "why banking" was two sentences: "I want to understand how large companies make the most consequential decisions of their existence. I want to be in the room when that happens." That is specific, credible, and notably absent of any language about passion.

Whatever your genuine reason is, write that. The filters for cover letters are sophisticated enough at this point that authenticity outperforms optimization.

## Length, Formatting, and Mechanical Details

**Length**: Three to four paragraphs. Approximately 300-400 words. Never more than one page. A letter that runs to 600 words is almost certainly padding.

**Font and format**: Match your resume. Same font, same margins, same header style. The two documents should look like they came from the same person on the same day, because they did.

**Salutation**: "Dear [Name]," if you know who is reading it, which you should if you have done any research. "Dear Recruiting Team," if you genuinely cannot find a name. "To Whom It May Concern" is dated and lazy.

**Closing**: "Sincerely," or "Best regards," - pick one and do not overthink it. "Warm regards" is fine. "Yours truly" is 1987.

**File name**: FirstName_LastName_CoverLetter_FirmName.pdf - not "coverletter_final_v3_FINAL.pdf"

## What to Cut Ruthlessly

Some specific phrases to eliminate from any banking cover letter:

- "I have always been passionate about finance" - Everyone says this and it means nothing.
- "I am a hard worker" - This is table stakes, not a differentiator.
- "I would be an asset to your team" - Let the reader conclude this.
- "I look forward to the opportunity to discuss my qualifications" - Too passive. State directly that you would like to speak.
- Any reference to "the fast-paced world of investment banking" - This phrase should be retired permanently.
- "I am writing to express my interest" - Start with the hook, not a meta-statement about the letter you are writing.
- "Happy to jump on a call" - Informal in a way that does not serve you.

## The Referral Cover Letter

If you have a contact at the firm - through [networking](/job-search/how-to-network-non-target/) or a mutual connection - the cover letter changes. Lead with the referral in sentence one: "Following my conversation with [Name], an associate in your [Group] team, I am applying for the analyst position."

This does three things: it establishes that you have done the work to build a relationship, it gives the reader a person to verify with, and it signals that you are serious enough about this firm to have invested time before the application.

The rest of the letter structure remains the same. The referral does not replace substance - it opens the door to the substance being read.

---

## The Cover Letter Nobody Talks About: The Follow-Up Note

After a superday or a final round interview, a short follow-up note is appropriate and underused. It is not a second cover letter. It is two paragraphs: one that references something specific from the interview (a discussion topic, a deal they mentioned), and one that reaffirms your interest and thanks them for their time.

This note works because almost nobody sends it, and the ones who do are remembered. At the margin - and hiring decisions are often made at the margin - this matters.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Write your first draft without opening ChatGPT or any other AI tool.** The point is to hear your own voice first. You can edit later, but you cannot add authenticity to a document that was never authentic to begin with.

2. **Research each firm before writing a single letter for them.** Find two real transactions, one thing about the culture, and the name of at least one person in the group you are targeting. This research is the raw material.

3. **Pair your cover letter with a strong resume.** Review the [investment banking resume guide](/job-search/investment-banking-resume/) to make sure both documents are consistent and formatted correctly before you submit anything.

4. **Have someone who works in finance read your draft.** Not for grammar - for whether it sounds like someone who knows what banking actually is. A family friend who is a CFO will do. An analyst at any bank will do. Their instinctive reaction to the letter is the signal you are looking for.

5. **Prepare a verbal version of the same answer.** Your cover letter "why this firm" answer will be asked in the first five minutes of every interview. Know it cold and make sure it is consistent with what you wrote.
