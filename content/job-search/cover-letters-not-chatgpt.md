# Cover Letters That Don't Sound Like ChatGPT

There is a version of this article that tells you cover letters do not matter in investment banking. That version is wrong in a specific and important way.

Cover letters at bulge brackets during on-cycle recruiting often do not get read - the volume is simply too high. That much is true. Bankers themselves acknowledge this openly: at bulge bracket banks, many recruiters on screening teams report never having read a single applicant cover letter during peak recruiting season. But at boutiques, at elite advisory firms, during lateral hiring, during off-cycle applications, and at any firm where a human being is making the first-pass decision rather than a keyword screen - cover letters matter significantly. And at the firms where they do matter, a bad cover letter will end your application faster than almost any other single document.

The other thing that has happened in the last two years: AI-generated cover letters have become so ubiquitous and so uniformly terrible that any letter that sounds like a human wrote it now stands out. This is genuinely good news if you are willing to write.

The numbers bear this out. According to research aggregated across multiple hiring studies, approximately 74% of hiring professionals say they can detect AI usage in applications. Around 80% of hiring managers are averse to AI-written cover letters. And 39% of recruiters - roughly two in five - consider a blatantly AI-authored cover letter a disqualifying factor that eliminates the candidate outright. Meanwhile, roughly 46% of job seekers have already used AI tools to write or assist with cover letters or resumes. That gap between how many candidates are using AI and how many recruiters can spot it is the opportunity. Use it.

## Why Most Investment Banking Cover Letters Are Unreadable

Open ten randomly selected cover letters from a banking application pool and you will find the same letter ten times with the names changed. The structure is: "I am writing to express my interest in the [role] at [firm]. I have always been passionate about finance. I believe my skills in [generic list] make me an excellent fit. I look forward to contributing to [firm's] continued success."

This letter communicates nothing. It does not tell the recruiter why this firm, why this group, or why this candidate. It is the verbal equivalent of a shrug.

The ChatGPT version is even worse because it adds elaborate vocabulary to the emptiness. "I am deeply enthused by the opportunity to leverage my multifaceted skill set within the dynamic landscape of investment banking" is a sentence that means absolutely nothing and now appears in a meaningful share of all banking applications.

Talent acquisition professionals who review high volumes of applications have been specific about how they detect AI-generated letters. One talent acquisition specialist quoted in HR editorial research described the pattern this way: "When someone has really technical words in there, really broad statements, really general statements, that's typically how I can tell that it's not written by them." The tell is not any single phrase. It is the combination of technical jargon with broad statements - the AI signature of sounding knowledgeable while saying nothing concrete.

A second detection signal is word-for-word job description mirroring. Recruiters wrote the job description. They know every phrase in it. When those exact phrases appear verbatim in a cover letter, they recognize it immediately. AI, trained to optimize relevance, defaults to reflecting the input it was given. A human who actually researched the role would use their own language to describe the same requirements.

A third signal: voice mismatch. An AI-generated letter often sounds like a McKinsey memo. When the same candidate walks into an interview and speaks the way actual humans speak, the disconnect is immediate. Recruiters notice when the document and the person do not match.

Senior bankers who review applications - and many do, particularly at smaller firms - have said openly that they can identify an AI-generated letter in three sentences. Real humans have specific reasons. AI generates plausible-sounding generalities. The difference is apparent instantly to anyone who reads applications regularly.

## The Strategic Reality: Filter, Not Differentiator

Before getting into structure, it is worth understanding what a cover letter actually does in an investment banking application. The framing that experienced practitioners use consistently is this: a strong cover letter will not get you an interview on its own, but a poor one can eliminate you. It is a filter, not a differentiator.

This framing matters for how you allocate your effort. At a bulge bracket, the cover letter is a hygiene item - get it right, make sure the bank name is correct, avoid anything embarrassing, and then invest your real energy in the resume and networking. At a boutique with a hands-on hiring process, the letter carries real weight and deserves proportional effort.

The cases where cover letters genuinely change outcomes: boutique firms where partners read every application, candidates with non-traditional or non-target backgrounds who need context beyond what a resume provides, international or cross-border applications (European markets in particular rely on cover letters more heavily than US markets), and any candidate with a resume gap that requires explanation. If you fall into any of these categories, your cover letter is not a formality. It is a substantive part of your application.

One additional practical point from practitioners: when a cover letter field is optional, always submit one anyway. It takes two minutes once you have a working template, and leaving it blank signals low effort to any firm that reads them. You do not know at the point of application which firms read them and which do not.

## The Structure That Works

A cover letter for banking applications should be three to four paragraphs, one page, and answer three questions:

1. Why this firm specifically?
2. Why are you qualified?
3. Why banking, and why now?

That is the entire framework. Everything else is execution.

**Paragraph 1 - The introduction and specific hook**: Your name, school or current role, how you found the position, and - critically - a specific reason why this firm. This is not the place for generic praise. "I am applying to Lazard's Restructuring group because of your advisory role on the [specific transaction] and your reputation for working on operationally complex situations rather than purely financial restructurings" is a real reason. "I am drawn to Lazard's storied history and commitment to excellence" is not. One note on format from practitioners who have reviewed thousands of letters: reference the bank name only twice - in the greeting and the first paragraph. Candidates applying to multiple firms have ended careers by leaving the wrong bank name in the body of a letter.

**Paragraph 2 - The qualification summary**: Two to three sentences mapping your background to what the role requires. The right format here is Action-Result: structure each experience as [action] + [project] + [outcome]. "Built a DCF model to value a logistics acquisition target and supported a $75M transaction" is a qualification. "I have strong valuation skills" is not. This paragraph is not a repeat of your resume - it is the narrative thread that connects your experiences to this specific role. Quantifiable outcomes are non-negotiable. AI defaults to describing roles without numbers. Real candidates have numbers.

**Paragraph 3 - Why banking, why now**: This is where you connect your trajectory to the role. It should feel inevitable, not aspirational. "I have been building toward this transition since [specific moment or experience]" is more credible than "I have always been passionate about finance." The goal is to make the reader feel that this application is the logical conclusion of a coherent path, not an optimistic reach.

**Closing paragraph**: Brief, direct, no hollow enthusiasm. "I would welcome the opportunity to discuss how my background aligns with what your team is building. Thank you for your time." That is sufficient. Do not add: "I am confident I would be a tremendous asset." Let the reader conclude that.

## The Specific Firm Research Problem

The most common failure mode in banking cover letters is claiming firm-specific interest without demonstrating any firm-specific knowledge. Recruiters see through this instantly because they know their own firm. The Leland career coaching platform, which works with practitioners across the industry, puts it plainly: "Many candidates use the same cover letter template for every firm and just swap out the name. Recruiters can tell."

Genuine firm research for a cover letter means knowing:

- Two or three recent notable transactions the firm advised on - not just "your work in M&A" but the actual deal names and what made them interesting
- The group's positioning or specialty within the broader market
- Something about the culture or approach that distinguishes it from competitors - and this has to come from a real source, not the firm's own marketing copy

"After reviewing your advisory work on the [Company A - Company B] merger..." beats any generic praise. Generic praise is the signature of a recycled template. Specific knowledge of a deal cannot be faked with a single prompt.

Sources for genuine firm research: news coverage of recent deals, LinkedIn posts from current analysts and associates, conversations with people at the firm (see [networking guide for non-targets](/job-search/how-to-network-non-target/)), and the firm's published tombstones and case studies.

One practical approach: set up a Google Alert for every firm you are targeting three months before applications open. By the time you are writing, you will have accumulated enough real material to write something genuine. This is not a trick - it is what people who are actually serious about a firm do.

## Tone and Voice: What "Not Sounding Like ChatGPT" Actually Means

The banking industry is not known for valuing literary flair, and that is not what this section is recommending. The goal is not creativity. It is specificity and authenticity - and in the IB context specifically, restraint.

IB culture values discretion. Candidates who are impassioned and emotionally demonstrative in cover letters raise a specific red flag: how will this person behave in front of clients? Brian DeChesare of Mergers & Inquisitions, who spent years as a banker before building the most widely read IB career resource, is direct about this: at large banks, an overly enthusiastic letter gets mocked and forwarded internally. The phrase he uses is stark - "your career will be destroyed." This is not hyperbole. The culture genuinely screens for judgment, and an overwrought cover letter is a judgment failure.

A few concrete elements that separate human-written from AI-generated:

**Sentence length variation**: AI tends toward uniform medium-length sentences. Humans write a short sentence for emphasis. Then a longer one that qualifies it. Then something brief again. This rhythm is almost impossible to fake and is one of the clearest signals of authentic writing.

**Specific proper nouns**: Real transactions, real deal names, real firm names within your experience. "The Sycamore Partners acquisition of Staples's B2B operations" is specific. "A major private equity transaction" is not. Specificity is the only real test of authenticity - generic praise is indistinguishable from AI, but specific knowledge cannot be faked with a template.

**Admitting a gap and addressing it**: AI almost never acknowledges a weakness because it optimizes for positive framing. A human can write: "My GPA is not at the level that Goldman typically screens for, but the three summers of deal experience I have built since then reflect something that a GPA cannot." This kind of self-awareness reads as genuine and often lands well with senior bankers who have seen thousands of letters that never acknowledge any imperfection.

**No filler transitions**: "Furthermore," "Additionally," "In conclusion," "It is worth noting that" - these are AI tells. Cut every one of them. They add length and subtract credibility.

**Humility as a differentiator**: The 10x EBITDA finance blog, which coaches candidates through the technical and qualitative aspects of IB applications, frames the goal clearly: "Stay humble throughout. The goal is not to sound impressive - it is to avoid sounding ridiculous." Do not describe yourself as an ideal fit. Do not use self-applied titles like "Managing Partner of our student investment club." Do not write "given my strong valuation skills" - practitioners flag this phrasing specifically as self-aggrandizing. Let your credentials make the case. Your job is to present them clearly, not to editorialize about them.

## The AI Detection Problem in Practical Terms

The five patterns that talent acquisition professionals have specifically identified as AI tells, drawn from research by Welcome to the Jungle and related hiring editorial sources:

1. **Technical jargon combined with broad statements** - Sounding knowledgeable while saying nothing. The jargon is there; the specifics are not.

2. **Lack of personalization** - Overly formal, buttoned-up prose that does not match how the candidate actually communicates. The voice in the letter and the voice in the interview are clearly different people.

3. **Suspicious skill claims** - AI may insert proficiencies the candidate never mentioned in their prompts. If the letter references Photoshop skills or a specific software platform that is not on the resume, the recruiter may ask about it. There is no answer ready.

4. **Word-for-word job description mirroring** - Recruiters know the JD by heart. Seeing their own phrases reflected back at them verbatim is an immediate flag.

5. **Vague closing lines** - "I can bring a fresh perspective" without specifying what that perspective is or where it comes from. Authentic candidates can explain their actual motivation because they have one.

On using AI legitimately: there is no ethical issue with using AI to overcome writer's block, organize structure, or check grammar and spelling. The ethical and practical problem is submitting AI-generated content without personal input, without tailoring to the specific role, and without significant rewriting. Research on AI cover letter methodology also shows that drafting paragraph-by-paragraph using AI produces more authentic results than asking for the entire letter in a single prompt - the iterative approach allows the candidate's actual voice and specific experiences to shape the output, rather than letting AI fill in blanks with generic content.

The correct use of AI in this context: generate a structural draft, then rewrite every paragraph with your own specific language, your own deal names, your own numbers. The goal is to use AI as a scaffold, not a finished product.

## The "Why Investment Banking" Question in Cover Letters

This is covered in more depth in the [Why Investment Banking interview guide](/interview-prep/why-investment-banking/), but the cover letter version needs to be concise and specific.

The honest answer to "why banking" for most candidates involves some combination of: the intellectual challenge of complex transactions, exposure to multiple industries in compressed time, building a financial toolkit early in a career, and yes, compensation. None of these are disqualifying. What is disqualifying is the version that sounds like a mission statement: "I want to create value for clients and society while developing as a professional."

Whatever your genuine reason is, write that. The most credible "why banking" answers are the ones that are specific about transaction type, sector, or skill. "I want to be in the room when large companies make the most consequential decisions of their existence" is specific and credible. "I am passionate about finance" is neither.

The filters for cover letters are sophisticated enough at this point that authenticity outperforms optimization. A recruiter who has read 400 letters in a week can identify genuine motivation in two sentences - and can identify manufactured motivation just as quickly.

## Length, Formatting, and Mechanical Details

**Length**: Three to four paragraphs. The target from practitioners is 250-300 words - under 300 signals respect for the reader's time, and achieving it requires discipline that AI, which tends to pad, does not naturally apply. Never more than one page. A letter that runs to 600 words is almost certainly padding. Any cover letter over one page is unread.

**Font and format**: Match your resume exactly. Same font (Times New Roman, Arial, or Calibri at 10-12pt are the standard options), same margins (0.75 to 1 inch), same header style. The two documents should look like they came from the same person on the same day.

**Salutation**: "Dear [Name]," if you know who is reading it - and you should, if you have done any research. "Dear Recruiting Team," if you genuinely cannot find a name. "To Whom It May Concern" is dated.

**On physical addresses**: Do not include the bank's physical address. For multi-office firms, this creates confusion about which office you are applying to and flags you as someone working from a recycled template.

**Closing**: "Sincerely," or "Best regards," - pick one. Do not overthink it.

**File name**: FirstName_LastName_CoverLetter_FirmName.pdf - not "coverletter_final_v3_FINAL.pdf."

**Submission format**: PDF unless explicitly instructed otherwise.

## What to Cut Ruthlessly

Specific phrases to eliminate from any banking cover letter:

- "I have always been passionate about finance" - Everyone writes this. It means nothing.
- "I am a hard worker" - Table stakes, not a differentiator.
- "I would be an asset to your team" - Let the reader conclude this.
- "I look forward to the opportunity to discuss my qualifications" - State directly that you would like to speak.
- "The fast-paced world of investment banking" - Retire this permanently.
- "I am writing to express my interest" - Start with the hook, not a meta-statement about the letter.
- "Happy to jump on a call" - Too informal and it does not serve you.
- "Hardworking, analytical, and passionate" - Every candidate writes this. No candidate provides proof.
- "Given my strong valuation skills..." - Flagged specifically by practitioners as self-aggrandizing.
- Any skill claim the resume does not support - AI invents proficiencies. If it is not on the resume, it does not belong in the letter.
- Generic praise ("I have long admired your firm's commitment to excellence") - Zero information, immediate filter-out signal.

## The Referral Cover Letter

If you have a contact at the firm - through [networking](/job-search/how-to-network-non-target/) or a mutual connection - the cover letter changes. Lead with the referral in sentence one: "Following my conversation with [Name], an associate in your [Group] team, I am applying for the analyst position."

This does three things: it establishes that you have done the work to build a relationship, it gives the reader a person to verify with, and it signals that you are serious enough about this firm to have invested time before the application opened.

One note from practitioners: do not force a name-drop if the relationship is thin. Mentioning a banker contact in the body of the letter when the connection was a two-minute coffee chat reads as unnatural and can work against you. The referral mention works when the relationship is real. If you spoke with someone meaningfully - in an informational, at a recruiting event, through a warm introduction - it belongs in sentence one. If you exchanged two emails, leave it out.

The rest of the letter structure remains the same. The referral opens the door to the substance being read. It does not replace the substance.

## The Follow-Up Note

After a superday or final round interview, a short follow-up note is appropriate and substantially underused. It is not a second cover letter. It is two paragraphs: one that references something specific from the interview - a deal they mentioned, a discussion you had about market conditions, a question that generated a real exchange - and one that reaffirms your interest and thanks them for their time.

This note works for a simple reason: almost nobody sends it. At the margin - and hiring decisions are often made at the margin - being the candidate who sent a thoughtful, specific note is a differentiator. The note demonstrates the same skill the cover letter is meant to demonstrate: that you pay attention, remember details, and can communicate in writing like a person rather than a template.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Write your first draft without opening ChatGPT or any other AI tool.** The point is to hear your own voice first. You can edit later - and AI can help you edit. But you cannot add authenticity to a document that was never authentic to begin with. The raw draft, however rough, has to come from you.

2. **Research each firm before writing a single letter for them.** Find two real transactions, one thing about the culture, and the name of at least one person in the group you are targeting. This research is the raw material. Without it, you are writing in generalities by default.

3. **Keep it under 300 words.** This constraint forces the discipline that AI cannot apply on your behalf. Every sentence must earn its presence. If it does not add specific information, cut it.

4. **Pair your cover letter with a strong resume.** Review the [investment banking resume guide](/job-search/investment-banking-resume/) to make sure both documents are consistent and formatted correctly before you submit anything. A strong letter attached to a weak resume does not save the application.

5. **Have someone who works in finance read your draft.** Not for grammar - for whether it sounds like someone who knows what banking actually is. An analyst at any bank will do. Their instinctive reaction to the letter is the signal you are looking for. If they say it sounds like a template, it sounds like a template.

6. **Prepare a verbal version of the same answer.** Your cover letter "why this firm" answer will be asked in the first five minutes of every interview. Know it cold and make sure it is consistent with what you wrote. If the letter and the verbal answer diverge, the recruiter notices.
