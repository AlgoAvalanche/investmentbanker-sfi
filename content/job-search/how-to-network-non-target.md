# How to Network into Investment Banking from a Non-Target School

The single most important thing to understand about non-target networking for investment banking is that it is not a compensation strategy for a weaker application. It is a replacement strategy for a distribution channel you do not have.

Target school students get in through a formal pipeline: on-campus recruiting, firm presentations, OCR bids, structured superdays. Non-target students do not have this pipeline. The network is not a workaround - it is the primary path. Mergers & Inquisitions puts it plainly: "if you're a career changer or you do not attend a top university or business school, networking is your only chance to break into the finance industry."[^1]

This distinction matters because it affects how aggressively and how systematically you need to approach it.

A student from the University of Michigan or Georgetown who half-heartedly networks can still get into banking through OCR. A student from a solid regional university with no OCR pipeline who half-heartedly networks will not get in at all. The margin for error is different. So is the required volume of work.

---

## What "Non-Target" Actually Means in Practice

The target/semi-target/non-target classification is not binary and it shifts by firm. Goldman and JPMorgan have a short list of core targets they recruit heavily from. Evercore and Centerview have an even shorter list. But a regional boutique or a middle-market firm may pull heavily from a state university that has no formal relationship with any bulge bracket.

Before you decide you are fighting from a non-target position, do the research:

- Check LinkedIn for where the analysts and associates at your target firms went to school. Filter by graduation year within the last five years.
- Check whether your school has any alumni in the groups you are targeting.
- Ask your school's finance club or alumni network for historical placement data.

You may find that your school is a semi-target for firms you had not considered, and a non-target for the ones you initially wanted. Adjust your target list accordingly. This is not settling - it is recruiting efficiently.

Wall Street Oasis maintains a regularly updated list of which schools are considered targets, semi-targets, and non-targets at various bank tiers.[^2] Cross-reference it against your actual LinkedIn research, because firm-level classifications vary and the crowd-sourced list will not capture every regional dynamic.

## The Numbers Reality

Before the strategy, the numbers.

Non-target students need to run a fundamentally higher-volume campaign than their target-school peers. The data from practitioners is consistent on this:

- **Target school students** typically send 30-50 cold emails and generate 8-15 informational interviews.
- **Semi-target students** typically send 50-100 emails and generate 10-20 interviews.
- **Non-target students** need to send 100-200 emails to build sufficient coverage - and can realistically expect 15-25 informational interviews from that effort.[^3]

The goal is not 15-25 conversations as an end state. The goal is 20-30 contacts across multiple firms who will actively advocate for your candidacy when recruiting decisions are being made.[^1] That number requires the higher volume of outreach because your conversion rate at each step is lower when you lack an institutional pipeline feeding you in.

Response rates are heavily influenced by connection type:

- **Completely cold, no shared connection:** 5-10%
- **College alumni:** 20-30%
- **High school or hometown connection:** 50-85%
- **Warm introduction from a mutual contact:** 70-90%
- **Generic or poorly written emails:** below 2%[^3]

The implication is direct: alumni outreach is not optional, it is the foundation. The gap between a cold stranger (5-10%) and a college alumnus (20-30%) is the difference between needing 200 emails to get 10 conversations and needing 50 to get 15. Build the alumni map first.

## The Non-Target Networking Playbook

The strategy has four components that need to happen simultaneously rather than sequentially:

**1. Build the alumni map**
**2. Execute cold email outreach with proper follow-up cadence**
**3. Convert conversations to advocates through the mini-ask/real-ask sequence**
**4. Create visible signals of seriousness**

These are not steps 1-2-3-4. They happen in parallel over the span of six to twelve months before you expect to receive an offer.

---

## Building the Alumni Map

Start with LinkedIn. Search for your school's name combined with the bank or group you are targeting. Set the filter to "Alumni." Look for everyone from your school who has worked in investment banking in the last ten years.

What you are building is a tiered list:

- **Tier 1:** Alumni currently working at your target firms in the groups you want. These are the most valuable contacts because they can advocate for you directly.
- **Tier 2:** Alumni who worked at target firms and have since moved to PE, corporate development, or other finance roles. Less direct access to pull you in, but can provide advice and potentially an internal referral.
- **Tier 3:** Alumni who work in adjacent finance roles (corporate banking, Big 4 advisory, financial consulting). Lower probability of direct pull but can sometimes provide warm intros.

This map should have fifty to one hundred names before you start outreach. Do not start outreach with ten names - you need volume because your conversion rate will be low.

One additional source that non-target students underuse: CFA Society chapters. Local CFA Society membership gives you access to finance professionals across roles - some of whom work in banking or have banking contacts - in a setting where networking is explicitly expected. This is particularly useful if your school's direct alumni network in finance is thin.[^4]

## Cold Email: The Mechanics That Get Replies

The most common failure mode in non-target networking is the email that is too long, too formal, or too obviously templated. Bankers receive a lot of these. The ones that get replies are short, specific, and ask for something achievable.

The practitioner consensus on email length is tight: five sentences maximum for initial outreach.[^1] Exceed that and your response rate drops materially.

**The five-sentence framework:**[^3]

1. **Personalized opening** - One sentence that references something specific: a shared background, a deal they worked on that you actually read about, or a mutual connection. This is not a placeholder - it requires 3-5 minutes of research per person before you write.
2. **Your credentials** - One sentence: your year, school, major, and one relevant credential or experience.
3. **Why this person specifically** - One sentence explaining why you are reaching out to them rather than anyone else at the firm.
4. **Clear, narrow ask** - "Would you have 20 minutes for a quick call?" Not "any advice you can offer," not "a job."
5. **Flexible closing** - Thank them and signal scheduling flexibility. Give them an easy out.

Subject line: "[School Name] student - quick question about [Group/Firm]."

The school name in the subject line is important. It triggers the alumni instinct even when the person does not know you. Mergers & Inquisitions notes this explicitly in their non-target breakin guides - the alumni connection is the strongest single lever you have when your institutional pipeline is absent.[^1]

**Targeting by seniority matters.** Junior bankers - Analysts and first-year Associates - reply at meaningfully higher rates than VPs and MDs. They remember recruiting more recently, they have more time, and they are more likely to see themselves in your position. Weight your outreach toward Analysts and Associates on your first pass.[^3]

**Timing matters too.** Send emails Monday through Thursday, between 8am and 10am. Emails sent Friday afternoon, over weekends, or during earnings season get buried. The goal is to hit an inbox when someone is starting their day with bandwidth to respond.[^3]

## The Follow-Up Cadence

Most non-target students either never follow up or follow up once and stop. Both are mistakes.

The correct cadence:[^3]

- **First follow-up:** Five to seven days after the initial email. Two to three sentences. Reference the original email briefly and restate the ask.
- **Second follow-up:** Five to seven days after that. Acknowledge explicitly that this is your final attempt. Something like: "I know you are busy and I do not want to crowd your inbox - this is my last follow-up."

After two follow-ups with no response, stop and move on. You have spent three attempts on this contact. Sending a third or fourth follow-up does not improve your odds and risks signaling poor judgment.

Mergers & Inquisitions describes this as being "politely persistent" - you follow up because bankers are busy and your first email genuinely may have been missed, not because persistence alone generates replies.[^1]

At scale - 100 to 200 initial emails plus two follow-up rounds - you are managing several hundred touchpoints. Track this in a spreadsheet: name, firm, group, date of initial email, follow-up 1 date, follow-up 2 date, response status, call date, notes.

## What to Do on the Call

The networking call is covered in detail in the [coffee chat strategy guide](/job-search/100-cups-of-coffee-strategy/), but the non-target-specific context matters here.

The structural guidance from practitioners: keep the call to 15-20 minutes, and listen 70% of the time and speak 30%.[^1] Most candidates invert this ratio and spend the call selling themselves. The purpose of the call is to learn, build rapport, and create the conditions for an ongoing relationship - not to pitch.

When you are from a non-target school, the person on the other end of the call knows it. They are not going to pretend your school has the same recruiting infrastructure as Wharton. Do not pretend it does either.

The most effective approach is direct acknowledgment followed by demonstrable preparation: "I know my school does not have a formal recruiting relationship with your group, which is exactly why I have been doing this outreach directly. I want to make sure I am spending time with people who actually understand what the role requires."

This does two things: it removes the awkward subtext, and it signals that you are self-aware and resourceful rather than oblivious. The Mergers & Inquisitions guide to breaking in from state schools makes the same point: work ethic and determination are what differentiate non-target candidates - not pedigree, but demonstrated hunger for the role.[^5]

Come prepared with:

- Two questions about the person's specific path and experience (not generic "what advice do you have" questions)
- One or two transaction questions that show you have done your homework on the firm
- A clear, thirty-second version of your story and why banking

Do not ask for a job. Do not ask for a referral on the first call.

## The Mini-Ask Before the Real Ask

This is the most commonly skipped step in non-target networking, and it is one of the most important.

The sequence practitioners recommend:[^1]

**Mini-ask:** At the end of the first conversation, ask something small and achievable - would they be willing to review your resume and give feedback? Is there anyone else at the firm or in their network they think it would be worth speaking with?

The mini-ask does two things. It gives the contact something concrete to do that does not require them to put their name on the line. And it extends the relationship naturally - you follow up with a revised resume based on their feedback, you connect with the person they referred, and the relationship has a second and third chapter.

**Real ask:** Four to six weeks later, once you have had multiple exchanges and they have formed a real impression of you, you make the direct ask - would they be willing to pass your resume to the recruiting team, or flag your application when recruiting opens?

The difference between this and asking on the first call is significant. On the first call, you are a stranger requesting a professional favor. After several exchanges, you are someone they have invested time in and want to see succeed. The referral conversion rate is categorically different.

## Converting Conversations to Advocates

A single good conversation is worth less than most candidates think. What matters is whether that person will mention your name when a recruiting decision is being made.

The way to build that outcome is through follow-up and relationship maintenance:

- Send a specific thank-you note within 24 hours that references something concrete from the conversation. Not "thank you for your time" - something like "Your point about the sector rotation in healthcare M&A was something I had not thought about - I read the Bain report you mentioned and it shifted my view."
- Check back in three to four weeks with something genuinely useful: an article relevant to a topic you discussed, a question that came up in your continued research, an update on a project you mentioned.
- When you do eventually make the real ask, it does not come out of nowhere. It comes from a person they have exchanged four or five messages with and formed a real impression of.

The number of advocates, not the quality of any single conversation, is what moves the needle. The Mergers & Inquisitions networking guide targets 20-30 advocates across different firms as the outcome of a full non-target campaign.[^1] That number requires volume, and it requires consistent follow-up to keep relationships from going cold.

## Cold LinkedIn Outreach: Lower Conversion, Still Necessary

LinkedIn InMail and connection requests are the lower-probability supplement to email, not the replacement. Response rates are lower because the format is associated with volume outreach.

If you use LinkedIn, the same principles apply: short, specific, non-generic. The mistake most people make is sending a generic connection request with no note, then messaging after the connection is accepted. The note on the connection request is your subject line - use it.

Wall Street Prep's guidance on landing interviews makes a related point: use LinkedIn to connect around shared backgrounds and specific common ground, not as a broadcast channel.[^4] The mechanics are the same as email - specificity and a narrow ask outperform generic outreach at every tier.

For non-target candidates specifically, LinkedIn has one significant advantage over alumni directories: it surfaces people who would not be findable through your school's official network. You can find the analyst who went to your school five years ago, bounced around, and is now at a firm you want to be at, even if they never updated an alumni database.

## The Geographic Arbitrage Strategy

One approach that rarely gets discussed but works consistently for non-target candidates: target regional offices and regional boutiques in the city where your school has alumni density.

If you went to a solid state school in the Midwest, your alumni network in Chicago finance is likely stronger than your alumni network in New York. A non-target in the context of a New York bulge bracket may be a semi-target in the context of a Chicago middle-market firm. The career trajectory from a solid Chicago MM role to a larger platform is a real path.

Staying geographically proximate to your school's alumni base increases the density of your network, which directly increases your conversion rate. This is not a consolation prize. It is a smarter first move that preserves the option to recruit up later from a position of experience rather than competing from a position of inexperience against target school candidates with two prior IB internships.

## The Timeline Problem

Non-target networking takes longer than target school recruiting because you are building something from scratch rather than plugging into an existing infrastructure.

The research consistently points to 6-12 months before your target start date as the correct window to begin.[^3] Three to six months is workable but compressed. Two months is not enough time.

The realistic timeline for someone starting from zero:

- **6-9 months before target start date:** Begin building alumni map, start first wave of outreach
- **4-6 months out:** Have 15-20 conversations, start identifying advocates, begin collecting referrals
- **3 months out:** Have a shortlist of firms where you have internal contacts, start applying with warm referrals
- **1-2 months out:** Targeted applications, active follow-up with advocates ahead of recruiting decisions

The Mergers & Inquisitions guide to breaking in from state schools adds one piece of advice worth taking seriously: "Start earlier." Even if you are not certain about the career path, building the network earlier costs you nothing except time, and starting six months too early is recoverable in a way that starting three months too late is not.[^5]

## What to Do If Your Alumni Network Is Thin

Some non-target students face a second problem on top of the school problem: their school genuinely has very few alumni in banking. The alumni response rate advantage (20-30%) disappears when there are no alumni to reach.

In this situation:

**Expand the connection definition.** High school or hometown connections get a 50-85% response rate - only slightly below warm introductions.[^3] If you grew up in a financial center or attended a high school with alumni in finance, that connection is real and worth using.

**Use professional associations as a substitute.** CFA Society membership, finance club networks from multiple schools, alumni groups from undergraduate programs where you did coursework - all of these create plausible connection points that are stronger than pure cold outreach.[^4]

**Target boutiques and smaller platforms first.** Cold outreach to the analysts and associates at regional boutiques works at higher rates than cold outreach to Goldman Sachs. Convert those conversations into the experience and references you need to compete at larger platforms later. This is not settling - it is sequencing.

**Accept the higher volume requirement.** If your conversion rate from cold to conversation is closer to 5% than 20%, you need four times as many emails. That is a tractable problem with a clear solution: build a bigger list and run the process more systematically.

## A Hard Truth About Non-Target Recruiting

The people who break in from non-target schools are not the ones who had the best single conversation or the most impressive extracurricular record. They are the ones who sent two hundred emails when most people stopped at twenty, who followed up when the silence was uncomfortable, and who maintained relationships for eight months before the recruiting window opened.

Mergers & Inquisitions describes the non-target path plainly: response rates improve dramatically when candidates refine their approach and persist through early failure. The candidates who cross the threshold are the ones who treat initial low response rates as a calibration problem - something to fix through better targeting and better emails - rather than as a signal to stop.[^5]

The students who do not make it are largely the ones who gave up after the first ten non-responses. Given that your expected response rate on cold outreach is 5-10%, ten emails generates zero to one response by design. The process has not failed - it has barely started.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Spend two hours this week building your alumni map on LinkedIn.** Fifty names minimum. Tier them by proximity to your target groups. Do not start emailing until the map is built - you will run the outreach more efficiently.

2. **Write three email templates.** One for Tier 1 (current bankers at target firms), one for Tier 2 (former bankers now in PE/corpdev), one for cold non-alumni outreach. Keep each to five sentences. Personalize each one before sending - templates are starting points, not final drafts.

3. **Set a weekly outreach quota.** Ten to fifteen emails per week is a reasonable non-target target. Track initial emails, first follow-ups, second follow-ups, replies, calls scheduled, and follow-up dates in a simple spreadsheet. The networking process needs to be managed like a pipeline, not a one-off effort.

4. **Send on Tuesday or Wednesday morning, between 8 and 10am.** This is a small variable with a real effect on open and response rates. It costs nothing to get right.

5. **Read the [coffee chat strategy guide](/job-search/100-cups-of-coffee-strategy/)** before your first call. The preparation makes a measurable difference in whether you get a referral from that conversation.

6. **Make sure your [resume](/job-search/investment-banking-resume/) is tight before any referral asks anyone to pass it along.** Every advocate who forwards your resume is putting their name next to yours. Make that easy for them.
