# How to Network into Investment Banking from a Non-Target School

The single most important thing to understand about non-target networking for investment banking is that it is not a compensation strategy for a weaker application. It is a replacement strategy for a distribution channel you do not have.

Target school students get in through a formal pipeline: on-campus recruiting, firm presentations, OCR bids, structured superdays. Non-target students do not have this pipeline. The network is not a workaround - it is the primary path. This distinction matters because it affects how aggressively and how systematically you need to approach it.

A student from the University of Michigan or Georgetown who half-heartedly networks can still get into banking through OCR. A student from a solid regional university with no OCR pipeline who half-heartedly networks will not get in at all. The margin for error is different.

---

## What "Non-Target" Actually Means in Practice

The target/semi-target/non-target classification is not binary and it shifts by firm. Goldman and JPMorgan have a short list of core targets they recruit heavily from. Evercore and Centerview have an even shorter list. But a regional boutique or a middle-market firm may pull heavily from a state university that has no formal relationship with any bulge bracket.

Before you decide you are fighting from a non-target position, do the research:

- Check LinkedIn for where the analysts and associates at your target firms went to school. Filter by graduation year within the last five years.
- Check whether your school has any alumni in the groups you are targeting.
- Ask your school's finance club or alumni network for historical placement data.

You may find that your school is a semi-target for firms you had not considered, and a non-target for the ones you initially wanted. Adjust your target list accordingly. This is not settling - it is recruiting efficiently.

## The Non-Target Networking Playbook

The strategy has four components that need to happen simultaneously rather than sequentially:

**1. Build the alumni map**
**2. Execute cold email outreach**
**3. Convert conversations to advocates**
**4. Create visible signals of seriousness**

These are not steps 1-2-3-4. They happen in parallel over the span of six to twelve months before you expect to receive an offer.

---

## Building the Alumni Map

Start with LinkedIn. Search for your school's name combined with the bank or group you are targeting. Set the filter to "Alumni." Look for everyone from your school who has worked in investment banking in the last ten years.

What you are building is a tiered list:

- **Tier 1**: Alumni currently working at your target firms in the groups you want. These are the most valuable contacts because they can advocate for you directly.
- **Tier 2**: Alumni who worked at target firms and have since moved to PE, corporate development, or other finance roles. Less direct access to pull you in, but can provide advice and potentially an internal referral.
- **Tier 3**: Alumni who work in adjacent finance roles (corporate banking, Big 4 advisory, financial consulting). Lower probability of direct pull but can sometimes provide warm intros.

This map should have fifty to one hundred names before you start outreach. Do not start outreach with ten names - you need volume because your conversion rate will be low.

## Cold Email: The Mechanics That Get Replies

The most common failure mode in non-target networking is the email that is too long, too formal, or too obviously templated. Bankers receive a lot of these. The ones that get replies are short, specific, and ask for something achievable.

**The formula for a cold email that works:**

- Line 1: One sentence establishing the connection (shared school, shared interest in a specific group, specific compliment on something they have done that you actually read).
- Line 2: One sentence about who you are and why you are reaching out - specifically, not generally.
- Line 3: A narrow, easy-to-say-yes-to request. Twenty minutes by phone. Not "any advice you can offer," not "a job."
- Line 4: One sentence giving them an easy out. "If timing does not work, I understand completely."

Total length: four to six sentences. Subject line: "[School Name] student - quick question about [Group/Firm]."

The school name in the subject line is important. It triggers the alumni instinct even when the person does not know you.

One thing Mergers & Inquisitions has noted in their non-target breakin guides: the conversion rate on cold emails to alumni is meaningfully higher than cold emails to non-alumni, typically 30-40% versus 10-15%. If your alumni pool in banking is thin, the school connection is still the strongest lever you have.

## What to Do on the Call

The networking call is covered in detail in the [coffee chat strategy guide](/job-search/100-cups-of-coffee-strategy/), but the non-target-specific context matters here.

When you are from a non-target school, the person on the other end of the call knows it. They are not going to pretend your school has the same recruiting infrastructure as Wharton. Do not pretend it does either.

The most effective approach is direct acknowledgment followed by demonstrable preparation: "I know my school does not have a formal recruiting relationship with your group, which is exactly why I have been doing this outreach directly. I want to make sure I am spending time with people who actually understand what the role requires."

This does two things: it removes the awkward subtext, and it signals that you are self-aware and resourceful rather than oblivious. Bankers, almost universally, respect resourcefulness.

Come prepared with:
- Two questions about the person's specific path and experience (not generic "what advice do you have" questions)
- One or two transaction questions that show you have done your homework on the firm
- A clear, thirty-second version of your story and why banking

Do not ask for a job. Do not ask for a referral on the first call. Ask for their perspective and, at the end, ask if there is anyone else at the firm or in their network who they think it would be worth speaking with. The referral chain is how non-target candidates build enough internal advocates to get a formal interview.

## Converting Conversations to Advocates

A single good conversation is worth less than most candidates think. What matters is whether that person will mention your name when a recruiting decision is being made.

The way to build that outcome is through follow-up and relationship maintenance:

- Send a specific thank-you note within 24 hours that references something from the conversation. Not "thank you for your time" - something like "Your point about the sector rotation in healthcare M&A was something I had not thought about - I read the Bain report you mentioned and it shifted my view."
- Check back in three to four weeks with something genuinely useful: an article relevant to a topic you discussed, a question that came up in your continued research, an update on a project you mentioned.
- When you do eventually ask them to pass your resume along or flag your application, it does not come out of nowhere. It comes from a person they have exchanged four or five messages with and formed a real impression of.

This is what WSO users who have broken in from non-targets consistently describe as the actual mechanism. One poster who broke into a middle-market bank from a regional state school described it this way: "I talked to twelve people at that firm over eight months. Three of them mentioned me independently during recruiting. That's why I got the call." The number of advocates, not the quality of any single conversation, is what moves the needle.

## Cold LinkedIn Outreach: Lower Conversion, Still Necessary

LinkedIn InMail and connection requests are the lower-probability supplement to email, not the replacement. Response rates are lower because the format is associated with volume outreach.

If you use LinkedIn, the same principles apply: short, specific, non-generic. The mistake most people make is sending a generic connection request with no note, then messaging after the connection is accepted. The note on the connection request is your subject line - use it.

For non-target candidates specifically, LinkedIn has one significant advantage: it surfaces people who would not be findable through alumni databases. You can find the analyst who went to your school five years ago, bounced around, and is now at a firm you want to be at, even if they never show up in your school's alumni directory.

For more on optimizing your LinkedIn presence as part of this outreach strategy, see the [LinkedIn optimization guide](/job-search/linkedin-optimization-ib/).

## The Geographic Arbitrage Strategy

One approach that rarely gets discussed but works consistently for non-target candidates: target regional offices and regional boutiques in the city where your school has alumni density.

If you went to a solid state school in the Midwest, your alumni network in Chicago finance is likely stronger than your alumni network in New York. A non-target in the context of a New York bulge bracket may be a semi-target in the context of a Chicago middle-market firm. The career trajectory from a solid Chicago MM role to a larger platform is a real path.

Staying geographically proximate to your school's alumni base increases the density of your network, which directly increases your conversion rate.

## The Timeline Problem

Non-target networking takes longer than target school recruiting because you are building something from scratch rather than plugging into an existing infrastructure. The realistic timeline for someone starting from zero:

- 6-9 months before target start date: Begin building alumni map, start first wave of outreach
- 4-6 months out: Have 15-20 conversations, start identifying advocates, begin collecting referrals
- 3 months out: Have a shortlist of firms where you have internal contacts, start applying with warm referrals
- 1-2 months out: Targeted applications, active follow-up with advocates ahead of recruiting decisions

If you are starting this process two months before you want to work, it will not work. There is no shortcut to the timeline.

---

## A Hard Truth About Non-Target Recruiting

A Wall Street Oasis thread from several years ago that accumulated hundreds of upvotes made a point that is worth quoting directly: "The dirty secret of non-target banking recruiting is that your school is not the problem. Your school is the excuse. The actual problem is that most non-target students give up after twenty emails. The ones who get in sent two hundred."

That is slightly reductive but directionally correct. The conversion rate from non-target cold outreach to offer is genuinely low. The students who make it through are not the ones who had the best single conversation - they are the ones who had the most conversations and maintained the most relationships.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Spend two hours this week building your alumni map on LinkedIn.** Fifty names minimum. Tier them by proximity to your target groups. Do not start emailing until the map is built - you will run the outreach more efficiently.

2. **Write three email templates.** One for Tier 1 (current bankers at target firms), one for Tier 2 (former bankers now in PE/corpdev), one for cold non-alumni outreach. Personalize each one before sending - templates are starting points, not final drafts.

3. **Set a weekly outreach quota.** Ten emails per week is a reasonable target. Track replies, calls scheduled, and follow-up dates in a simple spreadsheet. The networking process needs to be managed like a pipeline.

4. **Read the [coffee chat strategy guide](/job-search/100-cups-of-coffee-strategy/)** before your first call. The preparation makes a measurable difference in whether you get a referral from that conversation.

5. **Make sure your [resume](/job-search/investment-banking-resume/) is tight before any referral asks anyone to pass it along.** Every advocate who forwards your resume is putting their name next to yours. Make that easy for them.
