# OCR Strategy: Bidding Lists, Drop Days, and Backup Plans

On-campus recruiting is the most efficient path into investment banking for students at target and semi-target schools. It is also the path that most people navigate badly, either through over-concentrating on a small number of firms, misunderstanding how bidding systems work, or treating OCR as a passive process rather than an active strategy.

This article covers the mechanics and strategy of OCR from the perspective of someone who has seen it from both sides - as a candidate and as someone who has participated in firm presentations and recruiting days. The process has quirks that are not obvious until you understand them, and understanding them can meaningfully change your outcomes.

---

## What OCR Actually Is and How It Varies by School

OCR - on-campus recruiting - is the formal process by which banks come to university campuses (or conduct virtual equivalents) to recruit students for summer analyst and full-time analyst positions. At target schools like Penn, Columbia, Chicago, Duke, and Michigan, OCR is a well-oiled machine with specific timelines, bidding systems, and structured processes.

The specific mechanics vary by school:

- **Bid-based systems**: Students are allocated a set number of "bids" or points per recruiting cycle and spend them to secure interview slots with specific firms. The number of bids matters because it forces explicit prioritization.
- **Lottery-based systems**: Students enter a lottery for interview slots, with some preference given to students who attended firm events.
- **Direct application with campus screen**: Firms receive all applications submitted through the school portal and conduct first-round screens on campus.

Know exactly which system your school uses before you build your strategy. The optimal approach is different for each.

## Bidding Strategy: The Fundamental Principle

In bid-based systems, the core mistake is spreading bids too thin across too many firms. The logic for spreading seems sound - more bids means more shots - but it ignores the fact that most banks want to see genuine interest, and candidates who clearly want them over other options often get more traction in the early rounds.

The other mistake is concentrating bids entirely on reach firms. If your profile (GPA, school tier, prior internship quality) puts you in the middle of the pool for Goldman, spending heavily on Goldman bids while neglecting safer options is a low-expected-value bet.

A reasonable bidding framework:

- **30-40% of bids**: Reach firms. The banks you want most but where your profile is not clearly competitive.
- **40-50% of bids**: Target firms. Banks where your profile is solidly in the interview range.
- **15-20% of bids**: Safety firms. Banks where you are confident of getting an interview and want to ensure at least one offer anchor.

"Safety" in this context is relative. Safety is a middle-market bank that interviews broadly from your school, not a firm you do not want to work at. Having an offer from a good MM bank is an excellent position to be in - it removes the desperation from your remaining conversations.

## Pre-Bid Preparation: Firm Events Matter More Than You Think

At most target schools, attendance at firm events - information sessions, coffee chats with visiting bankers, firm-sponsored dinners - is tracked and affects your position in the recruiting process. The mechanism varies: some schools have explicit point systems, others have firms providing informal feedback to the career services office about which students attended and made an impression.

The practical implication: you cannot treat firm events as optional. Attending an information session is not sufficient if you sat in the back and asked nothing. The students who benefit from firm events are the ones who introduced themselves to at least one banker, asked a specific and intelligent question during the session, and followed up by email within 24 hours.

This is where recruiting starts to require actual preparation. You need to know enough about each firm's recent deals, group structure, and culture to have a real thirty-second conversation with a banker at a networking event. That preparation is not possible if you are attending twelve events in two weeks having read nothing.

Calendar these events strategically. You do not have to attend every firm's event. Pick the firms you are actually targeting and show up prepared for those.

## Drop Days: The Mechanics Nobody Explains Well

Drop days are the dates when firms release interview slots for students to claim. In schools with lottery or first-come-first-served systems, drop days create a sharp competitive dynamic that catches unprepared students off guard.

The key tactical points:

- **Know the drop day calendar in advance.** Your school's career services office publishes this. Track it.
- **Be ready at drop time.** Some slots disappear in minutes. If you are in class or sleeping when a preferred firm's slots drop, you may be locked out.
- **Have your materials ready before drop days.** Your resume should be finalized before recruiting season opens. If you are still editing your [investment banking resume](/job-search/investment-banking-resume/) on drop day, you have a process problem.
- **Drop order preference**: If you can choose the order in which you use bids or claim slots, prioritize firms where early-round interviews happen first on the calendar. Being prepared for a firm that interviews in week two is different from being prepared for a firm that interviews in week six.

One quirk that Wall Street Oasis members who have been through OCR at multiple schools consistently flag: many students do not realize that their bid usage and slot claims are visible to recruiters at some schools. Demonstrating that you used your highest-value bid on a specific firm signals genuine preference in a way that applications alone do not.

## The Interview Sequencing Problem

When OCR is running at full speed, you will likely have multiple first-round interviews in the same week or two-week period. The sequencing of these interviews is something you can partially control and should think about deliberately.

**Use your less-preferred firms as practice.** If you have a first-round with a firm that is genuinely your third or fourth choice, schedule it early. The first technical interview of the season is almost always worse than the fifth because you are still calibrating to the format, the difficulty, and the speed. Use early interviews to develop that calibration.

**Do not schedule your first-choice interview first.** This sounds obvious but many students do not have the self-awareness to recognize they are not ready. If your Goldman interview is on day two of recruiting season and you have not done a single mock interview, you are not ready.

**Back-to-back days are brutal.** If you have a superday at one firm on a Tuesday and a superday at another firm on Wednesday, you will not perform at your best in the second. OCR schedules are often compressed, but where you have flexibility, space out high-stakes interviews by at least 48 hours.

## What to Do When First Rounds Go Poorly

OCR has a specific psychological trap: a failed first round at a preferred firm early in the season can derail candidates who interpret it as a verdict on their overall candidacy. It is not.

First-round failures happen for reasons that are partially within your control (preparation, technical knowledge, how you framed your story) and partially outside of it (interviewer mood, question randomness, internal headcount decisions that were made before you walked in). The diagnosis matters for what you do next.

If your first-round failures are concentrated in technicals - meaning you could not calculate an accretion/dilution scenario or explain how goodwill affects an LBO - that is a preparation problem with a clear fix. Study harder, do more practice problems, and do not schedule the next important interview until you have filled the gap.

If your first-round failures seem to be in the "fit" or "story" category - you are not connecting with interviewers, your "why banking" answer is landing flat, you seem uncertain - that is a different fix. Work on the narrative, do more mock interviews with people who will give you honest feedback, and revisit your [cover letter and positioning](/job-search/cover-letters-not-chatgpt/) to make sure your story is coherent across documents.

## The Backup Plan Should Not Be an Afterthought

Every OCR strategy needs a backup plan, and that plan needs to be developed in parallel with the primary strategy rather than after OCR has gone badly.

The backup options in rough order of probability:

1. **Other banks through OCR**: If you concentrated bids and missed at your top choices, are there firms still running OCR where you have remaining bids or can still apply?
2. **Off-cycle applications**: Some banks hire for full-time positions in the spring and summer. This is covered in more depth separately, but it is a real option for students who have strong profiles but missed OCR timing.
3. **Boutiques and regional shops outside OCR**: Many smaller firms do not participate in formal OCR at any school. They hire through direct applications and networking year-round.
4. **Spring weeks and accelerated programs for the following year**: If you are a sophomore or junior and OCR does not go as planned, the [spring week and sophomore internship pipeline](/job-search/spring-weeks-sophomore-internships/) is worth understanding as a reset strategy.

The worst outcome from a strategic standpoint is being left without an offer in April with no remaining options in motion. Avoid this by maintaining the backup pipeline throughout OCR season, not starting it after OCR has ended.

## Semi-Target OCR: Different Dynamics

Students at semi-target schools face a different version of this problem. OCR exists but is thinner - fewer firms participate, fewer slots are available, and the firms that do come are typically not the most competitive.

At semi-target schools, the OCR opportunity is real but limited. Use it for what it is worth, then supplement aggressively through direct networking and off-OCR applications. A semi-target student who does well in OCR at the firms that come to campus, while simultaneously networking independently into firms that do not come to campus, has a full pipeline.

The mistake is treating OCR as the complete strategy when you are at a school where OCR is only a partial channel.

## Firm Presentation Etiquette: What Gets You Noticed and What Gets You Remembered Badly

A brief note on the soft side of OCR: the recruiting process is a two-way observation period. Bankers at firm events are watching candidate behavior as much as candidates are listening to presentations.

Behaviors that end applications before they start:

- Showing up to an information session for Firm A while wearing the swag from Firm B's competing event the same night (yes, this happens, and yes, it is noticed).
- Asking about compensation in a group setting with junior bankers. This is a recruiting event, not a salary negotiation. Save this question for the offer stage.
- Monopolizing a senior banker's time during a cocktail event. Get in, make your impression, and move on. Other candidates need access too.
- Being visibly disengaged at events. If you are on your phone during a presentation, the recruiter in the back of the room has noted it.

The people running these events have been doing them for years and have seen every version of this behavior. Do not be the story they tell at the office the next day.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Map the OCR calendar at your school now, not two weeks before recruiting season.** Get the firm event dates, drop days, and first-round interview windows into your calendar immediately. The students who do well in OCR are the ones who planned for it months in advance.

2. **Build your bid allocation using the portfolio framework above.** Reach, target, safety. Write it down and stick to it. Do not re-allocate in the moment based on anxiety.

3. **Prepare your resume before OCR season opens.** Review the [resume guide](/job-search/investment-banking-resume/) and have a final version ready before the first firm event. You cannot be revising your resume while you are attending OCR events.

4. **Do mock interviews before any real interview.** Schedule at least three mock technicals and two mock fit interviews before your first OCR first round. Your school's career services office likely offers this. Use it.

5. **Prepare for your [superday](/interview-prep/superday-what-to-expect/) specifically.** The jump from first round to superday requires a different level of preparation. Know what the superday format looks like at each of your target firms so you are not surprised by the structure when you get there.
