# What Actually Happens in Your First 90 Days as an IB Analyst

The first 90 days analyst experience in investment banking is the most disorienting professional stretch most people will ever endure. You arrive having aced every case interview, memorized three accounting frameworks, and convinced yourself you are prepared. You are not. Nobody is. The gap between what banks communicate during recruiting and what the first three months actually feel like is wide enough to drive a leveraged buyout through.

This is not a scare piece. It is a map - built from documented practitioner experience, not recruiting brochure copy. Here is what actually happens, phase by phase, and more importantly, how to not wreck your reputation while it does.

---

## The Training Period: Useful, But Not in the Way You Think

Most bulge brackets and upper-middle-market banks run formal training programs ranging from two weeks to eight weeks, depending on the firm. Goldman, JPMorgan, and Morgan Stanley run longer programs that include technical content, compliance training, and rotational exposure. Boutiques often compress this to a week or two of slides and an Excel crash course before putting you on a live deal.

The instinct most analysts bring to training is the wrong one. They arrive expecting the technical content to be the hard part. It is not. According to Peak Frameworks, which aggregates guidance from practitioners across the Street, the first months function as both a formal and informal assessment period - banks are evaluating not just whether you can build a model, but whether you can behave correctly inside a high-pressure hierarchy.[^1]

Training teaches you the firm's Excel conventions, its model templates, its formatting standards, and - if you pay close attention - the unwritten power structure. Watch who the trainers defer to. Watch which questions get answered and which get politely redirected. The political texture of a firm is legible during training if you know what to look for.

From a practical standpoint: use training to internalize your firm's specific templates cold. If your bank uses a particular LBO shell or a specific DCF format, commit it to muscle memory. You will never have time to learn it from scratch once you are on a live deal with a 7 AM client call attached to it.

## Weeks One Through Four on the Desk: The Fog of War

When you land on your group desk after training, expect a few days of relative quiet. Your associate or VP is sizing you up. They are deciding what to assign you and how much supervision you will need.

Then a request comes in at 9:45 PM on a Tuesday. A pitch needs updating for a 7 AM call. Welcome to the job.

The first month is characterized by a particular kind of cognitive overload that experienced bankers describe but analysts cannot fully anticipate. You are simultaneously learning:

- The firm's proprietary model architecture
- Your group's deal conventions and preferred formatting
- The names, preferences, and working styles of every senior person you support
- The actual content of whatever transactions your group is currently working
- The unwritten communication rules - when to email, when to message, when to physically walk to someone's desk

Mergers and Inquisitions has documented the analyst experience extensively, and their data on daily output is instructive: the primary deliverables are Excel financial models, PowerPoint pitch books, and deal logistics work including tracking buyer and seller lists, managing data rooms, and organizing transaction documentation.[^2] Most first-years have limited direct client contact for months. The work flows upward through your Associate and VP. That is not a knock on your status - it is simply how the pipeline functions.

The single most valuable skill in your first four weeks is not technical. It is the ability to clarify scope before starting work and confirm direction before delivering. Ask your Associate: "I want to make sure I am building what you need - are you looking for X or Y?" before spending four hours going the wrong direction. This is not weakness. It is operational efficiency, and experienced bankers recognize the difference immediately.

## The Hierarchy: Understanding Who Actually Runs Your Life

The org chart at a bank looks clean. MD at the top, then SVP or Director, then VP, then Associate, then Analyst. Reality is more nuanced, and your first 90 days are largely an exercise in mapping the actual power structure beneath the formal one.

Your Associate is your most important relationship in year one. Not the MD. Not the VP. Your Associate. They assign your work, review your output first, decide whether to escalate your mistakes, and determine whether your day-to-day experience is tolerable or not.

Financial-Modeling.com's practitioner guide lays out this dynamic clearly and with specificity: route all work through your Associate before it reaches VP or MD level. CC your Associate when emailing upward. Never shortcut around them, even when an MD makes direct contact.[^3] Bypassing the chain of command - even once - damages trust in a way that is disproportionately hard to repair.

The MD relationship develops slowly. Most first-years barely interact with MDs for the first two or three months. When you do interact - on a client call, in a deal meeting - the rule is simple: be precise, be brief, do not volunteer opinions unless asked. Your function in MD interactions is to demonstrate competence through your materials, not through conversation.

One relationship that recruiting materials almost never mention is the staffer. The staffer is the person who controls deal allocation - who gets assigned to which projects. Wall Street Oasis practitioners are unambiguous on this point: the staffer is the single most important contact in months one through three.[^4] First staffings determine what goes on your resume. You want high-visibility M&A deals, not low-visibility administrative work. Build rapport with the staffer early. Communicate your availability clearly and accurately. This relationship matters more to your trajectory than almost any other in the building.

## Live Deals vs. Pitch Work: Why the Distinction Matters

In most coverage groups, your first months will involve a mix of pitch work and, if you are fortunate, a live deal or two. The distinction matters enormously.

Pitch work teaches you the mechanics of banking - how to build a comparable companies set, run a DCF, assemble a management presentation. It is technically valuable but relatively low-stakes. Pitches go to clients, clients may or may not engage, and the process moves on.

Live deals are different in kind. On a live M&A process or a capital markets transaction, the stakes are real. The client is paying. The counterparty is real. The timeline is fixed. You will be asked to turn models faster, with less margin for error, and with more senior eyes on every number. This is where you learn what banking actually is.

If you get staffed on a live deal in your first 90 days, treat it as significant exposure. Most analysts at large banks spend the first two to three months primarily on pitches before getting meaningful live deal work. Use that pitch period to achieve technical fluency in your group's model architecture. When the live deal comes, you want the mechanics to be automatic so your cognitive load can go toward judgment, not calculation.

## The Technical Floor: What You Need to Know by Day 90

By the end of your first 90 days, you should be functionally competent in a specific set of technical areas.

**Excel at speed, not just accuracy.** Financial-Modeling.com sets the standard plainly: dynamic named ranges, INDEX-MATCH, conditional formatting for error-checking, and clean handling of circular references are non-negotiable floor skills.[^3] The color convention used across the industry - blue for hardcoded inputs, black for formulas, green for external links - is not optional; it is a communication protocol that every senior banker assumes you understand. One decimal error in an enterprise value calculation can destroy credibility that took weeks to build.

**Your group's standard model templates.** Every group has them. Learn them cold before your first live deal, not during it.

**Basic deal process literacy.** In M&A, you should understand the sell-side process timeline, what a CIM contains and why, and how a management presentation differs from a banker pitch. In leveraged finance, you should understand credit agreement basics and what a syndication process involves.

**Clean, client-ready deck production.** This means your firm's design conventions, formatting shortcuts, and - critically - knowing how to proof your own work before it goes out the door. Wall Street Oasis practitioners recommend reviewing final materials as a PDF rather than in PowerPoint, because formatting artifacts and errors appear differently in the two formats.[^4] Before sending any attachment, open the PDF from your own outbox and review it one final time.

You will not be a technical expert at day 90. What you need to be is reliable - reliable on mechanics, reliable in communication, reliable in delivering what you said you would deliver when you said you would deliver it.

## The Rules That Do Not Appear in Any Training Deck

Financial-Modeling.com identifies three cultural rules that function as load-bearing pillars in banking and that no training program will state directly.[^3]

The first is: never blindside seniors. Flag issues early. Surface bad news proactively before it escalates into a crisis. A VP who learns about a model error from a client rather than from you has been blindsided. That does not get forgotten quickly.

The second is: accuracy before speed. A correct model delivered late is better than a flawed model delivered on time. When you have a timeline constraint, communicate it upfront and propose an alternative. Promising a deadline and missing it is a worse outcome than negotiating a later deadline at the outset.

The third is: the chain of command is not optional. Route everything through your Associate. This protects you, protects them, and signals cultural competence to everyone above them watching how you operate.

Beyond these three, Jobaaj Learnings' year-one survival guide identifies a set of soft skills that most incoming analysts systematically undervalue.[^5] Articulating complex financial concepts in plain language is not a presentation skill - it is what MDs actually want from junior analysts when they are on client calls. Collaborating without breaking under extreme time pressure is not a personality trait; it is a technical skill that needs to be built. And time management in banking is not a productivity habit - it is a survival mechanism that determines whether you can sustain performance across 70-85 hour weeks without your work quality degrading.

## Common First-Year Traps: A Verified List

The practitioners documented across WSO, eFinancialCareers, and Financial-Modeling.com converge on a consistent set of first-year failure modes.[^3][^4][^6]

**Technical traps:**
- Submitting models without complete verification - formulas unchecked, external links unvalidated
- Reviewing final pitch materials in PowerPoint rather than PDF
- Using hardcoded numbers in model cells without flagging them
- Weak Excel fundamentals below the expected floor: no INDEX-MATCH, no dynamic ranges, no circular reference handling

**Cultural and behavioral traps:**
- Hiding errors instead of disclosing them immediately to your Associate
- Bypassing the chain of command by emailing VPs or MDs without CCing the Associate
- Accepting more work than you can execute well, then underdelivering on all of it
- Going back to the person who assigned a task with basic questions before exhausting every other resource first - this signals incompetence to the exact person evaluating your work
- Throwing a peer under the bus to look good - this backfires permanently and is remembered for years inside the same firm
- Disrespecting administrative staff - senior bankers notice this and treat it as a disqualifying character flaw, not a minor lapse

**Communication traps:**
- Not reading full email chains before starting work, which leads to duplicated effort or directionally wrong output
- Failing to communicate workload capacity before accepting new assignments
- Over-explaining mistakes - senior bankers want the corrected version, not the backstory
- Taking feedback as personal criticism - defensiveness destroys the feedback loop that enables improvement

The eFinancialCareers practitioner list on first-week failures is worth keeping in mind throughout the full 90 days, not just week one.[^6] Delivering incorrect work is the worst possible outcome. Delivering late work is the second worst. Arriving late is perceived as a character flaw in banking culture, regardless of technical severity. Every one of these applies on day 87 as much as day 3.

## The Six-Month Wall and the Inflection Point Ahead

Your first 90 days do not exist in isolation. They are the foundation of a trajectory that plays out over two to two-and-a-half years, which is how long most analysts now take to reach the Associate level at major banks - compressed from the traditional three-year track.[^2]

Around month six, something shifts. According to Financial-Modeling.com's practitioner synthesis, this is when basic tasks become automatic for analysts who have survived intact.[^3] The signals are concrete: Associates begin requesting you specifically. You anticipate questions before they are asked. Model revisions drop because your output is clean on first delivery.

This is also when the mental toll becomes most acute. Jobaaj Learnings and WSO practitioners both document the reality: 70-85 hour weeks are standard, with deal sprints reaching 90-100+ hours.[^4][^5] The chronic sleep deprivation, the loss of interest in anything outside work, the physical symptoms - these are not outliers. They are documented patterns that the culture stigmatizes but that every practitioner acknowledges in retrospect.

The way through months three to six is to identify one or two things you can execute at a genuinely high level right now, and do them consistently. Early excellence in discrete tasks - an impeccable comp set, perfectly formatted pitch materials, zero-error data room organization - builds both confidence and reputation in a period when both are in short supply.

On exit planning: Mergers and Inquisitions data shows that PE mega-fund on-cycle recruiting starts during the analyst's first year, often as early as months six to twelve.[^2] The compensation premium is significant - analysts who exit to PE mega-funds after Year 2 earn $300K-$400K in total compensation, versus $165K-$225K all-in at bulge bracket banks (with elite boutiques paying $250K+ for top performers). If PE is your path, LBO modeling fluency needs to be in place before that recruiting window opens. The first 90 days are not too early to begin building toward it.

## The Social Layer: Navigating the Group Before You Have Standing

There is a social geography to every banking group that takes months to fully map. Read [how group politics actually work as an analyst](/recruiting-insider/group-politics-read-the-room/) for the full treatment, but here are the basics for the first 90 days.

You have no standing yet. That is a fact about how seniority-based organizations function, not a judgment on your capability. You earn standing through demonstrated competence over time, not through being personable or having a good story about your summer internship.

The right posture in the first 90 days is low ego, high output, high responsiveness. Respond to messages quickly, even if only to acknowledge receipt and commit to a follow-up time. Do not complain about hours, workload, or deal quality in any context where it could be overheard. Do not form strong alliances with other analysts before you understand the group dynamics - some analyst cohorts are competitive in ways that are not immediately visible.

Jobaaj Learnings recommends securing a mentor - a second-year analyst or junior Associate - within the first 30 days.[^5] The value is specific: a mentor navigates the unwritten rules, serves as a sounding board for situations that are genuinely ambiguous, and provides a reference for internal opportunities that you would otherwise not know exist. This is not networking in the transactional sense. It is a working relationship with someone whose experience is directly applicable to your current situation.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Before your start date, master your firm's Excel shortcuts.** Keyboard-only navigation at speed is not optional - reaching for the mouse signals slower hands and slower thinking to anyone watching. Practice until it is muscle memory.

2. **Map the real hierarchy in your first two weeks.** Identify your Associate, the VP who will touch your work most often, and any senior analyst who operates as an informal guide to the cohort. Understand what each of them cares about and where they have strong preferences on work product.

3. **Find your mentor in the first 30 days.** A second-year analyst or junior Associate who knows the group's unwritten rules is worth more than any formal training content delivered in week one.

4. **Build a personal error log from day one.** Every mistake - formatting, modeling, communication - goes in the log with the fix. Review it weekly. The goal is simple: never make the same mistake twice, because making it twice signals that the first time was not a learning event.

5. **Set expectations clearly on your first live deal.** When you get staffed on a transaction, ask your Associate for a 15-minute walkthrough of the deal context, the key stakeholders, and current priorities. Do this in the first 24 hours. Do not wait to absorb it through osmosis.

6. **Start building toward your exit path by month six.** If PE is the target, LBO modeling fluency is required before on-cycle recruiting begins. If corporate development or VC is the target, the internal networking and deal exposure you pursue in year one shapes what is on your resume when those conversations start.

The first 90 days are not designed to be enjoyable. They are designed to filter. The analysts who get through them with their reputation intact and their technical skills improving are not the ones who worked hardest in an absolute sense - they are the ones who directed their effort correctly, communicated accurately, and built trust with the people closest to their work.

Everything that follows in a banking career - the exit opportunities, the compensation trajectory, the group placements that shape what you can actually do - traces back to those first 90 days and whether you treated them as the foundation-laying exercise they actually are.
