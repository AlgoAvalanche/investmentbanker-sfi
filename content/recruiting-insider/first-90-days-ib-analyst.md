# What Actually Happens in Your First 90 Days as an IB Analyst

The first 90 days analyst experience in investment banking is the most disorienting professional stretch most people will ever have. You arrive having aced every case interview, memorized three accounting frameworks, and convinced yourself you are prepared. You are not. Nobody is. The gap between what banks tell you during recruiting and what the first three months actually feel like is wide enough to drive a leveraged buyout through.

This is not a scare piece. It is a map. Here is what actually happens, week by week, and more importantly, how to not embarrass yourself while it does.

---

## The Training Period: Useful, But Not in the Way You Think

Most bulge brackets and upper-middle-market banks run formal training programs ranging from two weeks to eight weeks, depending on the firm. Goldman, JPMorgan, and Morgan Stanley run longer programs that include technical content, compliance training, and rotational exposure. Boutiques often compress this to a week or two of slides and an Excel crash course before throwing you on a live deal.

Wall Street Oasis forums are full of first-years who discovered during training that the technical content they spent six months practicing was actually not the hard part. The hard part is learning how to behave.

Training teaches you the firm's Excel conventions, its model templates, its formatting standards, and - if you pay close attention - the unspoken hierarchy. Watch who the trainers defer to. Watch which questions get answered and which get politely sidestepped. The political texture of a firm is legible during training if you know what to look for.

From a practical standpoint: use training to memorize the firm's specific templates. If your bank uses a particular LBO shell or a specific DCF format, internalize it completely. You will never have time to learn it from scratch once you are on a live deal.

## Weeks One Through Four on the Desk: The Fog of War

When you land on your group desk after training, expect a few days of relative quiet. Your associate or VP is sizing you up. They are figuring out what to assign you and how much supervision you will need.

Then a request comes in at 9:45 PM on a Tuesday. A pitch needs to be updated for a 7 AM call. Welcome to the job.

The first month is characterized by a particular kind of cognitive overload that experienced bankers describe, but junior analysts cannot fully anticipate. You are simultaneously learning:

- The firm's proprietary model architecture
- Your group's specific deal conventions and preferred formatting
- The names, preferences, and working styles of every senior person you support
- The actual content of whatever transactions your group is working on
- The unwritten rules of communication - when to email, when to Slack, when to walk to someone's desk

Mergers & Inquisitions has documented this extensively, and their assessment matches what practitioners report: the first month is largely about surviving intact rather than excelling. You will make formatting errors. You will miss a cell reference. You will send a version of a deck that has a typo in a number. These things happen to every first-year. The question is whether you catch them yourself or whether a VP catches them at 11 PM and has to explain to you why double-checking your own work is, in fact, your job.

The single most valuable skill in your first four weeks is not technical. It is the ability to clarify scope before starting work and confirm direction before delivering. Ask: "I want to make sure I'm building what you need - are you looking for X or Y?" before spending four hours going the wrong direction. This is not weakness. This is operational efficiency.

## The Hierarchy: Understanding Who Actually Runs Your Life

The org chart at a bank looks clean. MD at the top, then SVP or Director, then VP, then Associate, then Analyst. Reality is more nuanced, and your first 90 days are largely an exercise in mapping the actual power structure beneath the formal one.

Your Associate is your most important relationship in year one. Not the MD. Not the VP. Your Associate. They are the person who will assign your work, review your output first, decide whether to escalate your mistakes, and determine whether your life is tolerable or not. Invest heavily in this relationship early.

That said, every group has at least one VP who has strong views about how models should be built and will not hesitate to tell you, in detail, what you did wrong. Find this person in your first two weeks. Buy them coffee. Ask them their preferences directly. The information you get will save you dozens of painful redos later.

The MD relationship develops slowly. Most first-years barely interact with MDs for the first two or three months. When you do interact - on a client call, on a live deal - the rule is simple: be precise, be brief, and do not volunteer opinions unless asked. Your job in MD interactions is to demonstrate competence through your materials, not through conversation.

## First 90 Days Analyst Reality: Live Deals vs. Pitch Work

In most coverage groups, your first months will involve a mix of pitch work and, if you are fortunate, a live deal or two. The distinction matters enormously for your development.

**Pitch work** teaches you the mechanics of banking - how to build a comp set, run a DCF, put together a management presentation. It is technically valuable but relatively low-stakes. Pitches get sent to clients, the client may or may not be interested, and the world moves on.

**Live deals** are different in kind. On a live M&A process or a capital markets transaction, the stakes are real. The client is paying. The counterparty is real. The timeline is not flexible. You will be asked to turn models faster, with less margin for error, and with more senior eyes on every number. This is where you learn what banking actually is.

A common WSO thread from a former Goldman analyst captures the first live deal experience well: "I had done fifty pitch models by then. I thought I knew how to build a comp set. Then my associate handed me a real CIM at 6 PM and said the client call was at 8 AM and I needed a full operating model by then. That was the moment I understood what training was actually preparing me for."

If you get staffed on a live deal in your first 90 days, treat it as luck. Most analysts at large banks spend the first two to three months primarily on pitches before getting meaningful live deal exposure.

## The Social Layer: Navigating the Group Before You Have Standing

There is a social geography to every banking group that takes several months to understand fully. Read [how group politics actually work as an analyst](/recruiting-insider/group-politics-read-the-room/) for the full treatment, but here are the basics for your first 90 days.

You have no standing yet. That is not a criticism - it is a fact about how seniority-based organizations work. You earn standing through demonstrated competence over time, not through being personable or having a good story about your summer internship.

In the first 90 days, the right posture is: low ego, high output, high responsiveness. This means:

- Respond to messages quickly, even if just to say you received them and will follow up
- Do not complain about hours, workload, or deal quality in any context where it could be overheard
- Do not form strong alliances with other analysts before you understand the group dynamics - some analyst cohorts are competitive in ways that are not immediately obvious
- Show up to anything optional that senior bankers attend, especially in the first month

This does not mean being a sycophant. It means being professionally disciplined in a period where your reputation is being formed and you have little leverage to correct a bad first impression.

## The Technical Ramp: What You Actually Need to Know by Day 90

By the end of your first 90 days, you should be functionally competent in:

**Excel mechanics at speed.** The BIWS (Breaking Into Wall Street) benchmark is useful here - you should be able to build a three-statement model from scratch without referencing notes, and navigate a complex spreadsheet using keyboard shortcuts almost exclusively. Reaching for the mouse is a tell.

**Your group's standard model templates.** Every group has them. Learn them cold.

**Basic deal process literacy.** If you are in M&A, you should understand the sell-side process timeline, what a CIM contains and why, and how a management presentation differs from a banker pitch. If you are in leveraged finance, you should understand credit agreement basics, pricing mechanics, and what a syndication process looks like.

**How to build a clean, client-ready deck.** This means understanding your firm's design conventions, knowing how to use formatting shortcuts, and - critically - knowing how to proofread your own work before it goes out the door.

You will not be a technical wizard at day 90. That is fine. What you need to be is reliable. Reliable at the mechanics, reliable in your communication, reliable in your delivery of what you said you would deliver when you said you would deliver it.

## Common Traps in the First Three Months

See the full [top 10 mistakes analysts make in year one](/recruiting-insider/top-10-mistakes-analysts/) for a comprehensive breakdown, but the first-90-days-specific traps are worth noting here.

**Over-explaining your mistakes.** When you make an error - and you will - fix it first, then provide a brief explanation. Do not send a paragraph justifying why the error happened. Senior bankers do not want the backstory. They want the correct version.

**Asking the same question twice.** Write down everything. If an associate explains a model convention to you, document it. Asking the same procedural question twice signals that you are not learning from the process.

**Confusing effort with output.** Working until 3 AM does not earn you goodwill if the work product is sloppy. Late hours are a baseline expectation, not a credit. The credit comes from what you produce, not from how long you were in the office.

**Overclaiming your availability.** Some analysts in their first weeks tell every banker in the group they are available for anything, then get stretched thin and underdeliver everywhere. Better to be honest about your current workload and deliver well on what you take on.

## The Emotional Reality Nobody Talks About Openly

The first 90 days analyst experience has a psychological dimension that recruiting materials and training seminars do not address. A significant number of analysts describe a period - usually somewhere between weeks three and eight - where the novelty has worn off, the hours have accumulated, and the work feels relentless without yet feeling meaningful.

This is normal. It is not a signal that banking is wrong for you. It is the valley between competence-through-preparation and competence-through-experience. You are not yet fast enough to do the work without strain, and you are not yet senior enough to see the larger strategic context that makes individual tasks feel significant.

The way through this period is relatively simple: find one or two things you can do exceptionally well right now and do them consistently. Whether that is building an impeccable comp set or delivering perfectly formatted pitch materials, early excellence in discrete tasks builds the confidence and the reputation that carries you forward.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Before your start date, master your firm's Excel shortcuts.** Get the BIWS Excel course or equivalent and practice until keyboard-only navigation is muscle memory. This single investment will save you hundreds of hours over two years.

2. **Map the hierarchy in your first two weeks.** Identify your Associate, the VP who will touch your work most, and any senior analyst who acts as informal mentors in your cohort. Understand what each of them cares about and what makes them difficult.

3. **Build a personal error log.** Every time you make a mistake - formatting, modeling, communication - write it down with the fix. Review it weekly. The goal is never to make the same mistake twice.

4. **Set expectations clearly on your first live deal.** When you get staffed on a transaction, ask your associate for a 15-minute walkthrough of the deal context, the key stakeholders, and the current priorities. Do this in the first 24 hours. Do not wait to figure it out through osmosis.

5. **Read the room on culture fit.** Some groups run hot - high pressure, limited patience, transactional relationships. Others are collegial and invest in analyst development. Figure out which type of group you are in by day 30, and calibrate your approach accordingly. Treating a high-pressure group like a mentorship program will not end well.

The first 90 days are not meant to be enjoyed. They are meant to be survived in a way that sets you up for the two years that follow. If you get through them with your reputation intact and your skills improving, you are doing it right.
