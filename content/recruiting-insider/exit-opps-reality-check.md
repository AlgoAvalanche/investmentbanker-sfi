# Exit Opps Reality Check: PE, HF, Corp Dev, Startups

The exit opportunities narrative is central to the investment banking recruiting pitch. "Two years of banking opens every door." This is partially true and significantly overstated, depending on which door you want to open, where you banked, and what you built during your time there.

The exit opportunities landscape after banking is real, valuable, and more competitive than the recruiting pitch implies. Here is what each major path actually looks like, what it takes to access it, and what the lived experience on the other side delivers relative to what analysts expect.

---

## Private Equity: The Default Exit That Is Not Actually Default

Private equity is the exit that defines how most M&A and LBO-focused analysts think about their time in banking. It is also the exit that is most poorly understood, primarily because the narrative is dominated by the megafund track while the actual market is much broader.

**The megafund track (KKR, Blackstone, Apollo, Carlyle, TPG, Warburg)** is what analysts usually mean when they say "PE." These roles are genuinely exceptional - the compensation, the deal complexity, the portfolio company exposure, and the long-term career trajectory are among the best available. They are also ferociously competitive and increasingly concentrated among a small number of analyst cohorts.

The on-cycle recruiting process for megafunds now begins - routinely - before analysts have been in their seat for twelve months. Headhunters approach analysts from target banks (Goldman Sachs IBD, Morgan Stanley M&A, Evercore M&A, Lazard M&A) within their first six months. The process has compressed to the point where analysts are making career-defining decisions based on twelve months of experience and one or two deals.

The practical result: analysts from bulge bracket M&A and elite boutique practices have a legitimate shot at megafund roles. Analysts from coverage groups at mid-tier banks, from product groups with lower LBO deal flow, or from regional offices are competing for a different set of PE opportunities - mid-market funds, upper-middle-market funds, growth equity - that are excellent but different from the megafund narrative.

For a strong understanding of what PE firms are actually looking for technically, see the [LBO modeling framework from the sponsor perspective](/technical/lbo-modeling-how-sponsors-think/). The ability to build a clean LBO and articulate an investment thesis is the non-negotiable technical floor for any PE interview.

**What PE actually delivers:**
- Year one PE associate compensation: $200,000-$300,000+ all-in at mid-to-large funds
- Hours: 60-80 per week at most funds - a genuine improvement over banking but not a lifestyle transformation
- Intellectual content: significantly higher than banking, especially if you get portfolio company work
- Path to carry: real but long. Carried interest becomes meaningful at the VP level and above, which is seven to ten years out

**The honest caveat:** PE associates who expected the investment analyst role - originating deals, building theses, portfolio management - often find that their first two years as an associate are extension of the execution work they did in banking, just on the buy side instead of the sell side. The intellectual step-change comes at the VP level.

## Hedge Funds: Higher Ceiling, Narrower Path

Hedge fund exits from banking are genuinely attractive but require a different skill profile than PE and are available to a narrower slice of the banking analyst population.

The funds that recruit most actively from banking - L/S equity funds, event-driven funds, credit funds - want analysts who can generate investment ideas, not just execute transactions. This is a meaningful distinction. Banking trains you to execute and facilitate; hedge funds need you to originate and evaluate. The crossover skills are real but the gap is also real.

**Who accesses HF exits well:**
- Analysts from equity capital markets or equity research with genuine interest generation experience
- Analysts from M&A who have developed clear investment opinions about the sectors they covered
- Analysts with demonstrated interest in markets - people who followed specific companies and sectors before and during banking, not just as background research for pitches

**The lifestyle pitch:** Hedge funds are often sold as a lifestyle improvement over banking. This is true for most funds and deeply false for some. Pods within large multi-manager platforms (Citadel, Millennium, Point72) can be as intense as banking and carry significant performance pressure that is different in character but not lighter in weight. Smaller fundamental funds are generally more reasonable on hours. The variance is wide.

**Compensation:** Variable in a way that banking is not. Analysts at successful funds can earn $300,000-$500,000+ all-in within three to four years. Analysts at underperforming funds earn their base and not much else, and then look for their next role. The upside is real; so is the downside risk.

**The reality check from WSO:** A frequently cited thread from a former Goldman analyst who moved to a multi-manager platform in 2022 put it plainly: "I went from worrying about whether the VP liked my deck formatting to worrying about whether my positions would perform. The stress is different, not less. I prefer it - but I want to be honest that it's not a relaxing transition."

## Corporate Development: The Underrated Exit

Corporate development roles at large companies are consistently undervalued in the analyst exit narrative because they lack the brand name cachet of PE or the upside potential of HF. This is a mistake.

Corp dev at a large company - a Fortune 500, a fast-growing mid-cap, or a well-capitalized pre-IPO company - offers the most direct translation of banking skills into a context where you have genuine strategic authority and a visible path to CFO or COO.

**What corp dev actually involves:**
- M&A execution from the acquirer's perspective: sourcing, evaluation, diligence, negotiation, integration
- Strategic planning inputs, especially in companies with active M&A agendas
- Regular interaction with senior leadership in a way that analysts at banks rarely get

**The lifestyle differential is real.** Corp dev at most large companies is 50-60 hours per week, with genuine nights and weekends off outside of active deal periods. The compensation step-down from PE is real: corp dev at large companies pays $150,000-$200,000 all-in for a banking analyst joining at the analyst or associate equivalent level. But the quality of life difference is substantial and the equity component - especially at growth-stage companies where stock grants are meaningful - can partially offset the cash difference.

**Where corp dev consistently delivers:** If you want to operate a business rather than advise on or invest in businesses, corp dev is the best bridge. The analysts who flourish here are those who found the client-service aspect of banking more frustrating than motivating and want to own outcomes rather than support others' decision-making.

## Startups and Tech: The Lifestyle Exit That Is Not Actually a Lifestyle Exit

The startup exit from banking has grown significantly in popularity over the past decade, particularly among analysts who want equity upside and a culture shift. The reality is more complicated than the LinkedIn posts suggest.

**What you get:**
- Genuine responsibility earlier than in any other post-banking path
- Equity that may be worth nothing, worth a lot, or worth something in between
- Culture that is different from banking but not necessarily easier
- A faster feedback loop on whether you are actually good at building things

**What you give up:**
- The banking peer network, which has significant long-term value
- The structured development environment - startups do not invest in your growth systematically
- Compensation certainty - early-stage startups often pay $100,000-$130,000 base plus equity that is effectively illiquid

**The honest assessment from practice:** Banking analysts who join Series A and B stage companies in finance, strategy, or business development roles tend to have one of two outcomes. Either the company grows and their equity becomes valuable and their role expands dramatically, or the company struggles and they find themselves in a role with limited deal exposure, no clear promotion path, and a resume question mark that makes the next move harder.

The analysts who navigate startup exits best are those who join companies with specific strategic reasons, not those who join primarily to escape banking. The escape motive produces poor startup choices.

## The Exit Opportunity Matrix: Matching Path to Profile

Not all banking experience translates equally to all exits. A rough mapping:

**M&A / LBO-heavy coverage groups** - best preparation for PE, serviceable for corp dev, moderate for HF, applicable to startups
**Equity capital markets** - best preparation for HF (equity-focused), good for corp dev, limited for traditional PE
**Leveraged finance / debt advisory** - excellent for credit HF and credit PE, good for corp dev, moderate for traditional PE
**Restructuring** - excellent for distressed credit, good for operational PE, excellent for corp dev at distressed companies
**Industry coverage without heavy modeling** - weaker for PE/HF, good for corp dev in the sector, depends heavily on what you built

The practical implication: your exit options are shaped significantly by which group you are in and what work you actually did, not just by which bank your business card says. An analyst at a bulge bracket who spent two years in a relationship-heavy coverage role with minimal modeling exposure will have a harder PE exit than an analyst from a mid-market bank who built LBO models on every transaction.

## The Timing Question: On-Cycle vs. Off-Cycle

PE exit timing has become a source of significant stress for banking analysts, and for good reason.

**On-cycle recruiting** for megafunds happens absurdly early - often before month twelve. The process is run by headhunters who manage relationships with target funds, and the recruiting windows are short. If you miss on-cycle for your target funds, you are not necessarily out of options, but you are on a different track.

**Off-cycle recruiting** - for smaller funds, funds that do not participate in on-cycle, and roles that open organically - runs throughout the year and tends to favor candidates with more experience and more deal completions. Off-cycle is less frenetic and often produces better matching between analyst and fund.

**The strategic question:** Many analysts chase on-cycle aggressively and take the first offer they get from a recognizable fund name, rather than waiting for a better-matched opportunity off-cycle. Whether this is the right call depends on your risk tolerance, your financial situation, and how central the specific fund's reputation is to your long-term goals.

## What Exit Opportunities Actually Look Like Five Years Out

The exit opportunity narrative focuses on the immediate post-banking move. The more interesting question is where people are five to seven years out from their banking analyst year.

The consistent finding across informal surveys and alumni networks: the specific first exit matters less than the quality of experience and development at each stage. Analysts who went to mid-market PE and built genuine operational skills frequently outperform those who went to megafunds and did execution work for three years. Analysts who went to well-run corp dev teams and got promoted into strategic leadership roles frequently do as well financially as those who went to PE when you account for equity realization timelines.

The banking two-year investment creates optionality. What you do with that optionality over the following five years determines the actual outcome.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Identify your target exit before you start banking.** Not as a rigid plan but as a research direction. Knowing you want PE versus corp dev versus HF changes which deals to prioritize, which modeling skills to build, and which relationships to invest in during your banking years.

2. **Map your group to its exit pipeline.** Ask specifically: "Where did analysts from this group go over the last three years?" The answer tells you more than any general statement about the bank's PE placement record.

3. **Build LBO modeling competency regardless of group.** Even if your group is not LBO-heavy, this is the technical language PE firms speak. The [LBO modeling framework from the sponsor perspective](/technical/lbo-modeling-how-sponsors-think/) is the right place to start.

4. **Develop real opinions about the sectors you cover.** Whether your exit target is PE or HF, the ability to say "here is why I think this sector trades at this multiple and here is what I think the market is missing" is the difference between a candidate who gets offers and one who does not.

5. **Read [why analysts quit](/recruiting-insider/why-analysts-quit/) and [the honest IB lifestyle breakdown](/recruiting-insider/honest-truth-ib-lifestyle/) alongside this.** Exit opportunities are the benefit side of the banking trade-off equation. The cost side is real and deserves equal analysis before you commit.
