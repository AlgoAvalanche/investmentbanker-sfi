# Why Analysts Quit (And What Banks Don't Want You to Know Before You Sign)

Banks spend considerable resources on analyst recruiting. They spend considerably less on understanding why analysts quit - and even less on telling recruits the honest version before they sign. The attrition numbers are not hidden, but they are not exactly featured in the offer letter either.

The global annual attrition rate in investment banking runs 20-25%, with peaks concentrated in junior roles.[^1] In some private-sector investment banks in India and other emerging markets, that number climbs to 30-40%.[^1] According to practitioner estimates compiled on Wall Street Oasis, most shops internally expect 33-50% of their analyst cohort to burn out within the standard two-year program.[^2] When on-cycle private equity recruiting fires aggressively in the first year - as it now does at the largest funds - those numbers can run even higher in specific cohorts.

The question recruits rarely ask before signing, and rarely get answered honestly if they do ask: why, exactly, do analysts quit? The reasons break into distinct categories. Some are predictable from the outside. Others are almost impossible to anticipate without having lived them. Understanding the pattern before you commit is worth more than any amount of preparation once you are already in the seat.

---

## The Hours Are Not What You Think - Even If You Know the Number

This sounds obvious. It is not. Most analysts who leave banking citing hours are not leaving because they underestimated the hour count. The number 80-100 hours per week is widely published and widely discussed. The Mergers and Inquisitions analyst job description guide notes that analysts spend 70-85 hours per week in the office but are effectively on call 24/7.[^3] Elite boutiques - Lazard, Evercore, Centerview - push analysts to 80-90 hours per week. Regional boutiques run closer to 50-70.[^4]

The 2024 Wall Street Oasis Work Conditions Survey, which collected responses from 531 bankers between October 2024 and January 2025, found that 62% of respondents said work hours had negatively affected their relationships with family and friends, with 22% reporting a measurable decline in mental health and 26% in physical health.[^4]

Those numbers are the aggregate. The Goldman Sachs data from 2021 is sharper. Thirteen first-year analysts documented their conditions in a widely circulated internal presentation. They reported averaging 98 hours per week, with one peak week reaching 105 hours. The average nightly sleep: 5 hours. Mental health scores before joining Goldman averaged 9.0 out of 10. After less than a year in the role, that score had dropped to 2.8 - a decline of 6.2 points. Physical health went from 8.8 to 2.3.[^5] One analyst wrote: "I can't sleep anymore because my anxiety levels are through the roof." Another described their body as physically hurting "all the time." A third used the word "inhumane." Every single respondent reported facing unrealistic deadlines. Seventy-seven percent reported experiencing workplace abuse - being shouted at, sworn at, publicly criticized, or shunned.[^5]

For context: the average American worker logs 34.6 hours per week. These analysts were working nearly three times that, with no predictability about when demands would arrive.[^5]

What the hour count does not capture is the texture of the time. There is a meaningful difference between intellectually understanding you will work 90 hours and experiencing what 90 hours feels like across a sustained period when a significant portion of those hours are reactive, fragmented, and occur at midnight on a deal timeline you do not control. The waiting hours - where you cannot leave but cannot focus on anything else because you are holding for MD feedback on a deck - may be worse than the actual late nights working. The inability to plan or control your time is qualitatively different from the hour count alone.

Mergers and Inquisitions describes this cycle precisely: midway through every year, a whisper starts growing among analysts about leaving, driven not by one bad week but by the sustained, uncontrollable nature of the demands.[^3] The post-bonus window from January to March is the most common exit point - after the year-end payout clears, the calculus tips.

## Culture Problems You Could Not Have Detected in Interviews

Banking culture varies dramatically across groups at the same bank, and almost none of that variation is visible during the interview process. An analyst who joins a group with an abusive senior or a culture of blame-shifting has essentially no recourse and limited ability to transfer in the first year.

The Goldman 2021 data is the starkest evidence of how systemic this can be: 77% of survey respondents acknowledged experiencing workplace abuse.[^5] This is not an outlier figure. Wall Street Oasis forum threads document shouting, public humiliation, and favoritism as endemic patterns across firms and cohorts.[^2] The hierarchical structure means junior staff absorb all negative consequences with limited recourse upward.

The UpSlide Investment Banking Burnout Report, based on surveys of 200-plus finance professionals, found that only 43% of IB employees feel satisfied with existing workplace support systems, and 96% want additional support structures implemented.[^6] Burnout is the single top cause of banker attrition in that data set.[^6] Fifty-one percent of respondents were aware that colleagues were actively planning to leave.[^6]

The most damaging version of culture dysfunction - documented in multiple first-person accounts on finance forums - is what practitioners call "black hole staffing": an analyst is assigned almost entirely to one demanding senior banker, becomes unavailable to the rest of the group, develops a narrow skill set, and exits with weaker credentials than peers who got broader exposure. The senior banker gets efficient support. The analyst gets limited development and a thin deal sheet. This pattern is not malicious - it is a misaligned incentive structure. But for the analyst, the result is the same.

Groups with high analyst turnover tend to have this problem. Before you join any group, ask analysts who recently left what the staffing pattern was like and whether they felt they got broad deal exposure or were consistently siloed.

## Administrative Work That Eats the Job Alive

Analysts join banking expecting to do high-level financial work on important transactions. The UpSlide survey data introduces a number that rarely appears in recruiting materials: bankers spend up to 40 hours per week on manual, administrative tasks - roughly half their working week.[^6] Formatting pitch books, updating buyer and seller lists, making repetitive revisions to decks, and managing process logistics are not incidental. They are the plurality of the job.

The UpSlide report estimates these manual tasks cost firms approximately $2,000 per full-time employee per week in productivity.[^6] When asked what they would prefer to redirect that time toward, bankers cited client-facing work (25%), strategic tasks (23%), and creative or ideation work (23%).[^6] Ninety percent said document automation in Microsoft 365 would benefit them. Under 30% of banks are currently implementing any meaningful productivity or workflow improvements.[^6]

The gap between what analysts expected to do and what the job actually requires is a primary driver of disillusionment. This is not a deficiency in banking - the analyst role is explicitly an apprenticeship in execution, not strategy. The strategic dimension comes with seniority. But for analysts who joined expecting something closer to a consulting analyst role in its intellectual dimension, the realization that they are primarily a production resource in years one and two can be a significant disappointment that compounds weekly across an 80-hour schedule.

## Private Equity Recruiting Pulls Analysts Out Before Their Time

This category of departure is distinct from burnout and culture-driven exits. Analysts recruited aggressively by private equity firms in the on-cycle process - which at top funds now starts as early as month ten of the analyst's first year - often leave before their program ends to begin their associate role.

From the analyst's perspective, this is not an attrition problem. It is the intended pipeline working correctly. The analyst joined banking knowing or hoping PE would follow, prepared for on-cycle, received offers, and accepted. From the bank's perspective, they lose a trained analyst earlier than expected, which is a real cost - replacement runs 30-50% of the departing employee's annual salary.[^1]

The CaMonk data reinforces the directionality: tech, startup, and consulting sectors are primary poaching grounds alongside PE, offering comparable pay with better hours and culture.[^1] Goldman Sachs data indicates that 75% of junior bankers work 80-plus hours weekly - and those same analysts are being actively recruited by firms that will offer them better conditions.[^1] Sixty percent of bankers under 30 see no five-year future in the field.[^1]

The complication for analysts is timing. Leaving at month fourteen provides solid technical fundamentals but incomplete judgment. Analysts who exit before completing at least one full deal cycle have a harder time telling a coherent story in PE and hedge fund interviews. The funds that recruit aggressively in on-cycle know this and plan to develop candidates further in-house - they are buying technical skills and brand-name training, not complete professionals. Whether early exit is the right move depends heavily on the specific opportunity. A megafund exit from a top group is genuinely career-defining. Mid-market PE at month fourteen for the sake of leaving is not obviously better than staying to two years and exiting with a stronger credential.

## Health and Relationship Costs That Cross a Point of No Return

No bank tells you in recruiting that a meaningful percentage of analysts develop significant health problems during their two years. The Goldman data makes this concrete: physical health scores declining from 8.8 to 2.3 within a single year is not anecdote, it is a documented measurement of damage.[^5]

Sleep deprivation at sustained 5-hour averages causes measurable cognitive impairment and physical degradation. Sedentary work combined with poor nutrition and chronic stress compounds across eighteen months in ways that a 22-year-old new to the workforce does not fully model in advance. The relationship costs run parallel. Long-term partnerships end during analyst years. Analysts miss significant family events. Non-banking friendships attenuate because a 90-hour unpredictable schedule is genuinely incompatible with maintaining social connections outside the office.

A Bank of America associate's death in May 2024 triggered the latest round of industry-wide conversation about these conditions - and the most concrete policy responses yet.[^7] JPMorgan implemented an 80-hour weekly cap with "pencils down" windows from Friday evening to Saturday noon, except during live deals. Goldman Sachs introduced "Protected Saturday" from Friday evening to Sunday morning. Bank of America enforced stricter timekeeping with consequences for violations.[^7]

The critical phrase in all of those policies is "except during live deals." The protections collapse precisely when work is heaviest. The 2024 WSO survey found that 68% of bankers believe conditions have improved over the past year - but 24% disagree, and the structural problem of unpredictable live-deal demands remains unresolved.[^4]

For analysts considering their personal risk profile: the most vulnerable profiles are those with pre-existing commitments that banking is particularly hard on - serious relationships with partners on normal schedules, aging family members requiring attention, or health baselines that deteriorate under sustained stress and sleep deprivation. None of this is disqualifying. People with all of these variables complete banking programs every year. But knowing your risk factors before you start allows you to make the trade-off deliberately rather than hitting a threshold at month fifteen without a plan.

## What Banks Actually Know - And What They Do With It

Large banks have analytics teams that study attrition. They know the numbers. The institutional response has been a mix of compensation increases and cultural initiatives with uneven results.

The compensation response was more effective than the cultural one. Analyst pay rose significantly across the industry in 2021 and 2022 in direct response to documented dissatisfaction and the viral Goldman survey. In 2026, total compensation for banking analysts runs $165,000-$225,000 (base of $100,000-$125,000 plus a bonus between 0.5x and 1.0x base).[^3] That increase is partially a market-clearing response to supply and demand - and partially hazard pay in response to documented conditions. Both things are true simultaneously.

The industry's structural answer - positioning AI research and workflow tools as a way to reduce manual hours while maintaining productivity - is real but incremental. AlphaSense and comparable platforms can reduce early-stage due diligence from hours to approximately 15 minutes, with reported time savings of 50-75% on specific research tasks.[^7] But those tools address one part of the manual-work problem. They do not address the cultural hierarchy, the unpredictability of demands, or the structural design of the analyst role as a temporary production resource rather than a developing professional.

Under 30% of banks are currently implementing productivity or workflow improvements at any meaningful scale.[^6] The stated policies and the actual conditions remain separated by the live-deal carve-outs that make the rules functionally optional when they matter most.

The 2024 WSO survey identified culture as the primary driver of talent retention across a survey of 2,800 banking professionals - above compensation, above flexibility, above stated growth potential.[^4] Banks know this. Their response has been faster on the compensation lever because compensation is measurable, deployable, and does not require changing how senior bankers manage their juniors. Cultural change requires changing how senior bankers manage their juniors, which is a much harder institutional problem.

## Eight Mistakes Analysts Make When They Decide to Leave

The decision to leave is one thing. Executing it well is another. The most common errors, drawn from practitioner accounts and forum post-mortems:

**Announcing before having a signed offer.** Banks terminate access the same day a resignation is announced - departing employees are walked out immediately due to confidential data concerns.[^3] Announcing without a secured position leaves analysts stranded with no income and no leverage.

**Telling colleagues before management.** Office gossip reaches management fast. Once word circulates informally, control of the timeline and the narrative is gone. The conversation with your direct MD, delivered in person, should be the first conversation - not the last.

**Quitting after a bad week rather than a bad year.** Burnout cycles mean some periods are qualitatively worse than others. Resignations made during a live-deal crunch often look different two months later. The benchmark is the sustained pattern, not the worst month.

**Missing the buyside recruiting window.** PE and hedge fund firms recruit on a narrow, process-driven timeline. Quitting too early closes the on-cycle window permanently. Quitting too late without having engaged the process means starting a job search from zero.

**Conflating burnout with wrong career.** Some analysts need to leave banking entirely. Others need a different group, a different bank, or a different deal type. Not diagnosing which is which leads to lateral moves that replicate the same problems in a new seat.

**Attempting to negotiate at resignation.** Counter-offers and renegotiations at the point of handing in notice are almost universally ineffective and damage the professional relationship regardless of outcome.

**Leaving before closing a transaction.** Analysts who exit without at least one closed deal on their record have a harder time constructing a coherent story in buyside interviews. Timing the exit after at least one closed transaction materially strengthens positioning.

**Burning the exit relationship.** The IB network is small and has a long memory. MDs and VPs who were difficult managers are often LPs, clients, or references five years after departure. The exit conversation should be professional regardless of what the working relationship looked like.

## What This Means for Your Decision

Understanding why analysts quit is useful in three specific ways.

First, it helps you assess your personal risk factors. The culture-sensitivity profile, health vulnerabilities, and relationship commitments that appear in the high-attrition segments are identifiable in advance. If they apply to you, planning for them before you arrive is worth more than any amount of mental preparation after.

Second, it redirects your pre-commitment research toward the right level. Culture is a group-level variable, not a firm-level one. Detailed research into specific group cultures - which requires conversations with former analysts who have left, not with recruiters - is the most valuable diligence you can do before signing.

Third, it puts the compensation in context. The pay is real and significant. It is also partially structured to compensate for conditions that produce attrition at rates the industry considers acceptable. Knowing that makes the trade-off cleaner to evaluate on its actual terms.

## What to Do Before You Sign or Before You Leave

**Ask the attrition question directly.** Before joining any group, ask: "What percentage of the analyst class from two years ago is still at the bank?" Deflection is also an answer.

**Talk to departed analysts, not current ones.** Current analysts have strong incentives to present their experience positively. Analysts who have already left - particularly those who left before their program ended - will give you the more useful data.

**Assess your own risk profile against the categories above.** Health, relationships, intellectual expectations, culture sensitivity: which of these apply to you? Being honest about this before you start is worth considerably more than discovering it at month ten.

**Read the [full investment banking lifestyle breakdown](/recruiting-insider/honest-truth-ib-lifestyle/)** for the complete picture - hours, pay, and how the culture calculus works day to day.

**Have an explicit exit timeline in your head before you start.** Analysts who know they are targeting PE at month twenty-two and have built a preparation plan for that exit are more resilient than analysts who entered without a specific post-banking goal. The tunnel needs a visible end for most people to navigate it without losing the thread.
