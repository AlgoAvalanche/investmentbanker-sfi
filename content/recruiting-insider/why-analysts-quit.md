# Why Analysts Quit (And What Banks Don't Want You to Know Before You Sign)

Banks spend considerable resources on analyst recruiting. They spend considerably less on understanding why analysts quit - and even less on telling recruits the honest version before they sign. The attrition numbers are not hidden, but they are not exactly featured in the offer letter either.

The reality: somewhere between 20% and 35% of investment banking analysts leave before completing their standard two-year program, depending on the bank, the year, and the market environment. In the most active recruiting years for private equity - when on-cycle PE recruiting kicks off aggressively in the first year - that number can be higher in specific cohorts.

Why analysts quit breaks down into several distinct categories, each worth understanding separately. If you are deciding whether to take an offer, or already in a seat wondering how long to stay, the pattern of departures is informative.

---

## Why Analysts Quit Reason One: The Hours Are Not What They Expected

This sounds obvious. It is not. Most analysts who leave banking citing hours are not leaving because they underestimated the hour count per se - the number 80-100 hours per week is widely published and widely discussed. They are leaving because the texture of those hours was different than they imagined.

There is a meaningful difference between intellectually understanding that you will work 90 hours and experiencing what 90 hours per week feels like across a sustained period when a significant portion of those hours are reactive, fragmented, and occur at midnight on a deal timeline you do not control. The cognitive load is different. The social cost - missing birthdays, declining invitations, being unavailable for anything outside work - accumulates differently than the abstract hour count suggests.

Wall Street Oasis threads on this topic are extensive. A representative one from a former Morgan Stanley analyst in 2023: "I knew the hours going in. What I didn't know was that I would spend two hours every evening waiting to hear whether the MD wanted changes to the deck. That waiting time - where you can't leave but also can't really focus on anything else - was somehow worse than the actual late nights working."

Banks rarely address this in recruiting. They talk about deal experience and team culture. They do not talk about the specific texture of junior analyst time - the way it is owned by others' schedules in a way that makes even the non-working hours feel constrained.

## Reason Two: Culture Problems They Could Not Have Detected in Interviews

Banking culture varies dramatically across groups at the same bank, and almost none of that variation is visible during the interview process. An analyst who joins a group with an abusive senior or a culture of blame-shifting juniors has essentially no recourse and limited ability to transfer in the first year.

The Mergers & Inquisitions annual analyst survey consistently identifies "manager/team culture" as one of the top three reasons analysts leave banking before their program ends. This is not vague dissatisfaction. This is specific: analysts describe patterns of public criticism, last-minute requests made as tests of dedication rather than genuine business needs, and systematic exclusion of junior staff from deal context that would help them do their jobs better.

The most damaging version of this - documented in multiple first-person accounts on finance forums - is the "black hole staffing" pattern: an analyst is assigned to work almost entirely for one demanding senior banker, is effectively unavailable to the rest of the group, develops a narrow skill set, and then exits the group with weaker credentials than peers who got broader exposure. This is not malicious on the part of the senior banker - it is simply a misaligned incentive structure. The senior banker gets efficient support; the analyst gets limited development.

Groups with high analyst turnover tend to have this problem. Before you join any group, ask analysts who recently left what the staffing pattern was like and whether they felt they got broad deal exposure or were siloed.

## Reason Three: PE Recruiting Pulled Them Out Early

This category of departure is distinct from the burnout and culture-driven exits. Analysts who get recruited aggressively by private equity firms in the on-cycle process - which at top funds now starts as early as month ten of the analyst's first year - often leave before their program ends to start their PE associate role.

This is technically a departure from the bank, but it is not an attrition problem from the analyst's perspective. It is the recruiting pipeline working correctly. The analyst joined banking knowing (or hoping) that PE would be the next step, prepared for the on-cycle process, received offers, and accepted.

The complication is that leaving banking before two years is complete shortchanges both the bank and in some ways the analyst. An analyst who exits at month fourteen has solid fundamentals but has not developed the judgment that comes from seeing multiple full deal cycles. The PE funds that recruit aggressively in on-cycle are primarily buying technical skills and brand-name training - they know they are getting incomplete candidates and plan to develop them further in-house.

Whether this is the right move for a given analyst depends heavily on the specific PE opportunity. Megafund exits from top banks are genuinely career-defining. Mid-market PE at month fourteen for the sake of leaving is not obviously better than staying to two years and exiting with a stronger credential and more developed judgment.

## Reason Four: The Work Was Not What They Expected

Banking recruits heavily on the premise of doing meaningful, intellectually complex financial work on important transactions. The reality of the analyst role - particularly in the first year - is that most of the work is execution, not strategy. You are building models, updating presentations, running processes, and managing logistics. The intellectual content of what you are doing is real but often less elevated than the recruiting pitch implies.

Analysts who expected to be primary contributors to investment thesis development or client strategy - who joined banking expecting something closer to a consulting analyst role in its intellectual dimension - often find the gap between expectation and reality disorienting.

This is not a deficiency in banking. The analyst role is explicitly an apprenticeship in execution. The strategic dimension comes with seniority. But for analysts who did not fully understand this going in, the realization that they are primarily a production resource in years one and two - not a strategic contributor - can be a significant disappointment.

BIWS (Breaking Into Wall Street) describes this clearly in their analyst prep materials: the technical skills are real and valuable, but analysts should enter expecting to refine and build upon existing frameworks rather than to originate them. The candidates who do best entering banking are the ones who have made peace with the execution-heavy nature of the role before they start.

## Reason Five: Health and Relationship Costs Hit a Threshold

No bank tells you in recruiting that a meaningful percentage of analysts develop significant health problems during their two years. Sleep deprivation, sedentary work, poor nutrition, and stress are not transient inconveniences - they are chronic conditions that compound over eighteen months. Analysts who enter with marginal health baselines are more vulnerable, but the pattern appears broadly.

The relationship costs are also real and under-discussed. Multiple long-term relationships end during analyst years. Analysts miss significant family events. Friendships attenuate because banking schedules are genuinely incompatible with maintaining non-banking social connections.

At some point for some analysts, the cumulative cost - physical, relational, psychological - crosses a threshold where it is no longer a rational trade regardless of the exit opportunities on the other side. This is not weakness. It is a legitimate assessment of personal value function.

The analysts who are most vulnerable to this exit are those with pre-existing commitments that banking is particularly hard on: serious relationships with partners who work normal hours, aging family members requiring attention, or health conditions that deteriorate under sustained stress and sleep deprivation.

None of this is disqualifying - people with all of these variables complete banking programs every year. But knowing your risk factors going in allows you to make the trade-off deliberately rather than hitting a wall at month fifteen.

---

## Reason Six: They Got a Better Offer in a Different Field

A small but consistent percentage of analyst departures are simply opportunity cost realizations - an analyst receives an offer for a role (often in tech, venture, or an early-stage startup) that represents a better expected value trade-off given their specific goals.

This category is underrepresented in exit surveys because analysts often frame it in terms of the banking negatives rather than the alternative positives - it is easier to say "I left because of the hours" than "I left because I got a fintech offer and think the equity upside is better." Both may be true. The opportunity pull is real regardless of how it gets described.

## What Banks Actually Know About This

Large banks have analytics teams that study attrition. They know the numbers. The institutional response to high analyst attrition has been a mix of compensation increases (salaries rose significantly across the industry in 2021-2022 in direct response to attrition pressure) and cultural initiatives that have had mixed results.

The compensation response was more effective than the cultural one, which should tell you something about where the bank's priorities actually are. Pay enough and a meaningful number of analysts will tolerate conditions they otherwise would not. This is rational from the bank's perspective. From the analyst's perspective, it is worth understanding that the higher salaries are a retention mechanism in response to documented dissatisfaction - the compensation is partly hazard pay.

Banks do not want you to know the attrition rates before you sign because those rates are commercially inconvenient. The information is available if you look for it - the WSO compensation surveys, the Mergers & Inquisitions annual reports, and the financial press all cover it. But it is not volunteered, and recruiting presentations are structured to minimize its salience.

## What This Means for Your Decision

Understanding why analysts quit is useful in three specific ways:

**First, it helps you assess your personal risk factors.** If you have the culture-sensitivity profile, health vulnerabilities, or relationship commitments that appear in the high-attrition segments, you can plan for them before you arrive rather than discovering them at month ten.

**Second, it helps you evaluate specific groups, not just specific banks.** The culture variable is group-level, not firm-level. Detailed research into specific group cultures - which requires conversations with former analysts, not with recruiters - is the most valuable pre-commitment research you can do.

**Third, it puts the compensation in context.** The pay is real and significant. It is also partially structured to compensate for conditions that produce attrition. Knowing that makes the trade-off cleaner to evaluate.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Ask the attrition question directly.** Before joining any group, ask: "What percentage of the analyst class from two years ago is still at the bank?" The answer will be revealing. Deflection is also revealing.

2. **Talk to departed analysts, not current ones.** Current analysts have strong incentives to present their experience positively. Analysts who have already left - especially ones who left before their program ended - will give you more honest assessments.

3. **Assess your own risk factors before signing.** Use the categories above as a checklist. Which ones apply to you? Health, relationships, intellectual expectations, culture sensitivity? Being honest with yourself about this before you start is worth more than any amount of mental preparation once you are already in the seat.

4. **Read the [full investment banking lifestyle breakdown](/recruiting-insider/honest-truth-ib-lifestyle/)** for the complete picture - hours, pay, and how the culture calculus actually works on a day-to-day basis.

5. **Have an explicit exit timeline in your head before you start.** Analysts who know they are targeting PE at month twenty-two and have built a preparation plan for that exit are more resilient than analysts who entered without a specific post-banking goal. The tunnel needs to have a visible end for most people to navigate it well.
