# Group Politics: How to Read the Room as an Analyst

Group politics in investment banking is the subject that nobody talks about in recruiting and everybody wishes they had understood before starting. Technical preparation - modeling, accounting, valuation - is teachable and extensively documented. The political layer of banking groups is neither, which is why analysts who navigate it poorly pay a disproportionate price for what is essentially an information problem rather than a capability one.

Understanding group politics does not mean becoming a political operator. It means reading the actual social and power structure of your group accurately so that you do not inadvertently step into dynamics you did not know existed.

---

## The Formal Structure Is Not the Whole Picture

Every banking group has an org chart. Analysts know it. The chart has MDs at the top, VPs in the middle, associates above you, and you at the bottom. The chart is real and it governs formal authority, deal allocation, performance reviews, and compensation decisions.

It is also incomplete in ways that matter practically every day.

The informal structure - the actual power relationships, alliances, tensions, and information flows in the group - runs parallel to the org chart and sometimes contradicts it. An associate who has been in the group for six years and has the trust of the group head has more de facto authority than the associate title suggests. A VP who is two years into a political conflict with the coverage MD is in a weaker position than their title implies, and working primarily for them may limit your deal exposure without you realizing it.

The informal structure is knowable. It requires observation, not investigation. Watch how people interact in meetings. Watch who addresses whom first. Watch who gets interrupted and who does not interrupt. Watch who the senior partners go to when they need something done quickly. The answers reveal the actual hierarchy within three to four weeks if you are paying attention.

---

## The Most Important Political Unit: The Associate

For an analyst, the most politically consequential relationship is not with the MD or the group head. It is with your Associate.

Associates are the primary interface between analyst work and senior banker expectations. They receive the work, review it, decide whether to send it up the chain or send it back to you, frame your performance to VPs and MDs, and have substantial influence over which deals you get staffed on. Your Associate's perception of your capabilities shapes your trajectory in the first year in ways that direct MD exposure does not.

The political dimension: Associates are not a monolith. They have varying relationships with the senior bankers above them, varying career trajectories of their own, and varying levels of investment in analyst development. An associate who is politically well-positioned - who has the trust of the group head and is being developed for VP - will invest in you because your performance reflects on them. An associate who is stalling or who is in conflict with senior bankers is a politically risky primary relationship, not because they are a bad person, but because their ability to advocate for you is limited by their own position.

Without being calculating about it, it is worth understanding where your primary associate sits in the group hierarchy and calibrating accordingly.

## Understanding Alliance Structures in Your Group

Most banking groups have alliance structures - loose coalitions of senior bankers who cover for each other, advocate for each other's deals, and share client relationships. These alliances are often organized around shared history (bankers who came up together), shared client relationships (joint coverage of a key account), or shared practice area (all the healthcare specialists in a diversified coverage group).

As an analyst, you will be staffed across multiple senior bankers' deals over the course of a year. Understanding the alliance structure matters for two specific reasons:

**First, it explains information flows.** When MD A tells you something about the business development process and asks you to keep it internal, "internal" often has a precise meaning - it means within MD A's coalition, not within the full group. Understanding who is in that coalition prevents you from inadvertently sharing information across a coalition boundary.

**Second, it shapes how you are perceived.** If you work primarily for bankers on one side of a group tension, you will be perceived by the other side as belonging to that camp. This happens regardless of your intent - proximity to an alliance assigns you to it in the eyes of others. Be aware of this and, where possible, demonstrate value to senior bankers across alliance lines, particularly in the early months before your affiliation is set.

## The Senior Banker You Want to Work For vs. The One Who Is Available

One of the most consequential group politics decisions an analyst faces is which senior bankers to invest in working for. This is partially within your control - through proactive communication of your interests and through the quality of work you deliver for different people - and partially determined by staffing decisions you do not control.

The bankers you want to work for are those who:
- Treat juniors as developmental investments, not just execution resources
- Are politically well-positioned in the group - their deals get done, their clients are active, their standing with the group head is solid
- Have deal flow that is relevant to your target exit (PE-focused analyst wants M&A exposure; HF-focused analyst wants capital markets or coverage exposure)
- Give clear direction and constructive feedback

The bankers who are simply available - who are always looking for analysts because their reputation for working with juniors is poor - are a trap. Doing a large volume of work for a VP who is known for poor communication and erratic behavior will not build your reputation as effectively as doing excellent work for a VP who is respected.

You cannot always choose. When you cannot, deliver excellent work regardless and minimize the political associations that come with the staffing by ensuring your contributions to the group's broader deal activity are visible to multiple senior people.

## The Cohort Dynamics: How Analyst Peer Politics Work

Analyst cohorts are often described as collaborative, and many are. They are also competitive in ways that are not always obvious, because the competition is not always direct - it is for deal quality, for senior banker attention, and for the first-year review ratings that affect bonus and promotion timing.

The group politics mistake that analysts make most frequently with their peers is miscalibrated alliance selection. Analysts who align closely with peers who are already developing a negative reputation in the group inherit some of that reputation by proximity. Analysts who position themselves as the friend and confidant of every complainer in the cohort become associated with the complaining.

None of this requires being unfriendly to peers who are struggling. It requires being thoughtful about the difference between professional solidarity - helping a peer think through a difficult assignment, covering for someone who is overwhelmed on a deal - and political affiliation with a peer whose standing in the group is compromised.

The most useful cohort dynamic to cultivate: informal knowledge sharing. Analysts who freely share model conventions, client context, and group-specific knowledge with peers build reciprocal relationships that pay back in kind. This is genuinely collegial and also politically intelligent.

## Reading the Room in Meetings and on Calls

The ability to read a room in real time - to understand the dynamics operating in a specific meeting or client call and calibrate your behavior accordingly - is one of the most valuable skills in banking and one of the least discussed in analyst preparation.

**In internal meetings:** Understand before you walk in who is senior to whom, what the purpose of the meeting is, and what each participant's stake in the outcome is. An analyst who speaks before understanding these dynamics - volunteering opinions, asking questions that inadvertently surface internal tensions, presenting data in a way that lands wrong for a senior banker - has created a problem that no amount of technical competence will fix.

The general rule for analysts in internal meetings: listen significantly more than you speak. When you do speak, be precise and direct. Do not editorialize about strategic direction unless you are specifically asked, and even then, be careful. Your job in most internal meetings is to present materials accurately and answer specific questions about your analysis, not to participate in the strategic discussion.

**On client calls:** The hierarchy among bankers is suspended somewhat in front of clients - the client's needs and the deal's momentum take priority. That said, do not speak over or contradict a senior banker on a client call regardless of whether you think their representation is technically precise. If you see a material error in what is being represented, find a way to surface it privately, not publicly.

**The meeting-after-the-meeting:** Much of the meaningful communication in banking groups happens in the informal exchange after a formal meeting ends - the five minutes when the group head checks in with the coverage MD, when the VP clarifies something with the associate, when the informal deal direction gets set. Being available and attentive during these moments - without hovering presumptuously - is how analysts learn what is actually happening.

## Political Pitfalls That Derail Analyst Careers

The most common group politics failures at the analyst level are worth naming specifically because they are predictable and avoidable:

**Going around the chain.** Sending work directly to an MD when it should go through your Associate first, or asking an MD a question that your Associate could answer, signals poor judgment about the hierarchy regardless of your intent. Follow the chain, always.

**Taking sides in senior conflicts.** Groups with internal tensions have them for reasons that are complex and historically deep. Analysts who align visibly with one side - even if their personal assessment of the conflict is correct - limit their effectiveness with the other side and inherit political liability they cannot manage.

**Sharing group gossip outside the group.** Anything you say about your group's internal dynamics to people outside the group - at firm social events, to friends at other banks, in any context that could get back - will get back. The banking world is small and the financial community in any given city is smaller.

**Overclaiming credit.** Analysts who represent their contributions to deals more expansively than is accurate - in conversations with peers, in interactions with other senior bankers - will be caught and corrected, and the correction will be permanent. Let your work speak accurately. If you need a senior banker to know what you contributed to a deal, the right approach is to ensure the work itself is visible and excellent, not to narrate your contributions.

## The Long Game: How Group Politics Connect to the MD Track

The group politics skills that matter in year one are different from the political skills that matter for the MD track. Year-one politics are about navigating a hierarchy you do not control. MD-track politics are about building a position within the hierarchy - developing allies, building client franchises, and managing the organizational dynamics of competing with other senior bankers for the same client relationships and the same deal opportunities.

For a look at how the political landscape evolves at the senior level, see the [MD track analysis](/recruiting-insider/md-track-what-changed/). The consistent finding: the bankers who build the most sustainable MD businesses are those who invest in genuine relationships - with clients, with colleagues, with institutional stakeholders - rather than those who manage their organizational positioning most cleverly.

Genuine relationship building and good political reading are not in conflict. Reading the room accurately is what enables you to invest in the right relationships in the first place.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Map the informal hierarchy in your first four weeks.** Before your second month, you should have a clear picture of who the key alliance structures are, who the effective decision-makers are at each level, and where the group's internal tensions sit. Do not document this anywhere sensitive - keep it as working knowledge.

2. **Identify the two or three senior bankers who are worth investing in.** Use the criteria above: well-positioned, deal-active, invest in juniors. Direct your best work toward these relationships.

3. **Avoid visibly taking sides in senior conflicts.** You may have private views. Keep them private. Deliver excellent work for all senior bankers who staff you, regardless of their organizational position.

4. **Review the [top 10 analyst mistakes](/recruiting-insider/top-10-mistakes-analysts/)** for the overlap between political mistakes and execution mistakes - going around the chain, comparative complaining, and misreading the hierarchy all appear there.

5. **Practice room-reading in every meeting from your first week.** Before each internal meeting, spend two minutes reviewing who will be there and what each person's stake in the outcome is. After each meeting, spend two minutes noting what you observed about the dynamic. This habit builds the political intelligence that experienced bankers develop over years into something you can develop in months.
