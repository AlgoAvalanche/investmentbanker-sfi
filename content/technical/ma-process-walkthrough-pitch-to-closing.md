# M&A Process Walkthrough: Pitch to Closing Dinner

The M&A process is one of those things that sounds clean in textbooks and chaotic in real life. You will read that a deal takes 6-9 months from mandate to close. You will then work on a deal that takes 18 months, falls apart twice, gets relaunched with a different buyer, and closes on a Friday afternoon when three key documents are still not fully negotiated.

This article walks through the full M&A process - both sell-side and buy-side - with the level of detail that actually matters when you are sitting in the deal room at 2 a.m. understanding why the lawyers are arguing about a representation that has a 0.01% chance of ever being triggered.

---

## Two Mandates, Two Very Different Jobs

Investment banking M&A advisory comes in two forms: sell-side and buy-side. Most large transaction volume is sell-side because every deal has one seller and potentially many buyers. Sell-side advisory is also generally more lucrative per hour of banker time - you run a structured process, control information flow, and collect a success fee at close.

Buy-side advisory is more common in strategic M&A where the acquirer wants help identifying, valuing, and negotiating an acquisition. For PE-backed situations, buy-side advisory is less common because the fund has its own deal team, though smaller funds will sometimes bring in a banker for a large or complex acquisition.

The economics differ: sell-side fees are typically 1-2% of deal value for mid-market, 0.5-1% for large-cap, sometimes less for mega-deals. Buy-side fees tend to be lower relative to deal size. Both sides can earn retainers.

## Phase 1: Pitch and Mandate

### The Pitch

A sell-side pitch - called a "bake-off" when multiple banks are competing - is the beauty pageant phase of investment banking. The seller (often a board of directors or a founder) invites 3-5 banks to present their credentials, their valuation view, and their proposed process.

The pitch book will contain:
- Situational overview (who you are, what you do)
- Valuation analysis - comps, precedent transactions, DCF - presented as a valuation range
- Proposed buyer universe (strategic and financial)
- Process recommendation (broad auction vs. targeted vs. negotiated)
- Bank credentials and deal team

The valuation in a pitch book is almost always optimistic. Banks competing for a mandate have every incentive to show the highest number - the client wants to hear a big number, and you are being compared to three other banks who are also presenting big numbers. This is widely understood in the industry, which is why experienced sellers discount the pitch book valuation and focus on the proposed process and the deal team's experience.

One veteran M&A banker described it bluntly in a private forum: "The mandate goes to whoever the CEO played golf with, followed by whoever showed the highest valuation. In that order." This is reductive but not entirely wrong.

### Engagement Letter and Fees

Once selected, the bank and client sign an engagement letter. This document governs fees, expense reimbursement, tail periods (typically 18-24 months, meaning if the company sells to someone the bank contacted during the process within 18-24 months of termination, the bank still collects a fee), and indemnification.

Read engagement letters carefully if you are ever advising a client. The tail provisions in particular can create unexpected fee obligations.

## Phase 2: Preparation (4-8 Weeks)

This is the grinding phase. The deal team - usually a VP, an associate, and an analyst - works with the company to build the materials buyers will receive.

### Confidential Information Memorandum (CIM)

The CIM is the primary marketing document. Typical length: 50-80 pages. Contents:

- Executive summary with investment highlights
- Business overview - products, services, go-to-market
- Industry and competitive landscape
- Financial summary - 3 years historical, management projections for 3-5 years
- Management team bios
- Operational detail by segment

The financial section is the most scrutinized. Historical financials need to be adjusted (or "normalized") for one-time items, owner compensation in excess of market rate, and non-recurring revenue or costs. This is the beginning of the quality of earnings process, which will be revisited extensively in diligence.

For a discussion of how DCF inputs are structured in this context, see our article on [DCF valuation step by step](/technical/dcf-valuation-step-by-step/).

### Teaser

Before the CIM, the bank sends a one-to-two page anonymous teaser to potential buyers. The teaser describes the company without identifying it - "a leading North American provider of software solutions to the financial services industry" - and invites interested parties to sign an NDA to receive the full CIM.

### Buyer Universe Development

The deal team maps the full buyer universe: financial sponsors (PE firms with relevant portfolio companies or sector focus), strategic acquirers (direct competitors, adjacent businesses, international players looking for US entry), and sometimes family offices or sovereign wealth funds.

A broad process might contact 50-100 parties. A targeted process might contact 5-15. The right approach depends on the asset: highly strategic businesses may generate bidding wars among a small group of strategics; more financial-buyer-friendly businesses benefit from broad PE distribution.

## Phase 3: First Round (3-5 Weeks)

After NDAs are signed, CIMs go out. Interested buyers submit Indications of Interest (IOIs) by the stated deadline - typically 3-4 weeks after CIM distribution.

IOIs are non-binding and typically specify:
- Valuation range (usually expressed as EV or as an EBITDA multiple)
- Proposed financing structure
- Brief strategic rationale
- Due diligence requirements
- Proposed timeline

The sell-side banker compiles IOIs and presents them to the client. The typical process then selects 5-10 parties to advance to second round. Selection criteria: valuation (obviously), but also certainty of close, fit of the proposed transaction structure, and whether the buyer seems serious vs. information-gathering.

Some buyers use IOI processes purely for competitive intelligence. Experienced sell-side bankers know which firms are tire-kickers and price the IOI accordingly when selecting who advances.

---

## Phase 4: Management Presentations and Second Round (4-6 Weeks)

Second-round buyers get access to a management presentation - typically a 2-3 hour in-person or virtual meeting with the CEO and CFO - and a virtual data room (VDR) containing more detailed financial and operational information.

### Management Presentation

The management presentation is more than a dog-and-pony show. Sophisticated buyers are reading the room: Is the CFO on top of the numbers or does the CEO answer every financial question? Is there a real succession plan or is the founder the whole business? Are they aligned on the company's strategic position or do they give different answers when asked separately?

Buy-side teams often split up during Q&A to ask the same questions to different executives and compare answers later.

### Data Room

The VDR at this stage typically contains:
- Audited financial statements
- Monthly management accounts
- Customer contracts (often redacted)
- Key vendor agreements
- Employee data
- IP/technology documentation
- Legal - pending litigation, regulatory matters
- Real estate/leases

The data room is never complete. There will always be something missing that someone asked for. Managing VDR requests from 8 active bidders while also managing management's time is a genuine operational challenge that analysts and associates spend enormous effort on.

### Final Bids

Second-round bidders submit Final Bids (also called Best and Final Offers or BAFOs). These are more detailed than IOIs and include:
- A specific purchase price or range
- Proposed transaction structure (cash, stock, or combination)
- Financing commitment evidence (for PE buyers, this means debt commitment letters)
- Markup of the proposed purchase agreement
- List of remaining diligence items
- Proposed timeline to close

The markup of the purchase agreement is increasingly important. Sellers want certainty, and a buyer who has minimal markup of the SPA is signaling lower execution risk. A buyer who redlines 60 pages of a standard purchase agreement is creating uncertainty.

## Phase 5: Exclusivity, Diligence, and Negotiation (6-12 Weeks)

The seller selects a "winner" and enters exclusivity - typically 30-60 days, though this extends in practice. During exclusivity, the buyer conducts confirmatory due diligence and the parties negotiate final documentation.

### Quality of Earnings (QoE)

The buyer's accounting firm conducts a QoE - a detailed review of the company's EBITDA adjustments, revenue recognition policies, working capital dynamics, and customer concentration. This is one of the most common sources of purchase price renegotiation.

Common QoE findings that affect price:
- EBITDA adjustments that should not have been added back (recurring items labeled as one-time)
- Revenue that was pulled forward from future periods
- Deferred revenue that will convert at lower margins
- Customer revenue that is under contract for less than presented
- Owner perks that cannot be fully adjusted out

For a deeper look at how working capital affects deal price, see our article on [working capital adjustments in M&A](/technical/working-capital-adjustments-ma/).

### Legal Negotiation

The purchase agreement negotiation runs in parallel with diligence. Key commercial points that kill or reshape deals:

**Representations and warranties**: The seller makes extensive representations about the accuracy of the business description. Every rep is a potential indemnification obligation. Rep and warranty insurance (RWI) has become standard in mid-market deals, significantly reducing the escrow requirements and post-closing indemnification exposure.

**Working capital peg**: The agreement will specify a target net working capital amount; the purchase price is adjusted up or down based on actual working capital at close vs. the peg. This is technically straightforward but practically contentious. Sellers want the peg set low; buyers want it high.

**Material Adverse Change (MAC) clause**: Defines what circumstances allow the buyer to walk without paying a break fee. MAC definitions have become extraordinarily negotiated documents. The Delaware courts' interpretation of MAC clauses - notably in the Akorn v. Fresenius and AB Stable VIII LLC v. MAPS Hotels cases - has significantly shaped how these provisions are drafted.

**Earnouts**: When buyer and seller cannot agree on value (often because management has projected growth the buyer is skeptical of), they bridge the gap with earnouts - contingent payments tied to future performance. Earnouts sound elegant in theory and generate litigation in practice. If you must use them, keep them simple, keep the measurement period short, and hire a good lawyer to draft the anti-sandbagging provisions.

## Phase 6: Signing, Regulatory Approval, and Closing

Signing the definitive agreement is not closing. Between signing and closing, the parties must:

- Obtain required regulatory approvals (HSR antitrust filing in the US; foreign approvals if applicable)
- Complete any required financing (PE buyers must fund their equity and close their debt facilities)
- Obtain any required third-party consents (change of control provisions in key contracts)
- Satisfy remaining conditions to close

The HSR review period is typically 30 days; the DOJ or FTC can issue a "second request" for additional information, which extends the review substantially. In a competitive industry or for large acquirers, antitrust is a genuine risk that needs to be modeled into the deal timeline.

For PE deals, the financing close - the point at which the debt is actually funded - happens simultaneously with the equity close and the transfer of title. This "simultaneous sign and close" model is common in mid-market, while larger deals often have a gap between signing and closing to allow for syndication of the debt.

The closing dinner is, in theory, the celebration. In practice, most deals close over email and phone, with the lawyers on a conference call until midnight confirming that every condition has been satisfied and every document has been executed. The actual dinner happens weeks later, if at all.

## What Actually Kills Deals

Every banker has a graveyard of deals that died between LOI and close. The most common causes:

**Diligence findings**: The QoE finds that EBITDA is overstated. A major customer contract is not renewing. There is undisclosed litigation.

**Financing failure**: The debt markets move against the buyer. In 2022-2023, multiple large LBOs had committed financing repriced or pulled as rates spiked.

**Bid-ask price gap that cannot be bridged**: The buyer's diligence lowers their view of value; the seller will not accept the new price; neither side blinks.

**Key person departure**: A major executive announces they are leaving post-close. Especially toxic in professional services or technology businesses where the talent is the asset.

**MAC invocations and COVID-era litigation**: The AB Stable VIII case (Mirae Asset attempting to walk from a $5.8B hotel acquisition at the onset of COVID) showed that MAC clauses almost never actually allow a buyer to exit. The courts' threshold is extremely high. But the resulting litigation was expensive and damaging for both parties regardless of outcome.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Find a public M&A deal and read the proxy statement** - S-4s and definitive proxy statements (DEF 14A) for public acquisitions are on the SEC's EDGAR database. Read the "Background of the Merger" section. It is a detailed narrative of exactly how the deal process unfolded, including who was contacted, when bids came in, and how the board made its decision.

2. **Build a deal timeline template** - create a simple Excel or PowerPoint timeline for a fictional sell-side process: mandate, CIM preparation, first-round IOIs, second-round management presentations, final bids, exclusivity, signing, regulatory, close. Put realistic weeks next to each phase. This is what bankers use to pitch process plans to clients.

3. **Read one QoE report** - these circulate in the industry. Understanding what a QoE actually looks at, and what adjustments are commonly challenged, will make you more credible in any M&A diligence conversation.

4. **Understand the working capital adjustment mechanism** - this single concept generates more post-signing disputes than almost any other M&A provision. Our article on [working capital adjustments in M&A](/technical/working-capital-adjustments-ma/) covers the mechanics in detail.

5. **Track a live deal** - pick any announced acquisition involving a public company, set up Google Alerts for it, and follow it through to close (or failure). Read every press release, every regulatory filing, and every news story. Understanding the real-time dynamics of a live deal is irreplaceable experience.
