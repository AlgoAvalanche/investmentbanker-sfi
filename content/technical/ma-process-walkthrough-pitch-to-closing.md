# M&A Process Walkthrough: Pitch to Closing Dinner

The M&A process is one of those things that sounds clean in textbooks and chaotic in real life. You will read that a deal takes 6-9 months from mandate to close. You will then work on a deal that takes 18 months, falls apart twice, gets relaunched with a different buyer, and closes on a Friday afternoon when three key documents are still not fully negotiated.

The market itself has become harder to ignore. Global M&A deal values rose 36% in 2025 compared to 2024, according to PwC's Global M&A Industry Trends report - though deal volumes rose only 1%, meaning the gains were concentrated in a smaller number of very large transactions. Megadeals above $5 billion climbed from 63 in 2024 to 111 in 2025. The Americas accounted for 60% of global deal value, with deal values up 55% year-over-year. That is the environment in which bankers are operating: a market where headline numbers look strong but transaction volume remains uneven, and where large, well-capitalized buyers dominate activity.

Understanding the mechanics underneath those numbers - how a deal actually gets from a first meeting with a founder to a wire transfer at closing - is the core skill of any M&A professional. This article walks through the full sell-side and buy-side process with the level of detail that matters when you are sitting in the deal room at 2 a.m.

---

## Two Mandates, Two Very Different Jobs

Investment banking M&A advisory comes in two forms: sell-side and buy-side. Most large transaction volume is sell-side because every deal has one seller and potentially many buyers. Sell-side advisory is also generally more lucrative per hour of banker time - you run a structured process, control information flow, and collect a success fee at close.

Buy-side advisory is more common in strategic M&A where the acquirer wants help identifying, valuing, and negotiating an acquisition. For PE-backed situations, buy-side advisory is less common because the fund has its own deal team, though smaller funds will sometimes bring in a banker for a large or complex acquisition.

The economics differ meaningfully by deal size. According to market data from RoseBiz and MNA Community covering 2024-2025 transactions, the average sell-side success fee runs approximately 5.5% for a $5 million deal and falls to roughly 2.1% for a $100 million deal. Transactions above $100 million typically fall in the 1-2% range. The mid-market sweet spot - deals in the $10-30 million EBITDA range - generally generates success fees of 3-5%.

Fee structures themselves have evolved. According to Axial's 2024-2025 M&A Fee Guide, which surveyed 150-plus mid-market dealmakers, the Lehman Formula - where the commission rate decreases as deal size increases - remains the most common structure at 44% of firms. The accelerator formula, where rates increase with deal size, has grown to 20% of firms, up from 16% in 2023. Flat-fee structures account for 26% of firms. Most advisors also charge an upfront retainer of $50,000-$250,000, sometimes structured as monthly payments over 4-12 months, to cover pre-close costs and confirm client commitment.

## Phase 1: Pitch and Mandate

### The Pitch

A sell-side pitch - called a "bake-off" when multiple banks are competing - is the beauty pageant phase of investment banking. The seller (often a board of directors or a founder) invites 3-5 banks to present their credentials, their valuation view, and their proposed process.

The pitch book will contain:
- Situational overview covering the company's business and position
- Valuation analysis - comps, precedent transactions, DCF - presented as a valuation range
- Proposed buyer universe (strategic and financial)
- Process recommendation (broad auction vs. targeted vs. negotiated)
- Bank credentials and deal team

The valuation in a pitch book is almost always optimistic. Banks competing for a mandate have every incentive to show the highest number - the client wants to hear a big number, and you are being compared to three other banks who are also presenting large numbers. This is widely understood in the industry, which is why experienced sellers discount the pitch book valuation and focus on the proposed process and the deal team's experience rather than the initial headline.

For the underlying methodology behind pitch book valuations, see our article on [comparable companies analysis](/technical/comparable-companies-analysis/).

### Engagement Letter and Fees

Once selected, the bank and client sign an engagement letter. This document governs fees, expense reimbursement, tail periods, and indemnification. Tail provisions - typically 18-24 months - mean that if the company sells to a party the bank contacted during the process within that window after termination, the bank still collects a fee. Read engagement letters carefully if you are advising a client. The tail provisions in particular can create unexpected fee obligations.

## Phase 2: Preparation (4-8 Weeks)

This is the grinding phase. The deal team - usually a VP, an associate, and an analyst - works with the company to build the materials buyers will receive.

### Confidential Information Memorandum (CIM)

The CIM is the primary marketing document. Typical length: 50-80 pages. Contents:

- Executive summary with investment highlights
- Business overview - products, services, go-to-market
- Industry and competitive landscape
- Financial summary - 3 years historical plus management projections for 3-5 years
- Management team bios
- Operational detail by segment

The financial section is the most scrutinized. Historical financials need to be adjusted - or "normalized" - for one-time items, owner compensation in excess of market rate, and non-recurring revenue or costs. This is the beginning of the quality of earnings process, which will be revisited extensively in diligence.

For a discussion of how DCF inputs are structured in this context, see our article on [DCF valuation step by step](/technical/dcf-valuation-step-by-step/).

### Teaser

Before the CIM, the bank sends a one-to-two page anonymous teaser to potential buyers. The teaser describes the company without identifying it - "a leading North American provider of software solutions to the financial services industry" - and invites interested parties to sign an NDA to receive the full CIM.

### Buyer Universe Development

The deal team maps the full buyer universe: financial sponsors (PE firms with relevant portfolio companies or sector focus), strategic acquirers (direct competitors, adjacent businesses, international players looking for US entry), and sometimes family offices or sovereign wealth funds.

A broad process might contact 50-100 parties. A targeted process might contact 5-15. The right approach depends on the asset: highly strategic businesses may generate bidding wars among a small group of strategics; more financial-buyer-friendly businesses benefit from broad PE distribution.

With approximately one-third of the 100 largest 2025 transactions citing AI as a strategic rationale - per PwC's 2026 M&A outlook - technology businesses in particular are generating interest from buyers who a decade ago would not have been obvious candidates. A traditional industrial company with a proprietary data asset may now have a dozen technology acquirers in the buyer universe who would not have been relevant five years ago.

## Phase 3: First Round (3-5 Weeks)

After NDAs are signed, CIMs go out. Interested buyers submit Indications of Interest (IOIs) by the stated deadline - typically 3-4 weeks after CIM distribution.

IOIs are non-binding and typically specify:
- Valuation range (usually expressed as enterprise value or as an EBITDA multiple)
- Proposed financing structure
- Brief strategic rationale
- Due diligence requirements
- Proposed timeline

The sell-side banker compiles IOIs and presents them to the client. The typical process then selects 5-10 parties to advance to second round. Selection criteria include valuation, certainty of close, fit of the proposed transaction structure, and whether the buyer appears serious or is gathering competitive intelligence.

Some buyers use IOI processes purely for competitive intelligence. Experienced sell-side bankers know which firms are tire-kickers and adjust IOI weighting accordingly when deciding who advances.

## Phase 4: Management Presentations and Second Round (4-6 Weeks)

Second-round buyers get access to a management presentation - typically a 2-3 hour in-person or virtual meeting with the CEO and CFO - and a virtual data room (VDR) containing more detailed financial and operational information.

### Management Presentation

The management presentation is more than a dog-and-pony show. Sophisticated buyers are reading the room: Is the CFO on top of the numbers or does the CEO answer every financial question? Is there a real succession plan or is the founder the whole business? Are they aligned on the company's strategic position, or do they give inconsistent answers when asked separately?

Buy-side teams often split up during Q&A to ask the same questions to different executives and compare answers later. Management alignment is itself a diligence data point.

### Data Room

The VDR at this stage typically contains:
- Audited financial statements
- Monthly management accounts
- Customer contracts (often redacted)
- Key vendor agreements
- Employee data
- IP and technology documentation
- Legal - pending litigation, regulatory matters
- Real estate and leases

The data room is never complete. There will always be something missing that someone requested. Managing VDR requests from eight active bidders while also managing management's time is a genuine operational challenge that analysts and associates spend enormous effort on throughout second round.

### Final Bids

Second-round bidders submit Final Bids (also called Best and Final Offers or BAFOs). These are more detailed than IOIs and include:
- A specific purchase price or range
- Proposed transaction structure (cash, stock, or combination)
- Financing commitment evidence (for PE buyers, this means debt commitment letters)
- Markup of the proposed purchase agreement
- List of remaining diligence items
- Proposed timeline to close

The markup of the purchase agreement is increasingly important. Sellers want certainty, and a buyer who returns minimal markup of the SPA is signaling lower execution risk. A buyer who redlines 60 pages of a standard purchase agreement is creating uncertainty, regardless of their headline price.

## Phase 5: Exclusivity, Diligence, and Negotiation (6-12 Weeks)

The seller selects a winner and enters exclusivity - typically 30-60 days, though this extends in practice. During exclusivity, the buyer conducts confirmatory due diligence and the parties negotiate final documentation.

### Quality of Earnings (QoE)

The buyer's accounting firm conducts a QoE - a detailed review of the company's EBITDA adjustments, revenue recognition policies, working capital dynamics, and customer concentration. This is one of the most common sources of purchase price renegotiation.

Common QoE findings that affect price:
- EBITDA adjustments that should not have been added back (recurring items labeled as one-time)
- Revenue that was pulled forward from future periods
- Deferred revenue that will convert at lower margins
- Customer revenue that is under contract for less than presented
- Owner perks that cannot be fully adjusted out

The broader diligence statistics make clear why this phase matters. Research consistently finds that 70-90% of M&A transactions fail to create expected value for acquirers. A 2025 CFA Institute analysis identified the top three reasons deals fail to deliver: overpaying for the target (42% of failures), inadequate due diligence (31%), and poor post-merger integration (27%). Nearly 60% of executives in a separate survey attributed deal underperformance to due diligence that did not identify critical issues pre-close. Deals where buyers conducted 90 or more days of diligence showed 34% higher success rates than those with less than 45 days - which makes the push to compress timelines a double-edged sword.

For a deeper look at how working capital affects deal price, see our article on [working capital adjustments in M&A](/technical/working-capital-adjustments-ma/).

### Legal Negotiation

The purchase agreement negotiation runs in parallel with diligence. Key commercial points that kill or reshape deals:

**Representations and warranties**: The seller makes extensive representations about the accuracy of the business description. Every rep is a potential indemnification obligation. Rep and warranty insurance (RWI) has become a standard tool in mid-market deals, though the market has cooled from its 2021 peak. The American Bar Association's Private Target M&A Deal Points Study estimated that 55% of private transactions used RWI in 2023, down from a record 65% in 2021. On the pricing side, RWI premiums have fallen substantially - from roughly 5% of policy limits in early 2022 to 2.5-3% in 2025, per CBIZ's 2025 transactional risk analysis. Coverage is now available for transactions as small as $25 million. Financial representations, customer and contract reps, and compliance matters drive approximately 78% of all RWI claims when coverage is triggered.

**Working capital peg**: The agreement will specify a target net working capital amount; the purchase price adjusts up or down based on actual working capital at close versus the peg. This is technically straightforward but practically contentious - sellers want the peg set low, buyers want it high. It is a zero-sum negotiation with real dollar consequences.

**Material Adverse Change (MAC) clause**: Defines what circumstances allow the buyer to walk without paying a break fee. MAC definitions have become extraordinarily negotiated documents. The Delaware courts' interpretation of MAC clauses - notably in the Akorn v. Fresenius and AB Stable VIII LLC v. MAPS Hotels cases - has significantly shaped how these provisions are drafted. The AB Stable VIII case, in which Mirae Asset attempted to exit a $5.8 billion hotel acquisition at the onset of COVID-19, demonstrated how nearly impossible it is to successfully invoke a MAC. The court ruled against Mirae. Buyers who try to exit via MAC typically lose - and spend a significant amount on litigation in the process.

**Earnouts**: When buyer and seller cannot agree on value - often because management has projected growth the buyer is skeptical of - they bridge the gap with earnouts: contingent payments tied to future performance. Earnouts sound elegant in theory and generate litigation in practice. If you must use them, keep them simple, keep the measurement period short, and hire a good lawyer to draft the anti-manipulation provisions.

## Phase 6: Signing, Regulatory Approval, and Closing

Signing the definitive agreement is not closing. Between signing and closing, the parties must:

- Obtain required regulatory approvals (HSR antitrust filing in the US; foreign approvals if applicable)
- Complete any required financing (PE buyers must fund their equity and close their debt facilities)
- Obtain any required third-party consents (change of control provisions in key contracts)
- Satisfy remaining conditions to close

The HSR regulatory picture has become more complex. In FY 2024, there were 2,031 HSR filings. The overall Second Request rate was 3.0% - a return to pre-2020 norms after an anomalously low period. But that aggregate rate masks significant concentration at the top: mega-deals above $10 billion received Second Requests at nearly a 50% rate, and mid-range transactions between $1 billion and $10 billion at approximately 6.4%, according to ComplexDiscovery's analysis of FY 2024 HSR data. Of the 59 Second Requests issued in FY 2024, 32 transactions faced enforcement actions - with 24 ultimately abandoned or restructured rather than going to litigation.

Second Request compliance typically takes 2-4 months and can cost millions of dollars in outside counsel and document review fees - adding 10 months or more to the path to close in contested cases. The initial 30-day HSR waiting period is the floor, not the ceiling. New HSR rules effective February 10, 2025 require substantially expanded upfront documentation with the initial filing, which is changing how deal teams approach pre-filing preparation.

For PE deals, the financing close - the point at which the debt is actually funded - happens simultaneously with the equity close and the transfer of title. This simultaneous sign-and-close model is common in mid-market transactions, while larger deals often have a gap between signing and closing to allow for debt syndication.

The closing dinner is, in theory, the celebration. In practice, most deals close over email and phone, with lawyers on a conference call until midnight confirming that every condition has been satisfied and every document executed. The actual dinner happens weeks later, if at all. The wire hits, someone sends a congratulatory email, and the integration begins.

## What Actually Kills Deals

Every banker has a graveyard of deals that died between LOI and close. The most common causes:

**Diligence findings**: The QoE finds that EBITDA is overstated. A major customer contract is not renewing. There is undisclosed litigation. Environmental liability that was not in the data room. These are the kills that feel abrupt because they often are - one document in a data room can undo months of process.

**Financing failure**: The debt markets move against the buyer. In 2022-2023, multiple large LBOs had committed financing repriced or pulled as rates spiked. Committed financing is not the same as funded financing - the gap between a debt commitment letter and an actual wire is where many deals die.

**Bid-ask price gap that cannot be bridged**: The buyer's diligence lowers their view of value. The seller will not accept the new price. Neither side blinks. This is the outcome of inadequate preparation on the seller's side - if the QoE finds that EBITDA was overstated by 15%, the seller either takes the haircut or the deal falls apart.

**Key person departure**: A major executive announces they are leaving post-close. Especially toxic in professional services or technology businesses where the talent is the asset. Retention packages for key executives are often negotiated as part of the deal precisely because buyers are pricing in the risk of departure.

**MAC invocations**: The AB Stable VIII case is instructive. Mirae tried to walk from a $5.8 billion hotel acquisition at the onset of COVID, invoking the MAC clause. The court disagreed. MAC clauses are extraordinarily difficult to invoke successfully - Delaware courts have set the threshold very high. But the litigation itself was expensive and damaging for both parties regardless of outcome. The lesson: buyers who sign, finance, and then try to exit via MAC will almost certainly lose and will definitely spend money losing.

## Comparable Companies and Valuation in the M&A Context

The valuation work that underlies every M&A pitch is covered in depth in our article on [comparable companies analysis](/technical/comparable-companies-analysis/). The key difference in an M&A context versus a standalone valuation context is that you are also pricing in a control premium - the additional amount an acquirer pays to gain control of a business, which averages 20-40% over the unaffected stock price in public deals.

In 2025, with megadeals dominating value and technology transactions nearly universally referencing AI strategy, control premiums for assets with genuine AI-related data or platform value have expanded. That creates both opportunity - for sellers of the right assets - and pricing discipline challenges for acquirers who are bidding against strategic buyers with different synergy math.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Find a public M&A deal and read the proxy statement** - S-4s and definitive proxy statements (DEF 14A) for public acquisitions are on the SEC's EDGAR database. Read the "Background of the Merger" section. It is a detailed narrative of exactly how the deal process unfolded, including who was contacted, when bids came in, and how the board made its decision.

2. **Build a deal timeline template** - create a simple Excel or PowerPoint timeline for a fictional sell-side process: mandate, CIM preparation, first-round IOIs, second-round management presentations, final bids, exclusivity, signing, regulatory, close. Put realistic weeks next to each phase. This is what bankers use to pitch process plans to clients.

3. **Read one QoE report** - these circulate in the industry. Understanding what a QoE actually examines, and what adjustments are commonly challenged, will make you more credible in any M&A diligence conversation.

4. **Understand the working capital adjustment mechanism** - this single concept generates more post-signing disputes than almost any other M&A provision. Our article on [working capital adjustments in M&A](/technical/working-capital-adjustments-ma/) covers the mechanics in detail.

5. **Track a live deal** - pick any announced acquisition involving a public company, set up Google Alerts for it, and follow it through to close or failure. Read every press release, every regulatory filing, and every news story. Understanding the real-time dynamics of a live deal is irreplaceable experience.
