# Bulge Bracket vs Elite Boutique vs Middle Market: Pay, Hours, Exit Opps Compared

The bulge bracket vs elite boutique debate is one of the most reliably over-complicated conversations in investment banking recruiting. Candidates spend enormous energy trying to optimize a decision they often don't fully understand, using data that's frequently wrong or outdated.

This article is an attempt to give you the actual picture - based on real compensation surveys, honest exit outcome data, and what the work actually looks like in each category. The goal isn't to tell you where to go. It's to make sure you know what you're actually choosing between.

## Defining the Categories

Before comparing anything, you need a working definition of each tier. These have shifted somewhat over the last decade.

**Bulge Brackets** are the large, full-service global banks with capital markets capabilities, sales and trading, research, and investment banking under one roof. Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, Morgan Stanley, Bank of America, Citigroup, Barclays, Deutsche Bank, Credit Suisse (now absorbed into UBS), UBS. These firms can underwrite deals, provide financing, and advise on the same transaction simultaneously - which creates both conflict-of-interest questions and genuine competitive advantages.

**Elite Boutiques** are advisory-only or near-advisory firms that compete directly with bulge brackets for the largest, most complex transactions. The canonical list: Evercore, Centerview Partners, Lazard, Moelis & Company, PJT Partners, Perella Weinberg, Guggenheim. They don't take balance sheet risk, don't underwrite securities (with some exceptions), and rely entirely on advisory relationships and reputation. Centerview in particular has achieved a level of fee productivity per banker that no bulge bracket can match.

**Middle Market Banks** cover transactions roughly in the $50M-$500M enterprise value range, though definitions vary. Jefferies (arguably its own category), Houlihan Lokey, William Blair, Baird, Lincoln International, Harris Williams, Raymond James. Some of these firms - particularly Houlihan Lokey and Jefferies - punch above their weight significantly in certain sectors and deal types.

## Compensation: Where the Numbers Actually Land

This is where people get confused most often, partly because the data shifted dramatically between 2020 and 2024.

Elite boutiques, particularly Centerview and PJT, broke from the traditional compensation hierarchy around 2021-2022. They began paying analysts meaningfully more than bulge brackets - a reversal of the previous order where Goldman was presumed to lead the market.

According to the 2024 Mergers & Inquisitions compensation guide and Wall Street Oasis survey data:

| Firm Category | First-Year Analyst Base | All-In Comp (Base + Bonus) |
|---|---|---|
| Elite Boutique (top tier) | $110,000-$115,000 | $200,000-$250,000+ |
| Bulge Bracket | $110,000 | $170,000-$210,000 |
| Middle Market (top) | $100,000-$110,000 | $150,000-$185,000 |
| Middle Market (regional) | $85,000-$100,000 | $120,000-$155,000 |

At the associate level, the gaps widen. Elite boutique associates at Centerview or Evercore can earn $350,000-$450,000 all-in. Bulge bracket associates typically land in the $250,000-$350,000 range. Middle market associate compensation varies substantially by firm.

The important caveat: compensation data represents central tendencies. Group performance, firm performance, and individual ratings all create meaningful variation around these numbers. A top performer in a strong group at JPMorgan may out-earn an average performer at Evercore. ## Hours: The Honest Picture

The narrative that elite boutiques have better hours than bulge brackets is partially true and often overstated.

At the analyst level, hours across all three categories are genuinely brutal during active deal periods. The differences are real but smaller than the mythology suggests:

- Bulge bracket M&A groups: 80-95 hours per week during deal execution
- Elite boutique: 75-95 hours per week - roughly comparable, sometimes worse at firms like Centerview that run lean staffing models
- Middle market: Varies enormously. Top-tier middle market (Houlihan Lokey, Harris Williams) can run 70-85 hours. Regional middle market often runs 60-75 hours but with less interesting work

The WSO 2024 banking lifestyle survey showed that analysts at elite boutiques reported slightly higher satisfaction scores than bulge bracket peers despite similar hours. The difference was attributed to deal quality and direct senior exposure rather than hours alone.

One Reddit thread that captured this well: "The boutique hours argument is mostly a myth at the analyst level. Where boutiques actually win is that your 85 hours involves real work instead of 20 hours of formatting PowerPoint slides that will never be used."

That last point deserves emphasis. Staffing models differ. Elite boutiques run with fewer analysts per deal team, which means more substantive work per analyst. Bulge brackets staff more heavily and have more process work that flows to junior bankers. Neither is universally better - some people want the volume of exposure a large bank provides, others want depth over breadth.

## Deal Exposure and Training

This is where the bulge bracket vs elite boutique tradeoff gets interesting and where the "right answer" varies by individual.

**Bulge brackets** offer breadth. You'll see more deal types, more asset classes, more transaction structures. The training programs are more formal and better resourced. You'll be exposed to capital markets transactions (IPOs, follow-on offerings, investment-grade debt) that simply don't exist at advisory-only boutiques. If you're uncertain about what corner of finance you want to specialize in, the breadth of a bulge bracket training ground has real value.

**Elite boutiques** offer depth on M&A and restructuring advisory. If you're certain you want M&A or restructuring experience, Evercore or PJT will give you more substantive exposure per year than all but the best bulge bracket M&A groups. Transaction complexity at the top boutiques is legitimately exceptional - Centerview and PJT consistently advise on mega-cap M&A where the advisory fee alone can exceed $50 million.

**Middle market banks** offer a different kind of depth: more complete deal ownership. Analysts at Harris Williams or William Blair often run more of the process independently than analysts at large banks where the VP and associate layers absorb more of the client-facing work. The deals are smaller, the counterparties less sophisticated, but the hands-on experience can be substantial.

## Exit Opportunities: The Actual Data

The conventional wisdom is that bulge brackets - particularly Goldman and JPMorgan - produce the best exit opportunities into private equity. This is true in aggregate but less true in specific segments than it used to be.

**Large-cap PE (Blackstone, KKR, Apollo, Carlyle):** Bulge bracket and elite boutique analysts compete on roughly equal footing. Goldman IBD, JPMorgan M&A, Morgan Stanley M&A, Evercore, Centerview, and PJT all feed these programs. Middle market analysts rarely place at mega-funds directly.

**Upper-middle-market PE ($1B-$10B funds):** All three categories are competitive. Houlihan Lokey and Jefferies analysts regularly place at strong funds in this range. The brand matters less; deal experience and modeling skills matter more.

**Growth equity and venture:** Less bulge-bracket-dependent than buyout PE. Strong middle market analysts with relevant sector experience (healthcare, tech) place well at growth equity firms.

**Hedge funds:** Bulge bracket equities and Goldman TMT analysts have historically placed well at long/short equity funds. The edge is real because equity research exposure at bulge brackets is more relevant to hedge fund work.

**Corporate development:** Middle market analysts often place extremely well in corporate development roles at operating companies. The deal ownership experience and the smaller-deal mindset translate well.

Mergers & Inquisitions has tracked this data across multiple years and consistently finds that the boutique-to-megafund pathway has grown significantly since 2018. The old assumption that Goldman IBD was the only reliable feeder to KKR and Blackstone is empirically outdated. ## The Middle Market Case Nobody Makes Well Enough

Houlihan Lokey deserves special mention because it sits in an awkward categorical position. The firm is technically middle market by fee volume breakdown but runs a restructuring practice that competes directly with elite boutiques, a fairness opinion practice that handles the largest public company transactions, and an M&A advisory practice with genuine sector depth in healthcare, technology, and financial services.

An analyst at Houlihan Lokey in restructuring is not having a "middle market" experience in any meaningful sense. They're working on Chapter 11 reorganizations for companies with billions in debt. The exit opportunities from HL restructuring into distressed debt funds and special situations PE reflect that deal quality.

Similarly, Lincoln International and Harris Williams run efficient, well-structured analyst programs with strong PE placement outcomes in the upper-middle-market. The analyst who dismisses these firms because they're not Goldman is making a decision based on brand perception rather than outcome data.

## What the Choice Actually Comes Down To

| Factor | Bulge Bracket | Elite Boutique | Middle Market |
|---|---|---|---|
| Brand recognition | Highest | High | Moderate to high |
| First-year comp | $170K-$210K all-in | $200K-$250K all-in | $120K-$185K all-in |
| Deal complexity | High (varies by group) | Very high | Moderate to high |
| Training breadth | Best in class | Moderate | Adequate |
| Capital markets exposure | Yes - full access | No (advisory only) | Limited |
| Hours | 80-90 hrs/week | 75-90 hrs/week | 60-85 hrs/week |
| Mega-fund PE placement | Excellent | Excellent | Limited |
| Upper-MM PE placement | Excellent | Excellent | Very good |
| Senior exposure (analyst) | Limited | Higher | Highest |

## A Note on Recruiting Strategy

The practical reality of recruiting is that you apply broadly and take the best offer you receive. Most candidates don't actually choose between Goldman and Centerview - they receive offers from whichever firms made them. The useful framing is less "where should I aim?" and more "if I get offers from multiple categories, how do I decide?"

In that scenario: if compensation is the primary driver, compare specific offers rather than category averages. If deal quality matters most and you're certain about M&A, elite boutique. If optionality matters - the possibility of moving between banking and markets, or between advisory and capital raising - the full-service bulge bracket offers things the boutique structurally cannot.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Pull the actual league table data for your target sector.** Dealogic and Bloomberg publish M&A advisory rankings. Where does your target firm rank in the sector you care about? That's more relevant than overall firm prestige.

2. **Talk to analysts who have completed their two years and placed out.** Ask specifically where they went, what they did, and whether the firm delivered what it promised. Recruiting pitches and reality diverge.

3. **Factor in location.** Elite boutiques are overwhelmingly New York-concentrated. Bulge brackets have strong offices in multiple cities. If you have geographic constraints, your decision set may be narrower than you think.

4. **Don't overweight compensation differences at the analyst level.** The difference between $180K and $220K all-in is meaningful but doesn't compound the way deal experience and exit opportunities do. Over a career, deal quality and exit placement matter more than the first-year bonus differential.

5. **Consider the specific group, not just the firm.** A top-performing coverage group at a middle market bank will give you better outcomes than a slow, understaffed group at a bulge bracket. Research the group's recent deal history and speak to analysts in the specific team before accepting.

The bulge bracket vs elite boutique vs middle market question has no universal right answer. It has a right answer for you, given your goals, your tolerance for different tradeoffs, and the specific offer in front of you.
