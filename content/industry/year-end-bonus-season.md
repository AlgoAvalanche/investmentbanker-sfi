# Year-End Bonus Season: How Numbers Get Set

The year-end bonus is the defining financial event in an investment banker's calendar year. Most junior bankers arrive on the desk with a vague understanding that they will earn "a bonus" at the end of the year, and a vaguer sense that it will be substantial. What they rarely understand is the mechanics - how the number actually gets set, how much of it they control, and why two analysts sitting three desks apart with identical job titles can receive bonuses that differ by $45,000 or more.

This article explains the full system: from the pool-level calculation at the top of the house down to the five-minute meeting where your number gets handed to you. Understanding this process is not just academic. It determines how you position yourself, how you evaluate whether to stay or leave, and whether you will be surprised or informed when comp season arrives.

The short version: your bonus is a function of firm performance, division performance, group performance, and individual performance - in roughly that order of magnitude. Your individual contribution matters less than most junior bankers assume, and your group's revenue year matters more than almost anyone admits.

## The Bonus Pool: Where It All Starts

Investment banks set compensation as a percentage of revenue. The industry convention is that total compensation - salaries, bonuses, benefits - runs somewhere between 35% and 45% of net revenues for the investment banking division. In strong years, firms pay more in absolute terms but typically let the ratio compress slightly. In weak years, the ratio can hold steady while absolute numbers fall sharply.

The process unfolds in four stages.

**Stage 1: Revenue gets counted.** M&A advisory fees, ECM underwriting fees, DCM fees, leveraged finance fees. Each product line's revenue is tallied for the full calendar year. Fourth quarter is often heavily weighted because deals that started in Q2 and Q3 close in Q4.

**Stage 2: The divisional pool gets set.** Senior management and the compensation committee agree on a total dollar amount for the investment banking division. This is where the percentage-of-revenue calculation applies, modified by discretion, competitive positioning, and how many people the firm wants to retain.

**Stage 3: The pool gets subdivided by group.** The divisional pool gets allocated to individual groups - M&A gets its allocation, healthcare gets its allocation, TMT gets its allocation. Groups that generated stronger revenue get proportionally more. A group that completed a $50M fee transaction in October goes to the compensation committee with a different story than a group whose major deals fell through in Q3.

**Stage 4: Group heads allocate to individuals.** Within each group's allocation, the group head and relevant managing directors distribute across their people. This is where individual ratings matter - and where the process is least transparent and most political.

The critical takeaway from this structure is that by the time the conversation reaches the individual level, the total amount available is already determined by forces that have nothing to do with how hard you worked. Your individual performance shapes how a fixed pool is divided, not the size of the pool itself.

## Compensation by Level: The 2025 Numbers

Before getting into the mechanics of how bonuses are determined, it is useful to know what the actual ranges look like. The following table reflects bulge bracket New York City compensation for 2025, sourced from the Mergers & Inquisitions 2026 Investment Banker Salary and Bonus Report and corroborated by Prospect Rock Partners' 2025 market analysis.[^1][^2]

| Level | Base Salary | Bonus Range | Total Comp | Deferred % |
|-------|-------------|-------------|------------|------------|
| Analyst 1 | $110K | $70K-$110K | $180K-$220K | 0% |
| Analyst 2 | $125K | $85K-$140K | $210K-$265K | 0% |
| Analyst 3 | $150K | $100K-$170K | $250K-$320K | 0% |
| Associate 1 | $175K | $100K-$200K | $275K-$375K | 10-20% |
| Associate 2 | $200K | $150K-$250K | $350K-$450K | 10-20% |
| Associate 3 | $225K | $175K-$275K | $400K-$500K | 10-20% |
| Vice President | $250K-$300K | $250K-$400K | $500K-$700K | 20-30% |
| Director/SVP | $300K-$350K | $300K-$500K | $600K-$850K | 20-30% |
| Managing Director | $400K-$600K | $400K-$1M+ | $800K-$1.6M+ | 30-50% |

These ranges are not guarantees - they are the market distribution. Your actual number within a given range depends on the bucket system described below. Elite boutiques (Centerview, Evercore, PJT Partners, Perella Weinberg) paid a 20-40% premium over these bulge bracket ranges at the analyst and associate levels in 2025, while middle-market banks paid roughly 15-25% less for equivalent titles.[^2]

## The Bucket System: Where Your Individual Number Gets Set

Banks use a bucket system - typically three to five tiers - to translate performance ratings into bonus amounts expressed as a percentage of base salary. The mechanics at the analyst level at a bulge bracket look roughly like this, based on data from IB Interview Questions' 2025 compensation guide:[^3]

- Top bucket: 90-105% of base salary
- Upper-middle bucket: 80-90% of base salary
- Middle bucket: 70-80% of base salary
- Lower-middle bucket: 60-70% of base salary
- Bottom bucket: 50-65% of base salary

For a first-year analyst at a $110,000 base salary, the spread between top and bottom bucket is approximately $45,000 - roughly a 40% difference on the same job title, same bank, same year. That gap is not trivial. It is also not primarily a function of how good an analyst you are in isolation.

The key insight - and the one most junior bankers underweight - is this: bucket placement is determined by three variables simultaneously. Individual performance. Group revenue for the year. Overall bank profitability. A top-bucket analyst in a group that had a weak revenue year might receive a smaller absolute bonus than a middle-bucket analyst in a group that generated record fees.[^3] The bucket determines your percentage; the group's pool determines your dollar base.

This is not a design flaw. It is intentional. Banks are revenue-sharing enterprises at their core. Senior management has no interest in paying out above-market compensation for groups that did not generate above-market fees, regardless of how hard individuals in those groups worked.

## The 2025 Bonus Cycle: Where the Money Actually Went

The 2025 bonus cycle - covering the January-December 2024 performance year and paid in early 2025 - illustrates the system in operation. M&A deal volume globally rose 40-50% in 2024. Fee revenues grew 10-20% across the major banks. By any measure, it was a strong year for the investment banking industry.

What happened to junior banker bonuses? They rose approximately 5%.[^1]

This is not a rounding error or a measurement artifact. It reflects a structural reality: the bulk of revenue gains in a recovery cycle flow to senior revenue generators, not to the analysts and associates who staffed the work. According to Prospect Rock Partners' 2025 compensation analysis, total compensation increases by seniority showed a pronounced skew - roughly 5% for analysts and associates, 10-15% for VPs and directors, and 25% or more for managing directors.[^4]

The most dramatic data point from 2025: VP3 bonuses at bulge brackets jumped approximately 56%, from an average of around $309,000 to roughly $483,000. This spike was driven not by VP3 performance specifically, but by intense retention pressure at that level - the pre-MD population that competing firms were aggressively recruiting.[^4] At some banks, MD bonuses exceeded 120% of base salary for the first time in years.

The context for this senior skew comes from the prior cycle. The 2024 bonus season (covering 2023 performance) saw junior banker bonuses spike materially off 2023 lows as banks competed for junior talent following heavy 2022-2023 layoffs. According to eFinancialCareers, the 2024 recovery set a higher baseline from which 2025 growth was more modest.[^5] The senior skew in 2025 was partly a normalization - the junior-heavy 2024 cycle reverting toward the historical pattern where senior bankers capture a disproportionate share of good years.

There was also at least one notable outlier in 2025. Bank of America paid Year 3 Associates $110,000-$150,000 versus $200,000-$300,000+ at comparable institutions. This was widely discussed on Wall Street Oasis and contributed to accelerated lateral movement out of that population.[^4]

## The Stub Bonus: What New Analysts Actually Receive First

Most investment banking analysts join in June or July, midway through the calendar year. This creates a timing problem: the bank runs a December 31 year-end, and bonuses for the full calendar year are paid in January or February. An analyst who joined in July has only six months of service in their first year. They cannot receive a full year-end bonus in January.

The solution is the stub bonus - a prorated payment for partial-year service, paid in January of the first full calendar year. The typical stub bonus range at bulge brackets is $25,000-$40,000; MBA associates in the same situation typically receive $35,000-$50,000.[^3]

A few things to understand about stub bonuses that many new analysts get wrong:

First, the stub is not a precise mathematical fraction. A six-month stub is not exactly half of a projected full-year bonus. It is a prorated approximation based on what your group thinks your full-year bonus would have been, applied to your months of service - with additional discretion applied by the group head.

Second, the stub does not count toward the following year's full bonus. After receiving your stub in January, your bonus cycle resets. Your first full year-end bonus covers the subsequent January-December period and is paid in January of the year after that. Many new analysts are surprised to find they receive two relatively small payments (stub, then a first full bonus) before their comp starts to reflect actual seniority.

Third, if you are considering a lateral move mid-year, the stub calculation matters. Leaving in September means forfeiting your year-end bonus. The bank you are joining needs to make that whole through either a guaranteed bonus or a favorable stub arrangement. According to Prospect Rock Partners' 2025 bonus calendar analysis, this dynamic is one of the primary drivers of lateral move timing.[^6]

## The Bonus Calendar: When the Money Actually Arrives

Bonus season is not a single date. It is a rolling window from approximately December through March, with significant variation by firm type. The general pattern from Prospect Rock Partners' 2025 calendar data:[^6]

- Regional and mid-market banks (RBC, Raymond James, Scotiabank): December or early January
- Bulge brackets and elite boutiques: January through March, with most announcements in late January or early February

This staggered calendar creates a strategic asymmetry in the lateral recruiting market. Bankers at early-paying institutions enter the job market with compensation in hand while peers at bulge brackets are still waiting for January announcements. The practical effect is that early-bonus recipients have stronger positioning in lateral processes because they can negotiate start dates without forfeiting a major payout.

The flip side: October through December is a dry season for lateral recruiting across the industry. Professionals at bonus-dependent firms sit tight. No one wants to give notice in November and forfeit three months of accumulated bonus credit. Most good lateral opportunities surface in February through April after the major BB and EB bonus cycles clear.

## Guaranteed Bonuses: The Recruiting Tool

When banks recruit laterally - from another bank, from a law firm, from business school - they sometimes offer guaranteed bonuses. The candidate is promised a specific bonus amount for year one regardless of firm performance or individual rating.

Guaranteed bonuses serve a practical function: they compensate the incoming banker for the bonus they are forfeiting by leaving their current firm mid-cycle. If you leave in September, you walk away from eleven months of accrued bonus credit. A guaranteed bonus makes the move financially whole.

The terms almost always include clawback provisions. If you leave within one or two years, you must return some or all of the guaranteed amount. This is a cost to understand clearly before signing. The golden handcuff of a guaranteed bonus is real - if the group turns out not to be what you expected, leaving in year one is expensive.

Guaranteed bonuses are more common in buoyant markets when talent is scarce and firms are building groups. In downturns, the bank has more leverage and guaranteed offers become rare. The existence of a guaranteed bonus offer from a competing firm is also one of the few genuine negotiating levers available when an existing employer wants to retain you.

## Deferred Compensation: The Hidden Architecture

At more senior levels, a portion of the bonus is paid in deferred equity or restricted stock units rather than cash. The deferral schedule as of 2025 bulge bracket standards:[^1]

- Analysts: 0% deferred - 100% cash
- Associates: 10-20% deferred
- VPs: 20-30% deferred
- Directors/SVPs: 20-30% deferred
- Managing Directors: 30-50% deferred

Deferred amounts vest over two to five years and are forfeited upon voluntary departure before vesting. This structure is intentional. The firm is not doing you a favor by paying you in stock - it is creating a retention mechanism that becomes progressively more binding as you advance. An MD sitting on $2M in unvested restricted stock has a very different calculus about leaving than an analyst whose entire compensation is in cash.

For junior bankers evaluating offers, the all-cash nature of analyst compensation is genuinely valuable. You receive your full bonus in January. You can do with it what you choose. Once deferred comp enters the picture at the associate level, the headline total compensation number becomes less meaningful than the cash component. A $350,000 total comp package with 20% deferred is $280,000 in cash in year one - a meaningful difference if you are planning a private equity move within 18 months.

Geographic discounts compound this math. According to the Selby Jennings 2025 USA Investment Banking Compensation Guide, secondary U.S. markets (Chicago, Houston, Boston) run 10-15% below New York City; London runs 15-30% below New York at comparable levels; Hong Kong and Singapore are competitive with New York at junior levels but trail at the senior end.[^7]

## The Calibration Process and Its Politics

Banks run calibration sessions where group heads and division heads compare notes across groups. The stated purpose is internal equity - a "strong performer" in TMT should receive roughly similar compensation to a "strong performer" in healthcare, adjusting for group pool availability. The actual purpose includes identifying potential departures and allocating retention resources.

Calibration is where the politics of investment banking are most visible. Group heads advocate for their people. Senior MDs with long-standing relationships with compensation committee members can advocate more effectively than junior group heads with fewer allies. The process is more principled at large banks with robust HR functions and less principled at smaller groups where one powerful MD's opinion can dominate the outcome.

Wall Street Oasis crowdsourced data consistently confirms this dynamic. A common observation in the 2025 bonus megathread: practitioners at Perella Weinberg and Centerview reported materially higher associate and VP compensation than peers at other elite boutiques, driven partly by firm culture and partly by how aggressively senior partners advocated internally.[^8] The formal compensation structure is only part of the story.

The practical implication for junior bankers: visibility with the right senior people is not optional. An analyst who produced excellent work that no managing director witnessed is, from a calibration perspective, indistinguishable from an analyst who did adequate work. Documentation of your contributions matters, but so does the network of advocates who will speak on your behalf when you are not in the room.

## What "Comp Season" Actually Feels Like

The period from November through February involves several simultaneous experiences. The formal performance review process and feedback conversations with your manager. Unofficial signals from senior bankers about where you stand - always ambiguous, frequently misread. Industry-wide rumors about which banks are paying well this year, some accurate and much not. Intense benchmarking conversations with peers who are also operating with incomplete information.

Bonus day itself is typically a brief meeting - five to fifteen minutes - with your group head or a senior HR representative. At the analyst level, the number is presented more than discussed. The room for negotiation is limited, and pushing back on a number rarely changes it.

This does not mean the meeting is useless. The most useful response to a disappointing number is to ask directly: "What would I need to demonstrate in the next twelve months to be at the top of the analyst class?" That question signals seriousness and gives you actionable information about whether staying makes sense.

The gap between what analysts expected and what they received is one of the primary drivers of lateral movement. Many analyst-to-private equity transitions are triggered not by a plan to leave banking but by a disappointing bonus that resets the calculus about staying.

## Common Misconceptions Worth Correcting

**Misconception 1: Junior bankers receive a percentage of the deal fees they worked on.** They do not. A first-year analyst who staffed a $500M acquisition does not receive a commission on that fee. Bonuses come from a pool allocated top-down; the connection between specific transactions and individual comp is indirect and mediated by the group's overall performance.

**Misconception 2: The stub bonus is the first-year bonus.** The stub ($25,000-$40,000) is a partial-year payment, not a full bonus. New analysts often expect their first January payment to reflect a full year's performance. The full cycle begins only in the second January.

**Misconception 3: Elite boutiques always pay better than bulge brackets.** EBs pay 20-40% premiums at analyst and associate levels. MD comp structures at EBs are more variable and not universally superior. The comparison depends heavily on seniority and the specific firm.

**Misconception 4: A strong individual performance rating guarantees a top-tier bonus.** It does not. Top-bucket placement in a weak-revenue group still results in a smaller absolute payout than mid-bucket placement in a strong-revenue group. Group selection is a compensation decision as much as a career decision.

**Misconception 5: Deferred compensation is free money that comes later.** It is compensation subject to market risk and contingent on continued employment. An associate who leaves for private equity 18 months after joining forfeits a meaningful portion of their bonus that was deferred. This is intentional.

## What to Do Before Comp Season

**Know your group's revenue year.** Talk to associates and VPs before November. Were there major closes? Did significant deals fall through in Q3? The pool available for your group is the starting point - everything else is a refinement of that starting point.

**Document your contributions.** Keep a running list of deals worked on, your specific role, and outcomes. Many junior bankers enter review conversations unable to clearly articulate what they did. Your reviewer will not fill in the gaps in your favor.

**Know the market numbers.** Use the [compensation across levels breakdown](/industry/compensation-across-levels/) and the Mergers & Inquisitions annual guide to understand where you should land. You are unlikely to negotiate in the meeting, but knowing whether you are at market helps you decide what to do afterward.

**Read your employment agreement before the meeting.** Understand the deferred compensation terms, any clawback provisions, and the vesting schedule. These terms are binding. Many junior bankers discover them only at the moment they matter.

**Treat the bonus meeting as information-gathering, not negotiation.** At the analyst level, pushing back on a number rarely changes it. The more useful goal is to leave the room with a clear picture of where you stand and what it would take to improve that standing in the next cycle.

The bonus number you receive in January is not just a paycheck. It is your employer's most honest assessment of how much they want to keep you. Read it accordingly.
