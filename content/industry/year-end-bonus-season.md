# Year-End Bonus Season: How Numbers Get Set

The year-end bonus is the defining financial event in an investment banker's calendar year. Understanding how that number gets set - and how much of it you actually control - is more useful than any amount of benchmarking against industry surveys. This article explains the mechanics from the pool level down to the individual check.

The short version: your bonus is a function of firm performance, division performance, group performance, and individual performance - in roughly that order of magnitude. Your individual contribution matters less than most junior bankers assume, and the bank's overall revenue year matters more than almost anyone admits.

## The Bonus Pool: Where It All Starts

Investment banks set compensation as a percentage of revenue. The industry convention has historically been that total compensation - salaries, bonuses, benefits - runs somewhere between 35% and 45% of net revenues for the investment banking division. In strong years, firms pay more in absolute terms but typically let the ratio compress slightly. In weak years, the ratio can hold while absolute numbers fall significantly.

The process looks like this:

**Step 1: Revenue gets counted.** M&A advisory fees, ECM underwriting fees, DCM fees, leveraged finance fees. Each product line's revenue is tallied for the full year. Fourth quarter is often heavily weighted because deals that started in Q2 and Q3 close in Q4.

**Step 2: The divisional pool gets set.** Senior management and the compensation committee agree on a total dollar amount for the investment banking division. This is where the percentage-of-revenue calculation applies, modified by discretion, competitive positioning, and how many people the firm wants to retain.

**Step 3: The pool gets subdivided.** The divisional pool gets allocated down to groups - M&A gets its allocation, healthcare gets its allocation, TMT gets its allocation. Groups that had stronger revenue years get proportionally more of the pool. A group that completed a $50M fee transaction in October is going to the compensation committee with a different story than a group whose major deals fell through.

**Step 4: Group heads allocate to individuals.** Within each group's allocation, the group head and relevant managing directors decide how to distribute across the people in the group. This is where individual ratings matter - and where the process is least transparent and most political.

## Individual Ratings: What They Measure (And Don't)

Most banks run a formal performance review process in the fall. Analysts and associates receive ratings from their direct supervisors and sometimes from others they worked with during the year. At Goldman Sachs, this is a well-institutionalized process with calibration across groups. At other firms, the rigor varies significantly.

The ratings categories differ by firm but generally sort into: top performer, strong performer, solid performer, needs improvement. The distribution is not a pure bell curve - most banks artificially limit the number of "top performer" designations to create scarcity and contain costs.

What ratings actually capture is imperfect. They measure a combination of:
- Technical output quality (model accuracy, document quality, turnaround speed)
- Reliability and responsiveness under pressure
- Client-facing performance (for more senior staff)
- Internal visibility - did the right senior bankers see your work?
- Group-level performance - a strong group year raises all boats

That last point is under-appreciated. If your group had a strong year, the pool available for individual bonuses is larger, which means even average performers in a strong group often do better than strong performers in a weak group. The inverse is equally true and consistently frustrating to high performers in groups that had a slow year.

## The Calibration Process

Banks run compensation calibration sessions where group heads and division heads compare notes across groups. This process exists partly to ensure internal equity - a "strong performer" in TMT should receive roughly similar compensation to a "strong performer" in healthcare, adjusting for group pool availability - and partly to identify stars who might be poached and need to be retained with above-market packages.

Calibration is also where the politics of investment banking are most nakedly visible. Group heads advocate for their people. Senior MDs who have long-standing relationships with compensation committee members can advocate more effectively. The process is more principled at large banks with robust HR functions and less principled at smaller groups where one powerful MD's opinion can dominate.

Wall Street Oasis has extensive threads on this dynamic. A common theme: "You can be the best analyst in your class but if your group head doesn't like you or doesn't fight for you in calibration, you'll get average numbers." This is not cynicism - it's an accurate description of how the process works.

## What "Comp Season" Actually Feels Like

Most banks communicate year-end bonuses in January for the December year-end cycle, with some variation by firm. The period from November through January involves the following experiences, often simultaneously:

- The formal performance review process and feedback conversations with your manager
- Unofficial signals from senior bankers about where you stand (always ambiguous, often misread)
- Industry-wide rumors about which banks are paying well this year (some accurate, much not)
- Intense benchmarking conversations with peers who are also operating with incomplete information
- The actual delivery meeting, typically with your group head or a senior HR person, lasting five to fifteen minutes

The meeting itself is rarely a negotiation. At the analyst level especially, the number is presented more than discussed. Some banks allow associates and VPs to have more of a dialogue, but the room for movement in that conversation is limited. The most useful thing you can do before the meeting is understand the market - know what comparable performers at comparable firms received - so you can make an informed decision about whether to stay.

## Guaranteed Bonuses: The Recruiting Tool

When banks recruit laterally - from another bank, from a law firm, from a business school - they sometimes offer guaranteed bonuses. This means the candidate is promised a specific bonus amount for year one regardless of firm performance or individual rating.

Guaranteed bonuses typically have clawback provisions: if you leave within one or two years, you must return some or all of the guaranteed amount. The purpose is to compensate the incoming banker for the bonus they're forfeiting by leaving their current firm mid-cycle, and to provide certainty that makes the move financially viable.

From the bank's perspective, guaranteed bonuses are a cost of competitive recruiting. They're more common in buoyant markets when talent is scarce and firms are building out groups. They become rare in downturns when the bank has more leverage.

Important: guaranteed bonuses create a specific kind of golden handcuff. If you accept a guaranteed bonus and then want to leave in year one because the group isn't what you expected, the clawback makes that very expensive. Read the terms carefully.

## Stub Bonuses: The Calendar Quirk

A stub bonus is paid for a partial year of service - most commonly when you join mid-year and have not been at the firm for the full bonus cycle. The stub calculation is generally pro-rated against what a full-year bonus would have been, but the pro-ration method varies.

If you join in July and the firm pays bonuses in January, you might receive a stub bonus for six months of service in the first January, then your first full bonus in the following January. Some banks have moved to semi-annual or more frequent bonus payments, which reduces the stub issue but remains less common than the traditional annual cycle.

Stub bonuses matter most for:
- Mid-year lateral hires
- Analysts joining from summer internship conversions at unusual start dates
- Associates promoted mid-year

If you're considering a lateral move mid-year, factor the stub calculation into your total compensation picture. A move in September means leaving before your year-end bonus - the bank you're joining needs to make that whole, either through a guaranteed bonus or a more favorable stub arrangement.

## Deferred Compensation: The Part Nobody Likes Discussing

At more senior levels - associate and above at most banks, analyst level at some - a portion of the bonus is paid in deferred equity or restricted stock units (RSUs) rather than cash. The deferral percentage typically increases with seniority:

- Analyst: small or no deferral, mostly cash
- Associate: 10-20% deferred
- VP: 20-30% deferred
- Director/ED: 30-40% deferred
- MD: 40-50%+ deferred, sometimes higher

Deferred compensation vests over time, typically three to four years. This is both a retention mechanism and a risk-sharing tool - if the bank's stock falls, so does the value of your deferred comp. It also means that the "total compensation" number quoted in recruiting conversations is partially illiquid and subject to market risk.

For junior bankers evaluating offers, the cash component of compensation is more meaningful than the headline number when deferred comp is involved. A $300,000 "total comp" package at an associate level with 20% deferred is $240,000 in cash in year one. The deferred amount will vest if you stay - creating another set of golden handcuffs as you advance.

## Market Context: How Economic Cycles Move Bonuses

Year-end bonuses in investment banking are procyclical in a way that's more extreme than most industries. Deal activity tracks M&A cycles, IPO markets, and credit conditions. When those markets slow, fee revenue falls and bonus pools shrink accordingly. The 2022-2023 period saw significant bonus compression across the industry as M&A volumes dropped sharply from the 2021 peak. Banks that had expanded headcount during the boom laid off staff and cut pools aggressively.

According to the Johnson Associates 2024 compensation report, investment banking bonuses recovered moderately in 2024 as M&A activity rebounded from the 2023 trough, but remained below 2021 peak levels at most firms. The recovery was uneven - debt advisory and leveraged finance recovered faster than equity capital markets as interest rate expectations shifted.

The 2025-2026 period has shown continued recovery, with most major banks reporting increased M&A fee revenue year-over-year. This should translate to improving bonus pools, though the distribution across groups will depend heavily on which sectors have been most active.

## The Benchmarking Problem

Analysts and associates spend enormous energy trying to benchmark their bonus against peers. This is understandable but largely unproductive for several reasons:

1. **People lie.** In both directions. Some exaggerate to appear successful; some understate to avoid resentment. The data you collect informally is unreliable.

2. **Total comp is not all-in cash.** When a peer says "I got $250K," are they including deferred? Are they including the signing bonus? Are they at a firm with a higher or lower base? The comparisons are rarely apples-to-apples.

3. **Different groups in the same bank can pay differently.** A strong group can run above the firm average; a weak group runs below. "What did Goldman pay analysts this year?" has no single answer.

4. **The relevant comparison is your own retention decision.** The useful question is not "did I get what my peer at Morgan Stanley got?" but "is this number sufficient to make me want to stay for another year, given what I know about my alternatives?"

The Mergers & Inquisitions annual compensation guide and Wall Street Oasis surveys provide the best publicly available benchmarks. Use them for general calibration, not precise expectations.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Understand your group's revenue year before comp season.** Talk to associates and VPs. Were there big closes? Did major deals fall through? The pool available for your group is the starting point - everything else is a refinement.

2. **Document your contributions before performance reviews.** Keep a running list of deals worked on, your specific role, and outcomes. Many junior bankers go into review conversations unable to clearly articulate what they did. Your reviewer will not fill in the gaps in your favor.

3. **Know the market numbers going into your bonus meeting.** Use the [compensation across levels breakdown](/industry/compensation-across-levels/) and the M&I annual guide to understand where you should land. You're unlikely to negotiate in the meeting, but knowing whether you're at market helps you decide what to do afterward.

4. **Read your employment agreement before the meeting.** Understand the deferred compensation terms, any clawback provisions, and the vesting schedule. These terms are binding and many junior bankers discover them only when they matter.

5. **Treat the bonus meeting as information gathering, not negotiation.** At the analyst level, pushing back on a number rarely changes it. The more useful response to a disappointing number is to ask directly: "What would I need to demonstrate in the next twelve months to be at the top of the analyst class?" That question signals maturity and gives you actionable information about whether staying makes sense.
