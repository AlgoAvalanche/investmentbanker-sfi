# Goldman Sachs vs JPMorgan vs Morgan Stanley: What Group Culture Actually Looks Like

The Goldman Sachs vs JPMorgan debate has occupied Wall Street recruiting forums, alumni happy hours, and Sunday-night anxiety spirals for decades. Add Morgan Stanley into the mix and you have three institutions that collectively define the upper tier of bulge bracket banking - yet operate with surprisingly different internal cultures, deal flow patterns, and unwritten rules about how you advance.

This article will not tell you which bank is "best." That framing is lazy and wrong. It will tell you what each place actually feels like from the inside, where the cultural fault lines run, and how those differences should influence your decision if you are lucky enough to choose between them. It will also ground that conversation in current numbers - because the league table reality of 2025 is sharply different from the mythology that gets recycled on recruiting forums.

## The Numbers First

Before getting into culture, understand where these banks actually stand in the market today, because the data shapes the work you will do and the deal flow you will touch.

According to [ION Analytics' FY 2025 M&A rankings](https://ionanalytics.com/insights/mergermarket/goldman-sachs-jpmorgan-top-fy25-ma-rankings-in-a-year-where-size-mattered/), Goldman Sachs led global M&A advisory with $1.66 trillion in deal volume, representing 36.4% market share and 38.9% year-on-year growth across 515 transactions including 40 megadeals. JPMorgan ranked second at $1.44 trillion - up 44.2% year-on-year - while Morgan Stanley took third with $1.17 trillion, growing 32.8%. This happened inside a global M&A market that reached $4.8 trillion in 2025, the second-highest total on record, driven by 70+ transactions exceeding $10 billion each.

On investment banking revenue, JPMorgan has the largest absolute numbers. [According to compiled earnings data from SEC filings](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0000886982/000119312525079625/d750054dex991.htm), JPMorgan posted $7.29 billion in IB revenues across the first nine months of 2025, up 12% year-on-year. Morgan Stanley generated $5.21 billion over the same period, up 15%. Goldman's M&A advisory fees alone reached $3.37 billion in the first nine months of 2025, up 31% year-on-year - a number that reflects the premium on advisory work versus total IB revenues that include underwriting.

The pattern is consistent and has been for several years: JPMorgan is the largest investment bank by total revenue; Goldman leads in pure advisory market share and prestige deal quality; Morgan Stanley sits in a strong third with distinct advantages in equities and a uniquely large wealth management base that fundamentally changes the firm's character. Understanding this hierarchy tells you a great deal about what day-to-day life looks like inside each institution.

## The Baseline: Three Banks, Three Self-Images

Before getting into specifics, understand the lens each institution uses to view itself.

**Goldman Sachs** believes it is the standard. Not a standard - the standard. The culture is built around the premise that Goldman's judgment, Goldman's processes, and Goldman's people represent the correct way to do finance. This manifests as genuine confidence among the best people there and insufferable arrogance among the rest.

**JPMorgan** sees itself as the universal bank done right - combining investment banking, commercial banking, asset management, and markets under one roof without the dysfunction that has plagued competitors. The cultural ethos is more operational than philosophical. Scale, execution, process.

**Morgan Stanley** occupies an interesting middle ground. Historically the white-shoe counterpart to Goldman, the firm spent the 2008 crisis redefining itself and emerged with a stronger identity around wealth management and equities than pure M&A advisory. The culture today reflects that transition - more balanced, arguably less intense, depending heavily on which division you sit in.

## Goldman Sachs: Culture Deep Dive

### The Partnership Mythology

Goldman's internal identity is still organized around the partnership structure, even though the firm went public in 1999. Partners remain a class of their own - roughly 400-500 people out of 45,000+ employees. The elevation ceremony happens every two years and is treated with the gravity of a papal conclave.

For analysts and associates, this creates a specific psychological environment: you are never confused about where you stand in the hierarchy, and you are never allowed to forget that the people above you cleared a bar you have not reached yet. This can be motivating or suffocating, depending on your temperament.

### Deal Flow and Coverage

Goldman's Investment Banking Division is organized around industry coverage groups - TMT, healthcare, FIG, consumer retail, natural resources - plus product groups like M&A and ECM. The M&A group at Goldman is genuinely elite. The $1.66 trillion in 2025 deal volume was not an accident; it reflects decades of cultivating relationships at the CEO and board level, particularly in the technology sector where Goldman's TMT franchise has advised on some of the decade's defining transactions.

The caveat worth stating plainly: junior bankers do not always touch the interesting work. Process work - formatting, model updates, CIM drafts at 2am - is universal across banks. Goldman's version is done under more pressure and with higher internal expectations of what "good" looks like. The quality bar on deliverables is genuinely higher. Whether that is worth the additional stress is a personal calculation.

### Compensation and Hours

Goldman first-year analysts earn approximately $110,000 base with all-in compensation reaching $170,000-$200,000 after bonus in strong years. This is competitive with JPMorgan and Morgan Stanley but not necessarily the top of the market - elite boutiques like Centerview and PJT regularly pay more at junior levels in strong deal years.

Hours are long but not uniquely brutal. Survey data from Wall Street Oasis and comparable platforms shows Goldman IBD analysts averaging 80-90 hours per week during active deal periods, consistent with JPMorgan M&A and Morgan Stanley IBD groups. The difference is not the hours - it is the ambient pressure and the cultural expectation of constant availability and institutional pride that distinguishes Goldman from peers.

### The Goldman Identity Filter

One well-upvoted thread on Wall Street Oasis captured the culture accurately: "Goldman is the only bank where mid-level people will casually mention the firm's name in sentences as if it is a person with opinions. 'Goldman wouldn't do it that way.' 'Goldman thinks about this differently.' You either buy into that or you find it exhausting within six months."

That is a fair characterization. The Goldman identity is strong enough that it functions as a genuine filter. People who thrive there tend to genuinely believe in the institutional identity, not just the paycheck. If you find institutional mythology motivating rather than grating, Goldman will push you harder than anywhere else in a way that compounds over time.

## JPMorgan: Culture Deep Dive

### Scale as Strategy

JPMorgan is the largest bank in the United States by assets and runs one of the most profitable investment banking franchises globally. The culture reflects that scale. Where Goldman operates like a partnership with strong philosophical coherence, JPMorgan operates like a large, well-run corporation with clear processes, defined career ladders, and genuine diversity of business lines.

This is not a criticism. For many people it is a feature. JPMorgan's investment bank sits inside a universal banking structure, which means deal flow and client relationships often originate through the commercial bank, private bank, or asset management divisions. Cross-selling is real and institutionalized in a way it is not at Goldman or Morgan Stanley.

### Leveraged Finance - A Genuine Differentiator

JPMorgan's leveraged finance franchise deserves specific attention because it is a genuine competitive differentiator, not just a marketing claim. The firm consistently leads or co-leads leveraged buyout financing for major private equity sponsors, and the 2025 data reinforced this: JPMorgan co-led the Electronic Arts $55 billion leveraged buyout alongside Goldman Sachs - [the largest LBO in history](https://pe-insights.com/jpmorgan-and-goldman-land-biggest-lbo-in-history-with-55bn-ea-deal/). That a deal of that size required both Goldman and JPMorgan says something about the depth of JPMorgan's LevFin capabilities. One bank was not enough to absorb the risk.

If your goal is to understand the intersection of banking and private credit - how LBO financing is structured, how sponsor relationships work at the highest levels, how debt capital markets connect to M&A advisory - JPMorgan gives you proximity to more of that deal flow than either Goldman or Morgan Stanley as standalone franchises.

### Group Variation Is Significant

JPMorgan's IBD has some of the best industry groups on the Street - healthcare, industrials, and TMT consistently rank at the top of league tables in their respective sectors. The M&A group is strong but operates differently than at Goldman. M&A at JPMorgan is more integrated with coverage groups than it is a standalone elite unit. Whether that is better or worse depends on what you want.

### Culture on the Ground

JPMorgan's culture tends toward functional competence rather than philosophical intensity. The hierarchy is real but less mythologized. Senior bankers manage books of business with a commercial orientation - maintaining relationships, winning mandates, executing deals. The Goldman-style culture of "this is the correct way to think about things" is largely absent.

One former analyst described the difference on Reddit's r/FinancialCareers: "JPM felt like working at a very high-performing company. Goldman felt like being in a cult. I mean that as a neutral observation - both can work depending on who you are."

Managing Directors at JPMorgan tend to have longer average tenures at the firm than at Goldman, where partner-track pressure sometimes accelerates departures. That creates more institutional knowledge at the senior levels and arguably better mentorship in some groups.

### Career Path Considerations

JPMorgan's size creates genuine internal mobility that smaller institutions cannot match. Lateral moves between groups, between product and coverage, between geographies - all of these are more common at JPMorgan than at boutiques or even Goldman. For someone uncertain about their long-term direction, that optionality has real value. You can enter one group and move. You can discover a sector you like. The firm is large enough to absorb those transitions without it being treated as unusual.

## Morgan Stanley: Culture Deep Dive

### The Post-Crisis Identity

Morgan Stanley's current form was shaped significantly by the 2008 financial crisis and the subsequent strategic shift under James Gorman. The firm doubled down on wealth management through acquisitions including Smith Barney and E*Trade, making it structurally less dependent on investment banking cyclicality than Goldman or JPMorgan.

The numbers show exactly how far that transformation has gone. [Morgan Stanley's wealth management division generated $31.7 billion in net revenues in FY 2025](https://www.familywealthreport.com/article.php/Morgan-Stanley's-Group,-Wealth-Net-Revenues-Rise-In-Q4-2025) - an all-time record and representing approximately 44.5% of the firm's total revenue. To put that in context: nearly half of Morgan Stanley's revenue now comes from a division that has nothing to do with M&A advisory or capital markets. Goldman and JPMorgan have nothing comparable in relative terms.

Inside the investment bank, this creates an interesting dynamic. IBD at Morgan Stanley is excellent - the firm ranked third globally in M&A advisory in 2025 with $1.17 trillion in deal volume - but it operates within a firm where investment banking is one important division rather than the defining identity. That changes the internal culture in subtle ways. IBD bankers at Morgan Stanley do not carry the same "this is the whole firm" weight that Goldman bankers carry. Whether that feels like relief or irrelevance depends on your personality.

### Equities Franchise

Morgan Stanley's equities business is legitimately world-class and worth treating as a distinct draw. [Prime brokerage data from 2025](https://info.radientanalytics.com/blog/prime-broker-q1-2025) shows Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, and Morgan Stanley holding nearly 60% combined market share in prime brokerage, with Goldman and Morgan Stanley as the only two firms with more than 50% coverage of the BDC market. Morgan Stanley led all banks in Q1 2025 fund additions with 226 new relationships - ahead of JPMorgan's 210 and Goldman's 176.

Prime brokerage, equity research, and equity capital markets at Morgan Stanley consistently rank at or near the top of industry surveys. If you are interested in markets-oriented work rather than pure advisory, Morgan Stanley competes directly with Goldman rather than sitting behind it.

For IBD specifically, Morgan Stanley's technology coverage is where the firm's reputation is strongest. The TMT group has advised on some of the largest technology transactions of the last decade. If you want to work on tech M&A with an elite coverage franchise, Morgan Stanley belongs in any serious conversation alongside Goldman.

### Culture at the Junior Level

Morgan Stanley is often described - including by people who work there - as the most balanced of the three firms. The hierarchy exists but is less oppressive than Goldman's. The commercial scale of JPMorgan is absent, creating a somewhat more focused banking identity without the institutional mythology overlay.

[Career research from Wall Street Mastermind](https://wallstmastermind.com/goldman-sachs-vs-morgan-stanley-vs-jpmorgan/) describes Morgan Stanley as "slightly more human and relationship-oriented than Goldman - less intensely competitive internally, with a culture that emphasizes long-term client relationships over transactional deal-making." That tracks with what junior bankers consistently report: strong mentorship at the senior level, collegial teams, and an environment where competent execution is rewarded without requiring you to become a true believer in the institutional brand.

Hours are long but not materially different from peers. The compensation structure at the analyst level is identical to Goldman and JPMorgan - $110,000 base with all-in first-year compensation of $150,000-$190,000 depending on group performance. The marginal differences in compensation between these three banks at the analyst level are not a meaningful input into your decision.

## The Culture Factor Nobody Talks About Enough

Group politics vary enormously within each bank, and the group you land in matters more than the firm name in most respects. This is not a hedge or a caveat. It is the single most important variable in your two-year experience.

A strong group at JPMorgan - good deal flow, functional senior team, reasonable culture, active deal calendar - will produce better outcomes than a dysfunctional group at Goldman. The senior banker who will actually mentor you, the associate who will teach you how to model, the analyst class you will work alongside at 2am - these things are determined by group, not by firm. See [Group Politics: Read the Room](/recruiting-insider/group-politics-read-the-room/) for how to evaluate this during recruiting.

The data from analyst surveys on Wall Street Oasis and comparable platforms supports this consistently: within-firm variation by group exceeds between-firm variation in day-to-day experience. The Goldman healthcare group and the Goldman TMT group have different cultures, different senior teams, different deal types, and different reputations in the recruiting market. Generic Goldman vs JPMorgan comparisons miss this entirely.

The corollary is that your research should go a level deeper than firm. Talk to analysts who sat in the specific group at the specific bank you are targeting. Ask about the MD team, not just the firm. Ask about recent deal activity in that group, not just the firm's league table ranking. The information you need to make a good decision is available - it just requires more specific conversations than most candidates bother to have.

## What You Should Do Next

**1. Talk to people in specific groups, not just at firms.** The Goldman healthcare group and the Goldman TMT group have different cultures, different senior teams, and different deal types. Generic comparisons miss this entirely.

**2. Identify what you want from the two years.** If you want the most intense environment and the strongest brand name for conversations at cocktail parties, Goldman. If you want a commercially oriented, less ideologically charged experience with genuine institutional scale and the best leveraged finance platform on the Street, JPMorgan. If you want M&A depth in a more balanced firm culture with a world-class equities franchise alongside it, Morgan Stanley.

**3. Check recent league table performance in your target sector.** Use [LSEG's investment banking league tables](https://www.lseg.com/en/data-analytics/financial-data/deals-data/investment-banking-league-tables) or Bloomberg's public data to see which bank has been most active in the industry you care about. Where the deals are happening is where you will learn the most.

**4. Read the [compensation across levels breakdown](/industry/compensation-across-levels/) before making decisions based on pay.** At the analyst level, differences between these three banks are small enough that culture and deal flow matter more than the marginal compensation delta. The meaningful compensation divergence does not appear until VP and above.

**5. Do not romanticize the Goldman brand without validating it with real conversations.** The brand is real. The mythology around it is partially constructed. Talk to Goldman analysts who left after two years and Goldman analysts who stayed. You will get a more accurate picture than from any forum thread.

The Goldman Sachs vs JPMorgan vs Morgan Stanley question is ultimately a question about what kind of work environment you perform best in - not which bank has the best name or the highest league table ranking in a given year. The 2025 numbers confirm that all three institutions are running at elite levels. The $4.8 trillion global M&A market created record volume across all three franchises simultaneously. In that environment, the differentiation that matters is not firm brand - it is group quality, senior banker access, and the fit between your working style and the culture you are walking into.

Get that right and the rest follows.
