# Industry Group vs Product Group: Where Bankers Actually Want to Land

The industry group vs product group question is one of the first things new banking recruits confront once they have an offer. The answer matters - not because one path is objectively better, but because they produce meaningfully different skill sets, different deal exposures, and different exit opportunities.

Most candidates think about this question in terms of what sounds interesting. That's a reasonable starting point but an insufficient one. The more useful frame is: what do I want to know how to do in three years, and which group structure gives me the most direct path to that knowledge?

## The Basic Structure

Investment banking departments at major banks are organized into two broad categories:

**Industry groups (also called coverage groups)** focus on a specific sector of the economy and cover companies within that sector from cradle to grave - meaning they maintain relationships across all transaction types: M&A advisory, equity offerings, debt issuance, leveraged buyouts, restructurings. The banker in an industry group is a generalist within a vertical. Common industry groups:

- Technology, Media, and Telecom (TMT)
- Healthcare and Life Sciences
- Financial Institutions Group (FIG)
- Consumer and Retail
- Industrials
- Energy, Power, and Natural Resources
- Real Estate
- Financial Sponsors (coverage of private equity firms)

**Product groups** specialize in a specific type of transaction or capital markets activity and execute those transactions across sectors, typically in partnership with coverage groups. Common product groups:

- Mergers and Acquisitions (M&A)
- Equity Capital Markets (ECM)
- Debt Capital Markets (DCM)
- Leveraged Finance (LevFin)
- Restructuring (sometimes a separate division)

The organizational model varies by bank. At Goldman Sachs, M&A is a standalone group that co-advises with industry coverage. At JPMorgan, M&A is more integrated into coverage. At Morgan Stanley, the product-coverage interaction model has evolved several times. The key takeaway is that the label "M&A group" or "TMT group" means different things at different banks.

## Industry Groups: Depth in a Vertical

### What You Actually Do

In a coverage group, you are responsible for knowing everything about a set of companies - their business models, their capital structures, their competitive positioning, their strategic priorities - and being ready to advise them on any financial transaction they might undertake. The relationships are long-term. Bankers in strong coverage groups have relationships with CFOs and CEOs that span decades.

For analysts and associates, this translates to a broader but sometimes less technically deep experience than product groups. In a given week, you might be modeling an M&A scenario, updating an equity valuation for a pitch, working on a DCM financing model, and helping prepare materials for a follow-on equity offering. The variety is real. The breadth can be exciting or overwhelming depending on the person.

### The TMT Group Case Study

TMT is worth discussing separately because it's the most sought-after coverage group at most banks and illustrates the best version of what a coverage group can be.

Technology M&A has been one of the most active sectors globally for the last two decades. The transactions are large (megacap technology deals regularly exceed $20-50 billion), the strategic dynamics are fascinating (platform competition, vertical integration, regulatory risk), and the fee productivity is exceptional. An analyst in Goldman TMT or Morgan Stanley TMT will work on deals that define the industry.

The downside: TMT is highly competitive to get into, both at the recruiting stage and through internal rotations at banks with group selection processes. Overflow deals - smaller, less interesting transactions that pad the deal count - exist in TMT as everywhere. And the sector's valuation methods are sometimes more art than science, which creates room for both creativity and imprecision.

### FIG: The Misunderstood Group

Financial Institutions Group (FIG) is consistently misunderstood by recruiting candidates. It covers banks, insurance companies, asset managers, fintech companies, and other financial services firms. The sector is analytically demanding - financial institution valuation is genuinely different from industrial company valuation, requiring different frameworks for capital adequacy, regulatory capital, and embedded value.

FIG also has excellent deal flow. Bank M&A, insurance acquisitions, and asset management consolidation have all been active. The exit opportunities are different from other groups - strong FIG exits include private equity firms that specialize in financial services, growth equity firms focused on fintech, and directly to financial services operating companies.

The reputation issue: FIG is sometimes dismissed as "not real M&A" because the transaction structures and analytical frameworks differ from industrial M&A. This is wrong. FIG deal complexity is high, the regulatory overlay is substantive, and the analysts who come through FIG with strong technical skills are well-positioned for a range of opportunities.

## Product Groups: Depth in a Transaction Type

### What You Actually Do

In a product group, you become a specialist in a specific type of transaction. M&A groups focus on all phases of the deal process - strategy development, valuation, deal structuring, negotiation, regulatory considerations, and closing. ECM groups focus on equity offerings and IPOs. DCM groups focus on investment-grade and high-yield debt issuance. LevFin groups focus on leveraged loans and high-yield bonds for private equity transactions.

The depth is genuine. An analyst who spends two years in a pure M&A group will have significantly stronger deal structuring and valuation skills than an analyst who spent two years in a coverage group handling a mix of transaction types. Whether that deeper specialization serves your exit goals depends on where you want to go.

### M&A Groups: The Prestige Premium

At banks where M&A is a standalone product group - Goldman Sachs, Lazard, and several others - the group carries genuine prestige and genuine selectivity. The analytical standards are the highest in the building. The deal quality is exceptional. The hours are reliably brutal.

The trade-off: standalone M&A groups often have less client contact for junior bankers and more process-heavy execution work. You become technically excellent but may emerge without strong sector expertise or the ability to originate transactions independently. This matters for the specific question of whether you want to eventually become an investment banking MD (which requires origination skills that M&A execution doesn't always develop) versus exiting to private equity (where the M&A technical background translates directly).

Wall Street Oasis has an extensive debate on whether M&A group prestige justifies the narrower skill set development. The honest answer is: it depends on your exit. For top buyout PE, M&A group experience is highly valued. For a more traditional banking career or for sector-specialist PE, coverage group experience may serve you better.

### LevFin: The Private Equity Adjacency

Leveraged Finance is the product group with the tightest relationship to private equity. LevFin bankers structure and distribute the debt that funds leveraged buyouts - they work alongside financial sponsors on nearly every large PE deal, modeling capital structures, assessing debt capacity, and marketing loans and bonds to institutional investors.

The exit opportunities from LevFin are distinct from M&A or coverage: credit funds, direct lending firms, mezzanine funds, and private credit platforms are natural landing spots. For candidates interested in the credit side of private equity rather than the equity side, LevFin provides the most direct training.

The work is technical and market-dependent in a way that M&A advisory is not. LevFin analysts spend significant time tracking secondary credit markets, understanding CLO structures, and managing the syndication process - skills that are valuable but less portable to pure equity PE roles.

### Restructuring: The Counter-Cyclical Option

Restructuring is either a product group within investment banking or a standalone division, depending on the bank. It advises companies in financial distress - on the debtor side, helping companies navigate bankruptcy and financial reorganization, or on the creditor side, representing distressed debt holders.

The demand for restructuring advisory is counter-cyclical: when M&A volumes fall and credit markets tighten, distressed situations increase. This makes restructuring groups more insulated from economic downturns than M&A or capital markets groups. The 2008-2010 period and the 2020 COVID-related distress wave both generated substantial restructuring mandates while M&A activity cratered.

The analytical skill set is distinct: distressed valuation, priority of claims, plan of reorganization modeling, DIP financing. The exit opportunities include distressed debt funds, special situations PE, and credit-focused hedge funds. For candidates comfortable with complexity and ambiguity in the legal and financial structure of deals, restructuring is intellectually one of the most demanding areas in banking.

## How to Think About the Choice

The industry group vs product group decision comes down to three factors:

**1. Exit destination:** For buyout PE at large funds, M&A group or strong coverage group experience is the standard path. For credit funds and direct lending, LevFin or restructuring is more directly relevant. For sector-specialist PE (healthcare-focused funds, tech growth equity), coverage group sector knowledge is valued. Be honest about where you actually want to go.

**2. How you learn:** Some people develop skills through breadth - seeing many transaction types and building pattern recognition across sectors. Coverage groups are better for this. Others develop through depth - doing the same transaction type repeatedly with increasing sophistication. Product groups accelerate depth faster. Neither learning style is better in the abstract.

**3. Long-term banking path:** If you want to become an investment banking MD, coverage groups generally develop the client relationship skills and sector expertise that origination requires. Product group MDs exist, but the career path to the top of an M&A group is narrower and more competitive.

One well-upvoted WSO comment articulates the trade-off bluntly: "M&A group gives you the best technical training and the Goldman brand. Coverage group gives you relationships, sector knowledge, and a more realistic path to staying in banking long-term. Know which one you actually want before you spend two years optimizing for the other."

## The Reality of Group Placement

At most banks, analysts don't choose their groups - they're assigned to them based on headcount needs, recruiting preferences expressed during the process, and pure availability. The group you want and the group you get can diverge significantly.

This matters for evaluating an offer. Before accepting, find out specifically which group you're joining and do real diligence on that group: who runs it, what deals they've completed in the last two years, how analysts describe the culture, and where recent analysts placed after departing. A strong group in a non-glamorous sector is better than a weak group in a prestigious one.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Identify your target exit before you have a group preference conversation.** Recruiters ask about group preferences. Your answer should be informed by where you want to go, not just what sounds interesting. Large-cap buyout PE? M&A group or top coverage group. Credit investing? LevFin or restructuring. Sector-specialist investing? The relevant coverage group.

2. **Research the specific group at each bank you're interviewing with.** TMT at Morgan Stanley and TMT at JPMorgan are not the same. Pull recent deals, look at senior banker tenure, and talk to analysts who have been in each group for at least 18 months.

3. **Ask directly about staffing models during informational interviews.** How many analysts per deal team? What does the division of work look like between analyst and associate? How often do analysts attend client meetings? These questions tell you more about your actual experience than any categorical description.

4. **Don't dismiss non-glamorous groups without understanding their deal flow.** FIG, industrials, and natural resources consistently produce excellent exit opportunities for analysts who develop real sector knowledge. The TMT competition is fierce; the competition for a seat at a strong industrials or energy group is lower with comparable outcomes.

5. **See the [group preference interview questions guide](/interview-prep/group-preference-questions/) before your interviews.** Banks ask about group preferences explicitly, and your answer is an opportunity to demonstrate sector knowledge and strategic thinking - not just to state a preference.
