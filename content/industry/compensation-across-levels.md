# Compensation Across Levels: Analyst, Associate, VP, Director, MD

Investment banking compensation is not a secret but it is systematically misunderstood. Candidates overestimate first-year all-in numbers, underestimate mid-career variability, and almost universally underappreciate how dramatically the comp model changes between junior and senior levels.

This article draws on verified data from [Mergers & Inquisitions' 2026 salary and bonus report](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banker-salary/), [Wall Street Prep's analyst salary guide](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/investment-banking-analyst-salary-guide/), [Wall Street Prep's associate salary guide](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/investment-banking-associate-salary-guide/), and [Mergers & Inquisitions' managing director career guide](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banking-managing-director/). Where figures come from those sources, they are cited. Where they represent synthesis or structural observation, that is stated clearly.

One important framing note before the numbers: compensation in investment banking is a range, not a point estimate. The analyst at Goldman TMT in a strong year and the analyst at a regional middle market bank in a slow year both have "analyst salaries" - and those numbers can differ by $50,000 or more all-in. Use the figures below as calibration ranges, not guarantees.

A second framing note relevant to current market conditions: the 2025 year-end bonus cycle was not evenly distributed. According to Mergers & Inquisitions' tracking, total revenue rose 10-20% industry-wide that year, yet junior banker compensation increased only approximately 5%, while managing directors saw compensation rise 25% or more. Understanding that asymmetry matters when you're evaluating where you are in the hierarchy and what the near-term trajectory looks like.

## Analyst Compensation (Years 1-3)

The analyst base salary was standardized more completely than any other level after Goldman Sachs raised analyst pay in 2021 and peers followed within weeks. According to Wall Street Prep, "$100,000 became the new standard at bulge bracket investment banks" at that point. The market has since moved modestly upward, with elite boutiques now starting analysts at $110,000 versus the $100,000 bulge bracket baseline.

**Current market figures (New York, 2024-2025):**

| Year | Base Salary | Bonus Range | All-In (Total Comp) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Analyst Year 1 | $100,000-$110,000 | $70,000-$100,000 | $170,000-$225,000 |
| Analyst Year 2 | $105,000-$110,000 | $85,000-$115,000 | $185,000-$225,000+ |
| Analyst Year 3 | $110,000-$125,000 | $95,000-$140,000 | $200,000-$250,000 |

The ceiling figures reflect elite boutique performance. Mergers & Inquisitions reported that Year 2 analysts at firms like Lazard and Evercore reached $250,000 all-in in strong years - these represent top-of-range outcomes, not midpoints. The $165,000-$225,000 range is more representative of what a competent analyst at a bulge bracket or strong middle market bank actually earns.

At top-tier middle market banks - Houlihan Lokey, Harris Williams, William Blair - expect all-in compensation somewhat below the bulge bracket ceiling but within the same general range. Regional middle market banks vary more widely on the downside.

**London benchmarks** for analysts, per Wall Street Prep's data: Year 1 totals of approximately £95,000-£105,000, Year 2 of £100,000-£125,000, and Year 3 of £125,000-£145,000. Mergers & Inquisitions applies a broader rule of thumb: expect a "15-30% discount" to New York figures when converting to USD equivalents.

**What analysts actually take home:** After federal income tax, New York City state and local taxes, and the cost of living in one of the most expensive cities in the world, the analyst earning $190,000 all-in is not wealthy. The number is excellent relative to most early-career jobs. It is not what the headline suggests after you account for 80-100 hour weeks, a studio apartment in Midtown, and the general cost of surviving banker hours. This is not a complaint - it is context for why people who join purely for compensation often do not last long enough to see the associate jump.

## Associate Compensation (Years 1-3)

The associate level is where compensation trajectories begin to diverge meaningfully based on firm tier and individual performance. MBA associates and promoted analysts are typically paid on the same scale at most banks, though firms occasionally offer retention packages to prevent strong analysts from leaving for business school.

According to Wall Street Prep's associate salary guide, current base salaries in New York run $150,000 in Year 1, $175,000 in Year 2, and $200,000 in Year 3. Bonus ranges are $90,000-$130,000 in Year 1, widening to $120,000-$250,000 by Year 3. All-in totals range from $240,000-$270,000 in Year 1 to $320,000-$450,000 in Year 3.

**Current market figures (New York, 2024-2025):**

| Year | Base Salary | Bonus Range | All-In (Total Comp) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Associate Year 1 | $150,000-$175,000 | $90,000-$175,000 | $240,000-$375,000 |
| Associate Year 2 | $175,000-$200,000 | $100,000-$215,000 | $275,000-$415,000 |
| Associate Year 3 | $200,000-$225,000 | $120,000-$250,000 | $320,000-$450,000 |

The elite boutique premium at this level is well-documented. Mergers & Inquisitions specifically identified Perella Weinberg Partners and Centerview Partners as paying "$50,000-$100,000+ more" in bonuses than bulge bracket peers, predominantly in cash rather than deferred stock. A strong Year 3 associate at those firms can approach or exceed $500,000 all-in in a good year.

Deferred compensation begins in earnest at the associate level. At most bulge brackets, 10-20% of the bonus is paid in restricted stock units with a three to four year vesting schedule. This means the headline all-in total is partially illiquid - the cash component for an associate earning $350,000 total may be $280,000-$315,000 after deferral. This distinction matters when comparing offers and when evaluating the true cost of a lateral move.

One nuance noted by Mergers & Inquisitions is worth flagging: at the associate level, "bonuses do not necessarily have much to do with deal flow, reputation, or ranking of the bank" in the way candidates expect. Other factors - group performance, individual ratings, year-end pool mechanics - drive the outcome in ways that are not fully transparent from the outside. The [year-end bonus mechanics breakdown](/industry/year-end-bonus-season/) covers this in detail.

London associate compensation, per Wall Street Prep: Year 1 all-in of approximately £160,000-£165,000, rising to £210,000-£250,000 by Year 3.

## VP Compensation (Years 1-3)

The VP level is where banking compensation becomes significantly less standardized. Base salaries are set by firm policy, but bonus variation increases substantially because VP performance is harder to measure purely by execution output and begins to include early-stage client development and deal leadership.

According to Mergers & Inquisitions, VP total compensation ranges from approximately $525,000 to $800,000. Base salaries run $250,000-$300,000. Bonuses begin at roughly 100% of base in Year 1 and can reach 200-250% of base by Year 3 for strong performers. Year-over-year, VP compensation grew 10-15% in the 2025 cycle.

**Current market figures (New York, 2024-2025):**

| Year | Base Salary | Bonus Range | All-In (Total Comp) |
|---|---|---|---|
| VP Year 1 | $250,000-$275,000 | $175,000-$300,000 | $425,000-$575,000 |
| VP Year 2 | $275,000 | $200,000-$350,000 | $475,000-$625,000 |
| VP Year 3 | $275,000-$300,000 | $225,000-$400,000 | $500,000-$700,000 |

Deferred compensation increases at this level - typically 20-30% of the bonus at most bulge brackets. A VP earning $600,000 total comp may receive $420,000-$450,000 in cash that year, with the remainder vesting over three to four years. The unvested deferred compensation is a deliberate retention mechanism - leaving a senior VP role means forfeiting real dollars, not just an inconvenience.

The VP level is also where many people stall. Promotion from VP to Director is not automatic and requires demonstrated ability to manage deal processes independently and develop early client relationships. Banks are increasingly willing to wait out VPs who are not building a business case for advancement. The skills that earned the VP promotion - technical execution, managing analysts, running processes - are necessary but no longer sufficient for the next step.

For a full discussion of what the VP-to-MD transition actually requires, see [The MD Track: What Changed](/recruiting-insider/md-track-what-changed/).

## Director / Executive Director Compensation

The Director or Executive Director level (Goldman Sachs uses "Vice President" where most other banks use "Director" or "ED") is a staging area between VP and MD. It is not designed as a permanent rank - it exists to give banks and candidates time to evaluate MD readiness before making the formal promotion.

Compensation at this level reflects that positioning. Mergers & Inquisitions puts Director/SVP total compensation at $700,000-$900,000, with base salaries of $300,000-$350,000 and year-over-year growth of approximately 15% in the 2025 cycle.

**Current market figures (New York, 2024-2025):**

| Level | Base Salary | Bonus Range | All-In (Total Comp) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Director/ED (junior) | $300,000-$325,000 | $300,000-$500,000 | $600,000-$825,000 |
| Director/ED (senior) | $325,000-$350,000 | $400,000-$600,000 | $725,000-$950,000 |

Variation at this level is wide because it depends heavily on whether the Director has begun originating business. A Director who is clearly tracking toward MD promotion and generating early client revenue may be compensated closer to junior MD levels as a retention measure. A Director who is competent but not building a client book will be paid toward the bottom of the range and will eventually face an explicit conversation about their trajectory.

Deferred compensation at the Director level is typically 30-40% of the bonus, with increasingly complex vesting structures tied to multi-year performance metrics in addition to simple time vesting.

## Managing Director Compensation

The MD level is where banking compensation fundamentally changes structure. Below MD, you are paid primarily for execution - running deals, managing processes, producing output. At the MD level, you are paid primarily for origination - bringing in mandates, maintaining client relationships, generating revenue directly.

This shift has profound implications for how compensation is distributed. Mergers & Inquisitions states it plainly: MD "bonuses are literally percentages of fees they generate, so the sky's the limit in a great year." That is not an abstraction - it is the actual operating model at senior levels, and it explains why MD compensation is the most variable of any rank in banking.

**Current market figures (New York, 2024-2025):**

| MD Level | Base Salary | Bonus Range | All-In (Total Comp) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Junior MD (years 1-3) | $400,000-$500,000 | $500,000-$1,500,000 | $900,000-$2,000,000 |
| Senior MD (productive) | $500,000-$600,000 | $1,000,000-$3,000,000 | $1,500,000-$3,600,000 |
| Senior MD (group head) | $600,000+ | $3,000,000-$10,000,000+ | $3,600,000-$10,600,000+ |

Mergers & Inquisitions' managing director guide puts junior MD compensation at $1M-$2M annually in total, with senior MDs and group heads earning "well above" that range depending on their closed deal volume for the year. The same source applies an important correction to the outlier numbers that circulate on finance forums: "A normal MD is unlikely to earn $10 or $20 million in one year; they would need to be much more senior or in a buy-side role for that to be plausible." The truly exceptional MD-level earners - those running major sector coverage groups at Goldman or managing decades-long relationships with the largest companies in the world - represent a small fraction of the MD population.

Deferred compensation at the MD level is typically 30-50% of total comp, per Mergers & Inquisitions, often structured as a combination of restricted stock and long-term performance compensation tied to multi-year metrics. The retention implications are significant: the Mergers & Inquisitions guide specifically notes that "staying in the role for only 1-2 years doesn't make financial sense" because "you might lose out on the majority of your bonuses." This is not accidental design - it is deliberate.

## The Structural Economics Behind the Numbers

Several dynamics explain why banking compensation is structured the way it is:

**Revenue linkage at every level.** At each step up the hierarchy, compensation ties more directly to fee revenue. Analysts are furthest from the revenue line and compensated the most uniformly. MDs sit on the revenue line itself, and their upside reflects that exposure. This is why the 2025 cycle saw a 25% increase in MD compensation against only 5% growth for analysts even in a year of rising deal activity - the gains flowed to where the revenue decisions are made.

**Market competition and base salary standardization.** Banks compete for talent at every level. The periodic base salary increases at the analyst level - the 2021 Goldman raise and the immediate market-wide response - happen because one firm tests the labor market and others follow to protect their recruiting position. The same dynamic operates at all levels, though more quietly and with less public tracking at senior ranks.

**Retention through deferral.** As bankers advance, deferred compensation creates increasingly powerful financial incentives to stay. At the analyst level, deferral is minimal or zero. By the associate level, 10-20% of the bonus may vest over multiple years. At the MD level, 30-50% of total comp is illiquid at any given moment. Leaving forfeits real dollars - a structural lock-in that is explicit and intentional.

**The tournament model.** Investment banking compensation resembles a tournament more than a standard labor market. A large analyst class competes for associate spots. Associates compete for VP promotions. A much smaller fraction of VPs eventually reach MD. Those who survive to the top are compensated well in part because they have beaten a highly competitive, years-long selection process. The compensation at the senior levels reflects not just current productivity but the value of having cleared every prior hurdle.

## Geographic and Firm-Type Variation

New York figures dominate the public data, but geographic variation is meaningful:

- **London:** Mergers & Inquisitions applies a "15-30% discount" to New York figures as a general rule. Wall Street Prep's London-specific data shows analyst all-in of £95,000-£145,000 depending on year, and associate all-in of £160,000-£250,000. At senior levels, the gap narrows as global firms compete internationally for MD-level talent.
- **Hong Kong / Singapore:** Major bulge brackets pay broadly in line with New York at senior levels. Regional adjustment applies at junior levels.
- **Regional US cities (Chicago, Houston, San Francisco):** Bulge bracket and strong middle market bases are generally comparable to New York. Bonus pools can be smaller at the group level due to lower deal volume in certain markets and sectors.

For the firm-type dimension - bulge bracket vs. elite boutique vs. middle market - the elite boutique premium at analyst and associate levels has been the most notable structural shift in compensation over the last five years. ## The Real Question: Return on Investment

Banking compensation is most often evaluated in isolation - the annual number against a general sense of "is that good?" The more useful frame is return on the investment of time and the opportunity cost of the lifestyle it demands.

An analyst earning $190,000 all-in while working 90 hours per week is earning roughly $40-$42 per hour. That is better than most jobs at that career stage, but it is not as dramatic as the headline number suggests when the denominator is actual hours worked. The real argument for early-career banking pay is not the Year 1 number - it is the trajectory the years in banking unlock. An analyst who exits to a private equity fund or a hedge fund after two years enters a compensation track that the banking credential directly enabled. The banking salary is a means, not the end.

The compensation model changes most significantly between VP and MD, where the execution-to-origination shift happens and where most of the upside lives. Understanding that transition - what it requires in terms of skill development, client relationships, and institutional standing - is essential context for anyone making a long-term career decision about investment banking. ## What You Should Do Next

1. **Use verified, updated benchmarks.** The most reliable public sources are [Mergers & Inquisitions' annual compensation guide](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banker-salary/) and [Wall Street Prep's level-specific salary guides](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/investment-banking-analyst-salary-guide/). These are updated annually and broken down by level and firm type. Generic salary aggregator data (Glassdoor, Levels.fyi) is less reliable for banking-specific figures and tends to skew on self-reported bases.

2. **Separate cash from deferred when evaluating any offer.** A headline total comp number that includes significant restricted stock deferral is not equivalent to the same headline paid entirely in cash. Calculate the cash component explicitly and understand the vesting schedule before accepting a mid-career role.

3. **Understand your firm's deferral schedule before making a lateral move.** Unvested deferred comp you would forfeit is a real cost of departure - not just a number on paper. Factor it into any decision to move laterally, not just the new offer's headline figure.

4. **Talk to people one level above you about their actual trajectory.** Your VP can tell you honestly what the associate-to-VP comp jump looked like and how variable the bonus has been year to year. Your MD can describe what senior compensation volatility feels like in a down year. That information is more useful than any industry average.

5. **Read the [year-end bonus mechanics breakdown](/industry/year-end-bonus-season/) before your compensation conversation.** Understanding how the pool is set, how individual ratings translate into dollar outcomes, and what the firm's internal language around "top bucket" actually means will help you ask better questions and interpret your result more accurately.
