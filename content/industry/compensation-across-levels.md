# Compensation Across Levels: Analyst, Associate, VP, Director, MD

Investment banking compensation is not a secret but it is systematically misunderstood. Candidates overestimate first-year all-in numbers, underestimate mid-career variability, and almost universally underappreciate how dramatically the comp model changes between junior and senior levels.

This article gives you the actual numbers - drawn from Wall Street Oasis surveys, Mergers & Inquisitions annual guides, Johnson Associates reports, and Bloomberg compensation data - along with the structural mechanics that explain why the numbers look the way they do.

One important framing note before we start: compensation in investment banking is a range, not a point estimate. The analyst at Goldman TMT in a strong year and the analyst at a regional middle market bank in a slow year both have "analyst salaries" - and those numbers might differ by $50,000 or more all-in. Use the figures below as calibration ranges rather than exact predictions.

## Analyst Compensation (Years 1-3)

The analyst role has been standardized at bulge brackets and elite boutiques more than any other level. Base salaries moved in lockstep across major banks in 2021 when Goldman raised its analyst base and peers followed within weeks.

**Current market (2024-2025 data):**

| Year | Base Salary | Bonus Range | All-In (Total Comp) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Analyst Year 1 | $110,000 | $60,000-$100,000 | $170,000-$210,000 |
| Analyst Year 2 | $110,000 | $80,000-$120,000 | $190,000-$230,000 |
| Analyst Year 3 | $110,000-$115,000 | $90,000-$140,000 | $200,000-$255,000 |

At elite boutiques - specifically Centerview Partners, PJT Partners, and Evercore in strong years - bonuses at the top of the range have exceeded $150,000 for third-year analysts, pushing all-in compensation north of $260,000. These numbers represent the ceiling, not the midpoint.

Middle market banks vary more widely. Top-tier middle market (Houlihan Lokey, Harris Williams, William Blair) typically pay bases of $100,000-$110,000 with all-in compensation of $150,000-$185,000. Regional middle market banks may pay bases of $85,000-$100,000 with all-in in the $120,000-$155,000 range.

**What analysts actually take home:** After federal income tax, state and local taxes (significant in New York City), and the cost of living in New York, the first-year analyst making $190,000 all-in is not wealthy. Rent for a reasonable apartment, food, transportation, and the incidental costs of working banker hours still consume a substantial portion of that income. This is not a complaint - it's context for why people who join for the money alone tend not to survive the lifestyle.

## Associate Compensation (Years 1-3)

The associate level is where compensation trajectories begin to diverge based on whether someone joined from an MBA program (MBA associate) or was promoted from analyst (promote associate). Promotes and MBA associates are paid on the same scale at most banks, though promotes sometimes benefit from firm-specific retention packages designed to keep strong analysts from leaving for business school.

**Current market (2024-2025 data):**

| Year | Base Salary | Bonus Range | All-In (Total Comp) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Associate Year 1 | $175,000-$200,000 | $100,000-$175,000 | $275,000-$375,000 |
| Associate Year 2 | $200,000 | $125,000-$200,000 | $325,000-$400,000 |
| Associate Year 3 | $200,000-$225,000 | $150,000-$225,000 | $350,000-$450,000 |

Deferred compensation begins in earnest at the associate level. At most bulge brackets, 10-20% of the bonus is paid in restricted stock units with a three to four year vesting schedule. This means the "total comp" figure above is partially illiquid - the cash component for a first-year associate earning $325,000 total might be $260,000-$280,000 after deferral.

Elite boutique associates at Centerview and PJT have earned $400,000-$500,000+ all-in in strong years. These numbers circulate on Wall Street Oasis threads and are broadly accurate for top performers at the right firms in the right years.

The MBA premium is worth noting. An MBA associate entering at $175,000-$200,000 base is earning roughly 60-80% more than they made as an analyst two years prior. The compensation curve between analyst and associate is one of the steepest single-level jumps in professional finance.

## VP Compensation (Years 1-3)

The VP level is where banking compensation becomes significantly less standardized. Base salaries are set, but bonus variation increases substantially because VP performance is harder to measure purely by output and begins to include early-stage revenue contribution and client development.

**Current market (2024-2025 data):**

| Year | Base Salary | Bonus Range | All-In (Total Comp) |
|---|---|---|---|
| VP Year 1 | $250,000-$275,000 | $175,000-$300,000 | $425,000-$575,000 |
| VP Year 2 | $275,000 | $200,000-$350,000 | $475,000-$625,000 |
| VP Year 3 | $275,000-$300,000 | $225,000-$400,000 | $500,000-$700,000 |

Deferred compensation increases - 20-30% of bonus is typically deferred at this level. A VP earning $600,000 total comp may receive $430,000-$450,000 in cash that year, with the remainder vesting over several years.

The VP level is also where many people get stuck. The promotion from VP to Director/Executive Director is not automatic and requires demonstrated ability to manage deal processes independently and develop some degree of client relationships. Banks are increasingly willing to wait out VPs who aren't developing a business case for promotion.

A candid observation from one experienced VP on Reddit's r/FinancialCareers: "The VP years are where you either develop into someone who can manage up and manage clients, or you hit a ceiling and spend three years trying to figure out why you're not getting promoted. The technical skills that got you here are necessary but no longer sufficient."

## Director / Executive Director Compensation

The Director or Executive Director level (the title varies by bank - Goldman uses "Vice President" where other banks use "Director/ED") sits between VP and MD. It's a staging area for MD promotion rather than a permanent resting place, and compensation reflects that positioning.

**Current market (2024-2025 data):**

| Level | Base Salary | Bonus Range | All-In (Total Comp) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Director/ED (junior) | $300,000-$350,000 | $300,000-$500,000 | $600,000-$850,000 |
| Director/ED (senior) | $350,000 | $400,000-$700,000 | $750,000-$1,050,000 |

The variation at this level is wide because it depends heavily on whether the individual has begun originating deals. A Director who is clearly on the MD track and generating early client revenue may be compensated closer to junior MD levels to retain them. A Director who is competent but not developing a client base will be paid toward the bottom of the range and will eventually face a conversation about their path.

Deferred compensation is typically 30-40% of the bonus at this level, with increasingly complex vesting structures.

## Managing Director Compensation

The MD level is where banking compensation fundamentally changes structure. Below MD, you're paid primarily for execution - doing deals, managing processes, producing output. At the MD level, you're paid primarily for origination - bringing in mandates, maintaining client relationships, building revenue.

This shift has profound implications for compensation distribution. MD compensation is the most variable of any level in banking.

**Current market (2024-2025 data):**

| MD Level | Base Salary | Bonus Range | All-In (Total Comp) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Junior MD (years 1-3) | $400,000-$500,000 | $500,000-$1,500,000 | $900,000-$2,000,000 |
| Senior MD (productive) | $500,000 | $1,000,000-$5,000,000+ | $1,500,000-$5,500,000+ |
| Senior MD (elite) | $500,000-$750,000 | $5,000,000-$20,000,000+ | $5,500,000-$20,750,000+ |

The range for senior MDs is not a typo. An MD who runs a major sector coverage group or manages a long-standing client relationship with one of the largest companies in the world can generate tens of millions in annual fees. Compensation at that level reflects revenue contribution directly.

At Goldman Sachs, where partner status remains the apex of the institutional hierarchy, partners typically earn $5M-$15M+ in total compensation with significant variation by year and revenue contribution. High-performing partners in strong years have earned substantially more.

Deferred compensation at the MD level is typically 40-50%+ of total comp, often in a combination of restricted stock and long-term performance compensation tied to multi-year metrics.

## The Structural Economics Behind the Numbers

Several dynamics explain why banking compensation is structured the way it is:

**Revenue linkage:** At every level, compensation is ultimately linked to fee revenue. Analysts are furthest from the revenue and compensated accordingly. MDs sit directly on the revenue line and the upside reflects that.

**Market competition:** Banks compete for talent at every level. The periodic base salary increases at the analyst level - the 2021 Goldman raise and the market-wide response - happen because one firm tests the market and others follow to avoid losing recruiting position. The same dynamic operates at all levels, though more quietly.

**Retention through deferral:** As bankers advance, deferred compensation creates financial incentives to stay. Leaving a senior VP role means forfeiting unvested stock. Leaving an MD role might mean forfeiting millions in deferred compensation. This is explicit retention design.

**The tournament model:** Investment banking compensation resembles a tournament more than a standard labor market. A large number of analysts compete for associate spots; associates compete for VP promotions; a much smaller number of VPs ultimately make MD. Those who survive to the top are compensated well in part because they've beaten a highly competitive selection process.

## Geographic and Firm-Type Variation

New York numbers dominate the public conversation, but geographic variation is significant:

- **London:** Compensation is broadly similar to New York at bulge brackets and major boutiques, denominated in GBP. Brexit-related uncertainty has created some compression at the junior levels but senior MD compensation remains globally competitive.
- **Hong Kong / Singapore:** Major banks pay similarly to New York at bulge bracket levels, with regional adjustment for local cost of living.
- **Regional US cities (Chicago, Houston, San Francisco):** Bulge bracket and strong middle market pay is generally comparable to New York at base level. Bonus pools can be smaller at group level due to lower deal volume.

For firm-type variation, see [Bulge Bracket vs Elite Boutique vs Middle Market](/industry/bulge-bracket-vs-elite-boutique/). The elite boutique premium at the analyst and associate levels has been the most notable shift in the compensation landscape over the last five years.

## The Real Question: Return on Investment

Investment banking compensation is often evaluated in isolation. The more useful frame is return on the investment of time and the opportunity cost of the lifestyle.

An analyst who earns $190,000 all-in but works 90 hours per week is earning roughly $41 per hour - better than most jobs but not as dramatic as the headline number suggests. The real argument for banking compensation is not the annual number; it's the exit opportunity premium. A Goldman analyst who earns $190,000 in year one and exits to a private equity fund earning $350,000 all-in in year three has captured a career trajectory that the banking years enabled. The salary is a means, not the end.

The compensation model changes most significantly between VP and MD, where the execution-to-origination shift happens. Understanding that transition - what it requires and what it rewards - is essential context for anyone making a long-term career decision about investment banking. ## What You Should Do Next

1. **Use the Mergers & Inquisitions annual compensation guide and Wall Street Oasis surveys as your benchmarks.** These are updated annually and disaggregated by firm type and level. Generic salary website data (Glassdoor, Levels.fyi) is less reliable for banking-specific figures.

2. **When evaluating an offer, calculate the cash compensation separately from deferred.** A headline total comp number that includes significant deferral is not equivalent to the same headline paid entirely in cash.

3. **Understand your firm's deferral schedule before accepting a mid-career role.** The unvested deferred comp you'd leave behind is a real cost of departure. Factor it into any lateral move decision.

4. **Talk to people one level above you about their compensation trajectory.** Your VP can tell you, honestly, what the associate-to-VP comp jump looked like. Your MD can describe the volatility of senior comp in good and bad years. This information is more useful than industry averages.

5. **Read the [year-end bonus mechanics breakdown](/industry/year-end-bonus-season/) before your compensation conversation.** Understanding how the pool gets set and how individual ratings flow through the process helps you ask better questions and interpret your outcome more accurately.
