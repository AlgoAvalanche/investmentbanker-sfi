# The Restructuring Boom-Bust Cycle: When to Try to Get In

Restructuring banking is the only corner of investment banking where a collapsing economy is good for business. When credit markets freeze, interest rates spike, or an entire sector tips into distress, restructuring advisors get busy in ways that M&A bankers and capital markets desks simply do not. That counter-cyclical dynamic makes RX banking genuinely different from the rest of the industry - in terms of when to recruit, what you will learn, and where you will end up after you leave.

Understanding the restructuring boom-bust cycle is not academic exercise. It directly shapes when groups hire aggressively, what kind of deal experience you will accumulate if you join, and whether the exit opportunities you are targeting will be accessible when you want them. The current environment makes this question especially pointed: 2025 commercial Chapter 11 filings hit 7,940 - a decade high, the fourth consecutive year of increases - while a $1.2 trillion maturity wall looms for 2027 to 2029. [1][5] If you are considering RX, the pipeline looks constructive for several more years.

## What Restructuring Bankers Actually Do

Before discussing cycles, it is worth being precise about what restructuring advisory covers. The term gets used loosely across several structurally distinct activities.

**Debtor-side advisory** means advising companies that are in financial distress or approaching insolvency. The work involves assessing the capital structure, developing options - out-of-court exchange offers, covenant relief negotiations, pre-packaged bankruptcies, full Chapter 11 proceedings - modeling recovery scenarios for each class of creditors, and negotiating with creditor groups. The outcome might be a restructured balance sheet, a going-concern sale, or a liquidation. [2]

**Creditor-side advisory** means advising committees of creditors - typically an ad hoc group of bondholders or a bank syndicate - in bankruptcy or out-of-court negotiations. The work involves independently valuing the debtor, challenging management projections, and maximizing recovery. Debtor and creditor advisors are always separate; a bank never advises both sides of the same restructuring. Candidates who conflate the two in interviews signal shallow preparation. [1]

**Distressed M&A** covers the acquisition or sale of companies in financial distress, including Section 363 sales in bankruptcy where assets are sold free of most liabilities through a court-supervised process. This is where RX and M&A advisory overlap most directly.

**Liability management** has become a category in its own right. It involves helping companies manage debt obligations before distress reaches crisis levels - exchange offers, debt buybacks, consent solicitations, uptier exchanges, and drop-down financings. Liability Management Transactions (LMTs) represented 54% of U.S. high-yield defaults in 2024, surpassing traditional in-court restructurings as the primary distress resolution mechanism. [4] This is not an exotic niche. It is the majority of what distress resolution now looks like.

One important conceptual distinction: restructuring bankers advise on capital structure and financial claims. They do not fix broken businesses operationally. That is the job of turnaround consultants at firms like Alvarez and Marsal, AlixPartners, and FTI Consulting. RX bankers target companies that need to rightsize debt obligations while maintaining operational viability - not companies that are irredeemably broken at the business level. [3]

## The Firms That Dominate RX

Restructuring advisory is more concentrated than M&A advisory in terms of which firms carry genuine expertise and consistent deal flow. The structural reason is conflict of interest: bulge brackets (Goldman, JPMorgan, Citigroup) have lending and underwriting relationships that make it difficult to credibly advise a distressed borrower on restructuring the bank debt your own institution holds. You cannot simultaneously be the lender and the advisor maximizing recovery against yourself. [2]

Elite boutiques - with no lending balance sheets and no conflicts - dominate as a result.

**Houlihan Lokey** runs the largest restructuring practice globally by volume, completing 88 deals in 2024. The training is genuinely rigorous and the breadth of deal types is unmatched for analysts who want volume of experience.

**PJT Partners** (RX practice, formerly Blackstone Advisory) is the prestige floor for debtor-side flagship cases. Smaller deal count than Houlihan, but PJT consistently appears in the most complex, highest-profile Chapter 11 cases. For candidates targeting distressed funds with a bias toward landmark restructurings, PJT is the pipeline.

**Lazard** runs a restructuring practice with global reach and particular strength in cross-border cases. For large European or Latin American restructurings, Lazard is frequently in the room and the international exposure is genuinely differentiated.

**Evercore** has built out its RX practice significantly since 2015, with growing strength in sponsor relationships and liability management transactions.

**Moelis and Company** has a growing practice with particular emphasis on creditor-side advisory, which provides a different analytical vantage point than debtor-focused shops.

**Guggenheim and Rothschild** round out the elite boutique set with active RX practices across different size and sector concentrations.

The ecosystem around these banks matters equally for candidates to understand. Every restructuring involves four key players: the investment bank (debtor-side and creditor-side advisors, always separate), bankruptcy counsel (Kirkland and Ellis, Weil Gotshal, Davis Polk, Latham, Akin Gump, Paul Weiss), financial and operational turnaround consultants (Alvarez and Marsal, AlixPartners, FTI), and distressed credit funds - often the fulcrum security holders who become the new equity owners post-emergence. [4] Answering RX interview questions without demonstrating awareness of this ecosystem reveals incomplete preparation.

## The Cycle: What Drives Deal Flow

Restructuring deal flow is driven by three primary factors - credit market conditions, interest rate levels, and sector-specific stress. Understanding how these interact tells you when RX groups are stretched thin and when they are sitting quiet.

**Credit market conditions:** When credit spreads are tight and capital is abundantly available, distressed companies can refinance their way out of problems. High-yield bonds and leveraged loans trade near par; new issuance clears easily; maturities get pushed out. In that environment, RX deal flow compresses. The mid-2010s ZIRP era was a dry period precisely because cheap capital rescued companies that would otherwise have defaulted. As Wall Street Prep documents, the out-of-court workout - the least expensive option with the most room for negotiation - only works when credit markets are willing to refinance. [2]

**Interest rate levels:** The 2022-2023 rate shock was a restructuring catalyst not seen since 2008-2009. Companies that had issued debt at 4-6% in 2020-2021 suddenly faced refinancing at 8-11%. For highly levered companies - particularly PE-backed portfolio companies with LBO debt from the 2020-2021 vintage - the math stopped working. Liability management activity spiked first, followed by Chapter 11 filings in 2023 and 2024 across retail, healthcare services, media, and industrials. Loan defaults and distressed exchanges averaged 4.3% of all issuers in 2025, unchanged from 2024 but elevated above the pre-pandemic norm of 2-3%. [5]

**Sector-specific stress:** Restructuring waves often concentrate in specific sectors rather than blanketing the entire economy. The energy restructuring wave of 2015-2016 was driven by oil price collapse. The retail wave of 2017-2020 was driven by e-commerce disruption. COVID in 2020 drove restructurings across hospitality, travel, and entertainment. These sector waves can be significant even when the broader economy is healthy, which is part of why RX deal flow is not perfectly correlated with recession timing. The current stress concentrations - real estate, consumer goods, and energy and industrials - represented 80% of all Chapter 11 filings in 2025. [5]

## Historical Cycles: The Pattern

Looking at two decades of restructuring deal flow, the pattern is consistent:

| Period | Primary Driver | Market Condition |
|---|---|---|
| 2001-2003 | Dot-com bust, telecom overleverage | High activity |
| 2004-2007 | ZIRP, strong credit markets | Low activity |
| 2008-2010 | Financial crisis, credit freeze | Exceptional activity |
| 2011-2014 | Recovery, refinancing wave | Low-moderate activity |
| 2015-2016 | Energy sector collapse | High activity (energy-specific) |
| 2017-2019 | Tight spreads, low rates | Low activity |
| 2020 | COVID shock | Moderate-high (sector specific) |
| 2021 | Fed support, zero rates | Very low activity |
| 2022-2024 | Rate shock, PE vintage stress | Rising activity |
| 2025-2026 | Elevated rates, maturity wall | Moderate-high activity |

The current cycle has a structural feature worth noting: it is broader but shallower than prior downturns. Mega-bankruptcies - cases with assets or liabilities exceeding $1 billion - actually declined from 42 cases in 2024 to 31 cases in 2025, even as total Chapter 11 filings hit a decade high of 7,940. [4][5] This is not 2008. Distress is concentrated in small and mid-cap companies, industrials, consumer discretionary, and healthcare. The absence of a systemic financial crisis means the cycle is more diffuse, which changes the character of the deal flow but does not diminish it in volume.

The macro picture from Deloitte's 2025 restructuring outlook reinforces the severity of underlying stress: approximately 12% of borrowers now have negative cash flow, up from roughly 7-8% the prior year, and approximately 13% have interest coverage below 1.0x, also up from 7-8% previously. [6] Consumer sentiment, as measured by the University of Michigan index, fell from 74 at end-2024 to 51 by November 2025. The Economic Policy Uncertainty Index peaked at 7,955 in April 2025, up from 1,319 at end-2024, driven largely by tariff uncertainty. [6] These are not conditions that resolve in a single quarter.

## The Liability Management Revolution

The most important structural change in restructuring since 2020 is not the volume of Chapter 11 filings - it is the rise of liability management transactions as the dominant resolution mechanism for leveraged credit distress. LMTs representing 54% of U.S. high-yield defaults in 2024 is not a footnote. It means the majority of distress events are now resolved without formal bankruptcy proceedings, through structures that restructuring bankers help design and execute. [4]

The two dominant LMT architectures are uptier exchanges - amending credit agreements to create super-priority debt that effectively subordinates non-participating lenders - and drop-down financings, sometimes called J.Crew trapdoors, which transfer assets to unrestricted subsidiaries to raise new debt outside the original lender group.

The December 2024 Serta Simmons Fifth Circuit ruling reset the calculus on non-pro-rata uptier mechanics, finding that certain lender-on-lender violence structures violated credit agreement terms. The market response has been a shift toward more consensual LMT structures. [4] This case is not optional knowledge for RX candidates. Demonstrating fluency in how the Serta ruling reshaped 2025 deal structure is a meaningful differentiator in interviews.

The Envision Healthcare case provides the clearest illustration of what is at stake in LMTs: LMT participants recovered 73-85 cents on the dollar, while non-participating lenders recovered near zero. [4] The adversarial dynamic between participating and non-participating creditors in LMT transactions is structurally different from M&A, where all parties nominally work toward a shared outcome.

## The Current Sector Risk Map

For 2026, the sector concentrations where RX bankers are likely to be busy are identifiable with reasonable confidence:

**Consumer products and retail:** Margin pressure from tariffs and input cost inflation, combined with consumer trading down to value alternatives, is compressing margins for mid-market consumer brands. Many cannot pass cost increases through to price-sensitive customers. [5][6]

**Automotive suppliers:** Tariff-driven input cost increases and EV market stagnation are hitting suppliers hardest. Companies that built capacity assumptions around EV adoption curves are now sitting on fixed cost structures that do not support the actual demand environment. [5]

**Healthcare services:** Medicaid funding constraints, Medicare Advantage erosion, and labor cost inflation are converging on providers that were already operating on thin margins. Hospital systems, physician groups, and behavioral health providers are all under material pressure. [5][6]

**Industrials:** Tariff-driven inventory disruption and supply chain rerouting costs are hitting levered industrials that relied on stable cost structures when they took on LBO debt. [6]

The PwC bankruptcy outlook for 2025-2026 recommends initiating out-of-court scenario planning well before crisis conditions force reactive bankruptcy filings - which means a category of RX advisory work that predates the formal distress event and arrives earlier in the cycle than traditional Chapter 11 advisory. [5]

## The Private Credit Complication

A new wrinkle in the current cycle is the $1.5 trillion private credit market. More distressed companies are now restructuring bilaterally with private lenders rather than through public markets or bankruptcy, because the lender base is concentrated enough to negotiate directly. This is changing the character of some RX advisory work - less public process, more direct negotiation.

The First Brands case exposed what happens when private credit due diligence fails. The $4.4 billion DIP - comprising $1.1 billion in new money and $3.3 billion in roll-up - came after fraud allegations surfaced against the borrower, exposing lender due diligence gaps that are likely to become more common as the private credit market matures and its 2020-2022 vintage deals face stress. [4] As private credit lenders encounter their first real distress cycle, demand for RX advisory from that market is likely to grow.

## Timing Your Entry

For candidates thinking about RX banking, timing matters more than it does for M&A or coverage banking because the work experience is so deal-cycle-dependent.

**Join at the beginning or middle of a distress wave.** If you enter an RX group at the start of an active cycle, you will spend two to three years working on live, complex transactions and emerge with exceptional technical training and real deal experience. If you join at the peak of a quiet credit market, you may spend your analyst years on liability management analysis and early-stage distressed work without experiencing the full Chapter 11 process.

During the 2020 pandemic wave, restructuring banks had to turn down pitches and mandates - demand exceeded the capacity of the market's advisory infrastructure. [3] That created a generation of analysts who watched RX outperform every other banking group in deal volume and compensation simultaneously. The experience is now embedded in recruiting culture at top business schools.

**Recruiting signals to watch:** RX groups hire aggressively when deal flow is increasing. High-yield spread widening, rising default rates, and leveraged loan price discounts trading below 90 cents on the dollar are leading indicators of an upcoming cycle. The Federal Reserve H.15 data, FRED's high-yield spread series, and Moody's monthly default rate publications are all publicly accessible. When those signals are moving, RX group hiring tends to follow.

Lateral hiring from M&A groups and coverage groups into RX also accelerates during distress waves. Banks that need to staff up quickly pull from adjacent groups - which means the window for lateral moves into RX widens precisely when deal flow is building.

**The honest trade-off:** Joining Houlihan's RX group during an active cycle means concentrated, deep, specialized experience. You will run recovery waterfalls, model 13-week cash flows, diligence DIP financing terms, and negotiate plan confirmation mechanics before your second year is out. You will also emerge with limited exposure to equity markets, M&A process, or sponsor relationships. Whether that trade-off serves your goals depends entirely on where you want to go next.

## The Analytical Skill Set

Restructuring requires analytical skills that overlap with M&A but diverge in specific areas that matter for interview preparation and for understanding what the daily work actually involves.

**Waterfall analysis** is the fundamental tool - determining how value flows from the top of the capital structure (secured debt) down through unsecured creditors to equity. This requires precise understanding of claim priority, the legal structure of different debt instruments, and the assumptions driving enterprise valuation. The fulcrum security is where the waterfall breaks: cumulative claims exceed enterprise value at that instrument, making it the locus of negotiation and the instrument that typically converts to equity in reorganization.

**13-week cash flow modeling** is the liquidity tool. Distressed companies often face immediate cash constraints regardless of long-term viability. RX analysts model weekly and monthly liquidity - cash inflows, outflows, covenant headroom, DIP financing capacity - with granularity that standard M&A models do not require.

**DIP financing analysis:** Debtor-in-possession financing is specialized credit provided to companies in bankruptcy. Standard DIP pricing in 2024-2025 ran 12-18% all-in, typically SOFR plus 700-900 basis points, reflecting the priority claim but also the distress risk. [4] Understanding DIP economics and documentation is standard RX toolkit.

**Plan of reorganization modeling:** Chapter 11 cases resolve through a Plan of Reorganization specifying how different creditor classes will be treated and what the reorganized company looks like. This requires simultaneous handling of legal documentation, financial projections, and negotiation dynamics - which is a genuinely different cognitive task than M&A modeling.

**Credit agreement analysis:** Understanding covenant packages, collateral provisions, intercreditor agreements, and the specific mechanics that allow LMT structures like uptier exchanges is essential in RX in a way it is not in most other banking roles.

For the valuation mechanics that apply in distressed contexts, the [DCF valuation step-by-step guide](/technical/dcf-valuation-step-by-step/) covers terminal value and discount rate methodology relevant to distressed enterprise valuations.

## Compensation Across the Cycle

RX compensation follows standard investment banking structure with one material modification: deal-year bonuses can spike significantly during active cycles. When a group is staffing five live Chapter 11 cases simultaneously, the fee revenue is exceptional and the bonus pool reflects that. Year 1 RX analysts at top elite boutiques were earning $200,000 to $270,000 all-in during the recent active period. At the Director level, Lazard's recent cycle produced compensation around $842,000. MD-level compensation runs $1.5 million and above. [4]

Conversely, in quiet credit markets, RX groups may see softer bonus pools than M&A groups benefiting from robust deal activity. The countercyclical protection applies to deal flow and job security, not to absolute compensation in every year. Bonus volatility is real - it follows the deal cycle, not a straight line.

## Exit Opportunities From RX

Restructuring banking produces a distinct exit opportunity set that differs from M&A banking and is worth understanding before you choose a path.

**Distressed debt and special situations funds** are the most natural exit. Firms like Oaktree Capital, Apollo Global Management's credit platform, KKR Credit, Cerberus Capital, Varde Partners, and dozens of smaller distressed-focused funds actively recruit from RX groups at Houlihan, PJT, and Lazard. The analytical overlap is direct: you have spent two years valuing distressed credits and modeling recoveries, which is exactly what distressed portfolio managers do. This is a well-established pipeline that is difficult to replicate from M&A groups. [1]

**Private credit and direct lending:** The private credit boom has created significant demand for people who understand credit documentation, covenant structures, and downside scenarios. RX alumni have moved into origination and portfolio monitoring roles at direct lenders - with a particular edge in the portfolio monitoring and workout functions as that market now faces its first real stress cycle.

**Mega-fund buyout PE:** Possible but less natural than from M&A groups. RX analysts who have worked on distressed M&A transactions - 363 sales, going-concern sales - have transaction experience that transfers. The path to Blackstone's or KKR's buyout funds is better served by M&A or coverage banking experience. There is no structural bar, but the RX-to-buyout path requires more deliberate positioning.

**Corporate restructuring roles:** Operating companies facing financial challenges hire restructuring professionals directly. CFO office, treasury, and corporate development roles at highly levered companies value the specific expertise, particularly at PE-backed companies going through a workout.

## The Honest Assessment

Restructuring is the right choice for candidates who are genuinely interested in credit, legal complexity, and distressed situations - not candidates who want counter-cyclicality as a risk management strategy while waiting for M&A markets to recover. The work is intellectually demanding in specific ways: heavy legal document analysis, complex capital structure scenarios, adversarial negotiation dynamics under time pressure and legal constraint. The adversarial dynamic between debtor and creditor advisors, and between participating and non-participating LMT creditors, is structurally different from M&A where all parties nominally work toward deal completion. [7]

The surge in student interest in RX since 2020 is partly rational - the countercyclical protection and direct pipeline to distressed funds are real advantages - and partly reflects the pandemic mandate wave creating visibility. When banks were turning down work in 2020, every analyst in the industry noticed. That experience is now embedded in recruiting culture, which means RX competition has intensified. Getting in requires stronger preparation than it did five years ago.

The current data supports the strategic case. 7,940 Chapter 11 filings in 2025. Loan defaults at 4.3% against a 2-3% pre-pandemic baseline. 12% of borrowers with negative cash flow. A $1.2 trillion maturity wall arriving in 2027-2029. S&P projecting U.S. speculative-grade default rates potentially reaching 4.25% by mid-2026. [4][5][6] The pipeline is real and the institutions best positioned to serve it - Houlihan Lokey, PJT Partners, Lazard, Evercore - are well-established training grounds for analysts who want to build careers in distressed credit.

The institutional comparison matters for firm selection. Houlihan Lokey, PJT, and Lazard offer the best RX training platforms, but the character of the experience differs. PJT skews toward larger, more complex debtors. Houlihan provides more deal volume. Lazard provides international exposure. For firm-level comparison context, see [Bulge Bracket vs Elite Boutique](/industry/bulge-bracket-vs-elite-boutique/) - the elite boutique discussion is directly relevant given that the top RX shops are all independent advisory firms.

## What You Should Do Next

**Monitor credit market conditions before prioritizing RX recruiting.** High-yield spread widening, increasing default rates, and rising leveraged loan price discounts are leading indicators of an upcoming RX cycle. The Federal Reserve H.15 data, FRED's high-yield spread series, and Moody's default rate surveys are publicly available and worth tracking quarterly.

**Read the major restructuring deals of the recent cycle.** Bed Bath and Beyond, Rite Aid, WeWork, Yellow Corporation, and Envision Healthcare are well-documented recent cases. Understanding the capital structure issue, the advisory relationships, the LMT or bankruptcy path taken, and the outcome gives you credible conversation material for interviews. The Serta Simmons and Purdue Pharma rulings are mandatory additions to that reading list.

**Build the analytical foundations.** The [DCF valuation guide](/technical/dcf-valuation-step-by-step/) covers the valuation mechanics you will need. Beyond that, read the core provisions of the Bankruptcy Code's Chapter 11 - particularly the automatic stay, the DIP financing framework, and plan confirmation requirements. Understand secured versus unsecured debt priorities and what a plan of reorganization contains at a structural level.

**Talk to analysts at Houlihan Lokey, PJT, and Lazard RX specifically.** The culture, deal types, and exit outcomes differ between these firms more than the category description suggests. First-hand perspective on what the day-to-day actually looks like - not just the league table positioning - is worth pursuing before you commit a recruiting cycle to one of them.

**Apply during upticks in distress activity.** Your application to an RX group in a quiet credit market competes with fewer open roles and analysts who are not fully stretched. Your application during a distress wave is competing with groups that are already short-staffed and actively motivated to hire. The current environment - 2025-2026 with the maturity wall arriving - sits in that second category. The window is open.
