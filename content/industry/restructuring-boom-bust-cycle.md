# The Restructuring Boom-Bust Cycle: When to Try to Get In

Restructuring banking is the only corner of investment banking where a collapsing economy is good for business. When credit markets freeze, interest rates spike, or an entire sector goes into distress, restructuring advisors get busy in ways that M&A bankers and capital markets groups do not. This counter-cyclical dynamic makes RX banking genuinely different from the rest of investment banking - in terms of when to recruit, what you learn, and where you end up.

Understanding the restructuring boom-bust cycle is not just academic. It directly affects when groups are hiring aggressively, what kind of deal experience you'll accumulate if you join, and whether the exit opportunities you're targeting are likely to be accessible when you want them.

## What Restructuring Bankers Actually Do

Before discussing cycles, it's worth being precise about what restructuring advisory covers. The term is used loosely and encompasses several distinct activities:

**Debtor-side advisory:** Advising companies that are in financial distress or approaching insolvency. The work involves assessing the company's capital structure, developing options (out-of-court exchange offers, covenant relief negotiations, pre-packaged bankruptcies, full Chapter 11 proceedings), modeling recovery scenarios, and negotiating with creditor groups. The outcome might be a restructured balance sheet, a sale of the business, or a liquidation.

**Creditor-side advisory:** Advising committees of creditors - typically an ad hoc group of bondholders or a bank syndicate - in bankruptcy or out-of-court negotiations. The work involves analyzing the debtor's valuation, challenging management's projections, and maximizing creditor recovery. Creditor committees pay significant advisory fees and the work is analytically demanding.

**Distressed M&A:** Advising on the acquisition or sale of companies in financial distress - including Section 363 sales in bankruptcy, where assets are sold free of most liabilities through a court-supervised process. This is where restructuring and M&A advisory overlap most directly.

**Liability management:** A growing sub-category that involves helping companies manage their debt obligations before distress reaches critical levels - exchange offers, debt buybacks, consent solicitations, and related transactions. In the 2023-2024 period, liability management became a significant growth area as levered companies tried to address near-term maturities without filing for bankruptcy.

## The Firms That Dominate RX

Restructuring advisory is more concentrated than M&A advisory in terms of which firms have genuine expertise and deal flow:

**Houlihan Lokey** is the largest restructuring advisory firm globally by deal count. The firm has maintained restructuring leadership through multiple credit cycles and consistently appears at the top of Dealogic's restructuring league tables. The training at Houlihan's RX group is genuinely excellent.

**PJT Partners** (RX practice, formerly Blackstone Advisory) is the elite boutique equivalent for restructuring. Smaller deal count than Houlihan but consistently involved in the largest, most complex Chapter 11 cases. PJT's RX group has advised on some of the defining bankruptcies of the last decade.

**Lazard** runs a restructuring practice with global reach and particular strength in cross-border restructurings. For large European or Latin American restructurings, Lazard is frequently in the room.

**Evercore** has built out its restructuring practice significantly since 2015. Less dominant than the top three but well-regarded and increasingly active in liability management transactions.

**Moelis & Company** has a growing RX practice with particular strength in creditor-side advisory.

**Bulge brackets** - JPMorgan, Goldman, Citigroup - have restructuring capabilities but are more limited by conflict issues (it's difficult to advise a debtor on restructuring when your bank also holds the debt you're restructuring). Bulge bracket RX groups handle specific situations where conflict issues are manageable, but the independent advisory firms dominate the market for genuinely complex restructurings.

## The Cycle: What Drives RX Deal Flow

Restructuring deal flow is driven by three primary factors: credit market conditions, interest rate levels, and sector-specific stress. Understanding how these interact tells you when RX groups are busy and when they're not.

**Credit market conditions:** When credit spreads are tight and capital is abundantly available, distressed companies can refinance their way out of problems. High-yield bonds and leveraged loans trade near par; new issuance is easily executed; maturities get pushed out. In this environment, RX deal flow is thin. The mid-2010s ZIRP environment was a dry period for restructuring advisory as cheap capital saved companies that would otherwise have been in distress.

**Interest rate levels:** The 2022-2023 rate shock was a restructuring catalyst in a way not seen since 2008-2009. Companies that had issued debt at 4-6% in 2020-2021 suddenly faced refinancing at 8-11%. For highly levered companies - particularly those owned by private equity with LBO debt from the 2020-2021 vintage - the math stopped working. Liability management activity spiked, followed by Chapter 11 filings in 2023 and 2024 across retail, healthcare services, and media sectors.

**Sector-specific stress:** Restructuring waves often hit specific sectors rather than the whole economy. The energy restructuring wave of 2015-2016 was driven by oil price collapse. The retail restructuring wave of 2017-2020 was driven by e-commerce disruption. COVID in 2020 drove restructurings in hospitality, travel, and entertainment. These sector waves can be significant even when the broader economy is healthy.

## Historical Cycles: The Pattern

Looking at the last two decades of restructuring deal flow:

| Period | Primary Driver | Market Condition |
|---|---|---|
| 2001-2003 | Dot-com bust, telecom overleverage | High activity |
| 2004-2007 | ZIRP, strong credit markets | Low activity |
| 2008-2010 | Financial crisis, credit freeze | Exceptional activity |
| 2011-2014 | Recovery, refinancing wave | Low-moderate activity |
| 2015-2016 | Energy sector collapse | High activity (energy-specific) |
| 2017-2019 | Tight spreads, low rates | Low activity |
| 2020 | COVID shock | Moderate-high (sector specific) |
| 2021 | Fed support, zero rates | Very low activity |
| 2022-2024 | Rate shock, PE vintage stress | Rising activity |
| 2025-2026 | Elevated rates, PE maturity wall | Moderate-high activity |

The pattern is consistent: RX activity spikes when credit conditions tighten, and compresses when cheap capital is available. The current environment - persistently elevated rates relative to the 2010-2021 norm, a significant volume of highly levered PE-backed companies with approaching maturities - is generally supportive of restructuring deal flow through the near term.

## Timing Your Entry: The Strategic Consideration

For candidates thinking about RX banking, timing matters more than it does for M&A or coverage banking because the work experience is so deal-cycle-dependent.

**Join at the beginning or middle of a distress wave.** If you enter an RX group at the start of an active cycle, you'll spend two to three years working on live, complex transactions and emerge with exceptional technical training and significant deal experience. If you join at the peak of a calm credit market, you may spend your analyst years on liability management transactions and early-stage distressed analysis without the full Chapter 11 process experience.

**Recruiting signals:** RX groups hire aggressively when deal flow is increasing. If you're seeing significant restructuring news flow - sector-specific distress, high-profile Chapter 11 filings, increased credit spread volatility - RX groups are likely more receptive to applications and more willing to hire on a flexible timeline. Lateral hiring from M&A groups and coverage groups into RX also picks up during distress waves.

One WSO poster who joined Houlihan's RX group in late 2022 put it directly: "I joined at exactly the right time. The first 18 months were non-stop. I got more deal experience than people who'd been in M&A for three years. The tradeoff is that I knew nothing about equity markets or M&A process when I left - pure restructuring skills."

That's the honest description of the experience: concentrated, deep, and specialized. Whether that trade-off serves your goals depends on where you want to go.

## The Analytical Skill Set in RX

Restructuring requires analytical skills that overlap with M&A but diverge significantly in specific areas:

**Waterfall analysis:** The fundamental analytical tool in restructuring - determining how value flows from the top of the capital structure (secured debt) down through unsecured creditors to equity. This requires precise understanding of the priority of claims, the legal structure of different debt instruments, and the assumptions that drive enterprise valuation.

**Liquidity analysis:** Distressed companies often face immediate cash flow challenges regardless of long-term viability. RX analysts model weekly and monthly liquidity - cash inflows, outflows, covenant headroom, DIP financing capacity - with granularity that M&A models don't require.

**DIP financing:** Debtor-in-possession financing is the specialized credit provided to companies in bankruptcy. Understanding DIP economics, documentation, and market conditions is part of the standard RX toolkit.

**Plan of reorganization modeling:** Chapter 11 cases resolve through a Plan of Reorganization that specifies how different creditor classes will be treated and what the reorganized company will look like. Modeling this requires simultaneous handling of legal documentation, financial projections, and negotiation dynamics.

**Credit agreement analysis:** Understanding the legal terms of debt instruments - covenants, collateral provisions, intercreditor agreements - is essential and more directly relevant to RX work than to most other banking roles.

## Exit Opportunities From RX

Restructuring banking produces distinct exit opportunities that differ from M&A banking:

**Distressed debt and special situations PE:** The most natural exit. Firms like Oaktree Capital, Apollo, KKR Credit, Cerberus Capital, Varde Partners, and numerous other distressed-focused funds actively recruit from RX groups at Houlihan, PJT, and Lazard. The analytical overlap is direct - you've spent two years valuing distressed credits and modeling recoveries, which is exactly what distressed portfolio managers do.

**Direct lending and private credit:** The private credit boom of the last five years has created significant demand for people who understand credit documentation, covenant structures, and downside scenarios. RX alumni have moved into origination and portfolio monitoring roles at direct lenders.

**Mega-fund PE (M&A focused):** Possible but less natural than from M&A groups. RX analysts who have worked on distressed M&A transactions - 363 sales, going-concern sales - have transaction experience that transfers. But the path to Blackstone or KKR's buyout funds is better served by M&A or coverage experience.

**Corporate restructuring roles:** Operating companies facing financial challenges hire restructuring professionals directly. CFO office, treasury, and corporate development roles at highly levered companies can value the specific expertise.

## The Compensation Dimension

RX compensation follows the same general investment banking structure described elsewhere, with one important modification: deal-year bonuses can spike significantly during active cycles. When an RX group is working on five active Chapter 11 cases simultaneously, the fee revenue is exceptional and the bonus pool reflects that. Conversely, in quiet markets, RX groups may see softer bonus pools than M&A groups that benefited from robust deal activity.

The career compensation trajectory for successful RX bankers who move into distressed investing can be excellent. Senior portfolio managers at distressed funds earn well above comparable seniority in investment banking, and the skill set transfers directly.

## Should You Prioritize RX?

Restructuring is the right choice for candidates who are genuinely interested in credit, legal complexity, and distressed situations - not just candidates who want counter-cyclicality as a risk management strategy. The work is intellectually demanding in specific ways: heavy legal document analysis, complex capital structure scenarios, negotiation dynamics under time pressure and legal constraint. Candidates who find this genuinely interesting will thrive; candidates who tolerate it while waiting for better market conditions will struggle.

The institutional comparison matters. Houlihan Lokey, PJT, and Lazard offer the best RX training platforms. For firm comparisons, see [Bulge Bracket vs Elite Boutique](/industry/bulge-bracket-vs-elite-boutique/) - the elite boutique discussion is directly relevant given that the top RX shops are largely independent advisory firms.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Monitor credit market conditions before deciding to prioritize RX recruiting.** High-yield spread widening, increasing default rates, and rising leveraged loan price discounts are leading indicators of an upcoming RX cycle. The Federal Reserve H.15 data, FRED's high-yield spread data, and Moody's default rate surveys are all publicly available.

2. **Read the major restructuring deals of the last cycle.** Bed Bath & Beyond, Rite Aid, WeWork, Yellow Corporation, and Envision Healthcare are recent large cases. Understanding the basics of what happened - the capital structure issue, the advisory relationships, the outcome - gives you informed conversation material for interviews.

3. **Build the analytical foundations.** The [DCF valuation guide](/technical/dcf-valuation-step-by-step/) covers the valuation mechanics you'll need. Beyond that, read the basics of the Bankruptcy Code (Chapter 11 specifically), understand secured versus unsecured debt priorities, and learn what a plan of reorganization contains.

4. **Talk to analysts at Houlihan Lokey, PJT, and Lazard RX specifically.** The culture, deal types, and exit outcomes differ between these three firms more than the category description suggests. PJT RX skews toward larger, more complex debtors. Houlihan has more deal volume. Lazard has more international exposure.

5. **Apply during upticks in distress activity.** Your application to an RX group in a quiet credit market competes with fewer deals and fewer analysts being retained. Your application during a distress wave is competing with groups that are already stretched and more motivated to hire.
