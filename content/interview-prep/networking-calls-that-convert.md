# Networking Calls That Convert to Superdays

Most investment banking candidates treat networking calls as a social obligation before the real work begins. They fire off templated LinkedIn messages, schedule 20-minute calls, ask three generic questions, and then wait for callbacks that never come. Then they conclude that networking does not work and spend the rest of recruiting season refreshing their email.

The candidates who convert networking calls into superday invitations are doing something structurally different. This article explains what that looks like - based on data from practitioners, recruiters, and the documented experience of thousands of candidates who have navigated this process.

---

## The Purpose of a Networking Call Is Not to Be Liked

Start with the right mental model. The purpose of a networking call in investment banking recruiting is not to make a friend. It is to accomplish two specific things: demonstrate that you are competent enough to be worth vouching for, and give the banker a reason to remember your name when headcount opens or when recruiting is discussed internally.

Bankers are busy. An analyst at a bulge bracket during deal season is regularly working until 2 or 3 a.m. When they agree to spend 15 minutes talking to you, they are making a real sacrifice. The candidates who waste that time with vague questions about "what does your day look like" or "what advice would you give someone interested in banking" are not going to be remembered. They might even leave a mildly negative impression.

The candidates who show up prepared, ask sharp questions, demonstrate knowledge, and make the interaction feel worthwhile - those are the people who get the follow-up email saying "we have a superday coming up, you should apply."

According to Mergers and Inquisitions, one of the most widely used practitioner resources in IB recruiting, networking is defined specifically as "the process of finding, contacting, and speaking with investment banking professionals to build relationships, gain knowledge, and eventually win interviews and job offers via those relationships." That framing is precise. The endpoint is not a good conversation. The endpoint is an interview and eventually an offer. Every tactical choice you make on a networking call should be evaluated against that endpoint. [1]

## The Numbers Behind Networking

Before diving into tactics, understand the volume this process requires. The data here is not encouraging if you are hoping for a quick shortcut - but it is clarifying.

Mergers and Inquisitions estimates that a full networking campaign for IB requires 300 to 500 outreach emails, 5 to 10 hours of work per week, sustained over 6 months, totaling over 100 hours of effort. [1] That is before a single superday invitation. These numbers apply to candidates at target schools with reasonable profiles. For non-target candidates, the volume requirements are higher.

Cold email response rates for IB outreach run at 5 to 10% under normal conditions. If your response rate is below 1%, that is a signal of a messaging problem - either the emails are too long, too generic, or you are targeting people at the wrong seniority level. [2]

RecruiterBase's conversion framework puts the call-to-interview rate at roughly 25%, meaning that for every 4 networking calls you hold, approximately 1 will eventually contribute to a formal interview invitation. To generate 30 interviews, you need approximately 120 calls. However, outcomes vary considerably - some candidates get offers from 20 calls, others need 80 or more. The shape of your particular campaign depends heavily on the quality of each conversation and how deliberately you follow up. [3]

For boutique firms specifically, the math works differently. Expect 50 to 100 emails to produce 5 to 10 responses, 1 to 2 actual calls, and potentially one offer from the entire cycle. Boutique outreach requires tighter targeting and higher-quality messages, because the contact lists are shorter and you have fewer opportunities to correct early mistakes. [2]

The reason to understand these numbers is not to be pessimistic. It is to calibrate correctly. Candidates who expect a fast return from 10 outreach messages will quit when it does not work. Candidates who understand this is a 6-month, 100-hour process will pace themselves accordingly and stay in the game long enough to see results.

## Before You Even Send the First Message

The most important networking work happens before you pick up the phone. Do not send a single outreach message until you have completed this preparation.

**Research the specific person.** LinkedIn is the obvious starting point but do not stop there. Look for media mentions, deal announcements, conference panels, published interviews. Know what deals their group has done in the past year. Know whether the firm recently had major news - an acquisition, a leadership change, a high-profile mandate. Bankers can tell within 30 seconds whether a candidate has done their homework. [4]

**Know the bank's recent deal flow.** Before a call, you should be able to name two or three recent transactions the bank advised on. This is publicly available information from press releases, the bank's own deal tombstones, and trade publications like The Deal, Bloomberg, or Reuters. Referencing specific recent activity signals that your interest is serious and informed.

**Have a genuine point of view.** The best networking calls have the texture of a real conversation, not an interrogation checklist. If you are calling a TMT banker, have an actual opinion about a sector development - a mega-merger, a regulatory shift, a valuation anomaly. You do not need to be right. You need to be thoughtful.

**Know exactly what you want from the call.** Are you trying to understand the group better? Are you trying to get a referral into the formal process? Are you trying to get clarity on whether your background is competitive? Be clear about your objective before you dial in.

**Prepare a condensed personal story.** According to Mergers and Inquisitions' cold email and call guide, candidates on calls should be ready to deliver a 1 to 2 sentence version of their story, explain specific firm interest, and discuss recent deals the bank has worked on - all in the first few minutes. [2] Rehearse this. It should not sound rehearsed, but it should be clean and tight.

## Who to Contact First

A critical and commonly violated rule: target Analysts and second-year Associates first, not VPs or MDs.

Senior bankers have the least time, the most filtered inboxes, and the fewest reasons to help an unknown candidate. Analysts and Associates have more recent recruiting memory, are closer in career stage to your situation, and are more likely to respond to a well-constructed message. They are also the ones who actually handle internal recruiting logistics at many firms - flagging resumes, making introductions, and vouching for candidates in team conversations. [1]

The sequence is: Analyst and Associate outreach first, build those relationships, and let senior introductions come through those junior contacts later. A warm introduction from a second-year Analyst to their VP carries significantly more weight than a cold message to the VP directly.

Robin Judson Partners, a recruiter firm specializing in financial services, notes that alumni from schools with fewer banking alumni are often disproportionately helpful - they feel a stronger sense of connection to other graduates from their institution, which increases both response rates and the depth of engagement during calls. If you are from a non-target or semi-target, prioritize alumni from your own school above all other contacts. [5]

## Outreach Messages That Get Responses

Most cold outreach messages fail for the same reasons: they are too long, too generic, or ask for too much too soon.

A message that works is short, specific, and makes it easy to say yes. The structure that performs well:

> Subject: Quick question from [School] about [Bank] [Group] - [Your Name]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I am a [year] at [school] currently recruiting for [season] IB analyst positions. I have been following [bank]'s recent work in [sector] - particularly the [specific deal or initiative] announced last month - and would find 15 minutes of your time genuinely valuable.
>
> I have done the background research and I am happy to work around your schedule entirely. No pressure if this is a bad time.
>
> Best,
> [Name]

This works because it is specific, it signals preparation, it is short, and it removes friction. Initial emails should be no more than five sentences. Use formal titles (Mr./Ms.) for senior bankers. Name-drop a university, firm, or mutual connection in the subject line where possible. [2]

Mergers and Inquisitions notes that LinkedIn should be used as a discovery tool, not a primary communication channel. Emails sent to work or personal addresses convert at higher rates than LinkedIn direct messages. Find the person on LinkedIn, identify their firm email format, and reach out directly. [1]

The full outreach sequence is 3 to 4 total emails per person maximum - an initial message plus 2 to 3 follow-ups. Send the first follow-up after one week of silence. The guide from Mergers and Inquisitions is blunt on this point: "most networking consists of following up repeatedly" - it is more about sustained effort than any single clever message. [2]

## Running the Call Itself

You have 15 minutes. Here is how to use them.

**Prefer audio over video for first conversations.** Audio calls are lower stakes, easier for the banker to squeeze into a busy day, and less demanding for both parties. Unless the banker specifically requests video, default to a phone call. [1]

**The first two minutes: set up, not small talk.** Thank them briefly for their time, give them a 30-second version of who you are and why you wanted to connect specifically with them, and signal that you have specific questions prepared. Do not fill the first five minutes with weather-level pleasantries. They do not have time for that and you are burning the most valuable part of the call.

**Minutes three through twelve: your questions.** Have five or six questions prepared and expect to get through three or four. The best questions are specific to their experience, demonstrate your research, and invite genuine reflection rather than recitation of bank marketing material.

Questions that work well:
- "Your group advised on [specific deal] last year. Without getting into anything non-public, what made that mandate competitive from a pitch standpoint?"
- "I am coming from a [consulting/Big Four/non-finance] background. What skill gaps do candidates with similar backgrounds typically show in their first six months?"
- "How does the analytical work shift between a standard M&A deal and a more complex structure like a royalty acquisition or a carve-out? Do coverage bankers engage with those mechanics directly?"

Questions to avoid:
- "What does your day look like?" (generic, wastes time)
- "What advice would you give someone interested in banking?" (puts them in professor mode)
- "How do I break into the industry?" (your problem, not theirs)
- Anything about compensation, hours, or work-life balance on the first call

**The last three minutes: the pivot.** Before the call ends, make a clean transition. Robin Judson Partners recommends being explicit about what you are looking for without wasting time - open a clear statement of your objective before the closing. [5] The framing that works: "I really appreciate the insight - this has been one of the more useful conversations I have had in recruiting. I would not want to overstep, but if there is ever a position you think I would be a strong fit for, I would be grateful if you kept me in mind."

Do not ask them to refer you in those words. Ask them to keep you in mind. It is a softer framing and more likely to get a yes.

RecruiterBase identifies the specific phrasing that works best at this moment: "What is the best way to be in the loop regarding recruitment?" or "How can I best position myself to be considered?" These questions invite the contact to champion you rather than forcing a binary yes-or-no hiring decision. [3]

**Ask for one additional introduction.** "Is there anyone else in the group - or at a related firm - you think I would benefit from speaking with?" One well-targeted introduction from a banker is worth more than ten cold messages. This single ask, done politely, can double or triple your network in weeks.

Do not extend the call past 15 minutes unless the banker explicitly signals they want to continue. Going over time signals poor social awareness and damages the impression. [1]

## The Follow-Up Protocol

Send a follow-up email within 24 hours of the call. Keep it to four or five sentences:
- Thank them for their time
- Reference one specific thing from the conversation (this proves you were paying attention)
- Express that you remain interested in the group
- Close cleanly without making a new ask

You already made your ask on the call. The follow-up is pure relationship maintenance.

Set a 30-day calendar reminder for a second touch. A brief check-in, ideally timed to something relevant - a deal announcement from the bank, a sector development you discussed, an update on your candidacy. This second touch is where most candidates drop off and where consistent candidates separate themselves.

After the initial conversion period, maintain contact every 6 to 18 months with contacts who were warm but did not immediately convert. Recruiting cycles are long. A contact who had no headcount when you first spoke may be a group head with open spots 18 months later. [1]

## The One Advocate Rule

Multiple practitioner sources converge on a point that is underappreciated: you do not need a broad network at any single firm. One full-time banker who is willing to push your resume into the formal process and say something positive when your name comes up internally - that is sufficient. [3]

This changes how you should allocate effort. Once you have a genuine advocate at a firm - someone who has explicitly said they will keep you in mind or make an introduction - redirect your networking energy to other target firms. Do not keep making calls at that firm for social coverage. You have what you need.

If your contacts at a firm remain lukewarm after two or three conversations, that is a signal to increase volume at that firm, not to follow up more with the same lukewarm contacts. You need a champion. If the first contact is not going to be that person, keep networking until you find someone who will.

## The Real Talk Version

A thread on Wall Street Oasis from a former Goldman analyst summarized the networking reality this way: "The calls that turned into interviews were never the ones where I liked the person the most. They were the ones where I finished the call thinking 'this person clearly knows what they are getting into and they are not going to embarrass me if I recommend them.' That is the threshold."

This framing is useful. The question a banker is unconsciously asking throughout your networking call is: "Would I be comfortable putting my name behind this person?" The goal is to meet that bar - through demonstrated preparation, intelligent questions, and a professional manner - not to be charming or liked.

McKinsey research on professional relationship formation consistently finds that demonstrated competence matters more than interpersonal warmth in professional contexts. In investment banking, this pattern is particularly pronounced. Bankers recommend people they believe can do the work, not people they enjoyed talking to.

## Non-Target School Considerations

If you are coming from a non-target school, networking calls are not just useful - they are structurally necessary. At most bulge brackets and elite boutiques, resumes from non-targets often do not make it to the desk of a group head through the formal application process. The referral pipeline is the only reliable path to visibility.

Mergers and Inquisitions' non-target networking guide recommends targeting 50 to 100 contacts during a full recruiting cycle, with the expectation that 10 to 20 meaningful relationships will develop from that outreach. [1] That is not inspirational. It is a volume-and-quality game played simultaneously, and the only way through it is to start 6 to 12 months before recruiting opens.

Starting too late - meaning under 3 months before recruiting season - means relationships are too shallow to produce referrals. The 6 to 12 month window is the consensus optimal range across practitioner guides. [1] [3]

The silver lining for non-target candidates is that bankers from non-target schools are often more accessible and more willing to pay it forward. Finding alumni at target banks who attended your school is frequently more productive than cold outreach to Ivy alumni who have no connection to you.

## What Converts and What Does Not

Based on practitioner accounts and the documented data from Mergers and Inquisitions, RecruiterBase, and Robin Judson Partners, here is an honest ranking of networking activity by conversion probability:

**High conversion:**
- Warm outreach through a shared alumni connection who makes an introduction
- Follow-up calls after an initial meeting where you reference something specific from the first conversation
- Networking contacts at firms where you have already applied and are in the formal process
- Alumni from your specific school, especially those from non-target backgrounds themselves

**Medium conversion:**
- Cold outreach to alumni from your school with no shared mutual connection
- Calls initiated from campus recruiting events where you had a notable brief interaction

**Low conversion:**
- Purely cold LinkedIn outreach with no shared connection
- Mass-applied template messages
- Calls where you have done no preparation and rely on generic questions

The conversion pattern is consistent across sources: specificity and preparation increase conversion; volume without targeting does not.

## Common Mistakes That Kill Conversions

Six mistakes that practitioners flag most frequently:

**Asking directly for a job.** The fastest way to end a conversation and burn a contact. Use indirect framing: ask how to position yourself to be considered, not whether there is a role for you. [3]

**Contacting senior bankers first.** MDs and VPs have the least time and fewest reasons to help unknown candidates. Analysts and Associates first, always. [1]

**Long or generic emails.** Any email over five or six sentences gets ignored. Generic outreach - with no reference to the person's specific work - gets deleted. [2]

**Using LinkedIn as a communication channel.** LinkedIn is a discovery tool. Emails to work or personal addresses convert at higher rates. [1]

**No follow-up after the call.** Failing to send a thank-you within 24 hours, or failing to maintain periodic contact afterward, wastes all the relationship capital built during the call.

**Making it feel like a job interview.** The informational interview format must feel like genuine curiosity and learning. The moment it feels transactional, bankers become guarded. [5]

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Build a target list of 40 to 60 contacts** across your target banks and groups. Prioritize alumni from your school, then second-degree connections, then cold contacts. Use LinkedIn, your school's alumni directory, and deal announcements to identify specific people at specific groups.

2. **Write customized outreach messages for each tier** - not 60 individual messages, but three or four templates differentiated by relationship warmth. A message to a direct alumni connection should read differently from a cold message to a senior banker you have no connection to.

3. **Prepare a question bank of 15 to 20 specific questions** across sectors and deal types. Good questions come from doing real research - read deal announcements, analyst reports, and sector coverage before every call.

4. **Set a weekly cadence** - send 8 to 10 outreach messages per week, hold 2 to 3 calls per week during active recruiting season, and do all follow-ups within 24 hours. Consistency over time matters more than any single call.

5. **Track everything.** A simple spreadsheet with name, bank, group, date contacted, response status, call date, follow-up sent, notes from the call, next action. Networking without a system degrades into chaos. A system lets you maintain dozens of relationships simultaneously without losing threads.
