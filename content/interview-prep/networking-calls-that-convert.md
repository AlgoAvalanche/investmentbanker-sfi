# Networking Calls That Convert to Superdays

Most investment banking candidates treat networking calls as a social obligation before the real work begins. They fire off templated LinkedIn messages, schedule 20-minute calls, ask three generic questions, and then wait for callbacks that never come. Then they conclude that networking does not work and spend the rest of recruiting season refreshing their email.

The candidates who convert networking calls into superday invitations are doing something structurally different. This article explains what that looks like.

---

## The Purpose of a Networking Call Is Not to Be Liked

Start with the right mental model. The purpose of a networking call in investment banking recruiting is not to make a friend. It is to accomplish two specific things: demonstrate that you are competent enough to be worth vouching for, and give the banker a reason to remember your name when headcount opens or when recruiting is discussed internally.

Bankers are busy. An analyst at a bulge bracket during deal season is regularly working until 2 or 3 a.m. When they agree to spend 20 minutes talking to you, they are making a real sacrifice. The candidates who waste that time with vague questions about "what does your day look like" or "what advice would you give someone interested in banking" are not going to be remembered. They might even leave a mildly negative impression.

The candidates who show up prepared, ask sharp questions, demonstrate knowledge, and make the interaction feel worthwhile - those are the people who get the follow-up email saying "we have a superday coming up, you should apply."

According to a widely circulated post on Wall Street Oasis, one former analyst at a major bank estimated that roughly 70% of the people he networked with before his own interviews were selected partly based on internal advocacy from someone who had talked to them beforehand. The formal application process is real, but referrals and internal flagging are how candidates get noticed in a pile of thousands.

---

## Before You Even Send the First Message

The most important networking work happens before you pick up the phone. Do not send a single outreach message until you have completed this preparation:

**Research the banker.** LinkedIn is the obvious starting point but do not stop there. Look for media mentions, deal announcements, conference panels, published interviews. Know what deals their group has done in the past year. Know whether the firm recently had major news - an acquisition, a leadership change, a high-profile mandate. Walk into every call knowing more about the person's work context than they expect you to.

**Know the bank's deal flow.** Before a call, you should be able to name two to three recent transactions the bank advised on. This is publicly available information from press releases, the bank's own deal tombstones, and trade publications like The Deal, Bloomberg, or Reuters. Referencing specific recent activity signals that your interest is serious and informed.

**Have a genuine point of view.** The best networking calls have the texture of a real conversation, not an interrogation checklist. If you are calling a TMT banker, have an actual opinion about a sector development - a mega-merger, a regulatory shift, a valuation anomaly. You do not need to be right. You need to be thoughtful.

**Know exactly what you want from the call.** Are you trying to understand the group better? Are you trying to get a referral into the formal process? Are you trying to get clarity on whether your background is competitive? Be clear about your objective before you dial in.

## Outreach Messages That Get Responses

Most cold outreach messages fail for the same reasons: they are too long, too generic, or ask for too much too soon.

A message that works is short, specific, and makes it easy to say yes. Here is a structure that performs well:

> Subject: Quick question from [School] about [Bank] [Group] - [Your Name]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I am a [year] at [school] currently recruiting for [season] IB analyst positions. I have been following [bank]'s recent work in [sector] - particularly the [specific deal or initiative] announced last month - and would find 15-20 minutes of your time genuinely valuable.
>
> I have done the background research and I am happy to work around your schedule entirely. No pressure if this is a bad time.
>
> Best,
> [Name]

This works because it is specific, it signals preparation, it is short, and it removes friction. The "no pressure if this is a bad time" line is not weakness - it is a social release valve that makes the yes easier.

Response rates on cold outreach are low regardless of how good your message is. Expect to reach out to 30 to 50 people to generate 8 to 12 meaningful conversations. According to Mergers and Inquisitions, the conversion rate from cold contact to actual conversation typically runs around 20 to 30% for well-targeted outreach. Build the numbers accordingly.

Alumni networks are significantly more productive than cold LinkedIn outreach. A message routed through your school's alumni directory, especially if you can reference a common professor or club, will convert at two to three times the rate of a pure cold message.

## Running the Call Itself

You have 20 minutes. Here is how to use them.

**The first two minutes: set up, not small talk.** Thank them briefly for their time, give them a 30-second version of who you are and why you wanted to connect specifically with them, and let them know you have specific questions prepared. Do not fill the first five minutes with weather-level pleasantries. They do not have time for that and you are burning the most valuable part of the call.

**Minutes three through fifteen: your questions.** Have five to six questions prepared and expect to get through three or four. The best questions are specific to their experience, demonstrate your research, and invite genuine reflection - not recitation of bank marketing material.

Questions that work well:
- "In the healthcare group specifically, how does the analytical work shift between a standard M&A deal and a licensing or royalty deal? I have read about how companies like Royalty Pharma structure their acquisitions and I am curious whether coverage bankers engage with those mechanics directly or hand off to product specialists."
- "Your group advised on [specific deal] last year. Without getting into anything non-public, what made that mandate competitive from a pitch standpoint? I am trying to understand how coverage groups differentiate themselves."
- "I am coming from a [consulting / Big Four / non-finance] background. Are there specific skill gaps that you think candidates with similar backgrounds typically show up with in the first six months?"

Questions to avoid:
- "What does your day look like?" (generic and wastes time)
- "What advice would you give someone interested in banking?" (also generic and puts them in professor mode rather than conversation mode)
- "How do I break into the industry?" (this is your problem, not theirs)
- Anything about compensation, hours, or work-life balance in the first call

**The last three to five minutes: the pivot.** Before the call ends, make a clean transition: "I really appreciate the insight - this has been one of the more useful conversations I have had in recruiting. I would not want to overstep, but if there is ever an opportunity to apply for a position you think I would be a strong fit for, I would be grateful if you kept me in mind."

Do not ask them to refer you in those words. Ask them to keep you in mind. It is softer and more likely to get a yes.

**Ask for one additional introduction.** "Is there anyone else in the group - or at a related firm - that you think I would benefit from speaking with?" One well-targeted introduction from a banker is worth more than ten cold messages. This single ask, done politely, can double or triple your network in a matter of weeks.

## The Follow-Up Protocol

Send a follow-up email within 12 hours of the call. Keep it to four or five sentences:
- Thank them for their time
- Reference one specific thing from the conversation (this proves you were paying attention)
- Express that you remain interested in the group
- Close cleanly

Do not ask for anything in the follow-up. You already made your ask on the call. The follow-up is pure relationship maintenance.

Set a 30-day calendar reminder to follow up again. A brief check-in, ideally timed to something relevant - a deal announcement from the bank, a sector development you discussed, an update on your candidacy. This second touch is where most candidates drop off and where consistent candidates separate themselves.

## The Real Talk Version

A thread on Wall Street Oasis from a former Goldman analyst summarized the networking reality well: "The calls that turned into interviews were never the ones where I liked the person the most. They were the ones where I finished the call thinking 'this person clearly knows what they are getting into and they are not going to embarrass me if I recommend them.' That is the threshold."

This framing is useful. The question a banker is unconsciously asking throughout your networking call is: "Would I be comfortable putting my name behind this person?" The goal is to meet that bar - through demonstrated preparation, intelligent questions, and a professional manner - not to be charming or to be liked.

McKinsey research on professional relationship formation consistently finds that demonstrated competence matters more than interpersonal warmth in professional contexts. In investment banking, this pattern is particularly pronounced. Bankers recommend people they believe can do the work, not people they enjoyed talking to.

## Non-Target School Considerations

If you are coming from a non-target school, networking calls are not just useful - they are structurally necessary. At most bulge brackets and elite boutiques, resumes from non-targets often do not make it to the desk of a group head through the formal application process. The referral pipeline is the only reliable way to get visibility.

This means you need to run more calls, run them with more precision, and be more deliberate about follow-through. The standard non-target networking playbook from Mergers and Inquisitions recommends targeting 50 to 100 contacts during a full recruiting cycle, with the expectation that 10 to 20 meaningful relationships will develop from that outreach. That is not inspirational - it is a volume-and-quality game played simultaneously.

The silver lining for non-target candidates is that bankers from non-target schools are often more accessible and more willing to pay it forward. Finding alumni at target banks who attended your school is often more productive than cold outreach to Ivy alumni.

---

## What Converts and What Does Not

Based on recruiting experience and the consistent data from practitioner accounts on WSO and Mergers and Inquisitions, here is a honest assessment of what networking activity actually converts:

**High conversion:**
- Warm outreach through a shared alumni connection who makes an introduction
- Follow-up calls after an initial meeting where you reference something specific from the first conversation
- Networking contacts at firms where you have already applied and are in the formal process

**Medium conversion:**
- Cold outreach to alumni from your specific school
- Calls initiated from campus recruiting events where you had a notable brief interaction

**Low conversion:**
- Purely cold LinkedIn outreach with no shared connection
- Mass-applied template messages
- Calls where you have done no preparation and rely on generic questions

The conversion pattern is clear: specificity and preparation increase conversion; volume without targeting does not.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Build a target list of 40 to 60 contacts** across your target banks and groups. Prioritize alumni from your school, then second-degree connections, then cold contacts. Use LinkedIn, your school's alumni directory, and deal announcements to identify specific people at specific groups.

2. **Write a customized outreach message for each tier** - not 60 individual messages, but three or four templates differentiated by relationship warmth. A message to a direct alumni connection should read differently from a cold message to a senior banker you have no connection to.

3. **Prepare a question bank of 15 to 20 specific questions** across sectors and deal types. Draw from Damodaran's valuation principles for analytical questions, from BIWS for technical modeling questions, and from Mergers and Inquisitions and WSO for practical questions about day-to-day work. Good questions come from doing real research.

4. **Set a weekly cadence** - send 8 to 10 outreach messages per week, hold 2 to 3 calls per week during active recruiting season, and do all follow-ups within 24 hours. Consistency over time matters more than any single call.

5. **Track everything.** A simple spreadsheet with name, bank, group, date contacted, response status, call date, follow-up sent, notes from the call, next action. Networking without a system degrades into chaos. A system lets you maintain dozens of relationships simultaneously without losing threads.
