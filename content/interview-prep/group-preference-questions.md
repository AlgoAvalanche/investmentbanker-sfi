# Group Preference Questions: Industry vs Product vs Coverage Explained

One of the most underestimated questions in any investment banking interview is also one of the most predictable: "Which group are you most interested in, and why?" Every serious candidate prepares for valuation questions, walk-me-through-a-DCF, and why-investment-banking. Almost none of them prepare adequately for group preference questions - which is exactly why those questions are useful to interviewers.

Your answer will be scrutinized more carefully than you think. Interviewers are not just collecting seating preference data. They are testing whether you understand how the bank is actually organized, whether your stated interest reflects genuine thinking, and whether you have done enough work to form a real point of view. A candidate who can explain why they want the healthcare group - and can discuss a recent deal, a relevant valuation consideration, and a sector dynamic - is a materially different candidate from one who says "healthcare is growing." The first candidate has a story. The second candidate has a keyword.

This article covers how banks are structured, how group preference questions function in the interview process, the real tradeoffs between major groups, and how to construct an answer that demonstrates professional seriousness rather than internet research.

---

## How Investment Banks Are Actually Organized

Before you can answer a group preference question intelligently, you need to understand what you are being asked to choose between. Banks organize their advisory and underwriting businesses in two primary ways: by product and by industry.

**Product groups** execute specific transaction types across all industries. The major product groups at a bulge bracket are:
- Mergers and Acquisitions (M&A)
- Leveraged Finance (LevFin)
- Equity Capital Markets (ECM)
- Debt Capital Markets (DCM)
- Restructuring

**Industry groups** - also called coverage groups or sector groups - cover clients within a specific sector and manage the ongoing banking relationship. The most common industry groups at major banks are:
- Technology, Media, and Telecom (TMT)
- Healthcare
- Financial Institutions Group (FIG)
- Real Estate, Gaming, Lodging, and Leisure
- Consumer and Retail
- Industrials
- Energy and Natural Resources

The structural logic matters. Coverage bankers own the client relationship. When a deal is live, they bring in product specialists as needed. A healthcare coverage banker works on everything their healthcare clients execute - M&A, equity raises, debt deals, whatever comes up. An M&A banker at a product group has no sector allegiance; they execute M&A transactions for whichever coverage group brings the mandate.

At boutiques and middle-market banks, this distinction blurs considerably. Evercore, Lazard, Centerview, and PJT run largely integrated models. At a smaller regional firm, you might be covering healthcare clients and running their M&A simultaneously. Understanding whether the bank you are interviewing at separates these functions is the first piece of research you need to do. Arriving at an interview without knowing this is avoidable and visible.

## The Real Hours and Pay Picture

Group preference questions are partly a proxy for career strategy questions, and the honest answer to those questions requires understanding the economics of each group. The data here is specific enough to cite directly.

M&A analysts at bulge brackets work approximately 85 to 100 hours per week. The modeling load is the heaviest of any group - DCF, LBO, accretion/dilution, merger models, fairness opinions. On an hours-adjusted basis, M&A effective hourly pay comes out to approximately $47 per hour when you factor in total compensation against actual time worked, according to data compiled by ibinterviewquestions.com.

ECM analysts work roughly 70 to 80 hours per week. The work is flow-driven - bookbuilding, pricing, roadshow coordination. The modeling is lighter; the transaction volume is higher. Exits are narrower: investor relations, equity research, convertibles hedge funds. Private equity is largely not accessible from ECM.

DCM analysts work approximately 60 to 70 hours per week. The work centers on credit analysis, bond structuring, and debt capital markets execution. Hours-adjusted pay in DCM is actually higher than M&A - approximately $53 per hour on an effective basis - because total compensation is comparable but hours are materially lower. Exits from DCM run to credit funds and fixed-income asset management, not broadly to private equity.

Leveraged Finance is the exception to the capital markets pattern. LevFin sits structurally within capital markets at most banks, but the work primarily funds acquisitions and leveraged buyouts. That means the modeling - LBO models, credit analysis on leveraged capital structures - overlaps substantially with M&A work. LevFin is the one capital markets product group where private equity exits are genuinely realistic, according to Mergers and Inquisitions' analysis of exit paths by group.

The senior compensation picture matters too. At the MD level, capital markets bankers earn an estimated 20 to 25 percent less than advisory bankers, according to Mergers and Inquisitions' coverage of capital markets versus investment banking economics. The reason is structural: capital markets fees distribute across multiple banks that participate in a deal, while advisory mandates are typically awarded to one or two firms. If you are optimizing for long-run earning potential as a senior banker, the advisory track - M&A, coverage groups - has the higher ceiling.

## Exit Optionality: The Hierarchy Candidates Need to Know

This topic comes up in every group preference conversation, and it is worth addressing directly rather than dancing around it. Private equity recruiting operates on a clear hierarchy of group prestige, and if you are going to express a group preference in an interview, you need to understand where your group sits.

The hierarchy, roughly, is: M&A and LevFin at the top, followed by industry coverage groups, followed by ECM and DCM at the bottom. Per Mergers and Inquisitions, private equity recruitment is "not feasible from most ECM or DCM teams." LevFin is the stated exception due to the LBO modeling exposure.

This creates a real strategic dynamic for candidates. Because M&A preserves the broadest optionality - into private equity, hedge funds, corporate development, and every other coverage or product group - many analysts default to targeting M&A regardless of genuine interest. The lateral from M&A to capital markets is straightforward; the lateral from ECM or DCM to M&A is materially harder because of the modeling skill gap. Once you are in ECM or DCM, the M&A door mostly closes unless you are willing to take an aggressive path through business school or a lateral to a different bank.

If you are in an M&A interview, you can speak to exit optionality as one element of your interest. If you are in an ECM or DCM interview, do not bring up private equity exits. You are interviewing for a seat in that group. Frame your answer around the work itself - client impact, market judgment, execution skills, the depth of relationships with institutional investors. Telling an ECM interviewer that you want M&A for PE exits is insulting to the group and signals you have not thought about what you are actually interviewing for.

## Why Group Preference Questions Are a Trap

Most candidates walk into interviews having done surface-level research and settled on a preference they cannot defend. The standard answer - "I am interested in TMT because technology is transforming every industry" - is so common that experienced interviewers have stopped registering it. Wall Street Oasis community discussions on this topic consistently flag that recruiters at bulge brackets hear the "technology is disrupting everything" pitch dozens of times per day during recruiting season. It signals nothing except that you have access to a search engine.

The trap is that candidates treat group preference questions as soft questions requiring soft answers. They are not. A strong answer requires demonstrated knowledge of what the group actually does on a daily basis, a credible reason why your background or interests connect to that work, awareness of the group's competitive position or deal flow context, and evidence that you have thought about this beyond a Wikipedia summary of the industry.

As Wall Street Prep frames it, candidates who say "I want M&A for the exits" without being able to articulate the day-to-day work signal they have not done their research. The answer should not be purely strategic - it should show genuine interest in the actual work. Fairness opinions, deal structuring, synergy modeling, management presentations: these are the things M&A analysts spend their time on. If you want M&A, speak to that substance.

There is a further complication that many candidates miss. JPMorgan explicitly asks candidates to state group preferences on the application itself - before any interview. This is documented in Wall Street Oasis resources on IB recruiting. That means group preference is not purely an interview preparation exercise. Some banks want to know your answer before they even schedule a meeting. Treating this as an afterthought is not a viable approach.

## Industry Group vs. Product Group: Which to Express a Preference For

The answer depends on what the bank has and what role you are interviewing for.

**If you are interviewing at a bulge bracket for a generalist analyst position**, expressing a preference for a specific product group like M&A is defensible and often signals intellectual focus. M&A is universally understood and respected. Saying you want M&A because you want to develop deep skills in how businesses are valued and combined - and demonstrating that you actually understand M&A advisory mechanics - is a legitimate answer. Street of Walls' guidance on group preference directly recommends expressing industry group preference for the broader deal exposure, then pivoting to signal flexibility. The underlying principle: show conviction, then show adaptability.

**If you are interviewing for a coverage group role**, the answer is simpler. Express a genuine and informed preference for that sector. You were invited to interview for a healthcare seat. Show that you want the healthcare seat. Genuine interest in the sector, backed by sector knowledge, is the entire job at that moment.

**If you are interviewing at an elite boutique**, the product versus coverage distinction largely disappears. Evercore, Lazard, Centerview, and PJT run integrated models where bankers work across deal types. Express interest in the firm's deal flow and transaction types rather than trying to map to a specific group structure that may not exist.

## How to Answer the Question for Each Major Group

### TMT (Technology, Media, and Telecom)

TMT has been one of the most active M&A sectors globally for much of the past decade. Any credible answer for TMT interest needs to go beyond "technology is growing." Strong talking points include the deal structures common in software M&A - ARR multiples, customer retention adjustments, earnouts tied to product roadmaps - how streaming restructured the media landscape and what M&A logic followed, and infrastructure themes such as tower company consolidation, fiber buildouts, and spectrum transactions. Be specific about a deal the group has executed. Generic enthusiasm does not survive follow-up questions.

### Healthcare Banking

Healthcare is one of the most technically demanding coverage groups because the range of sub-sectors is enormous: large pharma, biotech, medical devices, healthcare services, managed care, life sciences tools. Each sub-sector has distinct valuation methodology and regulatory considerations.

Biopharma deals often require analysts to work with probability-adjusted NPV models for pipeline drugs - something you will not find in consumer or industrials banking. For sector-specific groups like healthcare, Mergers and Inquisitions' guidance on industry-specific interviews is direct: you must demonstrate genuine sector knowledge, not just general interest. PDUFA dates and how FDA approval timelines affect M&A timing, royalty monetization structures in pharma licensing deals, and how managed care consolidation has been challenged on antitrust grounds are the kinds of talking points that separate credible candidates from Google-level preparation.

### M&A Group (Product)

Expressing a preference for the M&A group is always an acceptable answer at banks that have a dedicated M&A product group. The key is demonstrating that you understand the intellectual substance of M&A advisory - not just that you want to do "big deals."

M&A analysts at product groups typically have less client interaction than coverage bankers but go deeper on transaction mechanics, per Mergers and Inquisitions' analysis of product group roles. Fairness opinions, deal structuring, synergy analysis, merger models - this is the technical core of the role. Speak to that content. Reference a transaction type you find analytically interesting. Do not reference deal league tables.

### Financial Institutions Group (FIG)

FIG is one of the most misunderstood groups by candidates, which is also its competitive advantage for prepared ones. Banks, insurance companies, and asset managers are not valued the way industrial companies are. There are no clean DCF models anchored to EBITDA multiples. Financial companies require price-to-book, tangible book value, regulatory capital ratio analysis, and ROE-based frameworks. If you express a preference for FIG, you need to demonstrate that you understand these structural differences. The reward for doing so is a thinner competitive field - fewer candidates can speak to FIG credibly.

### Restructuring

Restructuring is genuinely counter-cyclical. When the economy contracts, restructuring desks get busier. The work is intellectually distinct from M&A advisory: recovery waterfalls, priority claims analysis, distressed modeling. The honest pitch for restructuring interest is that you are drawn to situations where the financial analysis has real consequences, where capital structures are broken, and where the solutions are not obvious. If you claim this interest, be ready to discuss a recent bankruptcy case, a recovery analysis, or the mechanics of a Chapter 11 plan.

### Leveraged Finance

LevFin is the most misclassified group in candidate thinking. It looks like a capital markets group on an org chart but functions more like M&A in practice because the work - financing acquisitions and LBOs - requires LBO modeling, credit analysis on leveraged capital structures, and an understanding of how private equity sponsors underwrite deals. LevFin is the one capital markets group where credible PE exits are documented. If you are targeting LevFin, frame your interest around the transaction types it serves rather than positioning it as a lighter version of M&A.

## Structuring Your Answer: A Practical Framework

A strong group preference answer follows a clear structure. State the preference clearly without immediate hedging - "I am most interested in the healthcare group" is a clean opening. "I think I might be drawn toward a few different groups" is not. Connect the preference to something concrete you have done or read: a deal you analyzed, a company you followed, a course you took, a sector you researched. Demonstrate knowledge of what the group actually does through one or two specific examples that reflect genuine understanding. Tie it to a long-term professional interest, not exit optionality.

The full answer should run 90 to 120 seconds. Enough to show substance. Not enough to lecture. Practice it out loud before the interview. Timing yourself matters - 90 seconds of dense content is harder to deliver smoothly than it sounds.

Mergers and Inquisitions frames the group preference question as part of the broader "why this bank and group" cluster. The site's consistent advice is that a well-constructed personal narrative should answer most of these questions organically. Your story should already explain why this group at this bank makes sense given your background. If it does not, the group preference answer will feel disconnected and improvised.

## The Authenticity Problem

There is a real tension here that deserves direct treatment. Many candidates, particularly undergraduates, do not have strong group preferences. They want a job in investment banking and will take whatever seat they get. This is understandable and is the reality for the majority of people entering the industry.

The mistake is performing a passion you do not have. Senior bankers who have been running interviews for a decade detect fabricated enthusiasm quickly. The better approach is to identify a group you can speak to credibly, do enough research to have a genuine point of view, and then express that view honestly. "I have been particularly drawn to the healthcare group after reading extensively about biopharma deal structures and following several transactions closely over the past year" is more believable than a manufactured passion narrative. You do not need to have always wanted to do healthcare banking. You need to have thought seriously about it before walking into the room.

## Common Mistakes

**Saying you have no preference.** This signals you have not thought about your career. It reads as lack of initiative and self-awareness.

**Being so specific you appear inflexible.** "I will only join M&A" is a red flag. Interviewers want candidates who will be effective wherever they land. Conviction is good. Rigidity is not.

**Citing exit optionality as the primary reason.** Telling an ECM interviewer you want M&A for PE exits is not just strategically wrong - it is insulting to the group you are sitting in front of.

**Citing hours or lifestyle as your reason.** Saying you prefer DCM because of better work-life balance signals you are optimizing for comfort. Frame your answer around the work type and skills you want to develop.

**No sector knowledge to back the claim.** Saying you prefer healthcare without being able to name recent transactions or key sector metrics collapses under the first follow-up question.

**Mentioning a group the bank does not have.** Research the specific bank's group structure before every interview. This is non-negotiable.

**Conflating coverage and product groups.** Saying "I want to work in M&A coverage" at a bank where M&A is a product group is an error that will be noticed. Know the difference and know which structure the bank uses.

**Applying the same answer to every bank.** Goldman's TMT franchise is different from a mid-market firm's TMT practice. Tailor your preference to the specific bank's group reputation and deal flow history.

**Not preparing for deal-specific follow-ups.** Stating a group preference will trigger deal questions. If you claim interest in Energy, be ready to discuss an energy transaction in detail - structure, rationale, outcome.

**Framing capital markets work negatively when interviewing for it.** Never describe ECM or DCM as "less technical" or "less interesting" if you are interviewing for those groups. Focus on client impact, market judgment, execution skills, and the depth of institutional investor relationships.

## What You Should Do Next

Map the bank's group structure before every interview. Go to the bank's website and find their coverage and product group list. Know it before you walk in. Arriving without this is avoidable.

Pick two to three groups you can speak to credibly and build actual talking points for each - a recent deal, a sector dynamic, a valuation consideration specific to that space. Depth on two groups beats surface knowledge on seven.

Read at least one deal announcement or press release from the group you are targeting. Understand what the transaction was, why it happened, and what the structural logic was. Reference it in your answer.

Network with analysts at the groups you are targeting. Per ibinterviewquestions.com's guidance on group research, analysts give more honest views on group realities than recruiters do. Use that access.

Review Mergers and Inquisitions and Wall Street Oasis resources on specific groups to understand the actual day-to-day experience and honest trade-offs between coverage and product. The information is freely available. Your failure to use it will be apparent to anyone who has been doing this for more than a year.

Practice the answer out loud. Group preference questions sound easy but derail candidates who have not rehearsed them. Time yourself. Ninety seconds of dense, credible content delivered smoothly is the target.
