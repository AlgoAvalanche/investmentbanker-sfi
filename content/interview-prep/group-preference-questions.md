# Group Preference Questions: Industry vs Product vs Coverage Explained

One of the most underestimated questions in any investment banking interview is also one of the most predictable: "Which group are you most interested in, and why?" This is a group preference question, and your answer to it will be scrutinized more carefully than most candidates realize. Interviewers are not just collecting preference data. They are testing whether you understand how the bank is organized, whether your stated interest is genuine, and whether you have done enough work to have a real opinion.

This article breaks down how banks are structured, how group preference questions actually function in the interview process, and how to construct an answer that does not immediately disqualify you.

---

## How Investment Banks Are Actually Organized

Before you can answer a group preference question intelligently, you need to understand what you are being asked to choose between. Banks organize their advisory and underwriting businesses in two primary ways: by product and by industry.

**Product groups** execute specific transaction types across all industries. The major product groups at a bulge bracket are:
- Mergers and Acquisitions (M&A)
- Leveraged Finance (LevFin)
- Equity Capital Markets (ECM)
- Debt Capital Markets (DCM)
- Restructuring

**Industry groups** (also called coverage groups or sector groups) cover clients within a specific sector and manage the overall banking relationship. The most common industry groups are:
- Technology, Media, and Telecom (TMT)
- Healthcare
- Financial Institutions Group (FIG)
- Real Estate, Gaming, Lodging, and Leisure (REGL or similar)
- Consumer and Retail
- Industrials
- Energy and Natural Resources

At most large banks, coverage bankers own the client relationship. When a deal is live, they bring in product specialists as needed. A healthcare coverage banker works on everything their healthcare clients do - M&A, equity raises, debt deals, whatever comes up. An M&A banker at a product group has no sector allegiance; they execute M&A transactions for whoever needs them.

At boutiques and middle-market banks, this distinction blurs. Evercore and Lazard run largely integrated structures. At a smaller regional bank, you might be covering healthcare AND running M&A. The organizational logic matters because it shapes what your day looks like and what your exit options look like.

## Why Group Preference Questions Are a Trap (and How to Avoid Stepping in It)

Most candidates walk into interviews having done a superficial Google search and settled on "I'm interested in TMT because technology is transforming every industry." This answer is so common that experienced interviewers have stopped registering it. According to discussions on Wall Street Oasis, recruiters at bulge brackets hear the "technology is disrupting everything" pitch dozens of times per day during recruiting season. It signals nothing except that you have access to the internet.

The trap in group preference questions is that candidates treat them as soft questions requiring soft answers. They are not. A strong answer to a group preference question requires:

1. Demonstrated knowledge of what that group actually does day to day
2. A credible reason why your background or interests connect to that work
3. Awareness of the group's competitive position or deal flow context
4. Some evidence that you have thought about this beyond surface level

One WSO thread on this topic put it bluntly: "If you say TMT because you use an iPhone, you are done. If you can talk about a specific deal the TMT group closed last year and explain what made it interesting structurally, you are a different candidate."

## Industry Group vs Product Group: Which Should You Express a Preference For?

This is where candidates tie themselves in knots. The answer depends on what the bank actually has and what role you are interviewing for.

**If you are interviewing at a bulge bracket for a generalist analyst position**, expressing a preference for a specific product group like M&A is fine and often signals intellectual focus. M&A is universally prestigious and intellectually demanding. Saying you want M&A because you want to understand how businesses are valued and combined - and demonstrating you actually know how M&A advisory works - is a legitimate and respected answer.

**If you are interviewing for a coverage group role**, you had better express a genuine and informed preference for that sector. Walking into a healthcare banking interview and saying "I am actually most interested in the M&A group" is not a disqualifying move everywhere, but it is a puzzling one. You were invited to interview for a healthcare seat. Show that you want the healthcare seat.

**If you are interviewing at an elite boutique**, the product versus coverage distinction largely disappears. Evercore, Lazard, Centerview, PJT - these firms run integrated models. Express interest in the firm's deal flow and transaction types rather than trying to map to a specific group.

Damodaran's work on valuation is fundamentally sector-agnostic, but practitioners know that sector fluency matters enormously in coverage roles. A healthcare coverage banker who cannot speak to FDA approval processes, drug pricing dynamics, or biotech licensing structures is not going to be effective with healthcare CFOs. If you claim sector interest, you need sector knowledge.

## How to Answer the Group Preference Question for Each Major Group

### TMT (Technology, Media, and Telecom)

TMT has been the most active M&A sector for much of the past decade. Any credible answer for TMT interest needs to go beyond "technology is growing." Strong talking points include:

- The deal structures common in software M&A (ARR multiples, customer retention adjustments, earnouts tied to product roadmaps)
- How streaming has restructured the media landscape and what M&A has followed (consolidation plays, content acquisition logic)
- Infrastructure themes: tower companies, fiber buildouts, spectrum auctions

**Avoid**: Saying you are interested in TMT because you worked at a tech startup and found it exciting. Enthusiasm for the sector as an operator is not the same as demonstrating banking judgment about that sector.

### Healthcare Banking

Healthcare is one of the most technically demanding coverage groups. The range of sub-sectors is enormous: large pharma, biotech, medical devices, healthcare services, managed care, life sciences tools. Each has distinct valuation methodologies and regulatory considerations.

Macabacus has noted that biopharma deals often require analysts to get comfortable with probability-adjusted NPV models for pipeline drugs - something you will not find in consumer or industrials banking. If you are expressing interest in healthcare, show that you understand this complexity.

Strong talking points for healthcare:
- PDUFA dates and how FDA timelines affect M&A timing
- The royalty monetization structure common in pharma licensing deals
- How managed care consolidation has been challenged on antitrust grounds

### M&A Group (Product)

Expressing a preference for the M&A group is always an acceptable answer at banks that have a dedicated M&A product group. The key is demonstrating that you understand the intellectual substance of M&A advisory - not just that you want to do "big deals."

According to Mergers and Inquisitions, M&A analysts at product groups typically have less client interaction than coverage bankers but go deeper on transaction mechanics. Fairness opinions, deal structuring, synergy analysis, merger models - this is the technical core of the role. Speak to that, not to deal league tables.

### Financial Institutions Group (FIG)

FIG is one of the most misunderstood groups by candidates. Banks, insurance companies, and asset managers are not valued the way industrial companies are. There are no clean DCF models with EBITDA multiples. The financials work differently - price-to-book, tangible book value, regulatory capital ratios, ROE-based frameworks.

If you express a preference for FIG, you need to demonstrate you understand these structural differences. The reward for knowing FIG is that far fewer candidates can speak to it credibly, which means your competition is thinner if you do the work.

### Restructuring

Restructuring is genuinely counter-cyclical. When the economy contracts, restructuring desks get busier. The work is intellectually distinct - you are building recovery waterfalls, understanding priority claims, modeling distressed scenarios. BIWS has published detailed guides on restructuring modeling that are worth reviewing if you are targeting this group.

The honest pitch for restructuring interest: you are drawn to situations where the financial analysis has real consequences and the solutions are not obvious.

## Structuring Your Answer: A Framework

A strong group preference answer follows a simple structure:

**1. State the preference clearly.** Do not hedge immediately. "I am most interested in the healthcare group" is a clean opening. "I think I might be drawn toward a few different groups" is not.

**2. Connect it to something concrete you have done or read.** A deal you analyzed. A company you followed. A course you took. Something real.

**3. Demonstrate knowledge of what the group actually does.** One or two specific things the group works on that reflect actual understanding.

**4. Tie it to a genuine long-term interest.** Not "the exits are great" - a genuine intellectual or professional interest.

The full answer should run 90 to 120 seconds in delivery. Enough to show substance. Not enough to lecture.

## The Authenticity Problem

There is a real tension here that deserves honesty. Many candidates, particularly undergraduates, do not actually have strong group preferences. They want a job in investment banking and they will take whatever seat they can get. This is completely understandable and is the reality for most people entering the industry.

The mistake is pretending to a passion you do not have. Interviewers, particularly senior bankers who have been doing this for a decade, can detect fabricated enthusiasm quickly. The better approach is to identify a group that you can speak to credibly, do enough research to have a genuine point of view, and then express that view honestly. "I have been particularly drawn to the healthcare group after spending time reading about biopharma deal structures" is more believable than a manufactured passion narrative.

You do not need to have always wanted to do healthcare banking. You need to have thought seriously about it before walking into the room.

## Common Mistakes in Group Preference Answers

- **Mentioning a group the bank does not have.** Research the bank's actual group structure before the interview.
- **Saying M&A because "it is the most prestigious."** Everyone knows this is the reason. Saying it out loud is the mistake.
- **Expressing too many preferences.** "I am interested in TMT, healthcare, M&A, and also potentially restructuring" signals you have not thought about it.
- **Confusing coverage and product groups.** Saying "I want to be in the M&A coverage group" at a bank where M&A is a product group is an error that will be noticed.
- **Citing personal use of consumer products.** Saying you are interested in consumer banking because you are a loyal customer of various brands is not a banking argument.

---

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Map the bank's group structure before every interview.** Go to the bank's website and find their coverage and product group list. Know it cold. Arriving without this knowledge is avoidable and inexcusable.

2. **Pick two to three groups you can speak to credibly** and build actual talking points for each - a recent deal, a sector dynamic, a valuation consideration specific to that space. Depth on two groups beats surface knowledge on seven.

3. **Read at least one deal tombstone or press release from the group you are targeting.** Understand what the transaction was, why it happened, and what the structural logic was. Reference it in your answer.

4. **Review Mergers and Inquisitions and Wall Street Oasis threads on specific groups** to understand the actual day-to-day experience and the honest trade-offs between coverage and product. The information is freely available and your failure to use it will be apparent.

5. **Practice the answer out loud.** Group preference questions sound simple but derail candidates who have not rehearsed. Time yourself. Ninety seconds of substance is harder to deliver than it sounds.
