# Why Investment Banking: Answering Without Sounding Like Everyone Else

The most important question in any investment banking interview is also the one candidates prepare for least carefully.

Not because they skip it - they don't. They rehearse an answer. They just rehearse the wrong answer. They memorize a version that every other candidate has also memorized, delivered in language that was probably assembled from a prep guide written by someone who has never sat in a deal room, and the person across the table recognizes it in about four seconds.

"Why investment banking?" sounds like a soft question. It is not. It is the question that determines whether the interviewer spends the rest of the conversation trying to find reasons to pass you or trying to find reasons to hire you.

This article covers what interviewers are actually asking, what kills answers, what works, and how to construct a response that sounds like you wrote it - because you did.

---

## What the Interviewer Is Actually Asking

When a vice president or associate asks "why investment banking?", they are asking several things simultaneously.

**Are you here for the right reasons?** Banking is genuinely demanding. According to the [Wall Street Oasis 2024 Investment Banking Work Conditions Survey](https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/investment-banking/wso-2024-investment-banking-work-conditions-survey-results-4th-annual) - the fourth annual version of their industry-wide study - the average analyst workweek sits at 72 hours as of 2024, down from 82 hours in 2021. Peak deal periods push that to 70-90 hours. Interviewers have been burned by people who joined for the prestige or the bonus and were miserable and useless by month four. They want people who have at least done the work of understanding what the job actually involves before they walk in the door.

**Do you know what this job is?** Many candidates confuse investment banking with investing. They conflate it with hedge funds, private equity, or venture capital. Others have a vague sense that banking involves "big deals" but have never articulated what specifically they find compelling about advisory work, capital markets, or structured finance. The question surfaces whether you have done your homework. As [Wall Street Prep's interview preparation guide](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/why-investment-banking/) notes, strong answers demonstrate understanding of the actual functions: M&A advisory and capital markets underwriting - not some generalized notion of "working in finance."

**Will you last?** The real filter underneath the question: is this someone who will still be here in eighteen months, pulling their weight on a live deal at midnight? The [Investment Banking Council of America](https://www.investmentbankingcouncil.org/blog/the-truth-behind-100-hour-work-weeks-in-investment-banking) notes that while the 100-hour week is often a peak-deal exception rather than a weekly constant, the average analyst workweek still runs 50-70 hours, with significant spikes during closings. Interviewers want people who have internalized this and are genuinely prepared for it - not candidates who will check out the moment they realize the job involves building 200-page PowerPoint decks and updating data rooms.

**Will you actually stay?** This is less discussed but increasingly relevant. As [Mergers & Inquisitions' definitive interview guide](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/investment-banking-interview-questions-and-answers/) bluntly states: "almost everyone from 'elite schools' wants to get into private equity ASAP, which runs contrary to the long-term commitment that banks are now trying to encourage." Banks have grown skeptical of candidates who treat the two-year analyst program as a stepping stone and nothing more. A strong "why banking" answer does not require you to claim you will stay for twenty years - but it does require you to demonstrate that you actually want to do the work, not just collect a brand name on your resume.

**Can you communicate clearly about yourself?** How you answer this question is a preview of how you will answer questions from clients and senior bankers when the stakes are real. Vagueness, over-rehearsed boilerplate, and a failure to connect narrative to specifics are all red flags about how you will perform in client-facing situations.

## The Answers That Kill Candidacies

Before building a good answer, catalogue exactly what not to say.

**"I want to learn about finance and business."**

This is the single most common answer given and the single most damaging one. It tells the interviewer you have not thought hard about this. It is true of every candidate in the building. It is also true of people who want to be accountants, consultants, and corporate treasurers. It differentiates you from no one. [Mergers & Inquisitions identifies this category directly](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/why-investment-banking/): generic phrases like "fast-paced environment," "learning opportunities," and "well-rounded skill set" are immediate disqualifiers because they apply to any role in any competitive industry.

**"I want to work on big deals and have an impact."**

Vague aspiration with no substance. What kind of deals? What kind of impact? The word "impact" in particular has been so thoroughly emptied of meaning by candidates who picked it up from MBA career guides that it now functions as a signal of inauthenticity.

**"Investment banking is a great training ground."**

You are applying for an analyst program. Of course it is a training ground. This answer tells the interviewer you are already thinking about your exit and have given no thought to the actual work. Given that banks are increasingly trying to cultivate analysts who genuinely want to be there - rather than treating banking as a two-year holding pen before private equity - this answer now lands worse than it used to.

**"I was inspired by [famous banker or film]."**

Unless you have a genuinely interesting story about a real person you know personally or have followed closely for years, drop any reference to being inspired by famous financiers or pop culture. Saying you were inspired by watching a financial documentary is not a "why investment banking" answer - it is a viewing history.

**"I interned at a boutique and loved the experience."**

This can be part of a good answer but cannot be the whole answer. "I liked it when I did it" does not explain why you want to continue doing it. You need to connect the experience to specific things you found compelling - not just assert that the experience was positive. What did you actually do? What was it about that work that you found yourself thinking about after hours? That is the answer.

**Never mention compensation, exit opportunities, or prestige.**

[Wall Street Prep is explicit on this](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/why-investment-banking/): do not mention money, bonuses, or exit opportunities. Yes, these are real motivations for nearly everyone in the recruiting pipeline. You still cannot cite them. The interviewer knows they exist. Saying them out loud signals either naivety about interview dynamics or a lack of anything more substantive to say.

## What a Strong Answer Actually Contains

A strong "why investment banking" answer has three components. They do not need to be labeled or delivered in strict order, but all three need to be present.

**1. A specific experience or action - not just stated interest.**

[Mergers & Inquisitions makes a pointed observation on this](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/why-investment-banking/): "talk is cheap." Saying you are interested in banking is not evidence of interest in banking. Evidence is a class you took, an informational interview you had, a deal you followed obsessively, a student club you joined, a financial model you built on your own. Interviewers are evaluating whether you have taken concrete steps, not whether you can describe a feeling.

The specificity is the point. It signals that this is a real thought, not a performance.

**2. An explanation of what specifically about banking - as opposed to adjacent careers - appeals to you.**

This is where most candidates are weakest. What is it about banking specifically - as opposed to private equity, consulting, or corporate development - that you find compelling?

Legitimate answers include: the advisory relationship (working alongside management teams at critical moments in a company's life), the breadth of analytical exposure across sectors and transaction types, the pace and pressure of live deal environments, the capital markets work (understanding how debt and equity instruments are priced and structured), the chance to see how companies actually make strategic decisions when real money and real timelines are at stake.

None of those answers is a cliche. They are specific things about what bankers actually do. If one of them is genuinely true for you, say it and explain why. If none of them resonate, that is a different problem.

[Wall Street Prep's framework](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/why-investment-banking/) articulates three elements interviewers want to see: evidence that the career decision is based on logical reasoning (skill-building, personal development, genuine interest in the work), demonstrated understanding of what banking actually does, and acknowledgment that you understand the demanding nature of the commitment and have thought seriously about it.

**3. A connection to this bank and this group specifically.**

This is the anchor that makes the answer about the room you are sitting in, not about banking as an abstraction. For advice on differentiating between banks, see [/industry/bulge-bracket-vs-elite-boutique/](/industry/bulge-bracket-vs-elite-boutique/). For this portion of the answer, you need something demonstrably specific: a deal this bank recently advised on that you can discuss intelligently, a sector focus of the group you are interviewing with, a conversation with a banker from this firm that revealed something real about how they work.

[Mergers & Inquisitions notes](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/why-investment-banking/) that if mentioning the specific firm feels forced or extends the answer beyond 60 seconds, omit it rather than pad. The firm-specific component adds value when it is genuine - when it is manufactured, it subtracts value.

## The Answer Is the End of Your Story, Not a Standalone Question

One of the most useful reframes in [Mergers & Inquisitions' treatment of this question](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/why-investment-banking/) is that "why banking?" should not even feel like a question if you have structured your full narrative properly. Your answer to "walk me through your background" should build naturally toward banking interest - the experiences, the analytical work, the moments that sharpened your focus. The "why banking?" question is just the conclusion of that story.

This reframe changes how you prepare. Instead of rehearsing an isolated 60-second answer, you are constructing a coherent narrative arc: where you started, what drew your interest, what concrete steps you took, and why banking is the logical continuation. When the "why banking?" question comes, you are simply completing a sentence the interviewer already understands the beginning of.

M&I's target length - 30 to 40 seconds, or roughly 50 to 85 words spoken at average pace - is worth taking seriously. Most candidates over-answer this question. A tightly constructed 45-second answer that is genuinely specific is more persuasive than a two-minute recitation of generic motivations.

## The Undergraduate vs. MBA Difference

The standard of evidence is different depending on where you are in your career.

**Undergraduates:** Interviewers expect that your interest is primarily intellectual and directional. You have probably had one internship, possibly in a relevant area. Your answer can legitimately include a compelling course, a personal interest in how markets work, a summer internship at a smaller firm, or an academic research project in corporate finance. What you cannot do is pretend to a depth of conviction that would only come from years of experience. The concrete action you cite - the informational interview you scheduled, the deal you read about for weeks, the financial model you built to understand a transaction - is what carries the answer.

**MBA candidates:** The bar is significantly higher. You have had a pre-MBA career. You have seen the alternatives. Your "why banking?" answer needs to account for what you were doing before and why you are making this specific choice now rather than pursuing more of the same.

[Mergers & Inquisitions' guide on switching from adjacent fields](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/why-investment-banking/) gives concrete examples of legitimate switching rationales: from private equity, where you want more deal variety and less portfolio monitoring work; from venture capital, where you want fewer early-stage rejections and more transaction advisory; from hedge funds, where you want to be directly advising companies rather than following them from the outside. These are real distinctions that reflect an understanding of what each role actually involves.

The best MBA answers explain what the pre-MBA career provided - industry expertise, client relationships, analytical foundation - and what banking specifically adds that the previous path did not: transaction advisory, capital structure decisions, deal execution experience.

## Practical Answer Structures

**Structure 1: The Linear Path**

Used when your background builds naturally toward banking. Works best for candidates whose coursework, internships, and interests form a coherent narrative.

"My interest in banking came from [specific starting point]. During [specific experience], I became interested in [specific aspect of banking work]. That led me to [next step], where I [specific thing you did or learned]. What I found was that what I actually enjoyed most was [specific thing that connects to banking]. This firm specifically makes sense because [specific reason]."

**Structure 2: The Inflection Point**

Used when there is a clear moment - a transaction you followed, a conversation with a banker, an experience in an adjacent role - that crystallized your interest. Works well for candidates who came to banking interest through an adjacent path.

"I came to banking through [adjacent area]. [Describe the specific moment or experience]. What that experience made clear was [insight about banking work]. Since then I have [concrete steps taken toward banking]. I am interested in this group specifically because [specific reason]."

**Structure 3: The Honest Pivot (MBA Candidates)**

Used when the pre-MBA background is in an adjacent area and the MBA is the explicit transition vehicle. Honesty works better than retrofitting a false narrative.

"My background in [previous field] gave me [specific thing]. What I have consistently found most interesting, though, is [aspect of banking work that connects to that background]. Banking lets me [specific contribution or capability]. The MBA is the bridge I needed to make that transition credibly, and I am targeting [specific groups] because [specific reason related to their sector or deal flow]."

## Real Candidate Examples

The recurring themes in compelling answers are notable for what they are not - they are not polished, they are not particularly eloquent, but they are specific and they are honest.

One analyst at Goldman described an undergraduate candidate who had spent two summers working in the shipping industry through a family connection, had followed a large vessel acquisition deal closely as a personal project, and described in detail why the capital structure of the deal had been fascinating to him. The interviewer pulled the deal up on a Bloomberg terminal and they spent fifteen minutes discussing it. The candidate got the offer.

Another example: a candidate who had worked in strategy consulting, had been staffed on a major telecom merger, had been frustrated that her firm's role stopped at the point where the real deal structuring began, and was joining banking specifically to be in the room for the part she had been watching from the outside. She could name the deal, the client, the advisor, and what specifically she had been unable to do in the consulting context. The answer required zero polish because the story was real.

These examples illustrate the same principle that every credible prep resource - from [Mergers & Inquisitions](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/why-investment-banking/) to [Wall Street Prep](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/why-investment-banking/) - converges on: the answer that works is specific, grounded in real experience, and connected to things that banking actually does. Not banking as an abstraction. Banking as a specific set of activities that a specific person finds genuinely compelling for demonstrable reasons.

## What to Avoid in the Delivery

Beyond the content, the delivery matters.

**Do not apologize for your interest.** Some candidates hedge their answer with qualifications: "I know this might sound simplistic, but..." You are in the interview. Say what you mean without the disclaimer.

**Do not read your own answer.** The "why banking" answer should be conversational, not a rehearsed speech. If you have practiced it so much that it sounds like a memorized paragraph, pull back. You want it to flow naturally - slightly different each time, because you are having a conversation, not reciting.

**Do not ignore the specific question they asked.** If they asked "why this bank?" and you give a general answer about why you want to work in banking, you have not answered the question. "Why investment banking?" is different from "why this group?" is different from "why us?" Listen to the exact phrasing.

**Do not undersell the financial motivation.** Some candidates are so anxious to seem non-mercenary that they strip any acknowledgment of compensation from their answer entirely. That is unconvincing. Nobody is entering banking for altruistic reasons. You can acknowledge that the financial opportunity is part of the calculus without making it the only thing you say - the key is that it cannot be the only or primary reason you articulate.

**Keep it under 60 seconds.** [Wall Street Prep is explicit about this](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/why-investment-banking/): maintain conversational tone, weave personal anecdotes chronologically, stay under 60 seconds. Longer answers dilute the specific details that make the answer persuasive.

## The Follow-Up Questions

Once you give your answer, a good interviewer will push. Common follow-ups:

**"What would you do if you don't get into banking?"**

This is a test of whether banking is actually your first choice or your fallback. Answer honestly. If you would go back to your previous field, say so. If you would try again next cycle, say so. Interviewers respect clarity more than the "banking or nothing" answer, which usually sounds desperate.

**"What else have you considered?"**

They want to see that you have thought about adjacent paths and made a reasoned choice to pursue banking. Consulting, corporate development, and asset management are the most common comparisons. Be able to articulate what each offers and why banking is where you landed. The ability to explain this comparison is itself evidence that you have thought carefully about the decision.

**"What specifically about this group?"**

This is where your research pays off. Know something real about the group - their deal flow, their sector coverage, their recent transactions. The answer cannot be "you have a great reputation." Every bank at the level you are interviewing at has a great reputation. You need a reason that is specific to this group and could not be delivered word-for-word to the group at the next bank on your list.

For a broader framework on behavioral questions including this one, see [/interview-prep/behavioral-questions-star-framework/](/interview-prep/behavioral-questions-star-framework/).

## What You Should Do Next

**1. Write out your answer before you practice saying it.** The act of writing forces clarity that verbal rehearsal does not. Write 300-400 words in your own voice on why you want to be in banking. Then strip it down to the 3-4 sentences that actually carry the weight. [Mergers & Inquisitions targets 50-85 spoken words](https://mergersandinquisitions.com/why-investment-banking/) - that is a tight constraint that will force you to identify what actually matters in your narrative.

**2. Identify your specific concrete action.** What is the real thing - the deal you followed, the course you took, the model you built, the informational interview you had, the internship project that stayed with you - that is actually the source of your interest? That is your anchor. Build outward from that. If you cannot identify a concrete action, that is important information: you have more preparation to do before the interview.

**3. Research the specific bank and group.** Go to the bank's website, search recent press releases for deals they advised on, look at the sector coverage of the group you are targeting. Find one thing that is genuinely specific to them and can be woven naturally into the end of your answer. Not forced in - if it is not natural, leave it out.

**4. Prepare your comparison.** Know why banking over the two or three most obvious adjacent paths - consulting, private equity, corporate development, asset management. Being able to articulate a reasoned comparison demonstrates exactly the kind of clear thinking that banking interviews are designed to surface.

**5. Practice with someone who will tell you when it sounds rehearsed.** Not someone who will nod encouragingly. Someone who will say "that sounded like you were reading it" or "I didn't believe the part about the internship." That feedback is more valuable than any prep course. Record yourself if no one is available. Listen back. You will know immediately when it sounds like a performance.

**6. Read the full list of investment banking interview questions** at [/interview-prep/investment-banking-interview-questions/](/interview-prep/investment-banking-interview-questions/) to understand how "why banking?" fits into the broader interview arc. The behavioral questions form a coherent picture of who you are - the "why banking?" answer sets the frame for everything else.

## The Real Test

The "why banking?" question ultimately tests whether you have done the work of understanding what you are asking to be paid to do. Senior bankers spend their careers advising companies at critical moments - acquisitions, divestitures, capital raises, restructurings. They are looking for junior colleagues who are genuinely interested in that work, who have demonstrated that interest through concrete action, and who can explain their reasoning without retreating to language that belongs in a career pamphlet.

According to the [WSO 2024 survey](https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/investment-banking/wso-2024-investment-banking-work-conditions-survey-results-4th-annual), 72% of current bankers have at some point considered leaving due to burnout. The interviewers sitting across from you know this statistic from personal experience. They want to hire people who go in with open eyes and stay - not people who are surprised by the reality of the job six months in.

The answer that works is not the most polished one. It is the one that is most clearly true.
