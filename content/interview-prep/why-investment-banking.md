# Why Investment Banking: Answering Without Sounding Like Everyone Else

The most important question in any investment banking interview is also the one candidates prepare for least carefully.

Not because they skip it - they don't. They rehearse an answer. They just rehearse the wrong answer. They memorize a version of the answer that every other candidate has also memorized, delivered in language that was probably written by a consulting firm that has never worked in a bank, and the person across the table from them recognizes it in about four seconds.

"Why investment banking?" sounds like a soft question. It is not. It is the question that determines whether the interviewer spends the rest of the conversation trying to find reasons to pass you or trying to find reasons to hire you.

This article explains what interviewers are actually asking, what kills answers, what works, and how to construct a response that sounds like you wrote it - because you did.

---

## What the Interviewer Is Actually Asking

When a vice president or associate asks "why investment banking?", they are asking several things simultaneously:

**Are you here for the right reasons?** Banking is genuinely demanding. Analysts regularly work 80-100 hours per week during live deals. Interviewers have been burned by people who joined for the prestige or the bonus and were miserable (and useless) by month four. They want people who have at least done the work of understanding what the job actually involves.

**Do you know what this job is?** Many candidates confuse investment banking with investing. They conflate it with hedge funds, private equity, or venture capital. Others have a vague sense that banking involves "big deals" but have never articulated what specifically they find compelling about advisory work, capital markets, or structured finance. The question quickly surfaces whether you have done your homework.

**Will you last?** The real filter underneath the question: is this someone who will be here in eighteen months, pulling their weight on a live deal at midnight? Or is this someone who will check out the moment they realize the job involves building 200-page PowerPoint decks and updating data rooms?

**Can you communicate clearly about yourself?** How you answer this question is a preview of how you will answer questions from clients and senior bankers when the stakes are real. Vagueness, over-rehearsed boilerplate, and a failure to connect narrative to specifics are all red flags.

## The Answers That Kill Candidacies

Before building a good answer, it is worth cataloguing exactly what not to say.

**"I want to learn about finance and business."**

This is the single most common answer given and the single most damaging one. It tells the interviewer: I have not thought hard about this. It is true of every candidate in the building. It is also true of people who want to be accountants, consultants, and corporate treasurers. It differentiates you from no one.

**"I want to work on big deals and have an impact."**

Vague aspiration with no substance. What kind of deals? What kind of impact? The word "impact" in particular has been so thoroughly emptied of meaning by candidates who picked it up from MBA career guides that it now functions as a signal of inauthenticity.

**"Investment banking is a great training ground."**

You are applying for a two-year analyst program. Of course it is a training ground. This answer tells the interviewer you are already thinking about your exit and have given no thought to the actual work.

**"I was inspired by [famous banker or film]."**

Unless you have a genuinely interesting story about a real person who you know personally or have followed closely for years, drop any reference to being inspired by famous bankers. Saying you were inspired by watching The Big Short is not a "why investment banking" answer - it is a pop culture reference.

**"I interned at a boutique and loved the experience."**

This can be part of a good answer but cannot be the whole answer. "I liked it when I did it" does not explain why you want to continue doing it. You need to connect the experience to specific things you found compelling - not just assert that the experience was positive.

## What a Strong Answer Actually Contains

A strong "why investment banking" answer has three components. They do not need to be labeled or delivered in strict order, but they all need to be present.

**1. A specific experience or moment that created genuine interest.**

Not "I always liked finance" - something concrete. A transaction you followed closely. A company in an industry you know well that went through a capital raise or acquisition, and you found yourself reading everything you could about how it worked. A conversation with someone in the industry that shifted your thinking. A course or project that gave you a first-principles view of how capital structure decisions affect operating outcomes.

The specificity is the point. It signals that this is a real thought, not a performance.

**2. An explanation of what specifically about banking (as opposed to adjacent careers) appeals to you.**

This is where most candidates are weakest. What is it about banking specifically - as opposed to private equity, consulting, or corporate development - that you find compelling?

Legitimate answers here include: the advisory relationship (working alongside management teams at critical moments), the breadth of analytical exposure across sectors and transaction types, the pace and the pressure of live deal environments, the capital markets work (understanding how debt and equity instruments are priced and structured), the chance to see how companies actually make strategic decisions when real money is at stake.

None of those answers is a cliche. They are specific things about what bankers actually do. If one of them is genuinely true for you, say it and explain why. If none of them resonate, that is a different problem.

**3. A connection to this bank and this group specifically.**

This is the anchor that makes the answer about the room you are sitting in, not about banking as an abstraction. For advice on differentiating between banks, see [/industry/bulge-bracket-vs-elite-boutique/](/industry/bulge-bracket-vs-elite-boutique/). For this portion of the answer, you need something that is demonstrably specific: a deal this bank recently advised on that you can discuss intelligently, a sector focus of the group you are interviewing with, a conversation with a banker from this firm that revealed something about how they work.

## The Undergraduate vs. MBA Difference

The standard of evidence is different depending on where you are in your career.

**Undergraduates:** Interviewers expect that your interest is primarily intellectual and directional. You have probably had one internship, possibly in a relevant area. Your answer can legitimately include: a compelling course, a personal interest in how markets work, a summer internship at a smaller firm, or an academic research project involving corporate finance. What you cannot do is pretend to a depth of conviction that would only come from years of experience.

**MBA candidates:** The bar is significantly higher. You have had a pre-MBA career. You have seen the alternatives. Your "why banking?" answer needs to account for what you were doing before and why you are making this choice now rather than pursuing more of the same. The best MBA answers explain what the pre-MBA career gave them (industry knowledge, client relationships, technical foundation) and what banking specifically adds to that (deal experience, transaction advisory, capital structure sophistication).

According to Mergers & Inquisitions' recruiting data, MBA candidates who have done a pre-MBA stint at a hedge fund, corporate development team, or consulting firm - and can articulate a specific gap that banking fills - have significantly higher conversion rates than candidates who are pivoting from entirely unrelated fields.

## Practical Answer Structures

**Structure 1: The Linear Path**

Used when your background builds naturally toward banking. Works best for candidates whose coursework, internships, and interests form a coherent narrative.

"My interest in banking came from [specific starting point]. During [specific experience], I became interested in [specific aspect of banking work]. That led me to [next step], where I [specific thing you did or learned]. What I found was that what I actually enjoyed most was [specific thing that connects to banking]. This firm specifically makes sense because [specific reason]."

**Structure 2: The Inflection Point**

Used when there is a clear moment - an experience, a transaction you followed, a conversation - that crystallized your interest. Works well for candidates who came to banking interest later.

"I came to banking through [adjacent area]. [Describe the specific moment or experience]. What that experience made clear was [insight about banking work]. Since then I have [steps taken to build toward banking]. I am interested in this group specifically because [specific reason]."

**Structure 3: The Honest Pivot (MBA Candidates)**

Used when the pre-MBA background is in an adjacent area and the MBA is the explicit transition vehicle. Honesty works better than retrofitting a false narrative.

"My background in [previous field] gave me [specific thing]. What I have consistently found most interesting, though, is [aspect of banking work that connects to that background]. Banking lets me [specific contribution or capability]. The MBA is the bridge I needed to make that transition credibly, and I am targeting [specific groups] because [specific reason related to their sector/work]."

## Real Candidate Examples

A Wall Street Oasis thread from a few years ago asked analysts at top banks what answers they remembered as genuinely compelling. The recurring themes were notable for what they were not - they were not polished, they were not particularly eloquent, but they were specific and they were honest.

One analyst at Goldman described an answer from an undergraduate candidate who had spent two summers working in the shipping industry through a family connection, had followed a large vessel acquisition deal closely as a personal interest project, and described in detail why the capital structure of the deal had been fascinating to him. The interviewer pulled the deal up on a Bloomberg terminal and they spent fifteen minutes discussing it. The candidate got the offer.

Another example from a Morgan Stanley associate: a candidate who had worked in strategy consulting, had been staffed on a major telecom merger, had been frustrated that her firm's role stopped at the point where the real deal structuring began, and was joining banking specifically to be in the room for the part that she had been watching from the outside. She could name the deal, the client, the advisor, and what specifically she had been unable to do in the consulting context. The answer required zero polish because the story was real.

The pattern: specificity, honesty, and a clear-eyed understanding of what banking actually involves.

## What to Avoid in the Delivery

Beyond the content, the delivery matters. A few things to watch:

**Do not apologize for your interest.** Some candidates hedge their answer with qualifications: "I know this might sound simplistic, but..." You are in the interview. Say what you mean without the disclaimer.

**Do not read your own answer.** The "why banking" answer should be conversational, not a rehearsed speech. If you have practiced it so much that it sounds like a memorized paragraph, pull back. You want it to flow naturally, with pauses, with real sentences that land differently each time because they are slightly different each time.

**Do not ignore the specific question they asked.** If they asked "why this bank?" and you give a general answer about why you want to work in banking, you have not answered the question. Listen carefully to the exact phrasing. "Why investment banking?" is different from "why this group?" is different from "why us?"

**Do not undersell the financial motivation.** Some candidates are so anxious to seem non-mercenary that they strip the compensation out of their answer entirely. That is unconvincing. Nobody is entering banking for altruistic reasons. You can acknowledge that the financial opportunity is part of the calculus without making it the only thing you say.

## The Follow-Up Questions

Once you give your answer, a good interviewer will push. Common follow-ups:

**"What would you do if you don't get into banking?"**

This is a test of whether banking is actually your first choice or your fallback. Answer honestly. If you would go back to your previous field, say so. If you would try again next cycle, say so. Interviewers respect clarity more than the "banking or nothing" answer, which usually sounds desperate.

**"What else have you considered?"**

They want to see that you have thought about adjacent paths and made a reasoned choice to pursue banking. Consulting, corporate development, and asset management are the most common comparisons. Be able to articulate what each offers and why banking is where you landed.

**"What specifically about [the group you're interviewing with]?"**

This is where your research pays off. Know something real about the group - their deal flow, their sector coverage, their recent transactions. The answer cannot be "you have a great reputation." Every bank at the level you are interviewing at has a great reputation.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Write out your answer before you practice saying it.** The act of writing forces clarity that verbal rehearsal does not. Write 300-400 words in your own voice on why you want to be in banking. Then strip it down to the 3-4 sentences that actually carry the weight.

2. **Identify your specific experience.** What is the real thing - the deal, the course, the conversation, the transaction you followed, the internship project - that is actually the source of your interest? That is your anchor. Build outward from that.

3. **Research the specific bank and group.** Go to the bank's website, search recent press releases for deals they advised on, look at the sector coverage of the group you are targeting. Find one thing that is genuinely specific to them and work it into the end of your answer.

4. **Practice with someone who will tell you when it sounds rehearsed.** Not someone who will nod encouragingly. Someone who will say "that sounded like you were reading it" or "I didn't believe the part about the internship." That feedback is more valuable than any prep course.

5. **Read the full list of investment banking interview questions** at [/interview-prep/investment-banking-interview-questions/](/interview-prep/investment-banking-interview-questions/) to understand how "why banking?" fits into the broader interview arc. The behavioral questions form a coherent picture of who you are - the "why banking?" answer sets the frame for everything else.
