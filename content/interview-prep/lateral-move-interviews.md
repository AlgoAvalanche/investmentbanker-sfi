# Lateral Move Interviews: A Different Animal

If you are walking into a lateral move interview for investment banking with the same preparation strategy you used for campus recruiting, you are going to have a bad day. Not because the technical questions are necessarily harder - though at the associate and VP level they often are - but because the entire frame of what the bank is trying to figure out is different.

In campus recruiting, the bank is asking: "Can we teach this person to do this job?" In a lateral hire, the bank is asking: "Why hasn't this person already succeeded at what we need them to do?" The burden shifts from potential to demonstrated track record, and every question probes that shift relentlessly.

The structural reality of lateral hiring makes this even sharper. Banks do not run lateral searches on a calendar the way they run campus programs. Positions open when analysts and associates leave unexpectedly - and in investment banking, turnover is high enough that openings appear constantly. According to Mergers and Inquisitions, the typical lateral process runs 6 to 12 months from first outreach to offer, with no predictable start date and no standardized screening layer to hide behind.[1] The process is unstructured by design. Every conversation, including what feels like an informational call, is part of the evaluation.

That context shapes everything that follows.

---

## Who Does Lateral Hiring in Banking

Lateral moves into investment banking happen at several distinct career stages, and the interview dynamics differ significantly depending on where you are coming from.

**Analyst-level laterals** typically come from Big Four transaction services, corporate finance, commercial banking, or adjacent roles in financial services. The bank is filling a specific headcount need and the process moves quickly once it starts. Technical proficiency is weighted heavily. The behavioral bar is essentially "not a liability on a client call."

**Associate-level laterals** are the most common entry point for MBAs who recruit outside of campus channels, for consultants moving into banking without the MBA route, and for bankers moving between firms. This process is longer, more ambiguous, and more relationship-dependent than analyst laterals. Associate-level moves also happen for career changers from private equity and hedge funds who want the advisory side of the business.

**VP and above laterals** are almost entirely relationship-driven. By VP level, a banker's network is doing most of the work. The formal interview process exists, but it sits on top of a decision that has largely already been made through referrals and reputation. If you are a VP-level candidate reading a prep guide, the tactical advice in this article applies primarily to the formal rounds you will still need to pass.

What all three levels share: the bank expects you to hit the ground running. That expectation changes every aspect of how the interview is structured and what it actually tests.

## The Core Question Every Lateral Interview Is Answering

Every question in a lateral banking interview - technical or behavioral - is gathering data on one or more of the following:

**Can you do the specific work the bank needs done?** Not "can you learn to do it." Can you do it now, or within a very short ramp period. Banks are not making development bets on lateral hires the way they do with entry-level analysts. The cost of a lateral hire who takes six months to become functional is significantly higher than the cost of the same gap in a campus cohort.

**Why are you leaving your current role?** This question is asked directly and probed indirectly throughout the interview. Mergers and Inquisitions and Wall Street Oasis practitioner guides both flag this as the single question that takes down more lateral candidates than any technical failure.[1][2] "I want a new challenge" is insufficient. Bankers are trained to detect whether the real answer is "my current situation is bad" - and whether that badness reflects something about you rather than about your employer.

**Will you fit into this bank's culture and team dynamics?** Lateral hires who are technically strong but culturally disruptive are a well-known category of hiring mistake. Senior bankers have seen it happen. They are watching for the signals.

**What does your trajectory look like?** A 32-year-old making a lateral move into banking has a different trajectory question than a 26-year-old making the same move. Is this move going to accelerate your development, or is it a step sideways at a point in your career when you should be making concrete upward progress?

Understanding these four underlying questions helps you anticipate what every interview question is actually designed to assess - and build answers that address the real concern, not just the surface-level prompt.

## The Process: What to Actually Expect

Before getting into preparation specifics, it helps to understand the structural mechanics of the lateral process. Most candidates underestimate how different it is from campus recruiting in format, not just content.

According to practitioner accounts on Wall Street Oasis, the typical lateral process involves approximately five rounds, with the final round consisting of back-to-back 30-minute sessions with senior bankers.[2] The technical and behavioral split runs roughly 60 to 70 percent technical. For analyst-level moves, a modeling test is common - typically one to one and a half hours, covering a three-statement model, LBO, or DCF with provided assumptions.[2]

Mergers and Inquisitions notes that the formal process usually involves one or two phone screens followed by a Superday or a series of interviews spread over several weeks.[1] Unlike campus recruiting, there is no HireVue, no online assessment, no standardized screening layer. Every interaction is a direct conversation with people who will eventually decide whether you join their team.

The timing of the search matters. Private Equity Bro documents two historically active hiring windows: January through March, after year-end bonuses are paid, and July through August, after mid-year bonuses.[5] These windows are not fixed rules - a departing analyst creates an opening regardless of the month - but if you are actively pipeline-building, these periods reward being prepared and connected. Networking with hiring managers directly rather than through HR significantly improves conversion rates.[5]

The minimum experience threshold before applying is generally 6 to 12 months in your current role. Applying with less than six months of experience signals that you have not been through a complete deal cycle and makes it difficult to discuss meaningful transaction involvement at the depth lateral interviews require.[1] The window also has a soft ceiling: the process becomes progressively harder after two to three years out of undergrad without a clear promotion path, because the trajectory question becomes harder to answer credibly.

One critical reality on compensation: most banks do not offer signing bonuses for lateral hires, and you will likely forfeit accrued bonuses at your current firm by moving mid-cycle.[1] Model this into your decision. "Losing a year" in title is also possible depending on the firm and the specific role.

## Deal Experience: The Core Differentiator

This is the most important section in this article if you are preparing for an analyst or associate lateral interview.

Unlike campus recruiting - where GPA, school prestige, and technical memorization drive outcomes - lateral interviews are anchored almost entirely in your transaction history. Every deal on your resume is fair game for deep interrogation. According to Mergers and Inquisitions, candidates should prepare deal sheets summarizing each transaction with buyer and seller financials, relevant multiples, transaction rationale, and their specific contribution to the work.[6] The bank expects you to know these numbers cold.

The Wall Street Oasis lateral interview guide for analysts at elite boutiques and bulge brackets advises starting each deal walk-through with a brief summary - sector, company overview, mandate type - before moving into metrics.[3] Know the enterprise value, the EBITDA multiple, the deal rationale, and the outcome. Less emphasis falls on "400 questions"-style memorization and more falls on whether you actually understand why the deal happened and whether the valuation was fair.

Interviewers probe not just what happened in the deal, but what you personally thought about it. A candidate who can say "we valued the target at 9.5x EBITDA, which I thought was aggressive given the customer concentration risk in the top three accounts, but the strategic acquirer was paying a control premium for the technology platform rather than for current earnings power" is demonstrating judgment, not just memory. That distinction separates candidates who advance from those who do not.

The Office Hours practitioner reflection on switching banks makes a related point: skills acquired at boutique or non-traditional firms are genuinely transferable, but they need to be presented in deal-execution language rather than support language.[4] If you were doing transaction services work rather than advisory work, the reframe is deliberate: you were on the diligence side of transactions, you understand the mechanics from the buyer's analytical perspective, and you can speak to the valuation methodology used to stress-test the purchase price. That is real and specific. "I supported transactions" is not.

## Technical Expectations: High and Practical, Not Memorization-Based

The technical bar for lateral candidates is in some ways higher and in other ways more nuanced than for campus recruits.

For analysts, the expectation is functional competence from week three or four, not week twenty. This means the technical questions are applied rather than conceptual. Not "walk me through a DCF" but "walk me through how you would approach the DCF for a SaaS company with negative near-term free cash flow and high growth, and which inputs would you push back hardest on in a client discussion." There is a right answer, but more importantly there is a reasoning process the interviewer is evaluating.

For associates, technical proficiency needs to be paired with judgment. An associate lateral who can build models but cannot explain why a specific valuation multiple is or is not appropriate in a given sector context is only half-useful. The questions become evaluative rather than mechanical.

Wall Street Oasis practitioners note that modeling tests at the lateral level commonly run one to one and a half hours and include 3-statement models, LBO mechanics, or DCF analysis with provided assumptions.[2] Candidates who underestimate the probability of a modeling test - and fail to refresh the actual mechanics rather than just the theory - pay for that underestimation in real interviews.

One pattern worth knowing: lateral candidates most commonly fail technical rounds not on the hardest questions, but on the basics delivered under pressure. The "tell me what happens to all three financial statements if you write off $10M of inventory" question catches people who have been operating in narrower roles where integrated financial statement mechanics have not come up in a while. Review the foundations even if you consider yourself technically strong.

The technical split by background matters here. Mergers and Inquisitions documents the standard expectation as covering accounting, finance, valuation, and M&A and LBO modeling for experienced hires.[6] Big Four transaction services candidates are typically strong on diligence processes and quality of earnings analysis but weaker on standalone valuation methodology and deal structuring. Consulting candidates often have the strategic thinking but have been operating at the slide-deck level rather than the model level. Know which gaps apply to your background and address them specifically.

## The "Why Are You Leaving" Problem

This question takes down more lateral candidates than any technical stumble. It is asked early, taken seriously, and probed.

The framework has two components: a genuine pull toward the new opportunity, and a professional characterization of the current situation that does not raise flags.

**The pull** needs to be specific and substantive. "I want to work on more strategic transactions" is weak. "I have been on the diligence support side of transactions for four years and I want to be in the room advising on deal structure and deal strategy rather than supporting the process from the outside - I want to understand how the business judgment behind the valuation assumptions actually gets made, not just whether the assumptions are internally consistent" is a real answer that reflects real thinking. It also directly connects your background to what you are moving toward, which is the right structure.

**The characterization of the current situation** requires honesty without damage. If you are leaving because your firm is shrinking, say that. If the work has plateaued and you want more transaction exposure, say that. If you are leaving because your manager is impossible and the culture is toxic, do not say that - but do not construct a fiction either. Instead: "I have gotten significant development from my current role but I have reached a point where the next step requires the kind of deal exposure that this specific group is in a position to offer." That is honest, specific, and forward-looking.

What you cannot do is give an answer that falls apart under light probing. Interviewers at the lateral level have been doing this for years. They will probe. One Wall Street Oasis poster who successfully lateraled from Big Four to a bulge bracket advisory role wrote: "The question I was most nervous about was why I was leaving. I had prepared for it but not deeply enough - they asked three follow-ups. Be ready to go three levels deep. It is not going away after one answer."[2]

Prepare a three-layer answer: surface level, one level deeper when probed, and a third level for the persistent follow-up. Know all three versions cold before you walk in.

## The Trajectory Question

Senior bankers assessing a lateral candidate are always running a private mental calculation: does this person's career trajectory make sense, and does this move fit it?

A 28-year-old with four years of Big Four experience moving to a junior associate role is making a reasonable if slightly late move with a legible rationale. A 33-year-old with a similar background and no clear explanation for why the move did not happen earlier has a harder narrative challenge. This is not about age - it is about the bank trying to assess whether you are realistic about where you will fit in the hierarchy, and whether you have the drive and capacity to develop on an accelerated timeline relative to your peer group inside the bank.

Address this proactively. Do not wait for the interviewer to ask "why didn't you make this move earlier?" Fold the timeline explanation into your broader narrative about why banking, why now, and what you specifically bring that makes you a more valuable lateral hire than a fresh campus recruit. A campus recruit is cheaper and more malleable. You are more expensive and already formed. Make the case for what that costs more gets the bank in return.

What you bring as a lateral candidate: real-world professional experience, a demonstrated ability to function in demanding work environments, specific technical or sector knowledge that took years to develop, and in many cases a professional network that a 22-year-old campus recruit does not have. These are genuine competitive advantages. State them explicitly.

## Tier Jumps: Sequence the Move Realistically

One of the most common and costly mistakes in lateral recruiting is targeting an unrealistic tier jump. Moving from an unknown regional boutique directly to Goldman Sachs or Morgan Stanley is rarely successful in a single step. The typical successful lateral path moves one or two tiers at a time: regional boutique to middle market, middle market to elite boutique or lower bulge bracket, and so on.

Mergers and Inquisitions is explicit about this: unrealistic targeting wastes time and burns networking capital.[1] The banks you approach who decline you because the fit is too large a gap are now less likely to consider you when a more appropriate opening appears, because you have positioned yourself as someone who does not understand where they stand.

Moves within similar-tier banks emphasize cultural fit. Moves upward to larger institutions focus heavily on technical capabilities and deal pedigree. Know which dynamic applies to your specific target and prepare accordingly.

## Networking Comes Before Applications

Most lateral roles are filled before they are formally posted. Informational conversations with analysts and associates at target firms, followed by referrals to group heads, is the dominant success path. Cold applications to posted roles have a significantly lower conversion rate.[5]

This means that if you are thinking about a lateral move six months from now, the time to start building relationships is now - not when you decide you are ready to formally apply. Every internal advocate who knows your work and believes in your potential is worth ten cold applications. The pipeline needs to be built before the opening appears.

Mergers and Inquisitions makes a related point about informational calls: every conversation with a current employee is part of the evaluation.[6] Treating these casually, failing to prepare for them, or asking uninformed questions about the group damages your candidacy before a formal interview ever begins. Prepare for informational calls the way you would prepare for a first-round interview. Know the group's recent deals. Ask informed questions about deal flow and team structure. Leave the conversation having demonstrated that you can operate at the level required.

## Behavioral Preparation at the Lateral Level

The behavioral questions in lateral interviews are more sophisticated than their campus equivalents. The STAR framework still applies, but the scenarios are expected to be more complex and the leadership dimension is expected to be more prominent.

"Tell me about a time you led a team through a difficult situation" means something different for a lateral candidate than for an undergraduate. You should have a real story about real leadership with real stakes and a real outcome - not a student project or a committee role.

The specific behavioral areas lateral interviews probe most consistently:

**Client-facing experience:** Have you dealt with clients or senior stakeholders directly? How did you handle delivering bad news or managing a difficult expectation?

**Managing up:** Have you pushed back on a superior who was wrong? How did you handle it? Did it work?

**Cross-functional complexity:** Have you coordinated work across multiple teams, geographies, or functions? In banking, deal execution requires managing multiple workstreams simultaneously under time pressure. Show that you can do this.

**Handling ambiguity:** Have you operated in situations where the path was not clear and you had to make judgment calls without a playbook?

Prepare five to six specific stories that cover these areas. Write them out in full before the interview. The act of writing forces a specificity that mental rehearsal does not achieve - and in a high-pressure interview environment, you want the specificity already encoded.

## The Reference Check Dimension

Lateral hires are reference-checked far more seriously than campus recruits. The bank will contact people you have worked with. This means that how you have managed your professional relationships throughout your career - with managers, peers, and clients - is now part of the hiring process.

If there is a known difficult relationship in your background, think carefully about whether it is likely to surface, and if so, how you would address it directly if asked. Trying to conceal a known problem in a reference check is a high-risk strategy that tends to backfire. Better to address it briefly and honestly if asked than to appear evasive when a reference surfaces it unprompted.

## What to Do Next

**Audit your technical proficiency honestly before applying.** Not "can I pass a campus technical screen" - but "can I hold a substantive 45-minute conversation about deal mechanics and valuation judgment at the associate level, defending my assumptions and explaining my reasoning." The bar is higher than you probably think. BIWS and Macabacus both publish advanced modeling materials designed for experienced candidates that go beyond the campus prep standard.

**Prepare a deal sheet for every transaction on your resume.** For each deal: sector, company overview, mandate type, enterprise value, EBITDA or revenue multiple, deal rationale, outcome, your specific contribution, and your own informed view on whether the valuation was appropriate. Know these cold. Interviewers will probe until you break if you are not prepared.

**Develop a three-layer answer to "why are you leaving."** Surface level, one level deeper when probed, and a third level for the persistent follow-up. Know all three cold.

**Build deal-specific knowledge about the groups you are targeting.** Look up their recent transactions via Bloomberg, Reuters, or the bank's own deal announcements. Understand two or three of them at a structural level - mandate type, valuation approach, strategic rationale. Reference them specifically in conversations.

**Start networking now, before applications open.** Lateral hiring is even more relationship-driven than campus hiring. The earlier you build genuine relationships with people at target firms, the better positioned you are when a seat opens. That window is unpredictable. Your pipeline should not be.
