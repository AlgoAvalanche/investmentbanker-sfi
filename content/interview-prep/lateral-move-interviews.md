# Lateral Move Interviews: A Different Animal

If you are walking into a lateral move interview for investment banking with the same preparation strategy you would use for campus recruiting, you are going to have a bad day. Not because the technical questions are necessarily harder - though at the associate and VP level they often are - but because the entire frame of what the bank is trying to figure out is different.

In campus recruiting, the bank is asking: "Can we teach this person to do this job?" In a lateral hire, the bank is asking: "Why hasn't this person already succeeded at what we need them to do?" The burden shifts from potential to demonstrated track record, and the questions probe that shift relentlessly.

---

## Who Does Lateral Hiring in Banking

Lateral moves into investment banking happen at several distinct career stages, and the interview dynamics differ significantly depending on where you are coming from.

**Analyst-level laterals** typically come from Big Four transaction services, corporate finance, commercial banking, or other roles in financial services. The bank is hiring a replacement for a specific headcount need and the process moves quickly. Technical proficiency is weighted heavily and the behavioral bar is "not a liability."

**Associate-level laterals** are the most common entry point for MBAs who recruit outside of campus channels, for consultants moving directly into banking without the MBA, and for bankers moving between firms. The process here is longer, more ambiguous, and more relationship-dependent. Associate laterals also happen for career changers from private equity and hedge funds who want advisory experience.

**VP and above laterals** are almost entirely relationship-driven. By VP level, a banker's network is doing most of the work. The formal interview process exists but it is a formality layered over a decision that has largely already been made through referrals and reputation. If you are a VP-level lateral candidate reading a prep guide, the advice in this article applies primarily to the formal rounds you will still have to pass.

---

## The Core Question a Lateral Interview Is Answering

Every question in a lateral banking interview, technical or behavioral, is gathering data on one or more of the following:

**Can you do the specific work the bank needs done?** Not "can you learn to do it" - can you do it now, or within a short ramp period. The bank is not taking a development bet on a lateral hire the way it does with an entry-level analyst.

**Why are you leaving your current role?** This question is asked directly and probed indirectly throughout the interview. "I want a new challenge" is insufficient. Bankers are trained to detect whether the real answer is "my current situation is bad" and whether that badness reflects something about you rather than your employer.

**Will you fit into this bank's culture and team dynamics?** Lateral hires who are technically strong but culturally disruptive are a well-known category of hiring mistake. Senior bankers have seen it. They are watching for it.

**What does your trajectory look like?** A 32-year-old making a lateral move into banking has a different trajectory question than a 26-year-old making the same move. Is this move going to accelerate your development, or is it a step sideways at a time when a banker your age should be making concrete upward progress?

Understanding these four underlying questions helps you anticipate what every interview question is actually designed to assess - and prepare answers that address the real concern, not just the surface-level prompt.

## How Technical Expectations Differ for Laterals

The technical bar for lateral candidates is in some ways higher and in other ways more nuanced than for campus recruits.

For analysts, the expectation is that you can hit the ground running. A campus analyst is expected to need six months to become fully functional. A lateral analyst hire is expected to deliver at a functional level from week three or four. This means the technical questions will be more applied - not "walk me through a DCF" but "walk me through how you would approach the DCF for a SaaS company with negative near-term free cash flow and high growth, and what are the specific inputs you would fight hardest about?"

For associates, technical proficiency needs to be paired with judgment. An associate lateral who can build models but cannot explain why a specific valuation multiple is or is not appropriate in a given situation is only half-useful. The questions become more evaluative and less mechanical.

A common pattern in associate lateral interviews, noted by Mergers and Inquisitions, is the "test drive" question format: the interviewer presents a real or lightly disguised scenario from the group's recent deal flow and asks you to talk through how you would approach the analysis. There is no single right answer. The interviewer is evaluating your thought process, your instincts about what matters, and whether you can operate with ambiguity.

According to BIWS, lateral candidates most commonly fail technical rounds not on the hardest questions but on the basics delivered under pressure. The "tell me what happens to all three statements if you write off inventory" question catches people who have been working in narrower roles where they have not needed to think about integrated financial statement mechanics in some time. Review the foundations even if you consider yourself technically profished.

## The "Why Are You Leaving" Problem

This is the question that takes down more lateral candidates than any technical stumble. It is usually asked early, it is always taken seriously, and it is probed.

The framework for answering it well has two components: a genuine pull toward the new opportunity and a professional characterization of the current situation that does not raise red flags.

**The pull:** What specifically about banking advisory work, or this particular bank's work, attracts you in a way that your current role does not satisfy? This needs to be specific and substantive. "I want to work on more strategic transactions" is weak. "I have been doing transaction services work on the sell-side support for four years and I want to be in the room advising on deal structure and deal strategy rather than supporting the process from the outside - the Damodaran framework I have been using for valuation work is all well and good in a model, but I want to understand how the business judgment behind the assumptions actually gets made" is a real answer that reflects real thinking.

**The characterization of the current situation:** The standard here is to be honest without being damaging. If you are leaving because your firm is shrinking, say that. If you are leaving because the work has plateaued and you want more transaction exposure, say that. If you are leaving because your boss is impossible and the culture is toxic, do not say that - but also do not lie about it. Instead: "I have gotten significant value from my current role but I have reached a point where the next step in my development requires the kind of deal exposure that this bank specifically is in a position to offer."

What you cannot do is give an answer that, upon light probing, falls apart. Interviewers at this level have been doing this for years. They will probe. Have a second layer ready.

One WSO poster who successfully made the lateral move from Big Four to a bulge bracket advisory role wrote: "The question I was most nervous about was why I was leaving. I had prepared for it but not deeply enough - they asked three follow-ups. I learned from that interview and did much better in the next one. Be ready to go three levels deep on this question. It is not going away after one answer."

## The Trajectory Question

Senior bankers assessing a lateral candidate are always running a private mental calculation: does this person's career trajectory make sense, and does this move fit it?

A 28-year-old with four years of Big Four experience moving to an analyst or junior associate role is making a reasonable if slightly late move with a legible rationale. A 33-year-old with a similar background and no clear explanation for why the move did not happen earlier has a harder narrative challenge.

This is not about age discrimination - it is about the bank trying to assess whether the candidate is realistic about where they will fit in the hierarchy and whether they have the drive and capacity to develop on an accelerated timeline relative to their peer group inside the bank.

Address this proactively. Do not wait for the interviewer to ask "why didn't you make this move earlier?" Fold the timeline explanation into your broader narrative about why banking, why now, and what specifically you bring that makes you a more valuable lateral hire than a fresh campus recruit (who, from the bank's perspective, is often cheaper and more malleable).

What you bring as a lateral candidate: real-world professional experience, a demonstrated ability to function in demanding work environments, specific technical or sector knowledge, and in many cases, a professional network that a 22-year-old campus recruit does not have. Make the case for this explicitly.

## Behavioral Questions at the Lateral Level

The behavioral questions in lateral interviews are more sophisticated than their campus equivalents. The STAR framework still applies but the scenarios are expected to be more complex and the leadership dimension is expected to be more prominent.

"Tell me about a time you led a team through a difficult situation" means something different for a lateral candidate than for an undergraduate. You should have a real story about real leadership with real stakes - not a student project.

The specific behavioral areas lateral interviews probe most consistently:

**Client-facing experience:** Have you dealt with clients or senior stakeholders directly? How did you handle a situation where you had to deliver bad news or manage a difficult expectation?

**Managing up:** Have you pushed back on a superior who was wrong? How did you handle it? Did it work?

**Cross-functional complexity:** Have you coordinated work across multiple teams, geographies, or functions? In banking, deal execution often requires managing multiple workstreams simultaneously. Show that you can do this.

**Handling ambiguity:** Have you operated in situations where the path was not clear and you had to make judgment calls without a playbook?

Prepare five to six specific stories that cover these areas. Write them out fully before the interview. The act of writing them forces a specificity that mental rehearsal does not achieve.

## Technical Preparation Specifics for Common Lateral Backgrounds

**Coming from Big Four (Transaction Services, Valuation, Audit):**
Your technical foundation is real but narrow. TS candidates are often strong on quality of earnings analysis and diligence processes but weaker on standalone valuation methodology and deal structuring. Review DCF construction, LBO mechanics, and M&A accretion/dilution analysis with specific attention to how you would defend your assumptions. The gap between TS work and banking advisory work is not huge technically, but you need to demonstrate you understand the full picture.

**Coming from Consulting (McKinsey, BCG, Bain):**
The strategic thinking is there; the financial modeling depth is often not. Banking interviewers testing MBB consultants for associate roles are specifically looking for whether the candidate can build and defend a financial model or whether they have been operating at the slide-deck level throughout their consulting career. Be honest with yourself about which category you are in and prepare accordingly. McKinsey publishes substantial materials on corporate finance and valuation through its McKinsey Global Institute reports - these are useful both for content and for demonstrating currency in the subject.

**Coming from Private Equity:**
PE candidates interviewing for banking roles face a specific version of the trajectory question: why are you moving from the buy side to the sell side? The honest answers are: the modeling work is more interesting, the deal flow is higher volume, you want to develop breadth rather than depth, or your specific PE firm's trajectory has changed. What you cannot credibly say is that PE was not prestigious enough. Have a real answer.

**Coming from Another Bank:**
This is the cleanest lateral narrative but also the one that gets probed for specific reasons. Why this bank versus your current bank? What is wrong with where you are? Is this about deal flow, culture, sector focus, or something else? The specific story of why one bank is a better fit for you than another needs to be substantive and ideally relates to deal type, sector expertise, or team culture rather than compensation or prestige comparisons.

## The Reference Check Dimension

Lateral hires are reference-checked far more seriously than campus recruits. The bank will contact people you have worked with. This means that how you have managed your professional relationships throughout your career - with managers, peers, and clients - is now part of the hiring process.

If there is a known difficult relationship in your background, be thoughtful about whether it is likely to surface and, if so, how you would address it directly if asked. Trying to hide a known problem in a reference check is a high-risk strategy that tends to backfire.

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Audit your technical proficiency honestly before applying.** Not "can I pass a campus recruiting technical screen" but "can I hold a substantive 45-minute technical conversation about deal mechanics and valuation judgment at the associate level." The bar is higher. BIWS and Macabacus both have advanced modeling materials specifically designed for experienced candidates that go beyond the campus prep standard.

2. **Develop a three-layer answer to "why are you leaving."** Surface level, one level deeper when probed, and a third level for the persistent follow-up. Know all three cold.

3. **Identify your specific value proposition as a lateral candidate** - what do you bring that a campus recruit does not? Write it down explicitly. This will inform your behavioral stories, your career narrative, and your answer to every variation of "why should we hire you at this stage."

4. **Build deal-specific knowledge about the group you are targeting.** Look up the group's recent transactions using Bloomberg, Reuters, or the bank's own deal pages. Understand two or three of them at a structural level. Reference them in your interviews.

5. **Start networking now, before applications open.** Lateral hiring is even more relationship-driven than campus hiring. An internal advocate who knows your work and believes in your potential is worth ten cold applications. The earlier you build those relationships, the better positioned you are when a seat opens.
