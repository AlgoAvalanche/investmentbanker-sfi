# Closing Questions to Ask Your Interviewer (and Three to Never Ask)

Every investment banking interview ends the same way: "Do you have any questions for me?" Most candidates treat this as a polite formality. It is not. The closing questions you ask - and the ones you do not - are the final data point in an interviewer's assessment of you. Getting them wrong at the end of an otherwise strong interview is not a theoretical concern. It happens, and it costs offers.

This article covers the mechanics of closing questions, what strong questions actually signal, a practical list organized by interviewer seniority, and three categories of questions that candidates ask regularly and should never ask again.

---

## Why Closing Questions Matter More Than You Think

By the time an interviewer asks if you have questions, they have already formed a preliminary view of you. Closing questions do not usually change a strong negative impression, but they can meaningfully reinforce a positive one - and they can absolutely create a new negative impression where none existed before.

A strong closing question does three things simultaneously: it demonstrates that you researched the firm and the interviewer specifically, it shows genuine intellectual engagement with the work rather than just the job, and it extends the conversation in a direction where the interviewer can show some expertise or share an honest opinion. Questions that do all three create a notably positive final impression.

A weak or inappropriate closing question signals one or more of the following: that you did not do your research, that your interest in the firm is primarily about compensation or prestige, that you are anxious about the outcome, or that you do not understand professional norms in banking. None of these are impressions you want to leave on the way out.

According to Wall Street Oasis, the most commonly reported feedback from senior bankers after superdays who passed on an otherwise qualified candidate often includes something about "the questions they asked at the end felt off" or "they seemed more interested in the offer than the work." This is a consistent pattern across firms and seniority levels.

---

## The Principle: Make Your Questions Specific, Not Generic

The difference between a good closing question and a bad one is almost always specificity. "What do you enjoy most about working here?" is a question every candidate asks and every interviewer has answered three hundred times. It signals nothing positive and takes up time that could be used more effectively.

"I noticed your group closed the [specific deal] last year with a somewhat unconventional structure - the seller retained a minority stake rather than the typical clean exit. Is that kind of creative structuring something the group gets asked to do regularly, or was that a one-off driven by the situation?" is a question almost nobody asks, it signals real preparation, and it invites the interviewer to share actual expertise.

Specificity is the variable. Generic questions produce generic (or silently negative) outcomes.

## Questions to Ask Analysts and Associates

Junior bankers are your most honest source of information about day-to-day reality. They are also less likely to be put off by genuine questions about the work itself. This is the interviewer tier where you have the most latitude to ask questions with real operational texture.

**Strong questions for junior bankers:**

"I have been reading about the analytical differences between [sector-specific deal type] and more standard M&A - where does the modeling actually get more complicated in practice, and how long did it take you to feel comfortable with it?"

"When you think back to the first six months, what was the thing that took longest to really understand - the kind of thing that is hard to prepare for before starting?"

"I have been doing a lot of work with BIWS and Macabacus models on my own. In your experience, what does the bank's internal model methodology look like compared to those training models? Are there major structural differences?"

"What is a deal you worked on in the last year that you found genuinely interesting from a problem-solving standpoint - and what made it interesting?"

These questions work because they invite honest, specific answers about real work. Junior bankers generally like talking about deals they found interesting and are often candid about what the learning curve looks like. The conversation becomes a genuine exchange rather than an interview performance.

---

## Questions to Ask Vice Presidents

VPs occupy the middle layer - too senior to be in the weeds on every model, but close enough to recent deal execution to speak to it credibly. They are often responsible for the day-to-day management of analyst and associate teams, which means they have strong opinions about what makes a junior banker effective.

**Strong questions for VPs:**

"From your vantage point managing the team, what are the things that separate analysts who develop quickly from those who plateau - is it primarily technical, or is it something else?"

"Your group has been active in [specific sub-sector] over the past two years. Do you see that continuing as a major source of deal flow, or is there a thesis emerging around a different area that is starting to build?"

"I read that your group was involved in [specific transaction or mandate]. Without getting into anything non-public, how does the pitch process for a mandate like that typically work? Who on the coverage side is leading versus product specialists?"

"In your experience, what is the hardest thing to teach junior bankers that they cannot easily learn from materials - the kind of judgment that only comes from being in the room?"

VPs are also evaluating whether you are going to be manageable and effective. Questions that demonstrate you have thought about what it means to develop as a banker - not just to get the offer - register positively with this group.

---

## Questions to Ask Managing Directors

Senior bankers are evaluating something different from junior bankers. They care less about your technical proficiency (which the VPs will have assessed) and more about your judgment, your intellectual character, and whether you have the profile to grow into a client-facing role eventually. Your questions for them should reflect that shift.

**Strong questions for MDs:**

"As someone who has been advising clients through multiple cycles, how do you see the current environment changing what clients are asking you to help them think through versus five years ago? Is there a structural shift in how companies are approaching strategic decisions?"

"I am genuinely interested in [specific sector the MD covers]. What is the dynamic in that sector right now that is most underappreciated by people outside of it - the thing that changes your view of what is actually happening versus what is being reported?"

"When you think about the analysts and associates who went on to become effective deal-makers versus those who left the industry, what do you think was the actual differentiating factor - talent, work ethic, something else?"

"What has changed most about how the advisory business works in the past decade - from your seat?"

These questions invite MDs to share perspective and experience rather than evaluate you on knowledge. Senior bankers often enjoy this kind of question more than standard interview questions. You are treating them as a source of genuine insight, not just as a hurdle to clear.

---

## Questions That Work Across All Levels

Some questions are durable regardless of seniority:

"What do you think is most commonly misunderstood about this group from the outside?"

"What made you specifically choose [this bank] over other options when you were at my stage?"

"Is there a deal or a situation from your career that you would describe as genuinely formative - something that changed how you approach the work?"

The last question in particular tends to generate memorable answers and genuinely useful conversations. People enjoy talking about experiences that shaped them professionally. It produces the kind of mutual exchange that is more likely to leave a positive lasting impression than a technically correct but clinical conversation.

## Three Questions to Never Ask

These are the questions that experienced interviewers hear regularly and that consistently register negatively. Some are in this category because they signal the wrong priorities. Others because they signal poor judgment about professional norms. All of them are worth knowing and avoiding.

**1. "How long does the hiring process take, and when will I hear back?"**

This question signals anxiety about the outcome rather than interest in the work. Interviewers understand that candidates are waiting to hear back - they were once candidates too. Asking the question does not accelerate the process and makes you look like you are managing your own anxiety rather than engaging in a professional conversation.

If you genuinely need to know the timeline because you have a competing offer, that is a conversation to have with the recruiting coordinator, not in the closing minutes of an interview with a senior banker.

**2. "What is the work-life balance like here?"**

This question, asked at this stage, signals that your primary concern is lifestyle rather than the work. It is a legitimate question in the abstract - work intensity in investment banking is a real variable that matters for your life. But it is not a question you ask in an interview for a role with well-documented hours expectations. You already know banking is demanding. Asking about balance implies you may not be fully committed to those demands, which is not the note to end on.

If you have genuine concerns about culture and sustainability, explore them through networking calls with junior bankers who will give you honest answers - not in formal interview rounds.

**3. "I noticed [competitor firm] has been more active in this sector - why do you think that is?"**

Some candidates ask competitive comparison questions thinking they demonstrate market awareness. They do not. They put the interviewer in the position of either defending their firm or criticizing a competitor on the record, neither of which they want to do. The question also subtly implies that you might prefer the competitor - not the message to send in the final minutes of an interview.

Market dynamics and competitive positioning are interesting subjects. There are ways to engage with them that do not implicitly frame your interviewer's employer as the weaker option.

---

## Practical Considerations: Quantity and Timing

Ask two to three questions per interviewer. One question is too few - it signals disengagement. Five questions risks running over the allotted time and losing the interviewer's attention before you finish.

If you are in a superday with eight rounds, you cannot ask the same two questions in every round if the interviewers are likely to compare notes. Have a set of eight to ten strong questions and rotate through them. You will naturally get into real conversations that consume some of your question time, so the rotation will not be mechanical.

Prepare at least two interviewer-specific questions based on your research before the day. If you know you will be meeting with someone who has publicly discussed a sector or deal, have a question that references that directly. This level of preparation is rare and is uniformly appreciated.

---

## A Note on Authenticity

There is a version of this advice that turns into performance - candidates asking "smart questions" from a script without genuine curiosity behind them. Experienced bankers can detect this. The best closing questions come from actually thinking about the work and the firm before you walk in.

As one WSO contributor put it, reflecting on their own hiring experience: "The candidates I still remember years later were the ones who asked questions I had not been asked before - questions that made me think for a second before answering. That is a hard bar to meet but the effect is real."

You cannot fake that kind of preparation. The questions in this guide are a starting framework. Adapt them to your actual research and your genuine interests in the firm and the work.

---

## What You Should Do Next

1. **Research every interviewer you can identify before your superday.** LinkedIn, deal announcements, conference panels, anything you can find. For each person, identify one specific thing they have worked on that you can formulate a genuine question about.

2. **Build a question bank of 10 to 12 questions** organized by seniority tier - analyst/associate questions, VP questions, MD questions, and a few that work across levels. Know them well enough that they feel natural rather than rehearsed.

3. **Practice closing questions with a mock interview partner.** Ask them to respond to your questions as an interviewer would. Then evaluate: does the question generate a real conversation, or does it produce a flat two-sentence answer? Revise accordingly.

4. **Eliminate the three never-ask questions from your repertoire completely.** They are common enough that you might slip into them under fatigue. Consciously remove them as options so they do not surface in hour six of a superday.

5. **Prepare a bank-specific question about a recent deal or strategic development.** Every bank has current news - a recent high-profile mandate, a strategic hire, a market positioning decision. A question about something that happened in the last 90 days signals that your interest is current and specific.
