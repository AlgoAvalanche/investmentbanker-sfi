# Closing Questions to Ask Your Interviewer (and Three to Never Ask)

Every investment banking interview ends the same way: "Do you have any questions for me?" Most candidates treat this as a polite formality - a two-minute courtesy exchange before they walk out and check their phone. It is not. The closing questions you ask are the final data point in an interviewer's assessment of you. Getting them wrong at the end of an otherwise strong interview is not a theoretical risk. It happens constantly, and it costs offers.

Goldman Sachs received 315,126 internship applications in 2024 and hired approximately 2,600 people - a selection rate under one percent, with more than 70 candidates competing for every available seat [1]. At those odds, the closing Q&A is not a rounding error. It is a meaningful differentiator in a process where meaningful differentiators are rare.

This article covers the mechanics of closing questions: why they are weighted more heavily than most candidates realize, what strong questions actually signal to interviewers, a practical question bank organized by interviewer seniority, and three categories of questions that candidates ask regularly and should never ask again.

---

## Why Closing Questions Carry Disproportionate Weight

There is a well-documented principle in psychology about how people remember sequences: they retain the beginning and the end far more reliably than the middle. Interviewers are not exempt from this. Wall Street Prep notes explicitly that "first impressions and closing moments are the two most memorable parts of any interview" - the opening and the close receive disproportionate weight in how the conversation is ultimately evaluated [2].

This matters in a concrete, practical way. The technical portion of an IB interview is largely pass/fail - you either know your DCF cold or you do not. The closing Q&A is not graded on a binary scale. It is a qualitative assessment of judgment, curiosity, preparation, and cultural fit. Those are the variables that differentiate candidates who both cleared the technical bar - which, in a Superday, is most of the room.

Senior bankers at MentorMe Careers describe what they are actually evaluating during final-round and Superday interviews: genuine interest in the firm, cultural alignment, and evidence that a candidate intends to commit for two to three years minimum [3]. Closing questions are one of the few moments in the process where all three of those dimensions can be assessed simultaneously.

The practical implication is that the five to fifteen minutes at the end of each round - the window that most candidates treat as a formality - is actually the segment that carries the most judgment-dependent weight in the evaluation.

---

## The Governing Principle: Specificity Is Everything

The difference between a strong closing question and a weak one comes down almost entirely to specificity. Wall Street Prep is direct on this point: generic questions that can be answered by reading a firm's website are universally flagged as low-effort and signal inadequate preparation [2].

"What do you enjoy most about working here?" is a question every interviewer has answered three hundred times. It produces a rote answer, signals nothing positive about you, and consumes time that could be spent on something useful.

"I noticed your group advised on the Triton/Nordic acquisition last year with a seller-retained minority stake rather than a clean exit - is that kind of creative structuring something your clients are asking for more frequently, or was that situation-specific?" is a question almost nobody asks, it demonstrates genuine deal preparation, and it invites the interviewer to share real expertise.

Adventis Finance frames this as a "two-way street" dynamic: the questions candidates ask reveal their level of preparation and intellectual curiosity as clearly as their answers to technical questions do [4]. The best closing questions make this dynamic explicit - they show that you did the research and that you are genuinely engaged with the work, not just positioning for an offer.

Mergers and Inquisitions goes further, arguing that every strong closing question should be rooted in something specific to the individual interviewer - their prior employer, their career transition, their deal history - rather than generic questions that could be asked of any banker at any firm [5]. This approach signals three things simultaneously: you researched the person, you listened during the conversation, and your curiosity is genuine rather than scripted.

The baseline rule: prepare at least two interviewer-specific questions before every round. Look up the interviewer on LinkedIn, cross-reference their background against deal announcements and conference panels, and build questions that can only be asked of them. This level of preparation is rare in IB interviews. It is uniformly appreciated when it appears.

## Questions by Seniority: Analysts and Associates

Junior bankers are the most honest and operationally credible source of information about what the work actually looks like day to day. They are also less guarded than senior bankers, which means this is the tier where you have the most latitude to ask questions with real texture.

The Wall Street Oasis community - one of the most substantive practitioner forums on this topic - consistently recommends treating the post-interview Q&A as a genuine conversation rather than a rapid-fire checklist [6]. With junior bankers, this is easiest to execute because the conversational gap between you and them is smallest.

Strong questions for analysts and associates:

"When you think back to your first six months, what was the thing that took the longest to actually understand - the kind of gap that preparation materials do not cover?"

"What is a deal you worked on in the last year that you found genuinely interesting from a problem-solving standpoint, and what specifically made it interesting?"

"I have been doing independent work with BIWS models and Macabacus templates. How does the bank's internal model methodology compare in structure - are there major differences in how the outputs are organized or how assumptions are layered in?"

"What is the most useful thing a senior person on your team did for your development in the first year that you would not have expected going in?"

"I noticed you were at [prior firm / school / non-traditional background before banking] - how did you think about that transition and was there anything about it that surprised you once you were actually in the role?"

These questions work because they invite honest, specific answers about real work. Junior bankers generally enjoy talking about deals they found interesting and are often candid about what the learning curve actually looks like. The conversation becomes a genuine exchange rather than an interview performance on both sides.

One format note: prepare follow-up questions for the most likely answers. If an analyst describes a deal they found interesting, your follow-up - "What was the valuation methodology you led with on that one, and what made it the right approach for that situation?" - signals that you are genuinely tracking the answer and not just waiting for them to finish so you can ask the next scripted question.

## Questions by Seniority: Vice Presidents

VPs occupy the operational middle layer. They are too senior to be in the weeds on every model iteration, but close enough to recent deal execution to speak credibly to both the analytical and client management dimensions of the work. They are typically responsible for managing analyst and associate teams directly, which means they have formed strong views about what actually makes a junior banker effective versus what looks good on paper.

Strong questions for VPs:

"From your vantage point managing the team, what are the things that separate analysts who develop quickly from those who plateau - is it primarily technical ability, or is there something else that turns out to matter more?"

"Your group has been active in [specific sub-sector] over the past two years. Do you see that continuing as a major source of deal flow, or is there a thesis emerging around a different area that is starting to build momentum?"

"I read that your group was involved in [specific transaction]. Without getting into anything non-public, how does the pitch process for a mandate like that typically work - who on the coverage side leads versus product specialists, and how does the group decide when to bring in capital markets versus M&A?"

"In your experience, what is the hardest thing to teach junior bankers that they cannot learn from any materials - the kind of judgment that only comes from actually being in the room?"

"How has the relationship between coverage and product groups at your bank evolved over the past few years? Are there structural changes in how the groups collaborate on deals?"

VPs are also evaluating whether you have thought seriously about development - not just about getting the offer. Questions that demonstrate you understand the difference between being technically competent and becoming effective in the role register well with this group. The framing matters: you are not asking what the firm can do for you, you are demonstrating that you have thought about what the work actually requires.

## Questions by Seniority: Managing Directors

Senior bankers are evaluating something different. By the time a candidate reaches an MD in a Superday, the technical box has largely been checked. MDs care about judgment, intellectual character, and whether a candidate has the raw material to grow into a client-facing role over time. The questions you ask them should reflect that shift in register.

Strong questions for MDs:

"As someone who has been advising clients through multiple cycles, how do you see the current environment changing what clients are actually asking you to help them think through - is there a structural shift in how companies are approaching strategic decisions compared to five years ago?"

"I am genuinely interested in [specific sector the MD covers]. What is the dynamic in that sector right now that is most underappreciated by people outside of it - the thing that changes how you see what is actually happening versus what is being reported publicly?"

"When you think about analysts and associates who went on to become effective deal-makers versus those who left the industry, what was the actual differentiating factor - was it talent, work ethic, something about how they handled adversity, or something else entirely?"

"What has changed most about how the advisory business works in the last decade - from your specific seat? And is there anything about how the next generation of bankers is approaching the work that you find encouraging or concerning?"

"I noticed you moved from [prior bank / role] to this group. What was the deciding factor in that move, and has it played out the way you expected?"

These questions invite MDs to share perspective and experience rather than evaluate you on knowledge. Senior bankers often respond well to this format because it treats them as a source of genuine insight rather than as a final gatekeeping hurdle. The dynamic shifts from interview to conversation, and that shift is more likely to produce a memorable closing impression than any technically correct but clinical exchange.

## Questions That Work Across All Seniority Levels

Some questions hold up regardless of who you are asking them:

"What do you think is most commonly misunderstood about this group from the outside - by people who are interested in the work but have not been inside it?"

"What made you specifically choose this bank over other options when you were at the stage I am at now?"

"Is there a deal or situation from your career you would describe as genuinely formative - something that changed how you approach the work or how you think about advising clients?"

"What is it about my background or experience that made this group want to have a conversation?" (This question, documented by the WSO practitioner community, is psychologically sharp: it prompts the interviewer to articulate reasons to hire you, reinforcing your value in their own mind at the most memorable moment of the conversation [6].)

"Is there anything about the group, the role, or the industry that you think I should know - something that would not come up in a standard interview process?"

That last question is one of the most consistently effective questions in this entire list. It gives the interviewer agency, signals intellectual humility, and can surface information no candidate would think to ask for. It also functions as a natural close - a way of handing the interviewer the last word while positioning yourself as thorough and genuinely open.

## Three Questions to Never Ask

These are the questions that experienced interviewers hear regularly and that consistently register negatively. Some are problematic because they signal the wrong priorities. Others because they signal poor judgment about professional norms. All of them appear on this list because they are common enough that candidates - including strong candidates - slip into them under interview fatigue.

**1. "How long does the process take, and when will I hear back?"**

This question signals that you are managing anxiety about the outcome rather than engaging in a professional conversation about the work. Interviewers understand candidates are waiting to hear back - they were candidates once. Asking does not accelerate the timeline and creates the impression that the offer is your primary preoccupation. If you genuinely need timeline information because of a competing offer, have that conversation with the recruiting coordinator, not in the closing minutes of an interview with a senior banker.

**2. "What is the work-life balance like here?"**

Investment banking's intensity is not a secret. Asking about balance in a formal interview round signals that you may not be fully committed to the demands of the role - which is not the impression to leave on the way out. This is a legitimate subject to explore. Explore it through networking conversations with junior bankers who will give you honest answers. It is not a question for a Superday interview with an MD. MentorMe Careers flags this explicitly: senior bankers evaluate for genuine interest in the work and long-term commitment, and balance questions undercut both signals simultaneously [3].

**3. "I noticed [competitor bank] has been more active in this sector - why do you think that is?"**

Candidates ask competitive comparison questions because they believe they demonstrate market awareness. They do not land that way. They put the interviewer in the position of either defending their firm or criticizing a competitor on the record - neither of which they want to do in an interview setting. The question also implies, however subtly, that you may prefer the competitor. That is not the signal to send in the final minutes of a conversation that is still deciding whether to extend you an offer.

## How Many Questions, and How to Use Them

Ask two to three questions per interviewer. One question is too few and signals disengagement. Five questions risks running past the allotted time and losing the interviewer's attention before you finish.

In a Superday with eight rounds, you cannot ask the same two questions in every room if the interviewers compare notes - which they do. Build a bank of ten to twelve strong questions organized by seniority tier and rotate through them. You will naturally get into real conversations that consume part of your question time, so the rotation will not feel mechanical.

Prepare at least two interviewer-specific questions for each person you know you will be meeting. Cross-reference LinkedIn, deal announcements, industry conference panels, and press coverage. A question referencing something that happened in the last ninety days - a recent mandate, a strategic hire, a market positioning move - signals that your interest is current and not generic.

## A Note on Authenticity

There is a version of this guidance that turns into performance - candidates running scripted "smart questions" without genuine curiosity behind them. Experienced bankers detect this. The questions feel hollow in a way that is hard to articulate but easy to sense.

The best closing questions come from actually thinking about the firm and the work before you walk in. They come from reading the group's recent deals and forming a genuine view about what made them interesting or unusual. They come from looking at an interviewer's background and being genuinely curious about a transition or a decision they made.

As Mergers and Inquisitions frames it, the candidates who stand out are the ones whose questions are rooted in specific research and genuine intellectual engagement - not the ones running a checklist [5]. That bar is achievable. Most candidates skip the research entirely and pay for it at the exact moment they could have left a lasting impression.

You cannot fake the preparation. The question bank in this guide is a framework. Adapt it to your actual research, your genuine interests in the firm, and the specific people you are sitting across from.

---

## What to Do Before Your Next Interview

**1. Research every interviewer you can identify before the day.** LinkedIn, deal announcements, conference panels, any public record. For each person, identify one thing they have worked on or said that generates a genuine question from you.

**2. Build a question bank of ten to twelve questions** organized by tier - analyst and associate questions, VP questions, MD questions, and a set that works across levels. Know them well enough that they feel natural rather than recited.

**3. Practice closing questions in mock interviews.** Ask a partner to respond as an interviewer would. Evaluate whether the question generates a real conversation or a flat two-sentence answer. Revise accordingly.

**4. Eliminate the three never-ask questions from your options entirely.** They are common enough that you might default to them under fatigue in hour six of a Superday. Remove them as available choices so they cannot surface.

**5. Prepare at least one bank-specific question about a development from the last ninety days.** Every bank has current news. A question about something recent signals that your preparation is active and specific, not archived from a recruiting prep deck.

The closing Q&A is not a formality. Prepare for it with the same rigor you bring to valuation questions. The final impression is the one that lasts.
